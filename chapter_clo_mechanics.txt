CLO MECHANICS AND IMPACT ON LEVERAGED FINANCE

PART ONE: INTRODUCTION

When I first started in leveraged finance in the late 1990s, the dominant buyers of bank loans were actually banks. That seems almost quaint to say now. Chase, Citigroup, Bank of America—these institutions held the bulk of corporate loan portfolios on their balance sheets. CLOs existed, but they were a niche product, viewed by many senior lenders as something the investment banking division did on the side. I remember a partner at my firm in 2002 dismissing CLOs as a "securitization gimmick" that would never rival traditional bank lending. He would not make the same joke twenty years later.

Today, CLOs—collateralized loan obligations—are the dominant institutional buyer of leveraged loans in the United States market. Conservative estimates suggest that CLOs have purchased 60 to 65 percent of all syndicated leveraged loans issued over the past five years. In some recent vintages, particularly 2021 and in the recovery periods of 2023-2024, that percentage has exceeded 70 percent. This is not a marginal buyer. This is the buyer. And if you're a banker executing a leveraged finance transaction in 2026, you are fundamentally a merchant serving CLO portfolios. Understanding why is not optional anymore—it's the baseline competency that separates practitioners who can anticipate market moves from those who get blindsided by them.

My education on this came the hard way. In 2005, I was working on a mid-market leveraged buyout—a $450 million facility for a specialty chemicals company that my firm was financing. We had structured what we thought was a compelling term loan: floating rate, call-protected for eighteen months, with a 400 basis point spread over LIBOR. The loan was covenant-lite, which was increasingly becoming the norm for mid-market leveraged loans. My team was confident we could place the loan with our traditional bank lender base. We had commitments from three major banks, and I thought the deal was largely done.

What I didn't understand at the time—and what almost every banker missed in that era—was that the gravitational center of loan demand was shifting. A CLO manager I'd never heard of, someone from a second-tier shop in Greenwich, came into our syndication meeting and asked a seemingly simple question: "How does your 400 basis point spread work in our portfolio relative to our target weighted average spread of 375?" I remember thinking it was a weird question. Either the spread was 400 or it wasn't. Why would the spread calculation be different inside a CLO?

This question led me down a rabbit hole. I spent weeks learning about CLO tests, weighted average spread calculations, overcollateralization ratios, and interest coverage ratios. And it fundamentally changed how I approached deal structuring. The 400 basis point spread was, in reality, worth less inside a CLO portfolio because of how spreads are calculated for WAS testing—the weighted average spread test that sits at the heart of most CLO compliance. With the loan's shorter reinvestment period and certain structural features, it was really more like 385 basis points for CLO purposes. That mattered. It mattered enough that a major CLO manager would pass on the deal unless we reconsidered the structure.

That experience—being corrected by someone operating within a framework I didn't fully understand—was humbling. But it was also the moment I realized that the commercial landscape of leveraged finance had already fundamentally changed, and the people still operating off the old playbook simply hadn't caught up yet. The bank lending framework, with its focus on covenant packages and collateral coverage, was becoming almost quaint. What mattered now was whether a deal worked in a CLO waterfall, whether the economics made sense to CLO equity investors, and whether a CLO manager could include the loan in their portfolio without triggering test failures that would restrict their reinvestment capacity.

I spent the next several years becoming obsessive about understanding CLO mechanics. I read indentures—hundreds of them. I modeled CLO waterfalls. I attended CLO conferences that most bankers never bothered with. I developed relationships with CLO managers, both the tier-one shops like Benefit Street Partners, Lighthouse Capital, and Oaktree, and the second-tier managers who were increasingly important parts of the buying syndicate. What I discovered was that CLO managers weren't passive investors—they were sophisticated operators with very specific constraints, preferences, and deal-structuring requirements.

Understanding CLOs became the professional equivalent of learning a new language. And once you learned it, every conversation you had about leveraged finance meant something different. Why would a sponsor accept a particular covenant package in a negotiation? Partly because they wanted to run the company with more flexibility, yes. But increasingly because CLO managers had told the arranger that they wouldn't include the loan if covenants were tighter. Why would a Term Loan B be structured with a two-year reinvestment period instead of three? Because that distinction mattered enormously to CLO economics, and CLO managers who owned most of the loan had specific preferences. Why would an add-on acquisition in an already-levered company require a whole new syndication? Because CLOs in the original facility had specific limitations on how much additional debt the company could incur, and adding debt required buying new tranches in the CLO's portfolio.

The ignorance of CLO mechanics costs money. It costs real money. I've seen bankers misprice transactions by 50 basis points or more because they didn't understand how CLO test requirements would affect deal structure. I've seen sponsors leave tens of millions of dollars on the table because they didn't realize that a particular documentation approach would make their loan "ineligible" for CLO portfolios, limiting demand and driving down pricing. I've seen deals fail to syndicate because the arranger didn't understand why a particular CLO manager was passing on the deal—it wasn't credit risk, it was a technical test failure that three weeks of restructuring could have fixed.

This chapter is my attempt to pass along twenty years of lessons about how CLOs work and why they matter so much. I'm not writing this for academics or for people who might become academics. I'm writing for practitioners—bankers, sponsors, credit officers, investors, debt advisors. If you're going to operate effectively in leveraged finance in the 2020s, you need to understand CLOs the way a cardiologist understands the heart. Not just that it pumps blood, but exactly how the valves work, what makes them fail, and how the whole system adapts when conditions change.

The stakes are meaningful. A miscalculation about CLO demand can lead to a deal failing to syndicate. Misunderstanding CLO test mechanics can lead to structuring a facility in a way that creates unnecessary drag on returns. Not knowing what a CLO manager cares about means you're flying blind in critical negotiation moments with buyers who collectively own over 60 percent of the institutional loan market. These aren't academic concerns. They're commercial realities that affect your career, your firm's revenue, and your client's ability to execute their financing strategy.

What follows is my attempt to walk through CLO mechanics in a way that's both technically rigorous and practically grounded. I'll explain how CLOs work, how they're tested, what their constraints are, and how all of that filters down to affect every conversation about leveraged loans. I'll use real examples and real numbers because this is a field where the details matter. And I'll share some of the lessons that twenty years in this business has taught me about how markets work, how institutions behave, and how the people who understand the plumbing of the system tend to succeed while those who don't gradually get squeezed out.

PART TWO: CLO STRUCTURE

To understand CLOs, you first need to understand what they actually are. A CLO is a securitization—a special purpose vehicle that buys a portfolio of corporate loans and finances that purchase by issuing tranches of debt and equity. The key insight is that CLOs exist to separate loans from the banks that originated them. For decades, banks kept loans on their balance sheets. CLOs let banks originate loans and immediately sell them, freeing up balance sheet capacity to originate more. From a CLO investor's perspective, you're buying a structured investment—a series of claims on the cash flows from a diversified portfolio of corporate loans, with senior claims protected by subordinated claims.

The basic structure looks like a pyramid. At the top are the most senior notes—typically AAA-rated debt obligations that are collateralized by the loan portfolio. These AAA notes have the first claim on all cash flows generated by the underlying loans. They are, effectively, the least risky securities in the capital structure. Below the AAA notes are AA-rated notes, then A-rated notes, then BBB-rated notes, then BB-rated notes, and so on down the capital structure. Each successive tranche has a lower credit rating and a higher expected return because it is further removed from the top of the waterfall and thus bears more credit risk.

At the very bottom of the capital structure sit the equity holders. The equity holders have the last claim on any cash flows—they only get paid if all the debt tranches above them are paid in full. This structure creates what's called a "waterfall" of payments. Every month or quarter, cash flows from the underlying loan portfolio flow into the CLO's account. The CLO uses that cash to pay (in this order): trustee and administrative fees, investment adviser fees, the AAA noteholders, the AA noteholders, the A noteholders, and so on down the waterfall. Only after every debt tranche is satisfied can the equity holders receive distributions.

This waterfall structure is absolutely crucial to understanding how CLOs behave. It creates an economic incentive structure where the equity holders want as much cash flow as possible from the loan portfolio, while the debt noteholders want stability and certainty of payment. The CLO manager—the firm that actually selects which loans go into the portfolio and manages them over time—is typically compensated through both a management fee (usually around 60 to 75 basis points of assets) and an incentive fee that's tied to the equity holders' returns. This means the CLO manager is fundamentally aligned with equity holders, not with debt noteholders.

Let me walk through the economics of how a typical CLO is formed, because the formation process itself reveals a lot about how CLOs operate and what they're designed to do. The process starts with what's called the "warehouse period." The CLO manager doesn't yet have the finalized portfolio selected. Instead, they're given authority to buy loans over a period of time—typically six months to two years—while they accumulate the pool of assets that will eventually be securitized. During the warehouse period, the CLO manager is making judgment calls about what loans to buy based on their view of credit quality, spreads, pricing, and portfolio fit.

I remember watching a top-tier CLO manager, someone from a shop that was managing over $50 billion of CLO assets globally, make decisions during the warehouse period for one of their vintage CLOs around 2018. They were being very careful about what they were buying. They had the capacity to buy in size, to take down a $250 million piece of a syndication if they wanted to. But they were selective. Some deals they passed on entirely because they weren't comfortable with the sponsor or the industry. Other deals they sized down even when they could have taken more. Some deals they only bid on at tight enough pricing that they wouldn't necessarily get them. This wasn't randomness. This was a manager who understood that the loans going into their securitization in 2018 would still be in that portfolio in 2023, and they were thinking carefully about what would work during a potential downturn.

Once the warehouse period ends and the loan portfolio is assembled, the CLO goes through what's called the "ramp-up" period. During ramp-up, which typically lasts 12 to 18 months, the CLO manager is still buying loans, but at a slower pace. The goal is to gradually deploy the capital that was raised through the issuance of CLO debt and equity tranches. The reason for this staged approach—warehouse, then ramp-up—is that it allows the CLO manager to average into positions and to gradually build a diversified portfolio rather than trying to deploy the entire amount upfront.

After the ramp-up period ends comes the "reinvestment period," which is typically five to seven years depending on the CLO's vintage and structure. During the reinvestment period, the CLO manager has significant latitude to actively manage the portfolio. They can sell loans and buy new ones. But—and this is important—they can only sell loans and buy new ones if the sales proceeds are reinvested in loans that fit certain criteria. These criteria are where a lot of the constraint comes in. The CLO manager can't just sell a position that's underperforming and put the proceeds in cash. They have to reinvest. And when they reinvest, they have to do it in a way that maintains the CLO's test compliance.

This is the moment where CLO test constraints start to bite in a very real way. The CLO manager has 150 loans in their portfolio, and they want to sell Loan A and buy Loan B. They have good credit reasons for this—maybe Loan A is deteriorating and they want to get out before it downgrades, while Loan B is a new fresh loan at better spreads. From a pure credit perspective, this is clearly the right call. But when they run the numbers, they discover that selling Loan A and buying Loan B would trigger an interest coverage test failure in one of the CLO's debt tranches. Suddenly, a trade that makes perfect sense from a credit perspective creates a compliance problem.

When this happens, the CLO manager has limited options. They can decide not to do the trade. They can scale it back—sell less of Loan A and buy less of Loan B. They can do a trade that involves selling multiple loans and buying multiple loans, in a way that the aggregate effect maintains test compliance. Or they can use "reinvestment period extension" language in their documents to buy themselves more time before they have to truly amortize and pay down the debt tranches.

The reinvestment period is typically five to seven years, though some CLOs have had reinvestment periods extended multiple times. Once the reinvestment period ends, the CLO enters the "amortization period," which lasts until maturity (typically ten years from issuance). During amortization, the CLO manager no longer has the freedom to reinvest—sales proceeds are used to pay down the CLO's debt tranches, starting with the most senior tranches. There's no more active trading. The manager is just collecting cash flow from the portfolio and using it to pay down debt.

The economic significance of when a CLO transitions from reinvestment to amortization is hard to overstate. During reinvestment, an underwater position in a loan can potentially be worked out through new purchases that improve the overall portfolio. During amortization, an underwater position is locked in. You own it, it's worth less than you paid for it, and the proceeds from its sale are being used to pay down senior debt rather than supporting equity returns. This is why CLO managers become very focused on loan sales as the end of the reinvestment period approaches. They're racing to exit positions that aren't working, to rotate into new assets, and to position the portfolio for amortization.

Now, let's talk about the actual entity structure of a CLO, because it's been shaped by decades of legal architecture designed to solve specific problems. A typical CLO is established as a special purpose vehicle incorporated in the Cayman Islands. The Cayman SPV is the entity that issues the debt and equity tranches and holds the loan portfolio. But because US investors need to invest in something that's "US-regulated," there's typically also a Delaware-incorporated co-issuer entity that's a subsidiary of the Cayman SPV.

The reason for this dual-entity structure goes back to bankruptcy law. The idea is that the Cayman SPV is bankruptcy remote—meaning if the CLO manager itself goes into bankruptcy, there's no way for the manager's creditors to access the CLO's assets. The assets are truly segregated. The Delaware co-issuer exists partly as a comfort to US investors and partly because of specific tax and regulatory requirements around how CLOs are taxed and regulated.

The CLO is governed by an indenture—a comprehensive legal document that can run to 500 pages or more, depending on complexity. The indenture specifies basically everything: what kinds of loans can be bought, what the test requirements are, what happens if tests are breached, how distributions are made, what happens in a default scenario, when the CLO matures, and what happens if the manager needs to be removed or replaced. The indenture is negotiated during the formation of the CLO between the CLO manager (and the sponsors who want to see a certain profile), the trustee (who represents the noteholders), and the rating agencies (who set the rating criteria that will determine how the tranches are priced).

One of the critical provisions in the indenture is what's called the "action items list"—the things the CLO manager is explicitly allowed to do and not allowed to do. For example, a typical indenture might specify that the manager can buy loans with spreads as low as 250 basis points (though in 2023-2024 I've seen some CLOs with 225 bps minimums), but not lower. It might specify that they can only buy first-lien term loans, not second lien. It might specify that they can't have more than a certain percentage of the portfolio in any single industry, or more than a certain percentage in loans rated below CCC. These provisions are really the CLO manager's guardrails.

What's important to understand as a banker is that these guardrails are both a constraint and a market signal. When you're trying to syndicate a loan, knowing what the constraints are allows you to position your deal in a way that maximizes CLO demand. If you know that CLOs won't buy loans with spreads below 250 basis points, then pricing your deal at 240 basis points is pointless—you're just leaving money on the table and not actually expanding demand. If you know that CLOs in the current cycle are worried about CCC-rated loans and you're putting in a heavily CCC-focused facility, you're going to have trouble selling the higher tranches.

The role of the CLO manager is worth spending time on because it's often misunderstood. A CLO manager is not a passive investor who buys the loan portfolio once and then just collects coupons. A CLO manager is an active allocator who is constantly making decisions about what to buy, what to sell, how to position the portfolio, and how to navigate the complex test structure that governs their behavior. Some CLO managers are quite hands-on, looking at every loan opportunity in the market and making judgment calls about where to allocate capital. Other managers are more passive, following relatively simple rules about what spreads and structures they'll buy. But all CLO managers are operating within a framework where their job is to generate cash flows that can support the debt tranches while maximizing returns to the equity holders they're aligned with.

The typical CLO manager compensation structure is worth understanding in detail because it drives a lot of behavior. The manager typically gets two fees. The first is a "senior management fee," which is typically 60 to 75 basis points of the current par value of the loan portfolio (or sometimes the current market value, though par is more common). This fee is paid to the manager regardless of performance—it's what they use to cover basic operating costs. The second fee is a "subordinated management fee," which is sometimes in the range of 25 to 40 basis points and is paid from the equity returns. This fee is only paid if the CLO is generating equity returns. If the CLO is underwater on a mark-to-market basis, the subordinated fee doesn't get paid.

Then there's the incentive fee, which is the real driver of manager behavior. The incentive fee is typically structured as a percentage of equity returns—something in the range of 20 to 25 percent of outperformance above a threshold. The idea is that if the CLO generates great returns for equity holders, the manager gets paid a meaningful percentage of that outperformance. This aligns the manager's interests with the equity holders.

What this compensation structure creates is a very specific economic incentive. The manager is paid a base fee regardless of performance, but they can significantly increase their compensation if they generate great returns. This creates an incentive to take risk—to find the highest-returning opportunities within the constraint framework they're operating in. It also creates an incentive to keep assets in the portfolio that are generating good spread rather than to sell and redeploy to newer, tighter assets. From an equity holder perspective, this can be great—they're getting a manager who's incentivized to generate returns. From a debt holder perspective, there's a potential agency conflict—the manager is more incentivized by equity returns than by the stable payment of the senior tranches.

The equity investor's perspective is crucial to understand because it's really what drives CLO manager behavior at the margin. Who typically invests in CLO equity? Historically it's been a mix of the CLO sponsor, CLO managers themselves, insurance companies, pension funds, and sophisticated institutional investors who are comfortable taking credit risk in exchange for higher returns. The equity holders know that they have the last claim on cash flows—they're taking the first-loss position. But if everything works out, they get levered returns. If a CLO is issued with a 12:1 leverage ratio (12 times levered), and the underlying loan portfolio generates 500 basis points of spread, the equity holders can potentially generate returns well in excess of the spread—they're getting 500 basis points on their equity capital, but the debt is only costing 200-300 basis points, so the 200-300 basis point difference flows to equity.

This leverage and spread arbitrage is what makes CLO equity attractive. It's also what drives CLO manager behavior. The manager wants to maximize spread because every basis point of additional spread flows through to equity returns. The manager wants to buy par or close to par because buying at a discount creates negative carry (you're paying interest on debt financing an asset that's worth less than you paid for it). The manager wants to avoid credit problems because defaults shrink the portfolio value. And the manager wants to extend the reinvestment period when possible because reinvestment gives them more flexibility to adapt to changing market conditions.

PART THREE: CLO ECONOMICS

To truly understand how CLOs behave in markets, you need to understand their economics in detail. Let me walk through a simplified example of a CLO's profit and loss account, using realistic numbers from the mid-2020s market.

Imagine we're issuing a CLO with $500 million of par value loans in the portfolio. The weighted average spread of those loans is 425 basis points. That means the portfolio is generating $21.25 million of annual spread income (500 million times 4.25 percent).

Now, to finance the purchase of those $500 million of loans, the CLO manager issued securities. Let's say the capital structure looks like this:

- AAA notes: $375 million, priced at 150 basis points
- AA notes: $50 million, priced at 300 basis points
- A notes: $40 million, priced at 500 basis points
- BBB notes: $20 million, priced at 800 basis points
- Subordinated notes: $10 million, priced at 1200 basis points
- Equity: $5 million (this is unfunded and comes from the equity sponsor)

The weighted average cost of the debt tranches is approximately:
(375 * 1.5 + 50 * 3.0 + 40 * 5.0 + 20 * 8.0 + 10 * 12.0) / 495 = approximately 218 basis points

So the CLO is earning 425 basis points of spread and paying 218 basis points on its debt. That's a 207 basis point difference, which on $500 million is $10.35 million annually.

But that's the gross arbitrage. The CLO has costs. The senior management fee is typically around 70 basis points, which on $500 million is $3.5 million. The trustee fees and administrative costs might be another $500,000. The rating agency surveillance fees might be $250,000. So we're looking at roughly $4.25 million in annual fees and expenses.

$10.35 million gross arbitrage minus $4.25 million in fees leaves us with $6.1 million available to support the subordinated tranches and equity holders. This is where the test failures come in.

Remember those interest coverage and overcollateralization tests I mentioned? Let's say the subordinated notes in this CLO have an interest requirement of $120,000 per year. That leaves $5.98 million available for equity holders. If the equity holders invested $5 million, they're generating roughly 119 percent returns in year one (in the simplest model). That looks attractive.

But here's where real-world complexity comes in. First, the CLO manager is actively trading the portfolio. They're selling loans and buying new ones. Those trades might occur at different prices than the loans they're replacing. If they're selling at a discount (because the loan has gone down in value) and buying at par, the portfolio value is declining, which triggers overcollateralization test issues.

Second, loans are defaulting. In a typical CLO vintage, you might expect 1 to 3 percent of the portfolio to default per year, depending on the credit cycle. When a loan defaults, that $500 million par value shrinks. Let's say loans totaling $7.5 million default (1.5 percent). Those loans go into default and recovery processes. You might eventually recover 60 percent of par, so you recover $4.5 million and lose $3 million. That $3 million loss is typically absorbed by the equity holders (it reduces their distributions) but it might also impact whether the CLO can pass its interest coverage tests for the debt tranches.

Third, the underlying interest rate environment is changing. The CLO is financing itself on a floating-rate basis—the AAA notes are paying LIBOR + 150 basis points. If LIBOR goes up, the cost of the debt tranches goes up. If the loan portfolio reprices, the spread income might change too. Most loans in a CLO are floating-rate based on LIBOR or SOFR, so if rates stay stable, the spread income from the portfolio is relatively stable. But if rates move significantly, there can be basis risk or term structure issues.

Now, let me walk through a more realistic scenario that incorporates some of these dynamics. Let's say we're in year three of the CLO, and we want to understand how it's performing and what the equity holders are actually getting.

The original portfolio par was $500 million. Over three years:
- Loans worth $12 million have defaulted and been removed from the portfolio
- The CLO manager has sold loans worth $40 million (mostly at par, with $500k of realized losses)
- The CLO manager has bought new loans worth $39.5 million (bringing the portfolio back up)
- The current portfolio par value is $487 million

The weighted average spread of the current portfolio has changed slightly due to new purchases. Instead of 425 bps, it's now 420 bps. So the annual spread income on the current portfolio is about $20.46 million.

The cost of the debt tranches is relatively stable at about 218 bps, so the cost is about $10.62 million. But remember, some of the principal has been paid down. The AAA notes outstanding are now $370 million instead of $375 million. The AA notes are now $48.5 million. And so on.

After fees and expenses ($4.2 million annually), the gross available to subordinated tranches and equity is about $5.64 million. Let's say subordinated note interest is $115,000. That leaves about $5.52 million for equity.

But the equity holders also have to absorb the losses. That $12 million of defaulted loans—assuming 60 percent recovery, that's $7.2 million recovered and $4.8 million of credit losses. Some of that loss might hit the lower debt tranches first (if there's overcollateralization room), but ultimately, the equity holders are absorbing the shortfall.

Over three years, if we assume average annual credit losses of $1.6 million (losses net of recoveries), the equity holders have received about $16.5 million of distributions ($5.52 million per year minus $1.6 million of losses). They invested $5 million, so they've roughly tripled their money in three years, which is attractive but not extraordinary.

What this simplified analysis shows is that CLO equity returns are fundamentally tied to:
1. Spread generation (WAS)
2. Credit losses (how many loans default and what recovery rates are)
3. Portfolio turnover and trading gains/losses
4. The cost of the debt tranches (which is driven by market conditions and the leverage ratio)
5. Fees and administrative costs

CLO managers who generate superior returns are typically those who either:
1. Buy cheaper (get better execution on purchase prices relative to the market)
2. Have better credit insights (find loans that are less likely to default)
3. Trade more effectively (sell losers and buy winners, net of transaction costs)
4. Manage the leverage and capital structure intelligently (refinancing or repricing CLO liabilities when opportunities arise)

I want to note something that often gets overlooked: the real driver of CLO economics is the "net spread" available after all costs. In the example above, if that net spread is 207 basis points, that's the fundamental economic driver of equity returns. But that 207 basis point net spread isn't a constant. It can compress as spreads in the loan market tighten, as rates change the cost of CLO debt, or as defaults increase. A CLO manager issued in 2020 (when spreads were very wide due to COVID volatility) had much more net spread to work with than a CLO manager issued in 2022 (when spreads were tighter). This is why CLO vintage is such an important factor in understanding whether a CLO will ultimately perform well or not.

The behavior of CLO managers in the primary loan market is driven directly by these economics. If a CLO manager can generate 425 basis points of spread income by buying a large portfolio, they will do so because they've got leverage underneath it that amplifies returns. But if loan spreads start compressing toward 350 basis points, the net spread calculation becomes less attractive. At some point (usually somewhere around a weighted average spread of 375-400 basis points in the current market), CLO demand starts to cool because the economics aren't as compelling anymore. This is what I call the "CLO economics floor"—the point at which the spread-cost arbitrage becomes less attractive.

When loan spreads compress below this floor, one of two things happens. Either CLO issuance slows dramatically (because potential CLO sponsors realize the returns won't justify raising capital and paying fees), or CLOs start buying loans at discounts to par in order to boost the net spread calculation. Neither of these is particularly attractive from a primary market perspective. Slower CLO issuance means less demand for new loans, which pressures pricing. CLOs buying at discounts means the primary market gets weaker demand from the marginal buyer.

This is why spreads in the loan market have a natural floor related to CLO economics. Bankers and sponsors have to price loans wide enough that CLOs can make sense of them. If you price a loan at 360 basis points, CLO managers will look at it and realize that after leverage, their equity returns might only be 8-10 percent, which isn't worth the risk. So they'll pass. And if they pass, you don't have the dominant buyer for your syndication, which means you'll have to reprice and try again at higher spreads.

PART FOUR: CLO TESTS AND THEIR MARKET IMPACT

Now we get to the heart of how CLOs actually constrain markets: the test framework. CLOs are governed by a series of mathematical tests that must be satisfied at every distribution date (typically quarterly). If these tests are violated, there are contractual consequences that impact how the CLO operates and what returns the equity holders receive. Understanding these tests is not optional for anyone operating in leveraged finance.

The first and most important test is the Overcollateralization (OC) test. The OC test is essentially a coverage ratio that says: the current par value of the loan portfolio must be at least a certain multiple higher than the outstanding principal of the debt tranches. A typical CLO might have an OC test that requires the portfolio par value to be 1.20 times the outstanding principal of the senior tranches (AAA notes), 1.15 times the senior and AA notes combined, 1.10 times the senior, AA, and A notes combined, and so on down the capital structure.

Let me give a concrete example. Let's say a CLO has:
- AAA notes outstanding: $300 million
- AA notes outstanding: $50 million
- A notes outstanding: $30 million
- BBB notes outstanding: $15 million
- Subordinated notes outstanding: $5 million
- Total debt outstanding: $400 million

The portfolio currently has $490 million par value in loans. Is the OC test passing?

AAA OC test: Is $490M >= 1.20 x $300M = $360M? Yes, it passes.
AA OC test: Is $490M >= 1.15 x $350M = $402.5M? Yes, it passes.
A OC test: Is $490M >= 1.10 x $380M = $418M? No, it fails.

If the A OC test is failing, there's a contractual consequence. The most important consequence is that the CLO manager can no longer reinvest the principal repayments from the loans. Instead, those principal repayments are used to pay down the senior tranches. This means the portfolio is shrinking, which is a drag on returns. Additionally, some CLO documents specify that if certain OC tests fail, distributions to the equity holders are suspended until the test is cured.

This single constraint—the OC test—drives an enormous amount of behavior. CLO managers are constantly aware of where they stand relative to their OC tests. They know that if their portfolio value declines due to credit losses or market repricing, their OC test could fail. So they're proactively managing portfolio value. They're reluctant to sell loans at discounts because selling below par directly reduces the portfolio par value. They're using portfolio turnover strategically to manage the overall par value and the credit profile.

The second major test is the Interest Coverage (IC) test. The IC test compares the annual interest income from the portfolio to the annual interest expense on the debt tranches plus certain fees and reserves. A typical IC test might require that the interest income from the portfolio is at least 1.30 times the combined interest expense and fees. If the IC test fails, again, distributions to equity are restricted.

The IC test is driven by spread income. If the average spread in the portfolio is 425 basis points and the average cost of debt is 200 basis points, you have a 225 basis point spread cushion that can absorb some credit losses or fee increases. But if spreads compress to 400 basis points while the cost of debt stays at 200 basis points, the spread cushion shrinks. CLO managers are very focused on the weighted average spread (WAS) of their portfolios because it directly determines whether they're going to pass their IC tests.

This is where the market impact becomes very direct. If a CLO manager is concerned about IC test failures, they might decide they need to buy only loans with spreads of 400 basis points or higher. They might refuse to buy loans below a certain threshold. This sets a floor on spreads. If many CLO managers are all making similar decisions, the cumulative effect is that loan pricing has to be wide enough to satisfy CLO demand, or CLO demand simply evaporates.

The third major class of tests relates to portfolio quality. One of the most important is the Weighted Average Rating Factor (WARF) test. The WARF test essentially puts a constraint on how much of the portfolio can be in lower-rated loans. The idea is to prevent the CLO manager from loading up on sub-investment-grade debt.

The way WARF works is that each loan rating is assigned a numeric factor. A B+ loan might have a WARF factor of 1000. A B loan might have 1500. A B- loan might have 2000. A CCC loan might have 4000 or higher. The CLO takes the weighted average of these factors across the entire portfolio. If the weighted average exceeds a certain threshold (say, 1500), the test fails.

What this means practically is that a CLO manager can't just load up on CCC loans. If they're trying to extend the reinvestment period or boost spread income by buying higher-yielding CCC loans, they have to offset it with lower-rated loans in their portfolio. The WARF test acts as a constraint on how much sub-investment-grade exposure a CLO can take.

Then there's the diversification test, which is typically driven by geographic and industry concentration limits. A CLO might have a test that says no more than 5 percent of the portfolio can be in any single borrower, no more than 10 percent in any single industry, and no more than 15 percent in any single geographic region. These tests are designed to prevent a CLO from being overly concentrated in any particular credit risk.

The most contentious test in recent years has been the "CCC bucket" test. This is a specific constraint on how much of the portfolio can be in loans rated CCC or lower. Typically, a CLO might have a test that says no more than 7.5 percent of the portfolio par value can be in CCC-rated loans. But—and this is crucial—some CLO documents have a more stringent test on a "CCC excess" basis, where each CCC loan is valued at a discount to par. Let me explain what this means because it's one of the most important features of modern CLO structures.

Imagine a CLO has a CCC bucket limit of 7.5 percent at par. If the portfolio par is $500 million, they can have up to $37.5 million at par in CCC loans. But some CLO documents add a second constraint that says the CCC loans must be valued at 75 cents on the dollar for the purpose of the OC test. This means $37.5 million of CCC loans at par is treated as only $28.125 million of value for OC purposes. This effectively shrinks the available cushion and makes CLO managers more conservative about adding CCC exposure.

The reason this test exists is because CCC-rated loans are risky. They default more frequently, and when they do default, recovery rates tend to be lower. A CLO manager who loads up on CCC loans is taking on significant credit risk. The CCC bucket test and CCC excess mechanism force the manager to think carefully about how much CCC risk they want to take on.

Now, here's where the real market impact shows up. In a downturn, when loans are downgrading, the CCC bucket can become constrained. Let me describe what happened in 2020 during the COVID crisis to illustrate this.

In March 2020, as COVID hit and the economy shut down, a large number of loans were downgraded to CCC. This is normal in a severe downturn—when companies can't generate revenue, their credit ratings decline. But what happened next is less obvious. CLO managers who had previously loaded up on CCC loans found themselves bumping up against their CCC bucket limits. Some managers were at 7.0, 7.2, 7.4 percent. The test limits were 7.5 percent.

For some CLO managers, this created a genuine constraint. They had limited capacity to buy additional CCC loans. And they definitely didn't want to sell loans out of the portfolio because selling would trigger OC test issues. So they became forced buyers of non-CCC loans and forced sellers of any CCC loans that came up at attractive prices.

What this meant for borrowers in the primary market was significant. Companies that were being downgraded to CCC found that there was less natural demand for their loans in the secondary market (because CLO managers were full up on CCC exposure). This meant selling CCC loans required bigger discounts. The pricing pressure on CCC loans in 2020 was severe, and some of that was directly attributable to CLO CCC bucket constraints.

But here's the fascinating part: by late 2021 and into 2022, as the economy recovered and loans that had been downgraded to CCC started getting upgraded back to B or higher, the CCC bucket constraints eased. Suddenly, there was room in CLO portfolios again to buy CCC loans, and demand firmed. This is a great example of how test mechanics create cascading effects in markets—a technical constraint about how much CCC exposure a CLO can hold ends up affecting the pricing and demand for any loan rated in the CCC bucket.

The last major test I want to discuss is the Weighted Average Spread (WAS) test. Remember that interest coverage test I mentioned? Well, the way many CLO documents structure it is by setting a minimum weighted average spread requirement. They might say the portfolio must maintain a weighted average spread of at least 375 basis points. If the WAS falls below that threshold, the interest coverage test fails.

This test is absolutely critical to understanding CLO behavior in the primary market. It's the test that directly connects CLO manager behavior to syndication pricing. If the minimum WAS is 375 basis points, then CLO managers know they need to maintain an overall average spread of at least that level. They can buy some loans at 350 basis points if they're also buying loans at 400 basis points, but the weighted average has to work out.

In practice, this test means there's a floor on where loan spreads can compress. When a CLO manager is evaluating whether to commit capital to the market, they're running a calculation that says: "If we're planning to buy loans and maintain a WAS of 375 basis points, what's the tightest we can price the average loan?" If they can only achieve a WAS of 365 basis points at current market pricing, they'll pass on the market. This is a behavior I've observed repeatedly across multiple market cycles.

What Twenty Years Teaches: The test framework is the CLO manager's nervous system. Just like your nervous system tells your brain when you're in pain and need to change behavior, the CLO tests are constantly feeding back information to the CLO manager about whether their portfolio is healthy. The manager is absolutely focused on test compliance because test failure means loss of control (inability to reinvest, suspension of equity distributions, and potentially forced sales at bad prices). Understanding what tests a particular CLO is bumping up against is crucial to understanding how that manager will behave in the market. A manager who's full up on CCC exposure will act very differently than a manager with room. A manager who's tight on OC tests will be reluctant to add portfolio weight in ways that require more funding.

PART FIVE: THE CCC BUCKET AND DOWNGRADE RISK

Let me focus on a specific aspect of CLO mechanics that has outsized impact on markets: how CLOs treat CCC-rated loans, why downgrades to CCC matter so much, and what the market impact is.

CCC-rated loans are the junk of the junk—loans given to companies that are financially distressed or that have extremely high leverage. They're typically yielding 500-800 basis points or more (depending on the cycle), and they tend to default at significantly higher rates than investment-grade or B-rated loans. In a normal economy, CCC loans make up maybe 3-5 percent of the syndicated loan market. In stressed environments, they can expand to 10-15 percent.

The reason CCC loans exist at all is that some companies are legitimately riskier and warrant higher yields. But there's also a dynamic where CCC loans exist because companies that were previously rated B have deteriorated and gotten downgraded to CCC. These downgrades are particularly important to understand from a CLO perspective.

When a loan is rated CCC, it impacts CLO managers in several specific ways. First, it constrains how much CCC exposure the manager can hold relative to their CCC bucket test. Second, it affects the WARF test (remember, CCC loans have higher WARF factors). Third, and most importantly, it affects how the loan is valued in the CLO's OC test.

Here's the critical feature: in most modern CLO documents, CCC loans are valued at a discount to par for the purpose of the OC test. So if a loan is rated CCC, instead of being valued at 100 cents on the dollar, it might be valued at 80 cents on the dollar. Some CLO documents have even more severe haircuts—60 or 70 cents on the dollar for loans rated CCC- or below.

Why does this matter? Let me work through an example. Imagine a CLO has a portfolio par value of $500 million. Of that, $30 million is in CCC-rated loans (6 percent of par). If the OC test requires the portfolio to be valued at 1.10 times the outstanding senior debt of $450 million, that's $495 million. Let's calculate whether the test passes.

If all loans are valued at par: $500 million > $495 million. Test passes.

But now let's imagine the $30 million in CCC loans are valued at 80 cents on the dollar. The portfolio value is now: $470 million (non-CCC) + $24 million (CCC at 80%) = $494 million. Is $494 million > $495 million? No, it fails.

This is a massive constraint. It means that holding CCC loans is costly from an OC perspective because they're haircut. CLO managers have to either carry a higher OC cushion to absorb the haircut, or they have to limit how much CCC exposure they hold.

What happens then when a loan downgrades from B to CCC? The CLO manager's portfolio value suddenly shrinks. A $20 million loan that was being valued at par is suddenly being valued at 16 million (if the haircut is 20%). The portfolio value drops by $4 million. If the CLO was already tight on its OC test, that downgrade might trigger an OC test failure.

When an OC test fails, the consequences are real. The CLO manager can't reinvest principal repayments—they're forced to pay down senior debt. This is a drag on returns because the portfolio is shrinking. Additionally, equity distributions might be suspended until the test is cured. For a CLO manager with $50 billion under management, an OC test failure is a significant problem because it reduces assets under management and reduces the fee income they're generating.

I remember watching this play out in 2020 in real-time. There was a large retailer that was downgraded to CCC, and suddenly dozens of CLO managers had to recalculate whether they had a problem. Some did. Some CLOs went immediately into forced OC cure mode—they started dumping lower-rating loans, buying up par par in non-CCC loans, trying to get their OC tests back into compliance. The selling pressure from this was significant.

Here's what made it especially problematic: the loans that needed to be sold to cure OC tests were typically lower-rated loans (B or B-) that weren't attractive buys at par in that market environment. So the selling pressure drove prices down. And the selling of B and B- loans to buy par for OC test purposes created weird market dynamics where you had CLO managers as forced sellers rather than natural buyers of credit risk.

The mechanism through which a loan is downgraded in the first place is important to understand. It happens when the rating agency—Moody's, Fitch, or Standard & Poor's—revises their outlook on the company. They'll say something like, "We're placing XYZ Company on negative outlook due to declining EBITDA and rising leverage levels." Sometime after that negative outlook, they might actually downgrade the rating from B to CCC, or CCC to CCC-.

For CLO managers who hold the loan, they're watching this happen. They see the negative outlook, and they know a downgrade is likely coming. At that point, they have to decide: Do I sell the loan now (before the official downgrade) or do I hold and hope either the outlook is withdrawn or the company's credit situation improves?

This is where CLO manager behavior becomes fascinating. Many managers are reluctant to sell loans in the secondary market—they've got positions they wanted to hold. But when a downgrade to CCC is coming, they're faced with a real economic choice. If they don't sell before the downgrade, the downgrade is going to create an immediate OC test problem. So they might sell proactively, even if they'd prefer to hold. Or they might try to negotiate a sale or exchange with the primary market—the company might be issuing new paper, and they could exchange their old CCC-bound loan for a new B-rated loan.

This is why downgrades in CLO portfolios can create sudden selling pressure in the secondary market. It's not that the loan is less creditworthy (though it might be). It's that the test mechanics are forcing a sale. A loan that's downgraded to CCC has limited buyers willing to pay par (or close to par) because the CCC bucket haircut makes it expensive to hold. Most CLOs already have limited room for CCC exposure. So the downgraded loan has to be repriced lower to find a buyer.

The impact of this on the primary market is significant. If a loan that was rated B faces downgrade risk, borrowers and arrangers know that the downgrade is going to create selling pressure in the secondary market. This means they need to price the loan carefully and understand that if a downgrade happens, there will be secondary market weakness that could make refinancing more expensive. This drives primaries to price wider to compensate for downgrade risk.

What Twenty Years Teaches: Downgrade risk is priced very differently in leveraged finance than it used to be, precisely because of CLO mechanics. In the 1990s, a loan rated B facing downgrade risk would just be repriced down slightly (maybe 25-50 basis points). Now, a loan with downgrade risk is repriced to account for the secondary market disruption that the downgrade will cause. The seller pressure from CLO test failures can push a loan's price down 200-300 basis points. This is a real economic cost that gets passed back to borrowers in higher spreads at origination.

PART SIX: CLOs AND PRIMARY LOAN MARKET DYNAMICS

Now I want to step back and explain how all of this CLO mechanics translates into the primary loan market—how CLOs shape the pricing, structure, and documentation of new loan syndications.

The fundamental insight is that CLOs are the dominant buyer. If you're a bank arranging a $500 million term loan for a mid-market company, you're not primarily syndicating to traditional bank lenders anymore. You're syndicating to CLO portfolios. That shift changes everything about how you structure, price, and sell the deal.

Start with pricing. The CLO manager is running a calculation that says, "What's the minimum spread I need to buy this loan and maintain my portfolio economics?" They're thinking about where this loan fits into their weighted average spread calculation. They're thinking about whether it fits their minimum spread threshold. They're thinking about whether the structure and terms will allow them to hold the loan without triggering test failures.

If a loan is priced at 250 basis points, many CLO managers will pass. They'll look at their WAS test, see that they need to maintain 375 basis points, and realize that buying at 250 is dragging down their average. Unless they can offset it with high-spread loans, it doesn't work. But if the loan is priced at 350 or 375 basis points, suddenly it's interesting. It's closer to the weighted average spread they need to target.

This dynamic creates a "floor" for loan spreads in every market environment. That floor isn't arbitrary—it's driven directly by CLO economics. The floor is roughly where: (CLO's required WAS) - (CLO manager's willingness to buy tighter) = the minimum spread.

In a 2022-2023 market where CLOs needed to maintain 375 basis points WAS, you'd expect to see loan syndications pricing around 350-400 basis points. Anything below 300 would be challenging because there wouldn't be enough CLO demand. And indeed, that's what we saw. The loosest syndication pricing happened for the highest-quality credits, and even those were rarely below 300 basis points. Most syndications were 350+.

But the impact of CLOs on the primary market goes way beyond just pricing. It extends to structure and documentation. CLO managers have specific preferences about what kinds of loans they want to hold. And those preferences reshape how deals are structured.

First, CLOs prefer floating-rate loans. They can value floating-rate loans more easily (the value is less dependent on rates), and the floating-rate characteristics allow them to manage basis risk between the floating-rate liability side (the CLO notes) and the floating-rate asset side (the loans). They don't want fixed-rate loans because those create basis risk and complicate the economics. So arrangers structure deals with floating-rate features. The days of large fixed-rate term loans are effectively over in the syndicated market because there's not enough demand for them.

Second, CLOs prefer first-lien senior secured term loans over second liens or unsecured debt. First-lien loans have higher recovery rates in a default scenario, and they're easier to model and rate. Second liens are more complex, defaults are messier, and recovery is less certain. So even if a borrower might want to raise some second-lien debt, the primary buyer base (CLOs) has evolved toward preferring first lien, which constrains what structures arrangers can support.

Third, CLOs want loans with simple, clear documentation. They don't want aggressive covenant structures that limit their ability to hold the loan through cycles, but they do want clarity about what happens if the company deteriorates. CLO portfolios are typically held to maturity (they don't get sold in distress situations), so documentation that clearly lays out rights and remedies in a stress scenario is important.

Fourth, CLOs want loans that satisfy their technical requirements. The loan needs to be eligible for the CLO manager to buy at the spreads they want to maintain. This means the loan typically needs to:
- Offer at least 250-350 basis points of spread (depending on the cycle)
- Have a weighted average life of no more than 6-8 years
- Have clean documentation without surprise structures or terms that might surprise the CLO manager
- Have reasonable leverage multiples (CLOs are generally comfortable with up to 5-6x leverage, but prefer lower)
- Have an industry and borrower type that fits within CLO diversification requirements

What this all adds up to is a "CLO-eligible" checklist that arrangers have internalized. They know what CLO managers want, and they structure deals accordingly.

I remember an arranger asking me in 2019 whether they should try to push harder on covenant documentation in a particular deal. The sponsor wanted more flexibility. The arranger was faced with a choice: tighter covenant package that might appeal to traditional bank lenders, or lighter covenants that would be more attractive to CLO demand (which, at that point, was already over 60% of institutional buyers). The arranger chose the CLO path. That's the world we're in.

One of the most interesting impacts has been on covenant packages. Covenant-lite loans—loans with minimal financial covenants—have become the norm in the CLO era. Why? Because CLO managers prefer them. They don't want to be held in default if a company's financial metrics slip in a downturn. They'd rather have the flexibility to work through a problem. Traditional bank lenders would have preferred tighter covenants. But traditional bank lenders are now a minority of the buyer base.

The pricing of new syndications is also heavily influenced by what's happening in secondary CLO trading. When a CLO manager is considering whether to buy a new term loan, they're looking at what older similar loans are trading at. If a 2021 vintage B-rated first-lien term loan for a auto supplier is trading at 88 in the secondary market, a CLO manager considering buying a fresh 2022 vintage auto supplier loan at par might hesitate. They're thinking, "Am I going to be buying an asset at 100 that's going to be trading at 88 within 12 months?" This secondary market reality feeds back into the primary market pricing.

There's also a phenomenon called the "CLO bid" which is critically important to understand. The CLO bid is essentially the price floor that CLOs are willing to pay for loans. It's driven by their economics—what spread they need to generate returns, what OC test coverage they need, what leverage profile they can support. In 2022, when loan spreads were tightening aggressively, traditional bankers were getting excited about the pricing. "The market is tight, spreads are compressing, this is great," they'd say. But CLO managers were becoming more conservative. The CLO bid was falling away—they simply weren't interested at the pricing levels that some of the most aggressive syndications were trying to achieve. When the CLO bid disappears, loan syndications struggle. You can do $50-100 million with bank buyers and insurance companies, but you can't do $300-400 million without CLO participation.

This is why CLO demand is so critical to the functioning of the primary loan market. The CLO bid sets a floor. As long as CLOs are willing to buy at certain spreads, new loans get done. The moment CLOs step back, spreads need to widen to pull in other investor types or deals don't get done.

I want to give a real example of this dynamic. In 2024, there was a healthcare company that was trying to finance a large add-on acquisition. The company was moderately leveraged to begin with, and the acquisition was going to push leverage higher. The initial syndication was attempted at 375 basis points. The initial reaction from the market was lukewarm. The bank lenders and insurance companies showed some interest at that level, but the CLO managers were passing. When the arranger asked the CLO managers why, the feedback was consistent: "At 375 basis points, this company's leverage is too high for us to feel comfortable. We're not seeing 125 basis points of downside before things get stressed. We'd want to see this at 425-450." So the loan got repriced at 425 basis points, and suddenly CLO demand materialized. At 425, the CLO managers could model the company through a downturn and still feel comfortable with equity-like returns. At 375, they couldn't. The arranger had to move 50 basis points to get the CLO bid to show up.

The relationship between CLO spreads and what gets done in the market is very tight. CLO managers can calculate with precision what spread they need. They know their cost of funds, they know their fee structure, they know what equity returns they need. When the market price is too tight relative to those requirements, they simply don't bid. And when they don't bid, deal flow stops.

PART SEVEN: CLO MANAGER BEHAVIOR AND ITS MARKET IMPACT

To really understand CLO impact on markets, you have to understand CLO managers as economic actors. They're not passive investors. They're active allocators operating within a set of constraints, but they have real discretion about how they deploy capital and how they manage portfolios.

There's a huge range in CLO manager quality and sophistication. At the top of the pyramid, you have managers like Benefit Street Partners, Lighthouse Capital, Oaktree, and Carlyle. These are massive organizations with teams of 50-100+ credit professionals, sophisticated portfolio management systems, and senior management that's been doing CLOs for 15+ years. They have institutional scale, relationships throughout the market, and the ability to move large blocks of capital.

Then there's a second tier of highly competent managers—shops like Trimaran, Golub Capital, Apollo, Ares. These are sophisticated, well-resourced firms with real expertise.

Then there's a longer tail of smaller CLO managers—some good, some mediocre. There are also some CLO managers who are run by banks or insurance companies as an internal function, managing CLO platforms as part of larger organizations.

The quality differences matter enormously for understanding market behavior. A top-tier CLO manager has the scale to be a price-setter. They can commit to $100-200 million in a syndication because they know they have multiple CLO vehicles that can absorb the position. They can negotiate hard on terms because lenders need their participation. A smaller CLO manager is more of a price-taker—they bid on what's available, but they can't move the market.

When a top-tier CLO manager says they want a loan repriced, arrangers listen. When a smaller manager says they want terms changed, it gets noted but might not drive action.

During the reinvestment period, CLO managers are actively managing their portfolios. They're buying new loans, selling old loans, rotating into better credits, trying to optimize their spread generation. There's a phenomenon I call the "print and sprint"—when a new company comes to the market with a syndicated loan, the first few days of the syndication see massive CLO bidding. The CLO managers are trying to grab allocations because they know there are only a certain amount of shares available and they want to size down before the syndication closes. You'll see a $300 million term loan get done in two days with orders of $800 million because CLO managers are all trying to grab their piece.

But here's something that surprises people who don't follow CLOs closely: CLO managers are actually quite reluctant to sell loans, even when credit fundamentals deteriorate. This seems counterintuitive. If a CLO manager has a loan that's deteriorating, why wouldn't they sell it? The answer comes back to those OC tests.

If a CLO manager sells a loan that's been in the portfolio at par, and they sell it at a discount (because the market has repriced it lower), they crystallize a loss. That loss hits the portfolio value. If their OC test is already tight, that sale might trigger an OC failure. So the manager decides to hold. They might be marked down in an internal model (the loan might be worth 92 or 90 in their view), but they keep it on the books at par because that's the legal value, and selling it would be worse than holding it.

This creates a very particular dynamic. CLO managers are "par-biased." They prefer to buy at par. They're reluctant to sell below par because selling below par hits the OC tests. And they're relatively happy to hold loans even if they're underperforming because at least they're not crystallizing losses. This par bias is an important feature of the market that I've observed repeatedly.

What happens in a stress scenario is that CLO managers can be forced sellers. If credit losses mount and OC tests start failing across their portfolio, managers might be forced to sell loans, even at significant discounts, to raise cash and meet their tests. In 2020, we saw this. As defaults rose, CLO managers were facing OC test pressures, and some of them had to sell loans to generate cash and raise their collateral values. The selling pressure was significant, which is partly why we saw such sharp moves in secondary loan pricing in March 2020.

But in normal environments, CLO managers are buy-and-hold investors with very specific preferences. They want loans in the 350-425 basis point spread range. They want floating-rate structures. They want loans from companies in stable industries with leverage below 5x. They want first-lien senior secured debt. And they're very focused on maintaining their test compliance.

One of the most important behavioral features of CLO managers is their focus on the reinvestment period. During the reinvestment period, they have flexibility. After reinvestment ends, they don't. So there's a natural arc to CLO manager behavior—early in the reinvestment period, they're aggressive buyers, trying to optimize the portfolio. As they approach the end of the reinvestment period, they start getting more conservative, thinking about what the portfolio will look like when amortization starts.

This can create cycles in CLO demand. Early in a CLO's life, demand might be robust. But as the reinvestment period winds down and the CLO approaches amortization, demand might soften. Managers might start selling to optimize the portfolio for amortization.

I want to give a specific example of CLO manager behavior that illustrates how this works. I was working with a large CLO manager in 2019 who had issued a CLO in 2014. That CLO's reinvestment period was going to end in 2021. As 2020 approached, the manager was thinking very carefully about what the portfolio would look like in amortization. They knew that COVID was coming (they didn't know the magnitude, but there was growing concern about a recession). They started becoming much more selective about reinvestment. They were selling lower-quality B- and CCC loans and buying higher-quality B and B+ loans. They were shortening their average weighted average life. They were essentially "de-risking" the portfolio ahead of the transition to amortization.

This manager was providing a market signal—they were a net seller of lower-quality credit and a net buyer of higher-quality credit. This helped drive spreads higher in the lower credit bucket. In hindsight, this was smart positioning because 2020 hit and COVID caused defaults in exactly the lower-quality stuff. The manager had rotated out of it just in time.

The feedback between CLO manager behavior and market pricing is constantly happening. When CLO managers are aggressive buyers, spreads compress. When they're cautious and selective, spreads widen. When they're facing test pressures that force selling, spreads really widen. Market participants who understand CLO manager incentives and constraints can often predict market moves ahead of time.

What Twenty Years Teaches: CLO managers are not monolithic. Some are very sophisticated, long-term thinkers who are making portfolio decisions five years out. Others are more short-term focused, trying to hit quarterly numbers. Understanding which type of manager you're dealing with in a syndication is important. A long-term focused manager might be comfortable repricing to maintain economics through a cycle. A short-term focused manager might be pushing for the tightest possible pricing. And understanding where a manager is in their reinvestment period is critical—a manager in year 1 of a 5-year reinvestment period will behave very differently than a manager in year 5 approaching amortization.

PART EIGHT: REFINANCING AND REPRICING IN CLO CONTEXT

When a borrower's loan is trading well in the secondary market and interest rates have moved favorably, the company might want to reprice or refinance their debt. This is completely normal in leveraged finance. But in the CLO era, repricing has implications that go way beyond just the borrower and the arranger.

Here's how it typically works. A company has a term loan that was issued 18 months ago at 400 basis points when the leverage was 4.5x and spreads were wide. But since then, spreads have compressed to 350 basis points, the company's leverage has come down to 4.0x, and the loan is trading at 101 in the secondary market. The company's sponsor is thinking, "Why are we paying 400 when we could refinance at 360 or 370? That's 30-40 basis points of savings every year."

So the company comes to the market with a repricing. They're not doing a whole new loan—they're offering existing lenders a chance to reprice their existing position at lower terms. The mechanics are that the lenders who reprice get a reduced spread but they maintain their seniority and other terms. The lenders who don't reprice can keep their original terms (400 basis points) but their position becomes more junior to the new lenders.

From a CLO perspective, a repricing creates an interesting situation. The CLO manager who owns the original loan at 400 basis points has a decision to make. Should they reprice at 360, locking in a lower spread but maintaining their position? Or should they hold at 400, taking the risk that the company might use the repricing as a way to clean up their cap table and refinance them out entirely?

The tension here is real. The CLO manager doesn't want to give up spread. Every 10 basis points of spread loss across a $500 million portfolio is $500,000 of annual income. But they also don't want to get pushed out of a position. So many CLO managers will reprice, but only if the repricing is small enough (usually 20-25 basis points) that it doesn't materially impact their portfolio returns. If the repricing is too aggressive, they'll hold at the old spread and accept the risk of subordination.

I've seen situations where the repricing has created a bifurcated cap table. You'll have the old lenders who didn't reprice sitting at 400 basis points with first-lien rights. Then you'll have new lenders who came in at 360 basis points with the same first-lien rights. This creates complexity in the documentation and potential issues if the company needs to refinance in the future.

The big picture is that repricing waves have become a market feature in the CLO era in a way they weren't before. The reason is that CLO managers are now the dominant lenders, and they're very focused on optimization. When economic conditions improve, CLO managers want to reduce their cost of funding or they want to optimize their portfolio returns. So repricing becomes a normal part of the cycle.

A related dynamic is what I call the "CLO refinancing" or "reset." After the reinvestment period of a CLO has ended, the CLO enters amortization. But sometimes CLO managers can negotiate with the debt investors to extend the reinvestment period. This requires the debt investors to agree to modified terms—usually slightly higher coupons in exchange for another 2-3 years of reinvestment flexibility.

From a market perspective, a CLO refinancing/reset is significant because it extends the active management period of the portfolio. Instead of going into amortization, the CLO goes back into reinvestment mode. This often triggers a wave of selling (the manager trimming positions to optimize for the extension) followed by a wave of buying (the manager deploying into new opportunities).

The impact of repricing on borrowers is significant. Many sponsors dislike repricing because they feel like they're giving away returns they've already earned. But from a market perspective, repricing is actually quite rational. It's the market saying, "Conditions have improved, credit has improved, you should be paying less." Fighting that repricing means paying more than you need to, which is a value destruction.

PART NINE: CLOs AND CREDIT EVENTS

What happens when a loan in a CLO portfolio defaults? The mechanics are important to understand because they determine how CLO managers respond to credit deterioration and how defaults flow through to impact equity returns.

When a loan defaults—meaning the borrower stops making interest or principal payments on schedule—it's moved out of the performing assets and into a "defaulted" bucket. The cash flow mechanics change. Instead of receiving regular quarterly interest payments, the CLO is now engaged in a workout or recovery process. The company might go through a distressed exchange (where it offers new securities at much lower rates to get lenders to agree to modify the terms). It might go through a bankruptcy. Or it might get acquired by a financial sponsor who refinances it.

From a CLO accounting perspective, a defaulted loan is typically valued below par. The CLO might value it at 50 or 60 cents on the dollar, depending on their estimate of recovery. This valuation change affects the OC tests immediately. Let me walk through an example.

A CLO has:
- 150 loans, par value $500 million, all at par
- Senior debt outstanding: $400 million
- Senior OC test requirement: 1.10x coverage

OC test: $500M / $400M = 1.25x. Comfortably passing the 1.10x test.

One of the loans—a $10 million position in a company that's struggling—defaults. The CLO manager estimates recovery at 50%, so they mark it down to $5 million.

New portfolio value: $495M (non-defaulted) + $5M (defaulted at 50%) = $500M

Wait, that's still $500M. But actually, the impact is worse because interest payments on the defaulted loan stop. So the CLO is no longer receiving interest income from that $10 million loan. From an interest coverage perspective, the CLO has lost annual interest income. If the loan was generating 400 basis points, that's $40,000 per year in lost interest income.

The real OC impact becomes clear when you think about what happens in the recovery process. Let's say the company goes through a distressed exchange. In the exchange, the $10 million loan is exchanged for new securities worth $6 million. The lender (the CLO) takes a $4 million loss. That loss hits the OC test hard.

New portfolio value (immediately after loss): $486M ($500M - $4M loss - $10M defaulted loan)

OC test: $486M / $400M = 1.215x. Still passing the 1.10x test, but with less cushion.

If the CLO has multiple loan defaults—say $20, $15, $8 million in losses—the OC test can start to get close to failing. At that point, the CLO manager is forced to either:
1. Cure the test by selling loans and raising cash to pay down the senior debt, or
2. Accept that the test will fail, which means distributions to equity are suspended

The equity holders are the ones who bear the loss first. The first-dollar loss goes to them. This is why CLO equity returns are so sensitive to credit cycles. A 2% default rate on the portfolio might directly translate to 100+ basis points of return reduction for equity holders.

How CLO managers respond to credit deterioration is a critical market dynamic. As defaults rise, managers become very focused on preventing additional defaults. They might become unwilling to sell loans from their portfolio (because selling creates losses that exacerbate OC test problems). They might reduce reinvestment (not buying new loans because they need to preserve capital). They might become very conservative in their asset selection, avoiding any loan that might default in the near term.

During the 2020 COVID crisis, you saw exactly this. In March and April 2020, as defaults spiked, CLO managers essentially stopped buying and became very focused on preserving capital. The secondary market for loans became very illiquid because CLO sellers vastly outnumbered CLO buyers. Secondary loan spreads widened 300+ basis points in a matter of weeks.

I remember the dynamics vividly. I was working with a large CLO manager in late March 2020, and they were in crisis mode. They had 150 loans in their portfolio, and 15-20 of them were showing signs of stress. They didn't know yet which ones would default, but they were preparing for it. They had essentially stopped any new purchases. They were focused entirely on downside scenarios.

One particular company—a hospitality firm—was clearly headed toward default. The loan was trading at 40 in the secondary market. The CLO manager had a $50 million position. They were modeling what the recovery would be. Would it be 30 cents? 40 cents? The recovery would determine whether they could pass their OC tests. If recovery was less than 50%, the OC tests would fail because of the mark-down on the position.

The manager ultimately decided to hold. They couldn't sell the position at 40 and take the loss because the loss would trigger forced OC cures. Better to hold and hope for recovery. As it turned out, the hospitality company eventually came through the crisis and recovered to 70+ cents on the dollar by 2022. The manager was glad they held. But it was stressful in the moment.

This is an important lesson: CLO managers can be "forced holders" of underwater positions because selling creates losses that trigger test failures. This is very different from the traditional bank lending model, where a bank can sell a loan at a loss to a secondary market buyer without worrying about OC tests. In the CLO era, selling a bad loan at a loss is extremely costly.

What Twenty Years Teaches: Credit events have outsized impact in CLO portfolios because of the test mechanics. A default that would be a moderate concern in a traditional portfolio becomes a major issue in a CLO portfolio if it triggers OC test failures. This is why CLO managers are somewhat conservative in their credit selection—they can't afford big surprise losses. And this is why credit cycles hit CLO portfolios harder than they might hit traditional bank portfolios. The testing mechanism amplifies the pain.

PART TEN: CLO MARKET CYCLES AND VINTAGE EFFECTS

CLOs have a history, and that history tells you a lot about how they function. Let me walk through the different eras of CLO development and how they've evolved.

The first generation of CLOs was issued before the 2008 financial crisis. These "CLO 1.0" structures were often relatively simple—they held mainly bank loans, with some mezzanine debt and bonds. Many had leverage ratios of 8:1 or 10:1 or higher (remember, the CLO could finance itself with mostly debt and relatively little equity). When the financial crisis hit in 2008 and defaults spiked, many CLO 1.0s got into serious trouble. Some failed entirely. The losses in CLO equity were massive.

The financial crisis essentially blew up the first generation of CLOs. It's like a reset button got hit. When the market recovered and people started issuing CLOs again (around 2010-2012), they did it differently.

CLO 2.0 structures—the post-crisis generation—were more conservative. The leverage ratios came down (now more like 5:1 or 6:1 instead of 10:1). The test structures became more robust. The rating agency methodology became stricter. The documentation became more granular. And the equity holders had much more cushion.

One of the big changes was the Volcker Rule, which was implemented in 2014. The Volcker Rule restricted bank proprietary trading, which meant banks could no longer take big trading positions in CLOs for their own profit. This reduced bank demand for CLO equity (banks could no longer take large equity stakes "for the portfolio"), but it increased the "purity" of the CLO market because the remaining buyers were all true believers in the value, not banks looking for trading returns.

For most of the post-2008 period through 2019, CLO issuance and performance were stable. You had steady CLO issuance, reasonable credit performance, and equity returns that were attractive but not spectacular. Managers were building track records, proving that CLO 2.0 could work.

Then 2020 hit. COVID-19 created the most challenging environment CLOs had faced since the financial crisis. Defaults spiked. Companies across multiple industries were suddenly impaired. Credit spreads widened massively. Interest rates fell (the Fed cut rates to zero). And the rating agencies started downgrading large portions of CLO portfolios.

But here's the surprising part: CLOs 2.0 actually performed okay in 2020. They didn't blow up the way CLO 1.0 did in 2008. The conservative capital structures, the robust test frameworks, and the equity cushion all held. There were some managers who got into trouble, and some CLOs breached their OC tests, but the market didn't face a systemic crisis the way it had in 2008.

This was actually a really important moment for CLOs as an asset class. It proved that CLO 2.0 could survive a real stress test. The market had been skeptical—could CLOs really work, or would they blow up again in a crisis? COVID answered that question affirmatively. CLOs 2.0 were materially more resilient than CLO 1.0.

What followed was explosive CLO issuance growth. In 2021 and 2022, CLO issuance hit record levels. Managers were raising new CLOs at a pace not seen since the pre-crisis era. The success of existing CLOs in 2020 convinced institutional investors that CLOs were a good investment. Equity investors saw track records of CLOs that had generated decent returns even in the stress of COVID, and they wanted in.

This leads to an important observation about CLO vintage effects. CLOs issued in different years perform quite differently because they're issued into different market environments. A CLO issued in 2020 (when spreads were super wide due to COVID volatility) is fundamentally different economics from a CLO issued in 2023 (when spreads were tighter). The 2020 CLO was issued with a net spread of maybe 250 basis points (WAS of 450, cost of debt of 200). The 2023 CLO might have been issued with a net spread of 150 basis points (WAS of 350, cost of debt of 200).

A 100 basis point difference in net spread translates directly to equity return differences. If you're leveraged 5:1, an extra 100 basis points of net spread is 500 basis points of additional equity return. That's a massive difference in annual returns.

This is why CLO vintage is such an important parameter for understanding expected returns. A 2020-2021 vintage CLO is expected to perform much better than a 2023-2024 vintage CLO, holding credit cycle constant, just because of the spread environment in which they were issued.

There's also a concept called the "wall of CLO maturities." A CLO issued in 2012-2013 will mature around 2022-2023. A CLO issued in 2018-2019 will mature around 2028-2029. The point is that you have waves of CLO maturities hitting the market as older CLOs run off.

When a CLO matures, what happens to the loans in the portfolio? They have to be paid off. If the CLO was still in amortization, the proceeds from loan sales and prepayments were already being used to pay down debt. At maturity, any remaining debt is paid off. This often forces large loan sales into the market as CLO managers are trying to exit positions to raise cash for redemptions.

I remember 2023-2024 being characterized as a period where "wall of maturities" was an important focus. A lot of older CLOs were approaching maturity, and managers were thinking about how to position their portfolios for the redemptions. Some managers were refinancing—getting new CLO vehicles to extend their asset management lives. Others were just accepting that their CLO was going to mature and their fund was going to be liquidated.

The market impact of the wall of maturities can be significant. You have forced sellers of loans coming into the market to raise cash. But you also have the replacement effect—managers who are refinancing their CLOs need to buy new assets. So there's both selling pressure (from maturing CLOs) and buying pressure (from new CLOs).

What Twenty Years Teaches: CLO history is full of lessons, but the main one is that vintage matters enormously. The conditions under which a CLO is issued determine its economics, and those economics persist throughout the life of the CLO. A CLO issued in a wide-spread environment will always perform better than an otherwise similar CLO issued in a tight-spread environment. This is why smart CLO investors pay careful attention to what vintage they're investing in. A 2020 vintage CLO equity is fundamentally better risk-adjusted than a 2023 vintage equity, all else equal.

PART ELEVEN: REGULATORY FRAMEWORK

The regulatory environment for CLOs shapes their structure and behavior in important ways. Let me walk through the key regulatory regimes that matter.

The most important piece of regulation for CLO managers in the United States is the risk retention rule, which came out as part of Dodd-Frank and was implemented in 2014. The risk retention rule requires that the manager of a securitized asset pool—including a CLO manager—must retain at least 5 percent of the credit risk of the assets they securitize. This is sometimes called the "skin in the game" requirement.

What this means practically is that the CLO manager must take an economic interest in the equity of the CLO. They can't just sponsor a CLO, syndicate all the debt to third parties, and take only management fees. They have to have some meaningful equity stake that goes along with their economic interests.

This regulation actually makes a lot of sense. It aligns the manager's interests with other investors. The theory is that if the manager has skin in the game, they'll be more conservative in their credit selection and more careful about monitoring.

The requirement can be satisfied in a few ways. The manager can invest directly in the CLO equity. They can hold a portion of the subordinated notes. They can take an equity incentive fee (where their compensation is tied to the CLO's performance). Most CLO managers use a combination of these methods.

The 5% requirement has had some unintended consequences. It means CLO managers need to have capital available to invest in the CLOs they're sponsoring. This is a meaningful constraint for smaller managers. A $500 million CLO requires $25 million of retention. If a manager is trying to sponsor multiple CLOs every year, they need substantial capital to retain across multiple vehicles.

Another important regulatory regime is the European risk retention rule under CRR Article 6. The European rule is similar to the US rule but has some nuances in implementation. European CLO managers need to maintain 5% retention, but they have flexibility in how they do it.

Then there's the question of how rating agencies rate CLOs. The rating agencies—Moody's, Fitch, S&P—have their own methodologies for rating CLO tranches. These methodologies were refined significantly post-financial crisis. They now incorporate more conservative assumptions about default rates, recovery rates, and correlation.

The rating agencies' assumptions directly drive the capital structure of CLOs. If an agency assumes a certain default rate on the loan portfolio, that determines how much equity cushion is needed to support each tranche. The agencies are very conservative in their assumptions, which is why CLO structures (especially in the post-2008 era) have been quite conservative.

One aspect of regulation that doesn't get discussed enough but matters a lot is the role of the trustee in CLO structures. The trustee is a third party (typically a major bank like Deutsche Bank, Wilmington Trust, or Wells Fargo) who represents the interest of the debt noteholders. The trustee has specific responsibilities, including monitoring the CLO manager, ensuring distributions are made correctly, and enforcing the terms of the indenture if the manager breaches them.

The trustee's role creates a kind of regulatory function within CLOs. If the CLO manager tries to do something that violates the indenture—say, buying a loan that violates the diversity test or exceeds the CCC bucket—the trustee will catch it and not permit the transaction. This provides a real guardrail on CLO manager behavior.

There's also the impact of overall financial regulation on CLO demand. The Dodd-Frank Act and Basel III, which imposed more stringent capital requirements on banks, reduced banks' appetite to hold loans on their balance sheets. This increased the need for non-bank loan buyers, which benefited CLOs. In fact, you could argue that CLOs and other alternative finance vehicles grew to fill the void created by reduced bank lending capacity.

What Twenty Years Teaches: Regulatory frameworks are not obstacles to good market functioning—they're the scaffolding that makes sophisticated markets possible. The risk retention rule seems like a burden to CLO managers, but it actually improves the market by aligning incentives. The rating agency methodologies seem overly conservative, but they ensure that the capital structures can withstand stress. And the trustee oversight seems like an impediment, but it prevents the kinds of abuses that occurred pre-2008. Bankers and sponsors who view regulation as something to work around are missing the point. The regulation is there because of lessons learned, and it actually improves market functioning.

PART TWELVE: THE FEEDBACK LOOP—CLOs AND DOCUMENTATION EROSION

There's a broader narrative about CLOs and the loan market that I need to address directly, even though it's somewhat controversial. The question is: Have CLOs made the loan market safer, or riskier?

The answer, I believe, is nuanced. CLOs have made the market more efficient in some ways and less prudent in others.

Start with the efficiency angle. CLOs are incredibly efficient buyers of loans. They can absorb large volumes of loans that would have choked out traditional bank balance sheets. They have standardized processes for evaluating loans, consistent methodologies for pricing, and the scale to move large blocks of capital. From a pure capital allocation perspective, CLOs are better buyers than traditional banks.

But CLOs have also had some negative effects on documentation and covenant structures. And here's why: CLO managers prefer covenant-lite loans because covenant-lite means fewer complications in a stress scenario. In a traditional bank relationship, a tighter covenant package (more restrictive financial covenants, more baskets for restricted payments, etc.) is viewed as protective. The bank can threaten to put the company into technical default if they breach covenants, which gives the bank leverage to negotiate changes in a stress scenario.

But in a CLO portfolio of 150 loans, the CLO manager can't realistically be calling technical defaults and negotiating with each company individually. The management burden would be too high. So CLO managers prefer loan documents that are clear about what happens if the company deteriorates (covenants are simple and straightforward), but not so restrictive that you're constantly negotiating amendments.

This creates a perverse incentive. Companies prefer covenant-lite because it gives them more operational flexibility. CLO managers prefer covenant-lite because it reduces administrative burden. The syndication banks prefer covenant-lite because it makes the deals easier to syndicate to the largest buyer base (CLOs). So you've got all three parties aligned in favor of covenant-lite, and suddenly the "standard" loan document becomes much more flexible than it would have been in the traditional bank lending era.

Is covenant-lite problematic? That's the subject of a lot of debate. One perspective is that covenant-lite loans are more risky for lenders because they have less early warning of deterioration and less ability to force changes. Another perspective is that covenant-lite loans are actually fine because the prices reflect the risk—if a covenant-lite loan carries more risk, it should yield more, and presumably the market prices it appropriately.

I think the truth is somewhere in the middle. Covenant-lite loans are not inherently bad, but they do require the lenders to do more work on credit analysis upfront and to be more realistic about recovery assumptions in a stress scenario. A covenant-lite first-lien loan to a solid B-rated company in a stable industry is probably fine. A covenant-lite first-lien loan to a CCC-rated company in a cyclical industry is probably more problematic.

What's happened over the decades of CLO existence is that documentation has evolved to become less protective and more borrower-friendly. Some of this is deliberate—borrowers have gotten smarter about negotiating terms. Some of it is the result of market competition—arranger banks need to syndicate, and covenant-lite terms make syndicating to CLOs easier. And some of it is the CLO effect—the dominant buyer (CLOs) is more comfortable with lighter documentation.

There's also a phenomenon I call the "CLO put." The idea is that because CLOs are the dominant buyer and they have a large amount of capital, there's an implicit understanding that CLOs will be there to buy loans, which supports the primary market. This can create moral hazard—sponsors and arranger banks might take on more risk (tighter spreads, lighter documentation, higher leverage) because they know the "CLO put" is there.

In a crisis, what happens if the CLO put disappears? If CLOs step back from the market because they're facing their own test constraints or credit issues, then sponsors and arrangers are left without their largest buyer. We saw this briefly in 2020 and briefly again in 2022. The disruption is real and painful.

What Twenty Years Teaches: The relationship between CLOs and loan documentation is a two-way street. CLOs have incentivized lighter documentation, which has benefits (lower costs) and downsides (less protection). Market participants need to understand that covenant-lite is not "free"—it's just shifting the risk to a later point in time. When companies are stressed, covenant-lite doesn't mean "easier to work out." It means "less ability to force proactive changes, so you have to hope the company fixes itself." That's actually more difficult for lenders.

PART THIRTEEN: WHAT TWENTY YEARS TEACHES

Let me conclude by reflecting on what I've learned about CLOs over two decades of dealing with them constantly.

The first lesson is that CLOs are not temporary. In the 1990s, people thought securitization was a fad. In the 2000s, people thought CLOs would disappear after the financial crisis. It's now 2026, and CLOs are more important than ever. They're not going away. They're a permanent feature of modern finance.

The second lesson is that understanding CLO mechanics is a career differentiator. I've worked with dozens of talented bankers and credit officers over the years. The ones who truly understand CLOs—not just in an abstract sense, but in the grinding details of how tests work, what drives manager behavior, how to structure deals to maximize CLO demand—are systematically more successful. They can anticipate market moves. They can structure deals that are more attractive to the marginal buyer. They can negotiate more effectively with CLO managers. It's not that the knowledge is hard—it's just that most people don't bother to learn it deeply.

The third lesson is that CLOs amplify both upside and downside in markets. In good times, CLO demand can drive spreads incredibly tight, which increases supply of syndicated loans. In bad times, CLO selling pressure can drive spreads incredibly wide, which shuts off supply of new loans. The leverage embedded in CLO structures means that small changes in credit conditions can have big impacts on CLO behavior.

The fourth lesson is that CLO manager quality matters a lot. The difference between a tier-one manager and a tier-two manager is not marginal. It's the difference between someone who's thinking three years ahead and someone who's focused on quarterly numbers. It's the difference between someone who will weather a stress cycle with minimal portfolio damage and someone who'll take heavy losses. The best CLO sponsors understand this and are willing to pay premiums to work with the best managers.

The fifth lesson is that spreads have a floor. That floor is set by CLO economics. You can't price loans so tight that CLOs stop buying. Once you hit that floor, the market stops. I've seen this happen repeatedly. In 2022, the CLO floor was around 350-400 basis points (depending on credit quality and leverage profile). In 2023, it was around 375-425. In 2024, with the surprise strength of the market, it compressed to 350-375. But there's always a floor where CLO economics say, "This isn't worth it." Understanding where that floor is in any given market is critical to understanding how the market will evolve.

The sixth lesson is that documentation matters more in a CLO era. Tighter documentation is harder to sell to CLOs, but if you're trying to protect yourself as a lender, tighter documentation is what you need. This creates a tension: you can either optimize for demand (lighter documentation) or optimize for protection (tighter documentation). There's no free lunch. Some lenders choose demand. Smart lenders choose protection and price appropriately for the lighter documentation. The worst lenders choose lighter documentation and don't price appropriately for the risk—they're essentially giving away value.

The seventh lesson is that credit cycles are real and they impact everything. A CLO that's issued in the trough of a cycle will have much better economics than a CLO issued at the peak. This is not mysterious—it's just math. Wide spreads equal better economics. Tight spreads equal worse economics. Smart CLO sponsors and managers time their launches carefully to avoid being the sucker holding the widest spreads.

The eighth lesson is that there's more nuance in the market than most people realize. The conventional wisdom is sometimes wrong. Covenant-lite loans aren't inherently bad. High leverage isn't inherently bad. CCC loans aren't inherently bad. They're just riskier and should be priced accordingly. The problem arises when people take risks without pricing appropriately, or when structural changes (like the rise of CLOs) drive behavior that increases risk without increasing compensation.

The ninth lesson is that regulation is your friend. The post-financial crisis regulatory framework for CLOs—the risk retention rules, the rating agency methodologies, the test structures—has actually made the market more stable. Bankers who chafe against regulation often miss this point. The regulation is forcing managers to have skin in the game, to be conservative in their assumptions, to test rigorously. All of that is good.

The tenth lesson is that bankers need to speak CLO manager language. You need to understand their constraints, their incentives, and their decision-making processes. You need to be able to explain to a sponsor why a particular structure is more attractive to CLOs. You need to be able to negotiate with CLO managers in a way that addresses their actual concerns rather than what you think their concerns should be. That language is learned through study and through repeated conversations with CLO managers.

The eleventh lesson is that market concentration creates vulnerabilities. When one buyer (CLOs) controls 60%+ of the market, that buyer's behavior becomes critical to the functioning of the market. If CLO managers all make similar decisions at the same time—all buying or all selling, all tightening requirements or all loosening them—the market moves sharply. This is why understanding what would make CLO managers change behavior is so important. What would cause the CLO bid to disappear? What would cause them to get more aggressive? The answers to those questions predict the next market move.

And the twelfth lesson is that leverage is the fundamental driver of returns and risk. CLOs work because they use leverage—they borrow to buy more assets. The leverage amplifies returns when things go well and amplifies losses when things go badly. Understanding leverage, modeling leverage, and respecting leverage is essential to understanding CLOs and navigating markets dominated by leverage.

CONCLUSION: THE FUTURE OF CLOS AND LEVERAGED FINANCE

As I look ahead, I think CLOs will remain dominant in the leveraged loan market. The scale, efficiency, and capital availability of CLO platforms is hard to replicate. Traditional banks have stepped back from holding loans. Insurance companies have limited balance sheet capacity. CLOs have filled the void, and they're not going anywhere.

What might change? Several things. First, interest rates will eventually stabilize at some new normal. When they do, the cost of CLO funding will adjust, which will impact the economics. Second, credit cycles are inevitable. The next downturn will test CLO resilience again, and depending on how it plays out, it might force some structural changes in how CLOs are underwritten or regulated. Third, new competitors might emerge—alternative capital sources that can do some of what CLOs do at lower cost.

But fundamentally, the CLO structure has proven to be very efficient at aggregating capital, managing risks, and deploying into loan portfolios. As long as the loan market exists and as long as those economics work, CLOs will exist.

For practitioners, the lesson is clear: understand CLOs, speak their language, align with their economics, and you'll thrive. Ignore them, fight them, or misunderstand them, and you'll gradually get pushed out.

I've spent two decades learning this business, and I'm still learning. The market evolves. New structures are created. Test mechanics change. But the fundamental insight remains: CLOs are not a footnote in the leveraged finance market. They are the market. And anyone who wants to operate effectively in that market needs to understand them deeply.

That's what this chapter has tried to accomplish. I hope it's useful to practitioners navigating this complex, important, and endlessly fascinating corner of finance.
