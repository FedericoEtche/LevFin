REGULATORY FRAMEWORK FOR EUROPEAN LEVERAGED FINANCE

When I began my career in leveraged finance in 2004, regulation was something we paid attention to, certainly, but it was not the obsessive constraint it has become. The Basel II framework had just been fully implemented, and supervisors were still feeling their way through how to apply it to the rapidly growing leveraged lending market. If you wanted to lead a leveraged buyout, the main obstacles were finding a sponsor with conviction, structuring a deal that made economic sense, and securing commitments from a syndicate of willing banks. Regulatory capital consumption was a factor in pricing, but hardly the dominant one. The regulatory framework we navigate today — the cumulative product of Basel III, the Capital Requirements Directive and Regulation, years of ECB and PRA supervisory guidance, MiFID II, securitization regulation, and countless national requirements — would have seemed dystopian to us then. Yet in many respects, this framework has made the market healthier, more transparent, and more resilient.

This chapter explores the regulatory landscape that shapes European leveraged finance today. I write as a practitioner who has lived through the metamorphosis from Basel II to Basel IV, who has sat across desks from regulators, who has felt the economic impact of leverage limits tightening, and who has witnessed how rules intended for one purpose sometimes produce entirely different consequences. This is not an academic exercise in regulatory interpretation. It is the accumulated experience of someone who has had to make capital calculations work, negotiate exceptions with supervisors, and find market solutions to regulatory constraints.

ECB LEVERAGED LENDING GUIDANCE AND THE 6X LEVERAGE LIMIT

The ECB's leveraged lending guidance, which crystallized around 2015-2016 but has been refined continuously, represents the most direct regulatory constraint on the European leveraged finance market. The guidance is not law in the strict sense — it is best practice guidance issued by the ECB to banks within its supervisory remit (roughly 120 significant institutions and their consolidated groups). Yet the distinction between "guidance" and "requirement" has become increasingly academic. A bank that ignores ECB guidance on leverage limits does so at its peril, knowing that its next supervisory review will include pointed questions about why it deviated from published expectations.

The famous 6x leverage limit applies to loans that the ECB assesses as leveraged lending. The definition itself matters enormously: a leveraged loan is one where the borrowing company's total debt (including the new facility being underwritten) exceeds 4x EBITDA. This definition has always struck me as somewhat arbitrary — why 4x as the threshold, why not 3.5x or 4.5x? — but the answer is simple: it reflects where the ECB observed the market clustering, and where risk concentrations began to concern supervisors. Once you cross into leveraged lending territory, the 6x limit becomes binding. This means that the bank's total exposure to a borrower (measured as total debt capacity) should not exceed 6x EBITDA. It is not a hard ceiling — there is no bright-line prohibition — but rather a limit beyond which supervisors expect banks to have documented exceptions and clear justifications.

I recall sitting in a supervisory meeting in 2017 when one of my banks had underwritten a leveraged facility at 6.3x EBITDA for a continental European carve-out transaction. We knew it was above the guidance. We had explicitly documented why: the company had demonstrated significant deleveraging capability, management had a strong track record, synergies were credible and already being realized, and the sponsor was committing meaningful additional equity. The ECB supervisors were not angry — they were calibrating. They asked detailed questions about our stress testing assumptions, about what leverage would need to fall to if EBITDA contracted by 20%, about what we were assuming in terms of cash sweep and debt reduction. By the end of the meeting, we had implicit acknowledgment that this level of leverage could be justified in specific circumstances. But we also understood that we would need to demonstrate careful underwriting and rigorous credit analysis, and that we could not make this exception routine.

The 6x guidance is measured as total debt to EBITDA using the borrower's most recent annual audited accounts. This creates obvious procyclicality: in a soft market where multiples are expanding and leverage is reaching 6x, the denominator (EBITDA) is typically being measured at its peak, when optimism is highest. I have never believed that EBITDA measured at market peak is a reliable metric for stress testing leverage in credit analysis, yet it is precisely the EBITDA used for regulatory calculations. This is one of the many places where regulatory rules and economic reality diverge.

The exceptions to the 6x guidance are theoretically available but practically rare. The ECB recognizes that in certain circumstances, leverage above 6x can be appropriate: acquisition finance for strong, cash-generative businesses; infrastructure and utilities transactions where cash flows are contractual and stable; situations where there is clear, documented deleveraging capacity; and deals where the additional leverage finances acquisition of complementary businesses with identified synergies. But I have learned that "recognized exceptions" and "exceptions that supervisors will actually accept" are not the same thing. The burden of documentation is heavy. We need to present forward-looking cash flow analysis, stress testing under multiple scenarios, sensitivity analysis showing how leverage evolves under different economic assumptions, and narrative explanation of why the guidance is insufficient for this particular situation. More fundamentally, we need supervisory relationships that enable that kind of nuanced discussion.

One aspect of the 6x guidance that causes significant market friction: it is measured on a look-through basis for sponsor-backed transactions. When a private equity fund is financing a mid-market acquisition, the ECB wants to understand the total leverage of the financed business at the parent level, not just the operational company level. This creates a conceptual challenge because private equity sponsors deliberately separate debt at the holding company and operating company levels, with senior debt at the operating company and potentially mezzanine or other instruments at the holding company. The ECB's guidance requires banks to assess total leverage including those holding company instruments. This is economically sensible — a company financed by a PE sponsor where the sponsor has layered debt at the holding company is indeed more leveraged than one financed at the operating company level alone. But it has created a category of transactions that are marginally viable at one leverage architecture and unfinanceable at another.

I have watched sponsors restructure transactions specifically to reduce measured leverage for regulatory purposes. A transaction that was initially structured as a holding company acquisition with holding company debt gets restructured as a pure operating company acquisition with no holding vehicle debt. This is not fraud; it is legitimate deal engineering. But it demonstrates how the regulatory framework shapes market practice in ways that sometimes seem to go beyond the supervisory intent. The intent was to prevent excessive leverage. The effect has been to create incentives for legal and financial engineering that might not optimize economic structure.

BANK OF ENGLAND AND PRA SUPERVISORY EXPECTATIONS

The Bank of England's Prudential Regulation Authority has taken a somewhat different approach to leveraged lending than the ECB. Rather than publishing a specific leverage multiple limit, the PRA has taken a more principles-based approach, articulated in various supervisory statements and in the annual PRA supervisory priorities. This approach gives PRA supervisors more room for judgment and negotiation with individual institutions, which is both more flexible and potentially more arbitrary, depending on how one views discretionary supervision.

The PRA's foundational expectation, articulated clearly in supervisory statements since 2014, is that banks should apply robust risk management to leveraged lending portfolios and should not assume that leveraged lending will generate returns that compensate for the risks being taken. This sounds obvious when stated plainly, yet it represents a genuine shift in supervisory thinking. The pre-financial crisis assumption was that if something was priced competitively in the market, the pricing presumably reflected risks accurately. The post-crisis view is that market pricing is sometimes complacent, that the market can misprice risk during boom times, and that supervisors need to challenge assumptions that banks are making about return adequacy.

The PRA has been particularly focused on downside scenarios and reverse stress testing. Rather than telling banks "your leverage should not exceed X multiple," the PRA asks banks to demonstrate what scenarios would cause significant losses in their leveraged lending portfolios. This reverse stress testing obligation has been revolutionary in its impact. A bank might begin underwriting a portfolio of leveraged deals at 5.5x leverage, assuming 5% annual deleveraging. A reverse stress test reveals that if deleveraging stops entirely (perhaps because the credit deteriorates or because the borrower faces unexpected business headwinds), and if EBITDA contracts by 25%, the leverage would spike to 8.5x, and the bank would take material losses on its held portfolio. This kind of analysis forces banks to think about concentration risk and correlation risk in leveraged portfolios — the tendency for all deals to deteriorate simultaneously when economic conditions change.

The PRA has also been clear that it expects banks to maintain significant equity cushions in leveraged deals. The expectation is that banks will not be fully debt-financed (or nearly fully debt-financed) in deals where they retain credit risk. This means that sponsors expect banks to provide significant equity co-investment, and banks should size that equity participation based on the risk profile of the deal. A 5.5x leveraged deal requires more bank equity participation than a 4x deal, all else equal. This increases the capital consumption of leveraged lending from a firm-wide capital perspective, since bank equity participations count toward RWA calculations.

What distinguishes the PRA approach from the ECB approach, in my experience, is the quality of the supervisory dialogue. The PRA supervisors I have worked with over the years are genuinely interested in understanding credit decisions and business judgments. They will challenge those judgments, rigorously, but they do so in dialogue rather than through bright-line rules. This can lead to better outcomes in specific cases — supervisors might accept a deal at 6.5x leverage if the credit story is genuinely compelling and the supporting analysis is rigorous. But it can also lead to inconsistency across institutions, with some banks permitted to write leveraged deals at leverage multiples that others are constrained from underwriting.

CAPITAL REQUIREMENTS DIRECTIVE AND REGULATION: ARCHITECTURE FOR LEVERAGED LENDING

The CRD IV/CRR framework (which became the CRD V/CRR2 with significant amendments in 2019) is the European transposition of Basel III. Understanding it requires understanding that it is a complex construct: some elements are discretionary (member states can choose tighter requirements), some are harmonized across the EU, and some have been subject to ongoing political and supervisory negotiation. For leveraged lending, the relevant elements are capital requirements (what percentage of assets banks must hold in capital), risk weight requirements (how much of an asset's value counts toward risk-weighted assets), and various add-ons and adjustments.

The baseline capital requirement is that a bank must maintain a Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1) ratio of 4.5%, a Tier 1 capital ratio of 6%, and a Total Capital ratio of 8%, measured against risk-weighted assets. Additionally, banks must maintain a capital buffer above these minima — a capital conservation buffer (CET1 of 2.5% of RWA) and potentially a countercyclical buffer (which member states can adjust). For the largest systemic institutions, there is an additional systemic risk buffer (between 1% and 3% of RWA depending on the institution's assessed systemic importance).

For a leveraged loan, the capital requirement works as follows. First, the loan is assigned a risk weight (which can be anywhere from 75% to 1250% depending on the underlying credit quality and the approach the bank uses). Then, the loan amount is multiplied by the risk weight to arrive at RWA. The bank's capital requirement is then calculated as a percentage of the RWA. For a highly rated loan, the RWA might be 50-75% of the nominal loan amount, meaning the bank needs to hold capital equal to about 8% of the RWA, or roughly 4-6% of the nominal loan amount. For an unrated leveraged loan at high leverage, the RWA could be 250-300% of the nominal loan amount (using the standardized approach, which I will explain below), meaning the bank needs to hold capital equal to 20-24% of the loan amount.

This is the mechanism by which capital requirements influence market structure. The bank that leads and holds a leveraged loan faces a significant capital charge. The bank that syndicates the loan to other lenders faces minimal capital charges on the syndicated portions (since it no longer holds the credit risk). This asymmetry in capital treatment creates powerful incentives for distribution and syndication. In a world without capital requirements, a bank that had good credit analysis and competitive pricing advantage might choose to hold significantly more of a deal it had underwritten. With capital requirements, that same bank becomes heavily incentivized to syndicate away the risk to investors who may not have the same credit analysis capabilities or may have different risk appetite.

This has been one of the most important structural changes in European leveraged finance since the financial crisis. The "originate to distribute" model has become nearly universal. A bank leading a leveraged deal might retain 5-15% of the exposure (with the exact percentage depending on capital constraints, market conditions, and sponsor relationships), while syndicating 85-95% to funds, CLO managers, insurance companies, and other non-bank lenders. This is economically rational given capital requirements, but it has distributed leverage and credit risk beyond the banking system, which has its own regulatory implications that I will explore later.

CRD/CRR RISK WEIGHTING: THE STANDARDIZED VS. IRB APPROACH

For leveraged loans, a bank can use one of two approaches to calculate risk weights: the standardized approach (which uses regulatory formulas and credit ratings) or the Internal Ratings-Based (IRB) approach (which uses the bank's own credit models and rating systems).

Under the standardized approach, the risk weight depends primarily on the external credit rating of the loan. An A-rated loan receives a 50% risk weight. A BBB-rated loan receives a 100% risk weight. A BB-rated loan receives a 150% risk weight. An unrated loan receives a 100% risk weight (unless specific conditions are met, in which case it might receive a higher weight). For leveraged loans, which are typically rated below investment grade, the 150-200% risk weights are common. This means that the regulatory capital charge for a speculative-grade leveraged loan can be substantial — 12-16% of the loan amount.

The IRB approach is available only to banks that have been approved by their supervisors to use internal models. It requires that the bank develop rigorous models of probability of default (PD), loss-given-default (LGD), and exposure-at-default (EAD), and then calculate risk weights based on those parameters. For a well-modeled leveraged loan, the IRB approach typically produces lower risk weights than the standardized approach, perhaps 120-150% for a 5.5x loan to a strong sponsor-backed business. The savings in capital requirement can be material — enough to make a deal more attractive to a bank from a capital efficiency perspective, or to allow lower pricing to a sponsor.

The regulatory arbitrage between standardized and IRB approaches has influenced bank behavior significantly. A bank that has IRB approval for its leveraged lending business has a competitive advantage in pricing, since it faces lower capital charges. This has incentivized banks to invest in credit models and to seek IRB approval. However, the process of building an IRB-approved model for leveraged lending is complex and time-consuming. It requires historical data, validation, and regulatory approval. During the period 2016-2019, I watched several mid-tier European banks either invest heavily in IRB model development or gradually exit the market, because they could not compete with banks that had lower capital charges through IRB models.

The regulators have become increasingly skeptical of IRB models in recent years. There were concerns that banks were using models to systematically underestimate risk weights, that correlations and recovery rates embedded in models were unrealistic, and that models were not properly validated against actual defaults and recoveries. This has led to the development of capital output floors, which I will discuss in the Basel IV section below.

LARGE EXPOSURE LIMITS AND HOLD LEVELS

One of the most consequential regulatory constraints on leveraged lending is the large exposure regime, codified in CRR Article 395 et seq. A large exposure is a credit exposure to a single counterparty or group of connected counterparties exceeding 10% of a bank's capital. For a large bank, the capital base is substantial, but so the thresholds are high. For a mid-tier bank, even a 10% threshold can be constraining.

The regulatory limit on large exposures is that they cannot exceed 25% of capital. This is the core constraint. If a bank has 2 billion euros of capital, its total exposure to a single sponsor or operating company cannot exceed 500 million euros (25% of capital). This creates a ceiling on the size of leveraged deals that a single bank can commit to, relative to its capital base. For a 200 million euro deal in an operating company, a bank with 2 billion euros of capital can theoretically commit up to 500 million euros of that deal — but only if it commits the same proportion of its capital to this single credit exposure as it commits to the largest exposure across its entire portfolio.

In practice, banks use internal large exposure policies that are more conservative than the regulatory maximum. A typical internal policy might limit exposure to a single sponsor to 10-15% of capital, and exposure to a single operating company to 5-8% of capital. This is because banks want to maintain flexibility to respond to economic downturns, to have capacity to commit to new deals when they arise, and to avoid excessive concentration in any single counterparty.

The large exposure limit has a profound impact on how deals get syndicated and where risk ends up. When a sponsor wants to finance a large acquisition — say, a 1 billion euro debt package — no single bank can commit the full amount under large exposure rules. The deal must be syndicated across multiple banks. This means that the market structure for large transactions is inherently distributed. It also means that a sponsor seeking to maximize certainty must build a strong syndicate early, rather than assuming it can rely on a handful of lead banks.

I have worked on many deals where the large exposure limit forced difficult compromises. A sponsor might have preferred to keep debt with a small group of relationship banks that understood the business and the sponsor's strategy. But the size of the deal might require commitments from 8-10 banks to stay within each bank's large exposure limit. This distributes knowledge of the deal across a wide group, increases the number of negotiating parties, and makes it harder to implement amendments and refinancing quickly (since amendments typically require consent from a large percentage of the debt syndicate).

The large exposure limit also creates hold level constraints, which are distinct from but related to capital requirements. A bank's "hold level" in a deal is the amount of debt it commits to finance while intending to retain it (rather than syndicate it away). Hold levels are determined by capital availability, large exposure limits, portfolio risk considerations, and relationship management with the sponsor. Typically, a lead arranger will hold 10-20% of a leveraged facility, depending on the deal size, the bank's capital, and the competitive position of the banks in the syndication.

In recent years, I have observed hold levels declining. Banks are syndicating away more and holding less, for three reasons: capital requirements have become more stringent, CRR large exposure limits are being monitored more carefully by supervisors, and non-bank lenders (CLO managers, credit funds, insurance companies) have become more sophisticated and deeper pools of capital. A bank that might have held 25% of a deal in 2006 might hold only 10-12% of an equivalent deal in 2023. This has important implications for credit quality and market discipline, which I will return to.

RISK-WEIGHTED ASSETS FOR LEVERAGED LOANS

The calculation of RWA for leveraged loans is where abstract regulatory rules become concrete economic impact. A bank's total capital requirement is calculated as a percentage of its total RWA. RWA measures not the actual amount of lending, but the risk-adjusted amount. A bank with 100 billion euros of total assets might have 60-70 billion euros of RWA, depending on the composition of those assets and the risk weights assigned.

For leveraged loans, the RWA calculation is the meeting point of several regulatory inputs. There is the credit risk weight (the 150% I mentioned earlier for a BB-rated loan). There may also be an add-on for expected loss, there may be a scalar applied for leverage (a factor multiplying the risk weight up if the loan is particularly leveraged), and there are various adjustments. The result is that a single leveraged loan might have a risk weight of 150-250%, which means it consumes 150-250% of its face value as RWA.

This creates an important dynamic: a 100 million euro leveraged loan at 200% risk weight is treated as 200 million euros of RWA. If a bank needs to maintain an 8% capital ratio, it must hold 16 million euros of capital against that loan. The capital charge on a leveraged loan is thus typically 12-16% of the loan amount. This is substantially higher than the cost of capital for most banks (which is typically 7-10% return on equity when one factors in the cost of equity and the leverage in the bank's balance sheet). A bank that syndicated this loan, retaining only 10% (10 million euros), would hold 2 million euros of capital and face a 20-32% capital charge on the retained portion.

The leverage scalar — a factor that increases risk weights for leveraged loans — has become increasingly important. The Basel framework includes provisions allowing for an increase in risk weight for highly leveraged exposures. Some banks apply a leverage scalar that increases the risk weight by 25-50% for loans to borrowers above 4x leverage, or above 6x leverage. This is theoretically justified (more leverage means more risk, so risk weights should be higher), but it has the effect of making the most leveraged deals significantly less attractive from a capital efficiency perspective.

I have sat in portfolio management meetings where a trade was being evaluated for inclusion in the bank's leveraged lending portfolio. The deal was a 5.8x leveraged acquisition by a strong sponsor. The economics made sense on a credit risk basis — the spread was adequate for the risk, the sponsor had a track record, the business was solid. But when we applied the capital charge (which with the leverage scalar was nearly 18% of the deal amount), the return on capital was insufficient. The bank declined to participate. The deal proceeded without us, and the sponsor found other lenders. This is not a failure of the market — it is functioning as intended, with capital-constrained banks declining to commit capital to the most leveraged deals. But it has redistributed the leveraged lending market away from banks and toward non-bank lenders.

BASEL III IMPACT ON LEVERAGED LENDING

Basel III, which was introduced following the financial crisis and has been phased in through 2019, represented a fundamental repricing of leverage and risk in bank regulation. The key Basel III requirements that impact leveraged lending are higher capital ratios, the leverage ratio requirement, and the liquidity ratios (LCR and NSFR).

The capital ratio requirements are the ones I have mostly described above. Basel III increased the minimum capital requirements and added buffers on top of those minimums. The effect for leveraged lending has been to make capital-intensive, and thus to tighten the syndication model — more distribution, lower holds.

The leverage ratio is a separate constraint, distinct from risk-weighted capital requirements. The leverage ratio is designed to ensure that banks do not become excessively leveraged even with relatively low risk weights. It is calculated as Tier 1 capital divided by total exposures (not risk-weighted), and the minimum is 3%. For a leveraged loan, this means that the bank's exposure to the loan counts fully toward the leverage ratio denominator, regardless of the risk weight. A bank with 100 billion euros of Tier 1 capital can have total exposures of no more than 3,333 billion euros (100 x 33.33). If a large portion of those exposures are leveraged loans, with high nominal amounts and lower risk weights, the leverage ratio can become a binding constraint even if the risk-weighted capital requirement is satisfied.

In practice, the leverage ratio has been less binding than the risk-weighted capital requirement for leveraged lending, since leverage is typically well-contained in modern leverage ratios. But in high-leverage environments, or for banks with large portfolios of leveraged loans, the leverage ratio can be the binding constraint.

The liquidity coverage ratio (LCR) and net stable funding ratio (NSFR) are the other Basel III pillars that affect leveraged lending. These ratios measure the adequacy of a bank's liquid assets and stable funding. A leveraged loan that is difficult to syndicate, or that the bank intends to hold, consumes stable funding. If a bank's NSFR is tight (meaning it doesn't have much excess stable funding), the bank may be reluctant to retain leveraged loans that tie up stable funding, even if the capital charge is acceptable.

BASEL IV (BASEL III FINAL): OUTPUT FLOORS AND IMPLICATIONS

Basel IV — the final set of Basel III reforms, agreed in 2017 but still being phased in as of 2026 — introduced several features that have had significant impact on leveraged lending. The most important for our purposes is the output floor: a requirement that a bank's capital requirements calculated using internal models (IRB) cannot be less than 72.5% of the capital requirements calculated using the standardized approach.

The output floor is the regulatory response to the concern I mentioned earlier: that banks were using IRB models to systematically underestimate risk weights. By introducing an output floor, regulators ensured that even banks with sophisticated internal models cannot reduce their capital charges below a meaningful floor relative to the standardized approach. For leveraged lending, this has the practical effect of reducing the capital advantage of IRB models.

Consider a leveraged loan to a 5.5x borrower. Under the standardized approach, the risk weight might be 150-175% (for a BB-rated loan). Under an IRB model, the risk weight might have been calculated as 100-120%. But with the output floor, the IRB risk weight cannot be lower than 72.5% of the standardized approach risk weight, which would be around 110-127%. The result is that the IRB advantage is substantially reduced. Banks can no longer use sophisticated models to achieve dramatic capital savings on leveraged loans.

This has reinforced the trend toward syndication and distribution. If the capital advantage to using IRB models is modest, then the economic incentive to build IRB infrastructure for leveraged lending is reduced. Banks are more likely to use the standardized approach, which is simpler to apply. And with the standardized approach, the capital charges are higher, which increases the pressure to syndicate.

Basel IV also introduced the Credit Risk Mitigation Add-on: a requirement that certain exposures have additional capital charges if they fail to meet specific criteria for risk mitigation. This affects how leveraged loans are documented and structured — there are incentives to ensure that security packages, guarantees, and cross-defaults are clear and documented, since poor documentation can result in additional capital charges.

The impact of Basel IV has been to reduce bank appetite for leveraged lending by increasing capital charges and reducing the benefit of sophisticated modeling. This has accelerated the shift of leveraged lending from banks to non-bank lenders, which is the subject of a later section.

THE DEBATE OVER LEVERAGE LIMITS: REGULATORS VS. MARKET PARTICIPANTS

The question of whether leverage limits should be hard regulatory ceilings or soft guidance — and if guidance, where should the limits be — has been a persistent debate between regulators and market participants over the past decade.

The regulatory perspective, which I think is genuinely held by supervisors I have worked with, is this: there is a pattern in credit cycles of increasing leverage and declining underwriting discipline. As the economy strengthens, credit spreads compress, banks become more willing to take risk, and deals get done at higher leverage with weaker covenants. This is perfectly rational from a competitive perspective — a bank that declines to participate in a 5.5x deal while its competitors are pricing at 5.5x is going to lose market share. The problem is that when the credit cycle turns — when economic growth slows, when business performance deteriorates, when credit spreads widen — all of these leveraged deals become stressed simultaneously. The bank that competed by underwriting loose leverage is now facing a portfolio of distressed credits. Regulatory leverage limits are designed to break this procyclical pattern, to force discipline in booms, and to protect the financial system from excessive credit concentrations during busts.

This logic is economically sound. Yet it creates significant frustration among sponsors and some financial advisors, who argue that leverage is contextual. A company with stable, contractual cash flows can sustain higher leverage than a company with volatile operating performance. A sponsor with a strong track record and a clear value creation plan can execute at higher leverage than a less experienced sponsor. A transaction with identified synergies that will meaningfully improve EBITDA can be financed at higher leverage than one without synergies. Regulatory leverage limits that do not accommodate these differences are, from this perspective, blunt instruments that prevent economically sound transactions from being executed.

Over the years, I have come to see validity in both perspectives. The regulatory concern about procyclical leverage is not theoretical — I have lived through it. In 2006-2007, I saw deals financed at 6-7x leverage with minimal covenants being aggressively competed for by multiple lead banks. The credit analysis was often summary and optimistic. When the financial crisis hit, many of these deals failed. The financial system faced the consequences. From a regulatory perspective, preventing a repeat of this was a legitimate priority.

But I have also seen deals that deserved to be financed at higher leverage being turned down, or being financed at worse terms, because of regulatory constraints. I remember a roll-up transaction in the specialty chemicals space that made enormous economic sense at 5.5x leverage — synergies were identifiable, the sponsor had a track record, the business was solid. But the regulatory environment had tightened, and several lead banks declined to participate at that leverage multiple. The deal was ultimately financed through a club structure of non-bank lenders at a higher cost to the sponsor. Was value destroyed? Perhaps some, through higher financing costs. Was risk materially mitigated by refusing to finance at 5.5x? That is less clear.

The debate continues, but the regulatory constraints have become entrenched. The leverage limits are not going away. Market participants have adapted by finding ways to finance around them — through non-bank lenders, through alternative structures, through building equity cushions. But the baseline fact remains: in Europe, there are regulatory ceilings on leverage that constrain the market relative to pre-2008 levels, and these ceilings have become harder to exceed as regulatory scrutiny has increased.

THE ECB DEFINITION OF "LEVERAGED" AND ITS IMPLICATIONS

I spent considerable time above discussing the 6x leverage limit, but I did not fully explore the definition of what makes a loan "leveraged" in the first place. The ECB's definition — which has become the reference point for much of the European market — is: a loan is leveraged if the borrower's total debt (including the new facility being underwritten) exceeds 4x EBITDA.

This definition has important implications. First, it creates a bright line. A deal at 3.9x leverage is not "leveraged," and is subject to less supervisory scrutiny. A deal at 4.1x leverage is "leveraged," and supervisors will apply the leveraged lending guidelines. This creates obvious incentive to structure deals just under the 4x threshold, and I have seen many deals structured with careful attention to maximizing leverage while staying just under 4x. Whether this is economically sensible is debatable — there is nothing magical about 3.9x vs. 4.1x. But the regulatory definition creates behavioral incentives.

Second, the definition is based on EBITDA, which is a reported metric that can be adjusted and interpreted in various ways. The ECB's guidance allows for adjustments to EBITDA to reflect normalized performance, to add back costs that are not expected to recur, and to adjust for acquisitions and dispositions. This creates room for interpretation, and thus room for negotiation between banks and their supervisors about whether a deal truly exceeds 4x leverage under a normalized EBITDA.

I have sat in supervisory meetings where the critical question was: what EBITDA should be used to measure leverage for regulatory purposes? Should it be the trailing twelve-month EBITDA? Should it be normalized EBITDA excluding the impact of a one-time event? Should it be forward-looking EBITDA that assumes synergies from an acquisition are achieved? Different assumptions lead to materially different leverage calculations. A company with 100 million euros of reported EBITDA but 115 million euros of normalized EBITDA looks less leveraged when measured on a normalized basis. A 450 million euro debt package is 4.5x on reported EBITDA but 3.9x on normalized EBITDA.

The ECB's guidance is clear that normalized EBITDA is appropriate, but the application of normalization is genuinely complex. What costs are truly one-time? How much of a cost increase is expected to persist? Should exceptional items that are non-recurring be added back? There is legitimate professional judgment involved, and supervisors will challenge judgments that seem to them optimistic.

This has created a cottage industry of leverage calculation advisors who work with sponsors to find the maximum sustainable EBITDA definition that will support the leverage level needed to finance an acquisition. This is not dishonest — it is professionally competent work. But it does create opportunities for definitions to creep incrementally higher, which has implications for how much regulatory "give" is being taken out of the system in aggregate.

HOW BANKS MANAGE REGULATORY CONSTRAINTS: SYNDICATION AND DISTRIBUTION STRATEGY

The single most important adaptation by banks to regulatory constraints on leveraged lending has been the evolution of syndication and distribution strategy. In the pre-crisis era, banks that led deals often retained 25-35% of the exposure. The syndicate might expand from a small group of relationship banks to a broader group as the deal progressed through the market, but the lead bank maintained a substantial position. Today, the model is entirely different.

A typical leveraged deal today is distributed as follows: the lead banks retain 8-15% each, the co-lead/arranger banks might retain 5-8% each, and the remaining 60-75% of the deal is syndicated to institutional investors. This distribution is not based on some natural optimal division of the market — it is based on capital constraints and RWA limitations. The lead bank would often prefer to retain more, from the perspective of relationship management and upside participation. But given capital requirements, large exposure limits, and portfolio constraints, retaining more is simply not possible.

The consequence is that the risk of leveraged deals is now borne primarily by institutional investors — CLO managers, credit funds, insurance companies, pension funds — rather than by banks. Banks have shifted from being risk-takers to being intermediaries. This is rational from a capital efficiency perspective, but it has implications for credit market discipline and for where systemic risk accumulates. If CLO managers own 60% of a leveraged syndication, then the CLO market becomes the locus of leverage risk in the system.

Banks have adapted their business model accordingly. Rather than earning returns on capital by holding risk, banks earn fees for underwriting, arranging, syndication, and administrative services. A typical leveraged deal will generate 200-300 basis points of upfront fees for the arranger banks (depending on the size and complexity of the deal), and another 10-15 basis points of annual administration fees over the life of the loan. For a lead bank that retains only 10% of a 300 million euro deal, the upfront fee (60-90 basis points of the full deal amount, of which the bank gets credit for arranging and distributing) might be 2-3 million euros. The held position (30 million euros at maybe 250-300 basis points spread) might generate 750,000 to 900,000 euros of annual net interest income for the bank.

The financial incentive is now entirely aligned with distribution. A bank that syndicated 85% of the deal and collected full fees makes more money than a bank that held 35% of the deal but had to hold significant capital against it. This has changed the bank's incentive structure in ways that are subtle but important. Banks are incentivized to arrange deals and move them to market quickly, rather than to maintain deep credit analysis and to hold deals for the long term. The consequence is that there has been a shift in the nature of credit analysis — from buy-and-hold credit work (by banks) to syndication credit work (by institutions assembling CLOs and credit funds).

I have watched this transition over the past fifteen years, and I think it has real implications for credit discipline. When a bank held significant portions of its deals, the bank's investors (the bank's equity holders and creditors) had a material interest in the quality of credit analysis. A bank that made poor credit decisions would see its earnings deteriorate, its capital ratios compress, and its cost of capital increase. This created alignment between the bank's credit decisions and the bank's financial wellbeing. Today, a bank that makes poor credit decisions on syndicated deals still earns the upfront fees regardless of whether the underlying credits perform well. The moral hazard inherent in intermediation (in which the intermediary earns fees regardless of outcome) has become more prominent.

This is not to say that banks are indifferent to credit quality — they are not. A bank's reputation is valuable, and a bank with a reputation for poor credit analysis will find sponsors less willing to work with them, and will find it harder to syndicate deals. But the economic incentive to dig deeply on credit analysis is genuinely weaker when the bank will not be bearing most of the long-term credit risk.

RISE OF NON-BANK LENDERS AS REGULATORY ARBITRAGE

The regulatory constraints on bank leveraged lending have created a powerful opening for non-bank lenders. These institutions — including CLO managers, private credit funds, insurance companies, pension funds, and other non-bank financial institutions — are not subject to the same capital requirements, large exposure limits, or regulatory scrutiny that banks face. This is both economically rational (since they are not deposits-taking institutions creating systemic risk if they fail) and problematic (since they are not subject to the same regulatory oversight, and since leverage and systemic risk are accumulating outside the regulated banking sector).

The rise of non-bank lending in the leveraged finance market has been dramatic. In 2010, non-bank lenders provided perhaps 15-20% of leveraged facility volume in Europe. By 2023, non-bank lenders were providing 45-50% of leveraged facility volume. This shift is entirely explicable through the lens of regulatory capital constraints: banks can no longer profitably retain leveraged loans due to capital requirements, so they distribute to institutions that are not subject to those constraints.

The non-bank lenders have filled the gap with increasingly sophisticated products. CLO managers have developed bespoke CLO structures to hold leveraged loans. Private credit funds have raised capital specifically to invest in large leveraged financings. Insurance companies have allocated capital to leveraged loan portfolios. Pension funds and sovereign wealth funds have developed credit investment competence. The result is a vibrant leveraged lending market that does not depend on bank capital.

This is positive from the perspective of enabling deals to be financed. It is positive from the perspective of encouraging competition and innovation in financing products. But it has implications for systemic risk and financial stability. If the largest concentrations of leveraged loan exposure are in CLOs, and if CLOs themselves are leveraged vehicles (which they typically are, with leverage of 5-8x), then we have leverage piled on top of leverage. A stress scenario in which credit spreads widen, leveraged loans deteriorate, and CLOs face margin calls creates potential for amplified selling pressure and systemic stress.

From a regulatory perspective, this has prompted increased focus on shadow banking and systemic risk in non-bank financial intermediation. Regulators are increasingly concerned with correlation risk — the tendency for many non-bank lenders to be similarly positioned, and thus to exit positions simultaneously when stress arises. The Financial Stability Board has created working groups on non-bank financial intermediation. The ECB has issued guidance on CLO composition and risk management. But the fundamental fact remains that leveraged lending has shifted partially outside the banking system, creating new loci of risk.

I think this is a genuine challenge for financial stability. It is not immediately obvious that non-bank lending at 5-8x leverage in credit instruments is materially safer than bank lending at similar leverage when leverage factors are controlled for. If anything, the substitution of non-bank lending for bank lending has shifted leverage from institutions with stable funding, deposit insurance, and access to central bank liquidity, to institutions without those stabilizers. This is not an indictment of non-bank lenders — they have legitimate roles and can operate safely — but it is a reality that must be acknowledged.

MiFID II AND ITS IMPACT ON LEVERAGED LOAN RESEARCH AND DISTRIBUTION

The Markets in Financial Instruments Directive II (MiFID II), which came into effect in January 2018, has had subtle but important consequences for the leveraged lending market, particularly around research and distribution practices.

MiFID II created a requirement that banks unbundle research from trading services. Prior to MiFID II, a bank could provide research about leveraged loans and other credit instruments to institutional clients, and the bank would be compensated through wider bid-ask spreads or other trading advantages. This model was standard practice. MiFID II required that research be separately priced and that clients pay explicitly for research. For equity research, this unbundling had obvious consequences — the sell-side equity research business was restructured, research teams were reduced, and research providers consolidated. For credit research, including leveraged loan research, the consequences have been more subtle but meaningful.

First, MiFID II made it more expensive to provide detailed leveraged loan research. A bank that wanted to maintain research coverage of sponsors and operating companies had to incur the cost of that research and charge it to clients explicitly. Many clients were unwilling to pay for leveraged loan research — they expected access as part of the banking relationship. Consequently, banks reduced leveraged loan research coverage. The number of banks publishing syndication reports, credit analysis, and market commentary on leveraged deals has declined.

Second, MiFID II affected research conflicts. A bank that was underwriting a leveraged deal had incentives to publish positive research about that deal to facilitate syndication. MiFID II did not prohibit this, but it made the conflict more visible and subject to regulatory scrutiny. This created incentives for banks to be somewhat more balanced in their research, or to publish research through research providers rather than through the bank's own sales force.

Third, MiFID II affected how institutional investors received information. Investors now had to explicitly commission research from banks, or to access publicly available information. This reduced the flow of proprietary analysis from banks to investors, and increased reliance on public sources of information. For a sponsor or operating company wanting to communicate its story to the market, this meant less access to the syndication process for direct outreach. The syndicate was built more through the arranger's roadshow and less through individual bank relationships.

Overall, I think MiFID II has made the leveraged lending market somewhat more transparent and has reduced potential conflicts, but it has also made it more expensive to finance deals (since clients now bear the explicit cost of research and analysis that they previously received implicitly), and it has reduced the flow of proprietary information to the market.

SECURITIZATION REGULATION AND THE IMPACT ON CLOS

Securitization regulation in Europe has evolved significantly since the financial crisis, with particular implications for CLOs (collateralized loan obligations), which are securitization vehicles holding leveraged loans.

The first major post-crisis securitization regulation came through the RMBS/CMBS regulations (EU Regulations 1060/2009 and 1286/2014, and subsequently the Securitization Regulation 2017/2402). These regulations created specific requirements for securitization assets, including underwriting standards, transparency requirements, and liability frameworks. For CLOs specifically, the regulations required that CLO managers maintain a minimum percentage of the equity risk of the securitization (a "skin in the game" requirement), that they provide investors with detailed loan-by-loan information, and that they comply with various operational requirements.

The skin in the game requirement — typically 5% of the securitization structure — was designed to ensure that CLO managers had a material interest in the quality of the underlying assets and did not engage in excessive risk-taking. This makes theoretical sense: if a CLO manager owns 5% of the equity upside and downside, the manager is incentivized to underwrite assets conservatively. However, the actual impact is more nuanced. A CLO manager that is raising a 500 million euro CLO must commit 25 million euros of skin in the game (5%). But the CLO manager may fund this capital through investors in the manager's funds, or through co-investors, and the manager's personal capital commitment may be much smaller than 25 million euros. The economic incentive to avoid losses is real but attenuated by leverage.

The Securitization Regulation also created STS (Simple, Transparent, and Standardized) securitization criteria. CLOs that meet STS criteria benefit from reduced capital charges for banks and insurance companies that hold them, and benefits in regulatory treatment. The criteria include origination requirements (loans must meet quality standards), portfolio composition requirements (the CLO must have portfolio-level diversification), and operational requirements (the CLO manager must have adequate operational capability). The result is that STS CLOs have become the standard form for large-scale CLO issuance in Europe.

From the perspective of leveraged lending, STS securitization regulation has several implications. First, it has created standardization in the loans that go into CLOs. STS regulations require that loans meet specific origination standards (e.g., mandatory financial covenants, mandatory acceleration clauses). This has improved the quality of leveraged loans in the market, since loans issued into STS CLOs are subject to more stringent requirements than syndicated loans that are not destined for securitization. Second, it has increased capital costs for non-STS securitizations, which has encouraged CLO managers to structure STS CLOs. Third, it has created transparency requirements that have improved market participants' understanding of CLO portfolios and risks.

I think STS regulation has been on balance beneficial for the leveraged financing market. It has increased transparency, improved loan quality, and created standardization. The downside is that it has added complexity and costs to CLO issuance, and it has created incentives for CLO managers to follow the STS template even when slightly different approaches might be more economical for specific situations. But on balance, I believe the trade-off is favorable.

EUROPEAN COMMISSION WORK ON LEVERAGED FINANCE

The European Commission, the executive branch of the European Union, has become increasingly engaged with leveraged finance policy in recent years. The Commission has issued several reports and position papers on leveraged finance, with particular focus on the relationship between leverage growth and financial stability.

The Commission's concern, articulated in various reports since 2018, is that leveraged finance growth has been outpacing GDP growth, that leverage multiples have been increasing (particularly in large LBO transactions), and that the concentration of leveraged lending risk in non-bank lenders creates potential systemic risk. The Commission has suggested that greater convergence of regulation across member states would be beneficial, and that there may be a need for further harmonization of leverage limits and syndication practices.

One specific area of Commission focus has been the definition of SME lending, which can receive favorable regulatory treatment. The Commission has been concerned that some large acquisition financings are being classified as SME lending when they should not be, and that this creates regulatory arbitrage. The result has been increasing scrutiny of SME classifications and tighter definition of what qualifies for favorable treatment.

Another area of Commission engagement has been the regulation of non-bank financial intermediation. The Commission has proposed that non-bank lenders should be subject to greater transparency requirements and systemic risk assessments. There has been discussion of whether non-bank lenders should be subject to leverage limits similar to those facing banks. These proposals are contentious — non-bank lenders argue that leverage limits are inappropriate for non-deposit-taking institutions, and that competitive fairness requires that banks and non-banks not be subject to identical constraints. This debate continues.

NATIONAL REGULATORY DIVERGENCE WITHIN THE EU

Although the European Union has created a harmonized regulatory framework through CRD/CRR and various directives, significant divergence remains across member states in how leveraged lending is regulated and supervised.

Some of this divergence is intentional — member states are explicitly allowed under CRR to impose more stringent capital requirements, to set stricter leverage limits, or to apply different national discretions. Some divergence reflects different supervisory practices and priorities. The ECB's leverage lending guidance is issued to banks under its direct supervision (significant institutions in the eurozone), but not all European banks are directly supervised by the ECB. The PRA is the regulator for UK-regulated entities. The German regulator (BaFin), the French regulator (ACPR), and other national regulators have significant supervisory authority.

The practical consequence is that a leveraged deal that can be financed through one bank (regulated in one jurisdiction) at a certain leverage level might not be financeable through a bank in a different jurisdiction at the same leverage level. I have experienced this directly when trying to build a syndicate for a pan-European transaction. A German bank might have strict internal leverage limits based on BaFin guidance. A UK bank might be subject to PRA leverage expectations that differ from ECB guidance. A bank regulated by a different national regulator might have its own policies.

This divergence creates inefficiency — it means that the market for leveraged lending is not fully unified across the EU, but rather is somewhat fragmented by jurisdiction. It also creates incentives for regulatory arbitrage, where sponsors and banks try to finance transactions through the jurisdictions with the most accommodating regulatory environment. I have seen deals with no particular economic rationale for being financed through a specific jurisdiction, other than that the regulatory environment in that jurisdiction was more permissive.

The ideal from a market efficiency perspective would be full harmonization of leverage limits and underwriting standards across the EU. But the reality is that member states value supervisory discretion, and that central harmonization is politically difficult. I expect that divergence will persist, and that sponsors and banks will continue to make financing choices based partly on regulatory optimization.

PRUDENTIAL SUPERVISION OF LEVERAGED LENDING: THE SREP PROCESS

Banks in the EU are subject to Supervisory Review and Evaluation Process (SREP), which is the framework by which regulators conduct ongoing supervision and set bank-specific capital requirements and expectations. The SREP process has a specific component for credit risk management and credit risk concentration, and leveraged lending is a material focus area in SREP assessments.

During a SREP assessment related to leveraged lending, regulators typically examine: the bank's credit policies and underwriting standards for leveraged lending; the bank's exposure to leveraged lending and concentration by sponsor and sector; the bank's credit risk management practices; the bank's capital modeling for leveraged loans; the bank's stress testing assumptions; and the bank's return adequacy assumptions. The regulator will challenge credit assumptions that seem optimistic, will require adjustments to stress testing, and may impose Pillar 2 capital requirements above the Pillar 1 minimum if the regulator believes the bank's leveraged lending practices pose material risk.

I have been through many SREP cycles as a bank executive. They are detailed, demanding, and often contentious. The regulator's perspective is that banks have incentives to underestimate risk when markets are favorable, and that supervisors must therefore challenge banks' assumptions rigorously. The bank's perspective is that supervisors sometimes use hindsight bias, asking banks to explain why they did not assume in 2019 that the worst pandemic in a century would occur in 2020, and that overly conservative stress testing can undermine the bank's competitiveness if the stress scenarios are more severe than actual experience.

What I have learned through multiple SREP cycles is that the quality of the relationship between the bank and the supervisor matters enormously. A supervisory relationship built on trust, where the bank's credit team and the supervisor's credit team speak a common language and respect each other's competence, tends to be more productive than an adversarial relationship. The regulator is more willing to accept reasonable assumptions from a bank with a strong credit track record than from a bank with a history of credit losses. And the bank is more willing to make adjustments to policy if it understands the supervisor's reasoning, rather than if it feels the supervisor is being arbitrary.

The SREP process has also become increasingly focused on concentration risk. Regulators will calculate the bank's total leverage to the top 10 sponsors, the total leverage to specific sectors (telecom, utilities, healthcare), and will assess whether the bank's portfolio can withstand stress scenarios that are correlated across credits. This has driven banks to actively manage sponsor concentration and sector concentration, and to be thoughtful about the maximum leverage the bank will extend to a single sponsor.

IMPACT OF REGULATION ON MARKET STRUCTURE AND COMPETITION

The cumulative effect of the regulatory framework I have described has been to reshape market structure and competition in European leveraged finance. The days of a handful of mega-banks dominating leveraged lending are gone. The regulatory framework has distributed leverage and leverage risk across a much broader set of institutions.

The institutional landscape today includes: a small number of mega-banks (Deutsche Bank, BNP Paribas, Barclays, HSBC) that remain significant players in leveraged lending but now primarily as arrangers and distributors rather than hold players; a larger number of mid-tier banks that maintain leveraged lending franchises but with carefully managed leverage limits and hold levels; a very large number of specialized non-bank lenders (CLO managers, credit funds, insurance companies) that have become the ultimate holders of leveraged lending risk; and a growing number of fintech and alternative lenders experimenting with leveraged lending and credit underwriting.

The competitive dynamics have shifted. Rather than competing on ability to hold credit risk (which requires capital), banks now compete on ability to arrange deals and to syndicate them efficiently. The skills that matter are deal engineering, sponsor relationships, market intelligence about where demand exists, and the ability to structure deals in ways that optimize tax and regulatory treatment. Credit analysis remains important, but it is somewhat less of a competitive differentiator than it was when banks held significant portions of deals.

Pricing in the market has been driven down by excess capacity of non-bank lenders. When CLO managers have substantial dry powder (capital raised but not yet deployed), or when credit funds are trying to deploy capital quickly, pricing pressures on leverage loans increase. Spreads have compressed in favorable markets. This has made it harder for smaller banks to compete and has contributed to consolidation among regional banks and the exit of some mid-tier banks from the leveraged lending market.

The concentration of leverage in non-bank lenders has also changed underwriting standards. Banks are now willing to syndicate loans with weaker covenants or more favorable borrower terms than they would retain, because the bank is not bearing the long-term risk. Non-bank lenders have sometimes been willing to accept those terms because they have different incentive structures and risk tolerances. The result is that the average covenant package in a leveraged loan today is somewhat looser than it was 10-15 years ago.

I think on balance the market structure that has evolved is resilient and competitive, but it has created some risks. The concentration of leverage in non-bank lenders creates potential for systemic stress if credit conditions deteriorate significantly. The shift away from relationship banking toward transaction-based intermediation may reduce banks' incentives to deeply understand borrowers. The compression of spreads in favorable credit markets may have left insufficient cushion for stress scenarios. But these are challenges that supervisors are alert to, and that I believe the market and supervisors are reasonably well-equipped to manage.

WHAT TWENTY YEARS TEACHES

I began this chapter by noting that when I started my career in 2004, regulation was a constraint but not the defining characteristic of leveraged finance. I end by reflecting on what two decades of watching regulation evolve has taught me.

First, regulation matters enormously. The regulatory framework I have described in this chapter — the capital requirements, the leverage limits, the large exposure restrictions, the securitization rules — has shaped every major transaction I have worked on in the past decade. Any professional in leveraged finance who thinks regulation is peripheral is not paying adequate attention. Regulatory architecture determines what deals can be financed, at what leverage, with what terms, and at what cost. Understanding regulation is not optional; it is foundational.

Second, regulation is usually right in intent, even when it is sometimes awkward in execution. The leverage limits exist because of observed cycles of over-leverage followed by credit stress. The capital requirements exist because under-capitalized banks that take excessive risk create systemic risk. The securitization rules exist because transparency and proper incentive alignment reduce fraud and reduce risk-taking beyond economic sustainability. I have seen the consequences of deregulation (the pre-crisis environment) and over-regulation (some aspects of the post-crisis environment), and I believe the regulatory framework we have evolved to is closer to optimal than either extreme. Could it be improved? Yes, absolutely. But the core architecture is sound.

Third, regulation is best enforced through relationships rather than bright lines. The most effective supervisors I have worked with are those who understand the underlying credit economics, who are willing to engage in detailed discussions about individual transactions, and who adjust their perspective based on compelling arguments from bankers. The supervisors who are most problematic are those who apply rules mechanically, without considering context. The best regulatory frameworks leave room for supervisory judgment, applied by competent supervisors in dialogue with competent bankers.

Fourth, regulation creates behavioral incentives that sometimes produce unexpected consequences. Banks that are heavily capital-constrained become distribution machines rather than risk-takers. Non-bank lenders grow to fill the gap, creating new systemic risks. Sponsors learn how to structure deals to optimize regulatory treatment rather than economic treatment. These consequences are not bad — they are how markets adapt to constraint. But regulators should be alert to the possibility that regulations produce effects beyond what was intended.

Fifth, the leverage finance market in Europe is fundamentally healthier than it was in 2006-2007. The loans being underwritten today are documented with tighter covenants, there is more transparency around credit quality, capital buffers are more substantial, and leverage multiples have increased only modestly despite a decade-long favorable credit environment. This is not because banks have become more conscientious — it is because regulation and market discipline have created incentives to be more careful. This is progress.

Finally, the question of whether leverage should be higher or lower than it currently is will never be settled. From a prudential perspective, leverage could probably be higher than current regulatory limits in many cases without creating undue systemic risk. From a borrower's perspective, borrowers would often prefer to access higher leverage to minimize dilution to equity holders. But from a financial stability perspective, there is value in maintaining some discipline on leverage even when credit appears safe. The regulatory framework has chosen to maintain leverage discipline partly by limiting leverage multiples, partly by constraining bank capital to force distribution of risk, and partly by requiring strong documentation and covenants. This framework is imperfect, but I believe it is reasonable.

I sometimes think about what will seem obvious in 2045 that we are not seeing in 2026. Perhaps we will look back and realize that the leverage limits were unnecessarily restrictive, that we could have safely financed deals at higher leverage, and that regulatory conservatism destroyed economic value. Or perhaps we will look back and realize that we were too complacent about non-bank leverage, that concentrating leverage risk outside the banking system created systemic vulnerabilities we did not recognize, and that we should have regulated non-banks more heavily. My guess is that we will see elements of both — some regulation was too tight, some not tight enough, and the next crisis will reveal where we got the balance wrong.

But that is the nature of regulation. It is an iterative process of learning from history, adapting to new risks, trying to stay ahead of the next crisis while not overreacting to the last one. After twenty years in this business, I have developed deep respect for the challenge regulators face and for the genuine effort they make to get the balance right. I do not envy them their responsibility.
