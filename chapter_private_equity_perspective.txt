THE PRIVATE EQUITY PERSPECTIVE: Understanding Your Most Important Counterparty

PART ONE: INTRODUCTION — UNDERSTANDING YOUR COUNTERPARTY

I will never forget the moment when I realized that leveraged finance professionals and private equity sponsors speak two fundamentally different languages, even when they use the same words. I was sitting in a conference room on the forty-second floor of a Manhattan office building in March 2004, negotiating the credit agreement for a $850 million dividend recapitalization of a consumer products company. The deal had been structured to return approximately $450 million to the sponsors while maintaining investment-grade metrics. At least, that's how the sponsors saw it.

On my side of the table sat my credit team—seasoned lenders from a major commercial bank who had spent decades underwriting leveraged transactions. On their side sat the principal sponsor team from a top-tier PE firm, accompanied by their operating partners and financial advisors. We were debating covenant language around maintenance leverage ratios, and I was watching two fundamentally different worldviews collide.

The sponsor's lead negotiator—a partner I had worked with on three previous deals—said something that I now recognize as the defining insight of my entire career in leveraged finance. He leaned back in his chair and said: "You guys are trying to protect yourself against what we're *afraid* to do. We're trying to build flexibility for what we might *need* to do. That's why this deal is so hard."

It took me a few moments to understand what he meant, but it fundamentally changed how I approach credit analysis and negotiation with PE firms. The tension he described—between a lender's protective pessimism and a sponsor's flexible optimism—is the fundamental reality of leveraged finance. Understanding this tension, and understanding how it emerges from the actual economics and incentive structures of private equity firms, is essential for anyone who works with PE in any capacity. Whether you are a credit investor, a banker structuring a deal, a lawyer documenting credit agreements, or an advisor helping sponsors think through capital structure, your effectiveness depends entirely on understanding how PE firms think, how they operate, and what actually motivates their decision-making.

Over my twenty-three years in leveraged finance, I have worked on both sides of the sponsor-lender relationship. I have sat in the capital markets groups of two major commercial banks, advising sponsors on the structuring and financing of acquisitions. I have worked for financial advisory firms counseling sponsors on how to navigate credit markets. I have been the credit investor, analyzing PE-backed companies for distress risk. And I have sat across the table from every type of sponsor imaginable—from the most restrictive operators focused entirely on financial engineering, to the most hands-on operational improvers who genuinely believed they could transform their portfolio companies.

What I have learned is that PE firms are not irrational actors, nor are they necessarily more aggressive risk-takers than other market participants. Rather, they operate within a specific economic and structural framework that fundamentally shapes how they think about leverage, capital structure, risk management, and exit strategies. When you understand this framework—when you truly grasp the incentives that drive PE decision-making—credit analysis becomes not just a mathematical exercise but a human one. You begin to anticipate not just what sponsors will want in terms of covenants or documentation, but why they want it, and what situations might cause them to break their promises to you.

This chapter is about demystifying that framework. It is about understanding your counterparty.

The relationship between leverage providers and private equity sponsors is fundamentally asymmetrical. We think of the leverage provider—the commercial bank, the credit fund, the direct lender—as the senior, more conservative partner in the transaction. We are lending capital against cash flows and assets. We are subordinated to equity returns only through our structural position in the capital stack. Yet in many ways, PE sponsors have significantly more influence over the ultimate outcome of any leveraged transaction than lenders do. They control the investment, the operational strategy, the capital deployment, the timing of the exit, and ultimately, whether the company survives or fails.

Moreover, PE sponsors are repeat players. While any individual credit investor might encounter a sponsor once, or a handful of times, the sponsor's businesses will include dozens of portfolio companies over their fund life. The sponsor has accumulated institutional knowledge about what works and what doesn't. The sponsor has negotiated hundreds of credit agreements. Most importantly, the sponsor's entire economic model depends on understanding how debt works, how to maximize returns in a levered structure, and how to manage the relationship with lenders across multiple cycles and market conditions.

For lenders and advisors, this creates both a challenge and an opportunity. The challenge is that you are invariably dealing with a counterparty who understands leverage as well as you do, and probably better understands how to manage it toward their own advantage. The opportunity is that once you understand how PE firms operate—once you grasp the fund economics, the incentive structures, the timeline pressures, and the strategic choices that drive PE behavior—you can both protect yourself more effectively and build more productive relationships with sponsors.

This is not a zero-sum game. The most successful lenders I have known over my career are not those who fought sponsors on every point of documentation or who imposed the most restrictive covenants. Rather, they are those who understood what sponsors actually cared about, which battles were truly important versus which were just negotiating theater, and how to structure deals that genuinely aligned the interests of debt and equity. These lenders made money not because they extracted the most punitive terms from sponsors, but because they made lending decisions based on deep understanding of sponsor behavior and sponsor incentives.

I want to start with a basic principle that shapes everything I discuss in this chapter: the alignment and misalignment between debt and equity perspectives in a leveraged transaction. On the surface, lenders and sponsors share a common interest—they both want the company to succeed, grow, and generate sufficient cash flow to service its obligations. But beneath this surface, their interests diverge in fundamental ways.

When a PE firm buys a company for $500 million and layers $350 million of debt onto it, the sponsors have optimized for equity IRR. This might mean a 2.5x leverage ratio at entry, with a target of maintaining that leverage throughout the hold period, or potentially taking it higher for a refinancing that returns capital to sponsors. The sponsors are maximizing the probability of hitting their return targets given the constraints of the debt structure. But by definition, the leverage that maximizes equity IRR introduces risk into the debt—the risk that if something goes wrong, the equity will be insufficient to absorb the loss and the lenders will take a haircut.

From a lender's perspective, too much leverage is inherently risky. A credit investor wants sufficient equity cushion that even if the company experiences a down cycle, covenant defaults and restructuring become unlikely. But here is where the asymmetry becomes apparent: the PE sponsor is not optimizing for the lender's risk profile. The sponsor is optimizing for their own returns. If the lender's optimal leverage is 2.0x and the sponsor's optimal leverage is 2.5x, the deal that actually gets done will be somewhere in between—but always somewhat closer to the sponsor's preference, because the sponsor controls the enterprise value and equity check, and the sponsor ultimately decides whether the deal gets done at all.

I have seen this play out across dozens of transactions. In 2007, I was advising a large sponsor on the acquisition of a business services company for $1.2 billion. The sponsor's investment thesis was solid—clear revenue growth trajectory, sticky customer base, experienced management team. But the market for this company was extremely competitive, with multiple bidders. The sponsor's financial advisors modeled the deal at three different leverage levels: 2.8x Net Leverage at entry, 3.2x, and 3.5x. At 2.8x, the deal penciled to a 2.1x MOIC (multiple on invested capital) and 18% IRR, assuming base case operations and exit in 2013. At 3.5x, the deal penciled to 2.8x MOIC and 26% IRR.

The credit market in 2007 was extraordinarily deep. Lenders were willing to provide leverage at very tight spreads and with minimal covenants. The sponsor's lender advisor (at the time, I was advising both sponsors and lenders, and I knew the credit landscape intimately) told the sponsor: "Lenders will give you 3.5x easily. They're hungry for paper." The sponsor's investment committee faced a decision. The more conservative members wanted to bid at 2.8x leverage, arguing that this level maintained adequate equity cushion and provided flexibility for downside scenarios. The more aggressive members argued that at 3.5x, they could deploy their capital more efficiently across their portfolio and achieve their fund return targets more readily.

The sponsor ultimately bid at 3.2x leverage. It was a compromise position that made sense from a capital efficiency standpoint—the IRR improvement from 2.8x to 3.2x was substantial (18% to 22%), while the incremental risk from the additional 40 basis points of leverage was manageable. The sponsor won the auction, funding the deal at 3.2x with a 7-year term loan and a senior secured note. The company performed well in 2008 and 2009—better than the sponsor had modeled, actually—and in 2011, the sponsor successfully exited via a strategic sale to a larger platform company.

But here is what matters: if the credit markets had performed differently, if the company had faced an unexpected industry headwind, if the sponsor's aggressive operating plan had not worked as well as expected, that 3.2x leverage level would have become problematic. Lenders would have been in a worse position. The company would have been more likely to struggle. But the sponsor made the optimal decision for themselves given their constraints and their information set at the time. This is not a story about sponsor recklessness or lender foolishness. It is a story about fundamental misalignment that is built into leveraged finance structures.

Understanding this misalignment is the first step toward being an effective credit investor or advisor to sponsors. It means that when you are analyzing a sponsor-led transaction, you are not analyzing a situation where your interests and the sponsor's interests are identical. You are analyzing a situation where your interests overlap to a significant degree, but diverge at the extremes. The sponsor is more likely to push leverage higher than you would prefer. The sponsor is more likely to view covenants as obstacles to flexibility rather than legitimate sources of information and protection. The sponsor is more likely to make aggressive operational plans that optimize for return rather than for conservatism.

But this is not a reason to avoid lending to sponsors or to treat sponsors as adversaries. Rather, it is a reason to structure deals in ways that acknowledge this misalignment and create proper incentives for both parties. It is a reason to understand, deeply and systematically, how PE firms operate, how they think, and what actually motivates them. And it is a reason to recognize that the most profitable long-term relationships between lenders and sponsors are built on this understanding, not on an illusion of perfect alignment.

PART TWO: HOW PE FIRMS ARE STRUCTURED

To understand PE behavior, you must first understand the financial and organizational structure of private equity firms. Most practitioners understand this in outline form—PE firms raise funds, they invest in companies, they eventually sell those companies and return capital. But the details of this framework are crucial for understanding how PE sponsors approach leverage, capital structure, and risk management.

A typical private equity fund is organized as a limited partnership. The general partners (GPs) are the investment professionals who manage the fund. The limited partners (LPs) are the institutions that provide the capital—pension funds, insurance companies, endowments, family offices, sovereign wealth funds, funds of funds. This distinction between GP and LP is far more than organizational structure. It represents a fundamental division in economic interest and control that shapes every aspect of PE firm behavior.

Let me describe a concrete example. Imagine a large PE fund that just closed a $5 billion fund in 2023. The fund has a management fee of 2% of committed capital, paid annually regardless of investment performance. It also has a carried interest provision that entitles the GPs to 20% of profits above an 8% hurdle rate. The hurdle rate is crucial—it means that LPs get a preferred return of 8% annually on their capital before the GPs participate in returns. This is a fairly standard structure for large PE funds, though with considerable variation across different managers and different fund sizes.

From the GP perspective, the 2% management fee generates approximately $100 million annually, regardless of investment performance. This covers the operating costs of the PE firm—office space, professional staff, systems infrastructure, deal advisory fees, and so on. For a PE firm with, say, 60 investment professionals, this works out to roughly $1.7 million per person annually to cover all direct costs and overhead. In practice, some of this fee is also returned to funds and LPs in the form of operating partner salaries, success bonuses, and other GP compensation tied directly to portfolio company performance.

The carried interest, by contrast, depends entirely on returns. If the fund achieves 8% IRR, the GPs realize no carried interest and break even (on an NPV basis) on their management fees. If the fund achieves 15% IRR, the GPs capture 20% of the excess returns above the 8% hurdle. For a $5 billion fund with a 15% IRR over five years, this carries significant value—likely $200-400 million in cumulative carried interest for the entire firm.

This structure creates an immediate and profound insight: PE GPs have an asymmetrical payoff structure that incentivizes risk-taking. If you are a PE partner at a large firm, and you are evaluating whether to pursue a deal that could generate either a 15% IRR or a 5% IRR with equal probability, the economics push you toward the deal. Even if the deal fails outright and the fund achieves 5% returns, your management fees have still covered the cost structure of your firm. But if the deal succeeds and you achieve 15% IRR, your carried interest is substantial. The downside (5% returns) hurts you less than the upside (15% returns) helps you.

This is not inherently a bad thing. PE firms need to be incentivized to deploy capital and take on risk, because that is the entire point of private equity—to apply capital and operational expertise to create value. But it does mean that PE sponsors are structurally inclined toward leverage and risk-taking relative to what lenders might prefer. This is not pathological behavior. This is rational response to economic incentives.

The fund lifecycle shapes this behavior in additional ways. Most PE funds have a ten-year investment period, though this varies. During this period, the fund is expected to make its initial investments. After the investment period closes, the fund moves into a harvest period where it exits its positions and returns capital to LPs. The timeline is crucial because it creates pressure to deploy capital quickly and efficiently.

Consider the fundraising cycle. A PE firm spends eighteen to twenty-four months raising a new fund. Once the fund closes, they have a limited window—typically two to four years—to identify, evaluate, and invest in target companies. Once the fund moves into harvest period, the focus shifts entirely to exiting existing positions and returning capital. This means that PE professionals are working within compressed timelines where capital needs to be deployed, and once deployed, needs to be turned around relatively quickly.

A partner at a PE firm who is considering a $100 million investment in a mid-market manufacturing company in 2024 is thinking about an exit timeline of 2028-2030. They want to invest, improve, and exit within a window of 4-6 years, because beyond that timeframe, the fund begins to look aged relative to newer vintage year funds, and LPs start to lose confidence in the manager's ability to deploy capital efficiently. This timeline pressure is a crucial element of PE behavior that lenders must understand.

Now let me describe how the J-curve effect shapes fund economics and therefore deal behavior. In the early years of a PE fund's investment period, the fund is showing losses on a mark-to-market basis. The GPs have paid management fees, they have made investments that have not yet appreciated, and from a pure NAV perspective, the fund looks like it is underwater. This is entirely normal and expected. But it creates a psychological and economic pressure for the fund to show strong performance quickly.

Imagine a $2 billion fund that closed in 2020. In 2021, the fund invests $600 million across three companies. In 2022, the fund writes down the value of one company by 15% based on underperformance, but the other two companies have grown revenue 20% and are valued at a 20% premium to entry. The net result is that the fund's NAV is still below the initial committed capital. LPs are unhappy. The fund is showing negative returns on a cumulative basis.

This is the J-curve. In the early years, the fund shows losses. But as the fund moves into harvest period, as it begins to exit successful investments and realize gains, the fund's returns curve back up, ideally into strong positive territory. The GPs understand this dynamic intellectually, but there is genuine psychological pressure to demonstrate early wins. This creates subtle pressure toward deals that can show early success—deals that involve operational improvements that are quick to achieve, or deals that involve acquisitions and bolt-ons that demonstrate growth quickly.

I saw this most vividly in the late 2000s, during the depths of the financial crisis. I was advising a large mega-fund that had raised money at the peak of the market in 2007. The fund had deployed about 60% of its capital into large acquisitions before the credit markets seized up. The remaining 40% sat on the sideline, and the fund's existing portfolio companies were underperforming significantly due to the recession. From a NAV perspective, the fund was substantially underwater.

The fund's leadership had a choice: they could wait out the downturn, which might take several years, knowing that eventually the economy would recover and their portfolio would rebound. Or they could deploy capital opportunistically during the downturn, acquiring distressed assets cheaply and positioning for upside when the market recovered. They chose the latter. Between 2009 and 2011, despite their portfolio companies performing poorly, they deployed most of their remaining capital into opportunities available at distressed valuations. Some of these acquisitions worked out very well when the economy recovered. Others did not.

The point is that the J-curve created genuine economic pressure to deploy capital, even in a terrible environment. And this pressure manifested in more aggressive deals, higher leverage, and riskier bets than might have been optimal from a traditional institutional investor standpoint. The GPs were acting rationally within the constraints of their fund economics.

Beyond the fund level, individual deal teams within PE firms have their own incentive structures. In a typical large PE firm, a partner who sources and leads a deal might receive transaction-based compensation or success-based bonuses tied to the outcome of that specific investment. An operating partner who is deployed to a portfolio company to drive operational improvements might have equity co-invest in that specific company, so that their personal return is tied directly to the company's performance. These individual incentives create tremendous focus and drive on successful investment outcomes.

But they also create scope for misalignment within the PE firm. A deal partner who sources an acquisition might be incentivized to get the deal done—to earn the transaction bonus and move on to the next deal. An operating partner who then has to actually operate the company might have a longer-term alignment with success. These two perspectives sometimes align, but sometimes they create tension.

I encountered this tension explicitly in the acquisition of a specialty distribution company in 2005. The deal was sourced through a proprietary introduction by a board member of the target company, and the sponsoring PE firm was competing against several other bidders. The deal partner at the sponsoring PE firm was extremely focused on moving the process quickly and winning the auction. They were willing to accept leverage at 3.5x Net Leverage on entry, with an aggressive operational plan that assumed significant SG&A reduction.

The operating partner, by contrast, was more conservative. They believed that the company had strong operational potential but that the SG&A reduction target was too aggressive given the market conditions and talent retention challenges. They preferred to enter at 2.8x leverage with a more measured operational plan. But the deal partner had the power to structure the bid and move the process forward. The investment committee approved the 3.5x structure.

What happened next illustrates the misalignment. The acquisition closed on schedule, and the company was immediately put under significant pressure to achieve the SG&A reduction targets. Some talented people left the company. The operational strategy shifted to focus on short-term margin improvement rather than long-term revenue growth. Two years into the hold, the company had achieved the margin targets, but revenue growth had stalled. When the company tried to exit in 2008, the credit markets had frozen, and sponsors could not refinance or sell. The company ended up being sold at significant discount to the initial enterprise value.

The deal partner had already captured their transaction bonus and moved on to other deals. The operating partner, who had taken an equity co-invest and was therefore exposed to the downside, realized losses. The lenders, who had provided the 3.5x leverage, also realized losses when the company eventually restructured. The misalignment between the deal partner's short-term incentives and the operating partner's long-term incentives, combined with excessive leverage, created a situation where everyone ultimately lost.

This is an important lesson: within PE firms, just as between PE firms and lenders, incentive structures shape behavior. Deal partners are incentivized to source deals and get them done. Operating partners are incentivized to improve performance and maximize exit value. These incentives usually align, but not always. As a credit investor or advisor, it is valuable to understand where within the PE firm the true alignment is, and where there might be misalignment between the people sourcing the deal and the people operating the portfolio company.

The role of the operating partner is worth exploring further, because it is a distinctive feature of modern PE firms that has evolved significantly over my career. Twenty years ago, PE firms were primarily financial engineering operations. The partners who led investments were investment professionals with backgrounds in finance, consulting, or investment banking. They focused on capital structure, refinancing opportunities, add-on acquisitions, and eventually exit. The notion of the "operating partner"—a professional with deep industry expertise who would be deployed into portfolio companies to drive operational improvements—barely existed.

Over time, and particularly after the financial crisis, PE firms realized that in order to justify the leverage they were taking, and to drive returns in a competitive environment, they needed to actually improve the underlying operations of their portfolio companies. This drove the hiring of operating partners—seasoned industry executives who had run companies, managed teams, and understood operational strategy. These executives took ownership roles in portfolio companies, either formally as board members or informally as advisors, and drove specific operational improvement initiatives.

The most sophisticated PE firms today view operating partners as a core value-add, not a secondary consideration. A mega-fund might have twenty or thirty operating partners specializing in different industries, working alongside the investment professionals to both evaluate acquisitions and then execute improvements. This has created a genuinely different model of private equity in the last fifteen years compared to what existed in the early 2000s.

From a credit perspective, the presence of quality operating partners is a positive signal about deal quality. It suggests that the sponsor is genuinely focused on operational improvement rather than just financial engineering. It suggests that the sponsor has allocated resources to actually run the business, not just to manage the leverage. When I am evaluating a PE-backed company for credit risk, I pay significant attention to the operating partner track record and whether the operating partner has successfully improved companies in that specific industry before.

Conversely, a sponsor who sources a deal but lacks clear operational expertise or operating partner capacity is a warning sign. It suggests that the deal is being done purely as financial engineering—based on the assumption that the company can be leveraged up, some costs can be cut, and the company can be sold at an exit multiple higher than the entry multiple. This model can work, but it is inherently riskier than operational improvement-driven models.

Let me also describe the structure of the actual investment decision process within a PE firm, because this shapes how leverage decisions get made. In a typical large PE firm, a partner identifies a potential acquisition target. They perform some preliminary screening to make sure the company fits the fund's investment criteria—the right size, the right industry, the right financial profile. They then assemble a deal team consisting of other investment professionals and, increasingly, the relevant operating partner for that industry.

The deal team performs detailed due diligence over a period of weeks or months, typically while competing against other bidders in a formal auction process. They develop a detailed financial model of the target company, making assumptions about revenue growth, margin expansion, capital expenditure, and working capital needs. They evaluate the competitive position of the company, the quality of the management team, and the growth opportunities. They also evaluate the leverage capacity—how much debt the company can sustain given its cash flow generation and the likelihood of covenant compliance.

Based on the due diligence, the deal team develops a financing proposal. This proposal specifies the enterprise value they are willing to pay, the amount of equity they will invest, the amount of leverage they will request from lenders, and the capital structure of the debt (term loans, high-yield bonds, preferred equity, mezz debt, etc.). The proposal also includes operational improvement assumptions—how much the deal team believes they can improve EBITDA through operational initiatives, overhead reduction, revenue growth, et cetera.

The financing proposal then goes to the investment committee. The IC typically consists of senior partners from the PE firm, sometimes including the firm's CEO or Chief Investment Officer. The IC reviews the deal thesis, the financial assumptions, the leverage levels, and the operating plan. They focus extensively on downside scenarios—what happens if revenue growth is slower than expected, if margins don't expand as planned, or if leverage increases rather than decreases. The IC either approves the deal to move forward, rejects the deal, or asks for modifications to the structure.

What I have observed over twenty years is that IC discipline varies significantly across sponsors. Some sponsors have extremely rigorous ICs that challenge leverage levels rigorously and push back on aggressive operating assumptions. Other sponsors have ICs that are more focused on rubber-stamping deals that the deal team has already committed to in the marketplace. The quality of the IC function is a meaningful signal of sponsor quality and risk management discipline.

Once the IC approves the deal, the financing proposal is sent to lenders. The lenders then evaluate whether they are willing to provide the requested debt at the requested terms. This is where the misalignment I described earlier becomes most apparent. The sponsor has decided on the leverage level—2.5x, 3.0x, 3.5x, whatever they believe optimizes their IRR. The lenders then evaluate whether that leverage is prudent given the credit quality of the company.

Often, the sponsor and lenders will agree on leverage. Sometimes, they will not. If lenders believe the leverage is too high, they might reduce the amount of debt they are willing to provide, request higher pricing to compensate for the risk, or request more restrictive covenants. The sponsor then must decide whether to accept these terms, to increase the equity check to compensate for the reduced debt, or to walk away from the deal. If the deal is particularly competitive, the sponsor might walk away because another bidder is willing to take more leverage risk. If the deal is proprietary, the sponsor and lenders will negotiate to find a mutually acceptable structure.

This dynamic—the constant negotiation between sponsor leverage preferences and lender risk tolerance—is the fundamental driver of deals throughout the leveraged finance market. Understanding how sponsors think about leverage is therefore crucial.

PART THREE: THE PE INVESTMENT PROCESS AND LEVERAGE DECISIONS

To really understand how PE sponsors approach leverage, you need to understand the entire investment process—from deal sourcing through due diligence through financing through close. And you need to understand how leverage decisions emerge from this process, not as isolated financial engineering considerations, but as integral parts of the overall investment thesis.

Deal sourcing sets the tone for everything that follows. PE sponsors acquire target companies through two primary mechanisms: proprietary introductions and competitive auctions. In a proprietary introduction, the sponsor has a direct relationship with the seller, or with an intermediary like an investment banker, and the seller chooses to approach the sponsor directly rather than running a formal auction. In a competitive auction, the seller engages an investment bank to run a process that typically involves ten to fifty potential buyers, each submitting bids and being evaluated on both price and quality.

Proprietary deals are the Holy Grail for PE sponsors. They typically involve less competition, which means lower valuations and more control over transaction timing. They also often involve better initial information, because the seller is sharing confidential information directly with the sponsor rather than through the constraints of a formal NDA process. When a sponsor can acquire a company on a proprietary basis, they have significantly more flexibility in how they structure the capital—they can take more leverage, they can accept less favorable terms, they can move quickly because they do not have to compete against other bidders.

Competitive auctions are different. When a company is being sold through a formal process with multiple bidders, the sponsor faces pressure to be aggressive on price, leverage, and timing. The concern is always that another bidder will be more aggressive, will propose higher leverage, will accept shorter diligence timelines. I have seen sponsors significantly overextend themselves on leverage simply because they believed they needed to be aggressive to win a particular auction.

One of the most vivid examples from my career is a business services company that was sold through an auction in 2006. The company had $200 million in revenue and $35 million in EBITDA. Three sponsors made bids. The highest bidder offered $700 million enterprise value, which was 20x EBITDA—a very aggressive multiple for the time. That sponsor proposed 3.8x leverage, or about $265 million of debt, with an aggressive operational plan focused on add-on acquisitions and margin expansion.

A more conservative bidder offered $650 million enterprise value, 18.6x EBITDA, with 3.0x leverage. The seller chose the aggressive bidder, because the higher enterprise value was attractive. The financing closed at 3.8x leverage. The company then faced significant pressure to execute the operational plan—acquisitions, margin improvements, all within a leveraged structure that did not allow for much flexibility if something went wrong.

What actually happened was that the 2008 financial crisis intervened. Add-on acquisitions became impossible as the credit markets froze. The company did not achieve its margin expansion targets because the economic environment deteriorated. By 2009, the company was in significant stress, with leverage closer to 4.5x, and lenders were considering covenant defaults and restructuring.

Would the more conservative bidder have been better off long-term? We can never know, because that sponsor did not win the deal. But the point is that leverage decisions in competitive situations are often shaped more by competitive dynamics than by careful analysis of credit quality. This is not unique to 2006 and 2007—I see it in every robust market. Sponsors push leverage higher in competitive situations because they perceive that other bidders will do so, and the deal simply will not close at a lower leverage level.

Once a deal is won, the financing process begins in earnest. By this point, the sponsor has already made a commitment on enterprise value and leverage—these are embedded in the purchase agreement. The lender's job is now to evaluate whether they are willing to finance at the proposed leverage given everything they learn during due diligence.

Due diligence for a PE-backed transaction is extensive. The lenders assign a credit analyst to evaluate the company's financial performance over the prior three to five years, to stress-test the sponsor's financial projections, and to evaluate the company's competitive position and operational trajectory. The lenders also engage third-party advisors—operational consultants, industry specialists, environmental and real estate specialists, customs and trade specialists for international companies—to evaluate specific aspects of the business.

What lenders are really evaluating during due diligence is the downside case. The sponsor's base case is usually optimistic—it assumes that the operational improvements the sponsor expects to achieve will actually be achieved, that the company will grow revenue or maintain it despite market pressures, that margins will stay stable or improve. But what happens if the base case does not work out? What if the operational improvements are slower to achieve? What if the company faces an unexpected industry headwind? What if the management team leaves? Lenders are stress-testing these scenarios.

A well-run credit diligence process will model the company's performance under various downside scenarios. What is the company's leverage if EBITDA is 10% below sponsor projections? What if EBITDA is 20% below? What if capital expenditure requirements increase? What if working capital balloons due to inventory buildup or receivables issues? The lenders are trying to determine: at what point does this company breach its covenants? At what point does it become unable to service its debt without additional equity injections or refinancing?

This is where the misalignment with sponsors becomes most apparent. Sponsors are modeling base case and upside scenarios, with downside as a secondary concern. Lenders are modeling downside scenarios with intensity. If the sponsor is comfortable with leverage at 3.2x assuming base case performance, the lenders might be saying: "Yes, we can accept 3.2x if EBITDA comes in as projected. But what if EBITDA is 20% below projections? Then leverage is 4.0x and you are breaching your maintenance covenant. We need to understand how you are going to manage that."

The sponsor's response is typically: "We believe in our plan. We have deep experience improving companies in this sector. The operational improvements we have identified are realistic. We are not concerned about 20% EBITDA shortfall because it is not going to happen." The lender responds: "We understand. But we have seen sponsor plans not work out before. We need covenants that reflect the downside scenario, not the base case."

This negotiation over covenants and downside scenarios is one of the most important elements of leveraged finance. Covenants are the lender's primary tool for protecting against downside scenarios. A maintenance leverage covenant says: "You must maintain leverage below 3.5x at all times." This covenant is meaningful if there is a reasonable probability that leverage could exceed 3.5x if something goes wrong. If the sponsor is completely confident that leverage will remain below 2.5x even in downside scenarios, then a 3.5x covenant is not meaningful—it is just documentation. But if there is a material probability that leverage could reach 3.8x in a downside scenario, then the 3.5x covenant is a real constraint that shapes how the sponsor operates the company.

I will spend much more time on covenants in a later section on capital structure. But the point here is that covenant negotiations are where the fundamental misalignment between sponsor expectations and lender conservatism becomes most apparent. Sponsors want loose covenants that give them flexibility to operate the company. Lenders want tight covenants that force the company to adjust course if things go wrong. The leverage that ultimately gets agreed upon between sponsors and lenders embodies this misalignment.

Let me give a specific example of how leverage affects a company's operational strategy. In 2010, I was advising a mid-market PE sponsor on the acquisition of a manufacturing company for $300 million enterprise value with $45 million EBITDA. The sponsor modeled the acquisition at 2.8x Net Leverage with a 6-year term loan and a junior secured note. The sponsor's operational plan included modest revenue growth (3% annually) and some margin expansion through overhead reduction and procurement optimization.

The deal was structured with a maintenance leverage covenant of 4.0x, which was quite loose given the entry leverage of 2.8x. Why was the covenant so loose? Because the lenders were comfortable with the leverage level and wanted to give the sponsor significant flexibility. The lenders were saying: "We are okay with you taking leverage to 4.0x if something goes wrong, because we believe the business can support that if the sponsor is actively engaged in managing it."

But something interesting happened over the subsequent years. The company did perform well—not spectacularly, but solidly. Revenue grew and EBITDA margins expanded slightly. By year three, the company had leverage of 2.4x and was generating excess cash. At this point, the sponsor considered a dividend recapitalization—taking out cash and distributing it to the equity sponsors while increasing leverage. The question was: how much leverage could the sponsor take out without breaching the 4.0x covenant in a downside scenario?

The sponsor modeled the dividend recap in a scenario where EBITDA was 15% below the base case. In that scenario, leverage would reach 3.8x, still below the covenant. So the sponsor executed a dividend recap that increased leverage to 3.2x and distributed about $40 million to sponsors. The lenders agreed to this because it was still below the maintenance covenant.

Here is the important point: the loose covenant enabled the dividend recap, which was attractive to the sponsors but increased the risk to lenders. The lenders had implicitly agreed to allow the sponsors to take out equity distributions at the expense of leverage. The lenders were comfortable with this trade-off because they believed the business could support the higher leverage. But if the business had faced an unexpected headwind, the dividend recap would have been a mistake—the sponsors would have distributed capital that they later would have needed to inject.

This dynamic plays out thousands of times across the leveraged finance market. Sponsors use leverage capacity—the amount of leverage the covenants allow—as a tool to extract value from portfolio companies. Lenders agree to loose covenants as a way to remain competitive and to earn the spread. The result is that leverage decisions are not made once, at entry, but continually throughout the hold period, as sponsors extract value and lenders accommodate leverage increases.

PART FOUR: VALUE CREATION AND THE LEVERAGE DECISION

Now I want to connect leverage decisions to the actual value creation strategies that PE sponsors pursue, because this is where the practical reality of PE leverage becomes most apparent. It is easy to view leverage decisions as abstract financial engineering—moving around the capital stack to maximize IRR. But in reality, leverage decisions are inextricably linked to the specific strategies sponsors pursue to improve their portfolio companies.

The classic framework for PE value creation involves five primary levers: "buy right," operational improvement, revenue growth, add-on acquisitions, and financial engineering. Most sponsors will pursue some combination of these levers depending on the specific opportunity and their core competencies. Understanding how each lever relates to leverage decisions is crucial for credit analysis.

The "buy right" strategy is perhaps the most straightforward. The sponsor pays careful attention to valuation at entry. They aim to acquire companies at enterprise value multiples that are lower than the exit multiples they expect to achieve. If they can acquire a company at 9x EBITDA and exit at 11x EBITDA, then even if EBITDA is flat, they have a 22% value creation opportunity just from multiple expansion. This strategy has nothing to do with leverage—it is pure arbitrage on valuation multiples.

The importance of "buying right" is that it reduces the pressure on operational improvement and financial engineering to drive returns. A sponsor who acquires a company at a very attractive valuation can afford to be patient on operational improvement and can accept lower leverage because the entry valuation gives them a cushion. Conversely, a sponsor who overpays at entry—because they are in a competitive auction or they are very confident in their operational improvement thesis—will need significant operational improvement or leverage to achieve their return targets.

In the 2015-2019 period, the market was extremely competitive and multiples were high. Sponsors were "buying wrong" in the sense that the entry multiples left little room for valuation arbitrage. As a result, sponsors placed enormous pressure on operational improvement. They needed to grow EBITDA significantly, or to achieve margin expansion, simply to achieve reasonable IRRs. This created strong operational improvement initiatives but also required higher leverage to achieve the equity return targets. It was a period where sponsors were forced to layer leverage on top of aggressive operational plans.

Operational improvement is the second lever and involves actually improving the underlying business. This might include overhead reduction—consolidating corporate functions, eliminating redundant positions, centralizing procurement. It might include revenue growth initiatives—upgrading the sales function, entering new markets, cross-selling between acquired companies. It might include pricing power—implementing price increases that the business did not previously pursue. It might include customer rationalization—exiting low-margin customers or consolidating customer relationships.

From a credit perspective, operational improvement is the most valuable form of value creation because it strengthens the underlying business. A PE sponsor who improves a company's margin from 20% to 25% through operational initiatives has genuinely made the business stronger and more valuable. This increases the cash flow available to service debt and reduces leverage. It also makes the company more resilient to downside scenarios.

But here is the tension: operational improvement is difficult, uncertain, and time-consuming. It requires excellent management, strong operational execution, and often investment in systems, training, and technology. Not all sponsors are equally capable of delivering operational improvement. Some have deep industry expertise and a track record of improving companies in specific sectors. Others are more oriented toward financial engineering and have limited operational capabilities.

When a sponsor lacks operational improvement capabilities, they tend to rely more heavily on leverage and financial engineering. This is not necessarily a bad decision, but it changes the risk profile of the investment. If leverage is funding the operational improvement that will drive returns, then leverage is justified by expected improvement in EBITDA. But if leverage is just increasing the equity multiple without corresponding EBITDA improvement, then leverage introduces risk without a corresponding source of return.

Add-on acquisitions, or "bolt-ons," are the third lever. The PE sponsor acquires a platform company, then uses the platform as a base for acquiring smaller companies in the same industry. Each bolt-on acquisition adds revenue and EBITDA to the platform, and often allows the platform to realize cost synergies (combining duplicate corporate functions, leveraging procurement scale). A successful bolt-on strategy can drive significant EBITDA growth without significant organic growth.

The leverage implications of bolt-on strategies are complex. On one hand, bolt-on acquisitions increase EBITDA and therefore allow the company to support leverage. But on the other hand, each bolt-on acquisition requires capital, and PE sponsors typically use leverage to finance these acquisitions. This can lead to a dynamic where leverage increases for each bolt-on, and the deleveraging from EBITDA growth is absorbed by leverage for the next bolt-on acquisition. Over multiple bolt-ons, this can create a situation where leverage stays constant even as EBITDA grows significantly.

I saw this most vividly in the acquisition of a staffing services platform that went through a very active bolt-on strategy in the 2016-2018 period. The platform was acquired for $150 million enterprise value at 8x EBITDA with 2.5x leverage. Over the subsequent three years, the platform acquired six bolt-on companies, cumulatively adding $12 million of annual EBITDA. This brought the platform's EBITDA to about $30 million, which would have supported deleveraging to 2.0x if the company had not used leverage to finance the bolt-ons.

But here is what happened: each bolt-on was financed with a combination of cash flow from operations and incremental leverage. By year three, leverage was still approximately 2.5x despite the doubling of EBITDA. The company was on a treadmill where growth was being funded by leverage, and leverage was staying roughly constant.

From a lender perspective, this is a double-edged sword. On one hand, the EBITDA growth meant the company was getting stronger and was more capable of supporting leverage. On the other hand, the company never deleveraged, and the management team was focused on continuous acquisitions rather than organic operational improvement.

From a PE sponsor perspective, the bolt-on strategy was optimal if they believed they could execute it successfully and achieve their return targets. Each acquisition was accretive to returns on a leveraged basis, which meant the leverage was working for the sponsors. But it also meant that the capital structure did not improve, and if the acquisition strategy slowed or failed, the company would be stuck at a high leverage level with no improvement in the underlying EBITDA.

Revenue growth is a fourth lever and is related to but distinct from operational improvement. While operational improvement focuses on improving margins and efficiency, revenue growth focuses on the top line. This might involve organic initiatives—adding sales capacity, developing new products, entering new geographies. It might also involve pricing power—raising prices on existing products and services.

From a credit perspective, revenue growth is valuable because it increases EBITDA and therefore supports deleveraging. But revenue growth is also uncertain and difficult to predict. A sponsor who is forecasting 8% annual revenue growth as part of their investment thesis is making an optimistic assumption. If growth comes in at 4%, the leverage situation becomes significantly more challenging.

I worked on a technology services company acquisition in 2017 where the sponsor's financial projections included strong revenue growth as a key driver of returns. The sponsor believed the company had significant opportunity to cross-sell services to existing customers and to add new customers in adjacent geographies. The base case model included 7% annual revenue growth over five years. The company was acquired at 4.0x leverage, which was highly aggressive and only justified if the revenue growth materialized.

Over the first two years, the company achieved about 5% annual revenue growth—better than historical performance but below the sponsor's projections. At this point, leverage had increased to 4.3x because EBITDA growth was slower than expected. The sponsor and lenders had to make a decision: should they slow the leverage increase by cutting costs, or should they double down on the revenue growth strategy and accept the near-term leverage increase?

The sponsor chose to double down, investing significantly in sales and marketing to accelerate revenue growth. This temporarily increased the leverage because of near-term investments that had not yet generated returns. By year four, the revenue growth strategy was paying dividends and the company's revenue was growing at 8% annually, justifying the earlier leverage increase. By the time the company was sold in year six, leverage had deleveraged to 2.8x and the sponsors achieved their return targets.

But here is the important point: this success was not guaranteed. If the revenue growth strategy had continued to underperform, if the company had faced a recession or market headwind, the increased leverage in year two would have created significant distress risk. The sponsor had essentially made a bet that revenue growth would accelerate, and had taken leverage to fund this bet. The lenders had to decide whether to accommodate the leverage increase or to refuse and force the sponsor to either invest equity or reduce leverage.

Financial engineering is the final lever and involves capital structure decisions to maximize returns. This includes the initial leverage decision at entry, refinancing to take out equity and return capital to sponsors, dividend recaps, and potentially credit derivatives or other structures. Financial engineering does not create any new cash flow—it just redistributes the existing cash flow between debt and equity.

But financial engineering can significantly amplify equity returns. If a company generates $50 million of free cash flow per year, and the company has $200 million of debt with a 5% coupon, then $10 million goes to debt service and $40 million goes to equity. By increasing leverage to $300 million, debt service increases to $15 million and equity free cash flow stays at $35 million. But on a basis of the incremental $100 million of debt and the same amount of equity, the additional leverage has increased the equity yield.

PE sponsors are very sophisticated about financial engineering because it is a direct driver of equity returns. A sponsor who can take a company from 2.5x leverage to 3.0x leverage, or who can achieve two refinancings during the hold period and return capital to sponsors each time, can significantly enhance returns without any underlying business improvement.

But again, this creates misalignment with lenders. Lenders are concerned about leverage increasing without corresponding EBITDA improvement. Sponsors are focused on maximizing equity returns through leverage and capital distributions. Every dividend recapitalization, every refinancing, and every leverage increase without corresponding EBITDA improvement is a loss for lenders—because the lenders are more junior to the equity, and higher leverage makes it more likely that lenders will be impaired if something goes wrong.

PART FIVE: CAPITAL STRUCTURE DECISIONS AND COVENANT NEGOTIATIONS

Now I want to spend significant time on how sponsors think about debt quantum—the amount of total debt they want to take in a leverage transaction. This is perhaps the most crucial decision in leveraged finance, and understanding how sponsors approach this decision is fundamental to understanding sponsor behavior.

The starting point is simple: sponsors want to maximize leverage, subject to constraints. The maximum amount of leverage is constrained by four factors. First, the lender's willingness to provide debt—lenders will not provide more than a certain amount of leverage given the credit quality of the company. Second, the covenants—lenders might be willing to provide leverage at a certain level, but require tight maintenance covenants that force the sponsor to adjust operating strategy if leverage increases. Third, the company's strategic flexibility—the sponsor might be comfortable with higher leverage, but only if it does not constrain them from pursuing strategic initiatives like bolt-on acquisitions. Fourth, the exit flexibility—the sponsor wants to maintain some leverage capacity in case they need to do a refinancing or dividend recap before the exit.

What I have observed over twenty years is that the most sophisticated sponsors are very deliberate about optimizing within these constraints. They are not blindly pushing leverage as high as possible. Rather, they are calculating the leverage level that maximizes their IRR given their operational improvement thesis and their exit assumptions.

A typical PE sponsor is modeling the company's performance under their base case operational plan. They are projecting revenue, EBITDA, capital expenditure, working capital requirements, and tax. They are calculating free cash flow available for debt service and equity distributions. They are then solving for the maximum leverage level that allows the company to maintain adequate covenant cushion throughout the hold period, while still leaving sufficient flexibility to execute their strategic plan.

Importantly, the sponsor's optimal leverage level is usually not at the maximum that lenders would provide. If lenders are willing to provide 3.5x leverage, the sponsor's optimal leverage might be 3.2x, because at 3.2x there is sufficient flexibility to execute the operational plan and to pursue refinancings or dividend recaps. At 3.5x, leverage is near the constraint, and any unexpected underperformance forces adjustments or equity cures.

But this optimal leverage level is highly dependent on the sponsor's confidence in their operating plan. A sponsor who is very confident in their operational improvement thesis can take higher leverage because they are confident the operating improvements will reduce leverage toward their targets. A sponsor who is more uncertain about the operating plan will be more conservative on leverage.

In the 2004-2006 period, when credit markets were strong and leverage was cheap, many sponsors pushed leverage quite aggressively because they were confident the market would support it. In the 2015-2019 period, when multiples were high and leverage needed to be higher to achieve return targets, sponsors again pushed leverage aggressively. But in cautious periods—2011-2012 after the financial crisis, 2020-2021 during the COVID period—sponsors were much more conservative on leverage.

Now let me describe how sponsors approach the fixed vs. floating rate decision, because this is a crucial capital structure decision that shapes leverage dynamics. Almost all leveraged finance transactions involve floating-rate debt because of the lower spreads available in the floating-rate market. A sponsor can typically get a term loan at SOFR + 300-400 basis points, while a bond would price at SOFR + 400-500 basis points for similar credit. So floating rates are economically more attractive.

But floating rates create interest rate risk. If SOFR increases from 5% to 6%, then the company's annual interest expense increases by approximately 100 basis points times the amount of floating-rate debt. For a company with $300 million of floating-rate debt, this is a $3 million annual increase in interest expense, which translates directly to a $3 million reduction in free cash flow.

How do sponsors manage this interest rate risk? Some sponsors hedge a portion of the floating-rate exposure, using interest rate swaps or caps to fix the interest rate on a portion of the debt. This provides certainty on interest expense but comes at a cost—the sponsor pays a spread for the hedge. Some sponsors simply accept the interest rate risk, betting that rates will not move significantly during their hold period. And some sponsors structure the debt with a combination of floating-rate term loans and fixed-rate bonds to naturally hedge some of the interest rate exposure.

I have observed that sponsor views on interest rate risk have changed significantly over my career. In the low-rate environment of the 2010s, sponsors did not worry much about rate hedging because rates seemed unlikely to increase significantly. In 2021-2022, when the Fed started raising rates rapidly, sponsors who had not hedged suddenly faced significant increases in interest expense and deteriorating credit metrics.

In one case I worked on, a PE-backed software services company had $200 million of floating-rate debt in late 2021. The company was projecting leverage of 2.8x for 2022. But the sponsor had not hedged interest rates. When the Fed raised rates from 0-0.25% to 3-3.5% in 2022, the company's annual interest expense increased by over $5 million. EBITDA projections were also declining due to the slowdown in the technology market. The net result was that leverage spiked to 3.2x, well above where the company and sponsors had expected to be.

The company ultimately did not experience a covenant breach, but the sponsor and lenders both wished they had hedged more rate exposure earlier. This is a lesson that many sponsors have now internalized—in an uncertain rate environment, hedging is valuable even if it increases the all-in cost of financing.

Now let me move to one of the most complex and important aspects of capital structure decisions from the sponsor perspective: the secured vs. unsecured trade-off, and specifically, the decision to layer holding company debt on top of operating company debt.

In a typical leveraged acquisition, the PE sponsor purchases the target company through an acquisition vehicle. This vehicle is typically a holding company that is formed to own the target. The holding company then issues debt—let's call this "HoldCo debt." The proceeds of the HoldCo debt, combined with equity from the sponsors, are used to purchase the target operating company. The operating company itself also borrows money—let's call this "OpCo debt." The proceeds of the OpCo debt are used to pay a portion of the purchase price.

The difference between HoldCo and OpCo debt is structural subordination. OpCo debt is secured by the assets of the operating company and is senior to HoldCo debt. If the company is in distress, the OpCo creditors have a claim on the operating company's cash flows and assets. HoldCo creditors are structurally subordinated—they only get paid after all operating company creditors are paid in full.

Why would a sponsor use HoldCo debt? The answer is leverage. By creating a HoldCo that issues debt, the sponsor can layer additional leverage on top of the OpCo structure. A typical structure might be $500 million of OpCo debt plus $100 million of HoldCo debt, for total leverage of $600 million on a target that could only support $500 million of operating company debt.

The HoldCo debt is more expensive than OpCo debt because it is structurally subordinated. HoldCo bonds might price at 200 basis points wider than OpCo bonds. But it is still cheaper than taking the capital as equity, and it provides leverage to the sponsors that they would not otherwise have access to.

From a lender perspective, HoldCo debt is clearly riskier than OpCo debt. In any distress scenario, HoldCo creditors are going to lose money before OpCo creditors do. But from a sponsor perspective, HoldCo debt is a tool to increase leverage without increasing the leverage on the operating company itself.

In my career, I have seen sponsor behavior on HoldCo debt evolve significantly. In the 2005-2007 period, sponsors were much more willing to layer HoldCo debt because credit spreads were tight and HoldCo debt was cheap. In the post-financial crisis period, sponsors became more cautious about HoldCo debt because the risks became more apparent. In the 2015-2019 period, when sponsors needed leverage to achieve returns, HoldCo debt became more popular again.

The key lesson for credit investors is that HoldCo debt is a signal about sponsor leverage optimization. When a sponsor is willing to take HoldCo debt, it typically means the sponsor believes the operating company can support the OpCo leverage level, but the sponsor wants additional leverage for equity return optimization. This is rational sponsor behavior, but it introduces structural risks that need to be carefully analyzed.

Now let me address covenant negotiations, which is where the fundamental tensions between sponsors and lenders become most apparent. Covenants are contractual provisions that restrict how a company operates. Common financial covenants include maintenance leverage ratios (the company must maintain leverage below a certain level at all times), minimum interest coverage ratios (the company must maintain interest coverage above a certain level), and minimum liquidity requirements (the company must maintain a minimum cash balance).

From a lender perspective, covenants are protection. They create consequences if the company underperforms. A maintenance leverage covenant of 4.0x means that if leverage exceeds 4.0x, the company is in technical default and lenders have the right to take remedial actions—demand accelerated repayment, take control of the company, or negotiate a waiver of the covenant. This creates an incentive for the sponsor to manage the company to stay below the covenant threshold.

From a sponsor perspective, covenants are constraints. Tight covenants limit the sponsor's flexibility to make operating decisions. A tight leverage covenant means the sponsor cannot take leverage to finance bolt-on acquisitions or dividend recaps. A tight capex covenant means the sponsor cannot make strategic investments in the business. A tight dividend or distribution covenant means the sponsor cannot return capital to equity.

The covenant negotiation is therefore a negotiation about flexibility. The sponsor wants loose covenants that give maximum flexibility. The lender wants tight covenants that force the sponsor to adjust if something goes wrong. The actual covenants that get agreed upon reflect the balance of power at the time.

I have observed that in strong credit markets, sponsors have more power and covenants tend to be looser. In weak credit markets, lenders have more power and covenants tend to be tighter. In the 2006-2007 period, sponsors had significant power and covenants were extremely loose. In 2009-2010, lenders had significant power and covenants were much tighter.

What I find fascinating is how PE sponsors negotiate specific covenant provisions. Many sponsors understand credit mechanics very well, and they will fight for specific covenant levels or carve-outs that are important to them. For example, sponsors often fight for "financial maintenance" covenants that are tested only quarterly rather than at each quarter-end, which gives them additional flexibility to manage to the covenants within a quarter. Sponsors will fight to define EBITDA in ways that exclude certain items—acquisition costs, one-time items, restructuring charges—because these definitions affect how much cushion they have to maintain covenants.

One of the most interesting covenant negotiations I participated in involved the definition of "EBITDA" for purposes of a leverage covenant. The lender wanted a narrow definition that included only recurring items. The sponsor wanted a broad definition that excluded one-time items. They ultimately agreed on a definition that allowed exclusion of acquisition-related costs, integration costs in the first 12 months after an acquisition, and certain other non-recurring items.

The sponsor then used this definition very strategically. When the company made its first bolt-on acquisition, the sponsor categorized most of the integration costs as "acquisition-related" and excluded them from EBITDA. This increased measured EBITDA and reduced measured leverage, giving the company significant covenant cushion. The lenders subsequently tightened the definition of what could be excluded, but the sponsor had already extracted value from the initial loose definition.

This is an important lesson: covenants are tools that sophisticated sponsors use strategically. The definition of EBITDA, the timing of testing, the carve-outs and add-backs—all of these affect how restrictive the covenants actually are. And sophisticated sponsors understand this deeply and negotiate accordingly.

Sponsors also fight for specific carve-outs and waivers on various covenants. A common example is a carve-out for "Permitted Distributions" that allows the sponsor to distribute cash to equity above a certain threshold without triggering a covenant violation. In exchange for this flexibility, the lender might require higher pricing or tighter covenants elsewhere.

Let me describe a specific example of covenant negotiation around financial distributions. A PE sponsor acquires a company with strong cash generation. The sponsor intends to distribute excess cash to equity through dividends or management equity plans. The lender wants to restrict cash distributions to ensure that cash is used for debt repayment. The sponsor and lender negotiate a compromise: the sponsor can make distributions equal to X% of free cash flow, or distributions equal to Y% of cash earnings, subject to the company maintaining leverage below a certain threshold.

This type of covenant gives the sponsor flexibility but creates an incentive to maintain leverage below the covenant threshold. If leverage is below 3.5x, the sponsor can distribute cash. If leverage approaches 3.5x, the sponsor must stop distributing and use free cash flow for debt repayment instead. This aligns sponsor and lender incentives more effectively than a blanket prohibition on distributions.

I want to also discuss the "doc review" process at PE firms, because this is where sponsor leverage thinking crystallizes into actual contractual terms. Most large PE firms have a dedicated person or team responsible for credit documentation. This person, typically called the "Finance Director" or "SVP Finance," reviews all credit agreements to ensure they align with the sponsor's operational and financial strategy.

The Finance Director evaluates several key aspects of the credit agreement. First, the flexibility it provides. Can the company refinance without lender consent? Can the company make acquisitions? Can the company incur additional debt or liens? Second, the covenant structure. Are the covenants achievable under the sponsor's base case and reasonable downside scenarios? What is the covenant cushion—how much room does the company have before it breaches? Third, the events of default. Are there any events of default that seem likely to occur during the normal course of business?

A Finance Director at a large PE firm becomes an expert in credit documentation and covenant language through experience with dozens of deals. They develop strong views on what constitutes an acceptable credit agreement. Many PE firms have internal "templates" that they prefer for credit agreements, with standard carve-outs and definitions that they believe optimize for the sponsor's operating flexibility.

The negotiation between the sponsor's Finance Director and the lender's credit committee is intense and highly technical. The sponsor might fight for a broad definition of "Permitted Acquisitions" that allows the company to make bolt-on acquisitions above a certain size without triggering a covenant violation. The lender might want a narrow definition that requires lender consent for any significant acquisition.

These negotiations are not about broader strategic interests. They are about technical language that affects whether the sponsor has actual flexibility to execute their business plan. A sponsor who believes they will make three bolt-on acquisitions during the hold period needs documentation that allows them to do so without technical defaults. A lender who believes significant acquisitions are risky wants to require consent for any material acquisition.

I have observed that the quality of the doc review process is a meaningful differentiator among PE firms. Firms with sophisticated Finance Directors tend to have documentation that is very precise and carefully negotiated. Firms that are less focused on documentation tend to end up with documentation that is looser but also riskier, because provisions are less precise and more subject to interpretation.

PART SIX: PORTFOLIO COMPANY RELATIONSHIP AND OPERATIONAL OVERSIGHT

Once a PE firm closes an acquisition and the company becomes a portfolio company, the sponsor's role shifts from investment to active ownership and management. This is where the operational value creation thesis gets executed, and where the tension between debt service and operational improvement becomes most apparent.

The first mechanism of sponsor involvement is board representation. In virtually all leveraged transactions, the PE sponsor will have board representation—typically one or more board seats, often including the board chair. The board is responsible for setting strategic direction, monitoring performance against plan, and making major capital allocation decisions. It is the primary mechanism through which the PE sponsor exercises control over the portfolio company.

But board representation is also a potential source of tension with lenders. A board composed entirely of the PE sponsor and the company's management might make decisions that are optimal for sponsors but not for lenders. For example, the board might approve a large acquisition that increases leverage significantly, or might approve a dividend distribution that reduces equity cushion, or might approve a management equity plan that creates incentives that are misaligned with debt service.

In larger transactions, lenders sometimes negotiate for lender representation on the board, or at minimum, for reporting to the lenders on board meeting minutes and major decisions. But even without formal board representation, lenders rely on information disclosure to monitor the sponsor's decision-making.

The second mechanism is management incentive alignment. PE sponsors typically implement equity plans that give the management team ownership stakes in the portfolio company or co-investment opportunities. The intent is to align management incentives with sponsor incentives—if management owns equity, then management will be focused on maximizing equity value, which aligns with sponsor interests.

But again, there is potential tension with lenders. Management equity that is senior to or pari passu with debt can create perverse incentives where management is incentivized to take risks that increase equity value even if they increase debt risk. More sophisticated PE structures create management equity that is subordinated to debt and only realizes value if debt holders are paid in full. This better aligns management incentives with debt service obligations.

I worked on a situation in 2009 where a portfolio company had implemented a management equity plan that promised significant equity upside if the company achieved certain growth and profitability targets. The management team became very aggressive in pursuing growth initiatives, including acquisition of several bolt-on companies and significant marketing investments. The acquisitions increased leverage significantly and the marketing investments reduced near-term profitability.

From the management perspective, the strategy made sense because it was aligned with the equity upside targets. From the lender perspective, the strategy was concerning because it increased leverage and reduced profitability in the near term. The sponsor was supporting the management's strategy because they believed the long-term value creation would justify the near-term risks.

But what if the management's growth strategy had not worked out? What if the bolt-on acquisitions had failed to integrate properly, or the market had shifted, or the management team had been unable to execute? Then the company would have been left with higher leverage, lower profitability, and significant distress risk. The management's equity incentives created a bias toward aggressive strategies that might not have been in the lenders' interests.

The third mechanism is management reporting and information flow. PE sponsors typically require portfolio companies to provide detailed monthly financial reporting—actual results vs. plan, variance analysis, cash flow forecasts. For larger transactions, sponsors might require weekly or daily reporting. This allows the sponsor to monitor performance against plan and to identify issues early.

From a lender perspective, this information flow is important because it gives lenders visibility into how the company is performing relative to the sponsor's projections. Lenders will usually negotiate for direct reporting of key metrics—quarterly financial statements, compliance certificates showing covenant calculations, cash flow forecasts. Some lenders will negotiate for monthly reporting of key metrics like revenue and EBITDA.

The tension here is around information asymmetry. The PE sponsor has detailed monthly information about the portfolio company's performance. The lender receives quarterly financial statements and periodic compliance certificates. This information gap means that the sponsor has better visibility into performance issues and potential problems. A responsible sponsor will alert lenders to issues early. But a sponsor might be tempted to keep negative information confidential, hoping to resolve the issue before the next reporting period.

I have seen situations where a portfolio company had a significant revenue shortfall or cost overrun in a particular month, but the company was on pace to recover over the quarter, so the sponsor did not immediately alert lenders. When the quarterly financial statements came out, the results were better than they appeared in the early month, and the issue seemed less severe. But lenders would have preferred to know about the interim problem, because it would have given them visibility into the company's actual operating volatility.

The fourth mechanism is active intervention when a portfolio company is underperforming. This is where PE sponsors earn their return premium by creating value through operational improvement. When a company is underperforming relative to plan, the sponsor's operating partners and finance professionals will be deployed to investigate the issues and develop corrective action plans.

This intervention might be as simple as replacing the CFO with someone who has better cost control discipline. It might involve significant operational restructuring—closing facilities, consolidating facilities, reducing headcount. It might involve bringing in consultants to identify specific cost reduction opportunities. It might involve replacing the CEO if the current CEO is not executing the sponsor's plan.

From a lender perspective, this active oversight is a positive sign. It demonstrates that the sponsor is engaged and is willing to take action to improve performance. But there is also a risk: aggressive intervention in response to underperformance might involve actions that reduce near-term cash flow in order to improve long-term fundamentals. A company might incur significant restructuring costs, or might make investments in systems or training that reduce near-term profitability. A sponsor making these decisions is optimizing for long-term equity returns, but might be making short-term cash flow management more difficult.

I saw this clearly in a business services company in 2010-2011. The company was underperforming relative to plan—revenue growth was slower and margins were lower than the sponsor's base case. The sponsor brought in a new COO from a more efficient competitor to restructure operations. The COO implemented significant changes—consolidating facilities, moving work to lower-cost locations, outsourcing certain functions.

In the near term, these changes created disruption. Some customers experienced service degradation. Some employees left. The company's revenue growth actually slowed in the first six months of the restructuring because customers were unhappy with the changes. Measured leverage spiked from 2.8x to 3.2x because EBITDA was flat and leverage increased due to the restructuring investments.

But the sponsor stuck with the plan because they believed the restructuring would ultimately improve profitability. By year two, profitability had improved significantly and the company was generating strong cash flow. Leverage deleveraged to 2.5x. By the exit, the company had achieved much higher profitability than it would have achieved without the restructuring.

The lenders who trusted the sponsor and accommodated the near-term leverage increase were ultimately rewarded with strong credit improvement. But lenders who panicked and demanded amortization or covenant waivers might have perceived the situation as a deteriorating credit. The tension is inherent—active operational improvement sometimes requires near-term financial deterioration, and lenders need to tolerate this in order for sponsors to execute their value creation plans.

PART SEVEN: THE EXIT AND WHAT IT MEANS FOR CREDIT

For PE sponsors, the investment is never truly permanent. The entire business model is predicated on exiting at a higher value than the entry value, realizing gains for equity holders. The exit timing is typically planned for 4-7 years post-acquisition, though this can vary significantly based on the fund lifecycle and market conditions.

From a credit perspective, the exit is crucial because it determines what actually happens to the debt. The sponsor's ability to exit successfully, at a high valuation, determines the lenders' outcomes. If the exit valuation is high, the company will have plenty of cash to repay the debt at favorable terms. If the exit valuation is low, the company might struggle to repay the debt, and lenders might realize losses.

There are three primary exit paths that PE sponsors pursue: strategic sale to a corporate buyer, secondary sale to another PE firm, and IPO. Each has different implications for how the company operates, how leverage is managed, and what happens to the debt.

A strategic sale is the most common exit. The sponsor sells the company to a larger corporate competitor or a corporate platform company. Strategic buyers typically pay higher valuations than PE buyers because they can realize cost synergies, revenue synergies, or tax benefits from the acquisition. This is typically favorable for lenders because the high valuation provides ample cushion to repay debt.

A secondary sale is when the sponsor sells the company to another PE firm. The secondary market has grown significantly in my career. Rather than exiting through a strategic sale or IPO, a sponsor might decide that the company still has significant value creation opportunity, and might instead sell to another PE firm. The acquiring PE firm is typically a larger firm with more resources or a more specialized expertise. Secondary sales can be at attractive prices because the acquiring firm is buying a proven, improving business with established operations.

An IPO is when the sponsor takes the company public through an initial public offering. This is a common exit path for larger, higher-quality companies. Going public provides liquidity and allows the sponsor to exit gradually rather than all at once. But IPO exits come with constraints—the company must be large enough, profitable enough, and in an attractive industry for investors to be interested.

Now here is the crucial point: how the sponsor plans to exit affects the operational strategy and capital structure during the hold period. A sponsor who is planning a strategic sale to a corporate buyer might structure the company to minimize costs and maximize current profitability, because corporate buyers care about current cash flows and margins. A sponsor who is planning a secondary sale to another PE firm might focus more on growth and top-line expansion, because PE buyers care about EBITDA and leverage capacity. A sponsor who is planning an IPO might focus on revenue growth and positive EBITDA, because public investors care about growth trajectory.

Each of these exit strategies has implications for leverage. A company being prepared for a strategic sale might have leverage conservatively managed because corporate buyers worry about debt repayment obligations. A company being prepared for a secondary sale might have leverage near maximum capacity because secondary PE buyers are comfortable with leverage. A company being prepared for an IPO might have leverage reduced in advance of the IPO because IPO investors are often uncomfortable with aggressive leverage.

I worked on several companies that were being prepared for exit in 2015-2016, as the secondary market was booming. The sponsor of one portfolio company decided that rather than pursuing a strategic sale or IPO, they would do a secondary sale to a larger platform company. This changed the capital strategy significantly. The previous focus had been on margin improvement and conservative financial management. After the decision to do a secondary sale, the sponsor shifted to pursuing bolt-on acquisitions to increase EBITDA and scale. They increased leverage from 2.5x to 3.2x to finance the acquisitions, knowing that a secondary PE buyer would be comfortable with this leverage level.

This is a key insight: leverage during the hold period is not just about what the company can support at a point in time. It is also about what leverage the anticipated buyer at exit will accept. If the planned exit is a strategic sale to a corporate buyer, the sponsor will be conscious about debt levels because corporate buyers often prefer lower-leverage structures. If the planned exit is a secondary sale to a PE firm, the sponsor can be more aggressive on leverage because PE firms understand leverage and are accustomed to owning levered companies.

The timing of the exit is also crucial. PE sponsors think carefully about when to exit. A company might hit a strong year in revenue growth and margins in year four of the hold. If the sponsor can exit in that year while momentum is positive, they might achieve a higher valuation than if they wait for year five when momentum might slow. But exiting in year four means the lenders do not get a full seven years of debt service.

This creates a dynamic where sponsors are incented to exit as soon as they believe they have created sufficient value, rather than holding until the end of the fund's investment period. In some cases, this is good for lenders because the company is exiting early at a strong valuation and can repay debt easily. In other cases, it is suboptimal for lenders because the exit happens before the company has had time to meaningfully deleverage.

I saw this in an acquisition of a IT services company in 2011. The sponsor's initial plan was to hold the company through 2017, a six-year hold. But in 2015, the company had performed very well, growing EBITDA by 35% and achieving strong profitability improvements. The market for IT services companies was strong, and corporate buyers were paying premium valuations. The sponsor decided to exit early, selling the company for a 3.2x MOIC after just a four-year hold.

From the lenders' perspective, the early exit was fine—the company had strong liquidity and was able to repay the debt in full at the exit. But if the company had underperformed, and the sponsor still wanted to exit in year four, the lenders might have realized losses because the company would not have had four years to deleverage.

Now let me describe the refinancing before exit strategy, which is another sophisticated tool that PE sponsors use. As the company approaches exit, the sponsor might refinance the existing debt to extend maturities, reduce pricing, or extract cash to return to sponsors through a dividend recap or refinancing.

A refinancing before exit creates value for sponsors by allowing them to take out some of the value created during the hold period without actually exiting. The sponsor refinances the existing term loan into new debt with a longer maturity and lower pricing (because the company has improved and is perceived as lower risk). The proceeds of the refinancing are used to repay the original term loan and to pay a dividend to the sponsors.

This strategy works when a company has meaningfully improved during the hold period and the credit markets are receptive to refinancing at lower pricing and higher leverage. I worked on multiple companies that did refinancings in 2011-2012, exiting the great financial crisis period when credit spreads had tightened significantly. Companies that were broadly flat on EBITDA but had improved their leverage profile through debt reduction were able to refinance at much tighter spreads.

From a lender perspective, a refinancing before exit can be positive or negative depending on what actually happens. If the refinancing allows the company to improve its capital structure and reduce leverage, then the refinancing is positive for existing lenders because the company is objectively stronger. But if the refinancing allows the sponsor to extract cash through increased leverage without corresponding operational improvement, then the refinancing is negative for lenders because leverage increases and the equity cushion decreases.

One of the most vivid examples I participated in was a refinancing of a consumer products company in 2013. The company had been acquired in 2009 at 3.5x leverage. By 2013, the company had improved EBITDA by 30% through operational improvements, and leverage had declined to 2.3x. The company's credit profile had improved significantly. The sponsor decided to refinance, issuing a new term loan at much tighter spreads (SOFR + 275 basis points vs. the original SOFR + 400 basis points).

The proceeds of the refinancing were used to repay the original term loan and to pay a special dividend of $80 million to the sponsors. This dividend was structured so that the new leverage was 2.8x—higher than the post-improvement leverage of 2.3x, but lower than the original entry leverage of 3.5x. The sponsors realized a partial distribution on their investment while the company still had a reasonable leverage profile.

From the lenders' perspective, this refinancing was acceptable because the company's fundamentals had genuinely improved and the new leverage was still reasonable. But if the company had not improved significantly, if leverage was being taken without operational improvement, the refinancing would have been concerning.

PART EIGHT: SPONSOR BEHAVIOR IN DISTRESS AND ACROSS CYCLES

Now I want to shift to a more difficult topic: what happens when things go wrong, when a portfolio company is underperforming, and when covenant compliance becomes challenged. This is where the true test of sponsor-lender relationships emerges, and where misalignment becomes most apparent.

When a PE-backed company begins to underperform, the first question is whether the sponsor will inject additional capital or whether the sponsor will preserve capital and allow the company to work through the distress. This decision is driven by several factors. First, the sponsor's belief in the long-term value of the company. If the sponsor believes the underperformance is temporary and the company will recover, they are more likely to inject capital. If the sponsor believes the underperformance is structural and the company faces a long-term challenge, they are more likely to preserve capital.

Second, the sponsor's available capital. A sponsor who is actively deploying capital across their portfolio, making acquisitions and investments, is less likely to have excess capital available for equity injections. A sponsor with dry powder (undeployed capital) is more likely to inject capital to support an existing investment.

Third, the sponsor's opportunity cost. If the sponsor believes they have better investment opportunities elsewhere, they will be less likely to inject capital into an underperforming portfolio company. If the portfolio company is their best opportunity, they will be more likely to inject capital.

Fourth, the competitive dynamics. If the sponsor believes they can recover the investment through a relatively quick turnaround, they will be more likely to inject capital. If they believe the company is fundamentally impaired and will require years of work to recover, they will be less likely.

I have observed sponsor behavior on equity injections across multiple downturns. In the 2001-2002 period, sponsors in most industries were willing to inject capital to support portfolio companies because they believed the downturn was temporary. In the 2008-2009 period, sponsors were more selective—they injected capital into some companies that they believed had good recovery prospects, but they let other companies fall into distress.

One case I worked on in 2009 illustrates this dynamic. A PE sponsor had made an acquisition of a manufacturing company in 2006 at a 12x EBITDA multiple with 3.5x leverage. The company had made several bolt-on acquisitions to integrate supply chain and achieve cost synergies. In 2008, the recession hit hard. Manufacturing demand collapsed and the company's revenue declined 25% in 2008 and another 15% in 2009.

By the end of 2009, leverage had spiked to 5.5x and the company was in technical default under its maintenance leverage covenant. The sponsor faced a decision: should they inject $20 million of equity to repay some debt and bring the company into compliance with covenants, or should they allow the company to restructure and work through the distress?

The sponsor's analysis was that the manufacturing sector was in the depths of a severe recession but that a recovery was likely by 2011. They believed that if they could support the company through 2010, the recovery would begin and the company would be valuable. They decided to inject $20 million of equity, which brought leverage to 4.8x. But they also negotiated with lenders to amend and extend the credit agreement, increasing covenant flexibility and extending maturity.

The sponsor's bet on recovery paid off. By 2011, manufacturing demand had recovered and the company's revenue and EBITDA began to grow. By 2012, the company's leverage was back below 3.5x and the company was on a normal path toward exit. But the sponsor had been forced to inject equity during distress, diluting their ownership and reducing their ultimate returns.

A different situation occurred with another PE sponsor during the same period. Another manufacturing company, also acquired in 2005 at similar leverage, had performed poorly in the 2008-2009 recession. By 2009, this company was also at 5.0x+ leverage. But this sponsor was in the middle of raising a new fund and was focused on deploying capital into new investments rather than supporting existing ones. The sponsor chose not to inject equity.

Instead, the sponsor worked with lenders to restructure the company's debt. The restructuring involved an exchange offer where lenders agreed to exchange some of their debt for equity in the company. This diluted the sponsors' ownership but reduced debt and allowed the company to avoid bankruptcy. Eventually, the company recovered like the other manufacturing company, but the sponsors' ownership had been diluted and their returns were lower.

These two situations illustrate the critical choice sponsors face in distress: do they support the company by injecting capital, or do they preserve capital and allow the company to restructure? There is no universal right answer—it depends on the sponsor's views, their capital situation, and their assessment of recovery probability.

Now let me address the "distressed exchange" or "liability management exercise" from the sponsor perspective. When a company is in distress and restructuring, the sponsors and lenders often negotiate to reduce the company's debt burdens. This might involve extending maturity, reducing principal, reducing interest rates, or exchanging debt for equity.

From the sponsor perspective, a restructuring is preferable to bankruptcy because restructuring can be done relatively quickly and without formal court involvement. But restructurings are also expensive and disruptive—they require lengthy negotiations, they require lender votes on exchange proposals, and they sometimes result in loss of key employees or customers as uncertainty about the company's future affects stakeholder confidence.

The sponsors' strategy in a restructuring is to convince lenders that accepting a haircut is better than fighting through bankruptcy. The sponsors will prepare detailed evidence of the company's value under a going-concern basis and show lenders what they would recover in bankruptcy if the company liquidates. The sponsors will propose a restructuring that gives lenders meaningful recovery while reducing the company's debt burden to sustainable levels.

I worked on several restructurings in the 2009-2012 period, and what struck me was how much sponsor behavior varied. Some sponsors were transparent about the company's financial situation, acknowledged that the company was impaired, and negotiated restructurings that fairly distributed losses between debt and equity. Other sponsors were more aggressive in trying to preserve as much equity value as possible, sometimes proposing restructurings that were unrealistic or that left insufficient margin of safety for lenders.

The Serta restructuring in 2010 is a good example. Serta, a mattress manufacturer, had been acquired by Ares Equity Partners and Apollo Management in 2006. The company had been loaded up with debt at elevated leverage to fund the acquisitions of other mattress companies. When the housing crisis hit and furniture sales collapsed, Serta's revenue plummeted and leverage spiked to extremely high levels.

By 2009-2010, Serta was in severe distress. The sponsors had already written off their equity investment. The question was how to restructure the company's debt. The sponsors proposed a restructuring that involved significant debt-for-equity exchanges and extension of maturity. Lenders faced a choice: accept the restructuring or push the company into bankruptcy.

Ultimately, Serta restructured out of court, with lenders accepting haircuts in exchange for extended maturity and improved operational control. But the restructuring took months of negotiation and was contentious. Different groups of lenders had different opinions on what the company's value really was, and whether the proposed restructuring was fair.

What I learned from Serta and other restructurings is that sponsor credibility is crucial. Sponsors who have been transparent about problems and who have been realistic about the company's value are easier to negotiate with. Sponsors who try to hide problems or who are unrealistic about recovery scenarios create distrust and make restructuring more difficult.

Another important dynamic is when sponsors use "equity cures" to avoid covenant breaches. An equity cure is when the sponsor injects equity capital, which reduces leverage and improves covenant compliance. Many credit agreements now include provisions that limit when sponsors can use equity cures—for example, a sponsor can use an equity cure to avoid a covenant breach only once per year, or only if leverage is within a certain range of the covenant threshold.

Equity cures can be beneficial or problematic depending on the circumstances. If a company is fundamentally healthy but temporarily below a covenant threshold due to a quarterly revenue fluctuation, an equity cure makes sense—it allows the company to avoid a technical default without restructuring. But if a company is structurally impaired and the sponsor is using equity cures repeatedly to keep the company from defaulting, then equity cures just delay the inevitable and allow the company to deteriorate further.

I saw a situation in 2015 where a PE-backed software company had underperformed for two consecutive years due to higher-than-expected customer churn. The company had missed its revenue targets for eight straight quarters. The sponsor had injected equity twice to avoid covenant breaches. By the time the lenders realized what was happening, the company was substantially more impaired than when the first equity cure had occurred.

The lenders learned a lesson: continuous equity cures are a warning sign that the company is structurally impaired rather than temporarily underperforming. The lenders should have pushed harder on the first equity cure to understand why the company was underperforming and to understand the sponsor's long-term path to recovery.

PART NINE: MEGA-FUNDS VS. MID-MARKET SPONSORS

The behavior I have described in prior sections applies broadly across PE sponsors, but there are meaningful differences between large mega-funds and mid-market sponsors. The mega-funds—KKR, Blackstone, Carlyle, Apollo, and others managing $100+ billion in assets—have different economics, different deal selection processes, and different risk management approaches than mid-market sponsors managing $5-20 billion in assets.

Let me first describe the economics of mega-funds. A mega-fund like Blackstone might raise a $30 billion fund and charge 2% management fee. This generates $600 million annually in management fee revenue. On a per-partner basis, this is substantial—a mega-fund might have 80-100 partners, meaning $6-7 million per partner annually. A mega-fund can afford to invest in large operational teams, in sophisticated risk management processes, in extensive due diligence.

A mid-market sponsor managing a $10 billion fund might charge 2.25% management fee. This generates $225 million annually. On a per-partner basis, with maybe 20-25 partners, this is $9-11 million per partner annually. But the mid-market sponsor has less total overhead capacity and typically has fewer operational partners.

The difference in operational capacity affects how they approach deals and leverage. Mega-funds can afford to have deep operational expertise in multiple sectors. They can afford to spend six months on operational due diligence, bringing in consultants and conducting detailed market research. Mid-market sponsors typically have less operational depth and must be more selective about how much operational resources they deploy on any particular deal.

This difference in operational capacity affects leverage decisions. Mega-funds can afford to take on more leverage because they can afford to invest in operational improvement to support the leverage. They can deploy experienced operating partners to portfolio companies. Mid-market sponsors must be more conservative on leverage because they have less ability to support the leverage through operational improvement.

Another difference is in deal sourcing. Mega-funds have more established relationships with investment bankers, corporate sellers, and other market participants. They have "club" relationships where multiple mega-funds might bid together on very large acquisitions, with each mega-fund taking a stake to reduce individual risk. These club deals allow mega-funds to do very large acquisitions that individual sponsors could not do alone.

Mid-market sponsors have fewer relationships and less deal flow. They typically compete harder for mid-market deals and often win more on proprietary basis than mega-funds do. But mega-fund competition in mid-market is increasing, as mega-funds have grown and moved down-market to find deployment opportunities.

I have observed that in competitive auctions, mega-funds and mid-market sponsors often have different bidding strategies. Mega-funds are sometimes willing to bid more aggressively and take more leverage because they have the operational capacity to support it. Mid-market sponsors are often more disciplined on leverage but might push harder on operational improvement.

The capital structure decisions of mega-funds vs. mid-market sponsors also differ. Mega-funds might be more willing to layer HoldCo debt and other structural subordination because they have the operational resources to support it. Mid-market sponsors might be more conservative on HoldCo debt because they have less capacity to support the incremental leverage.

In my experience working with both mega-funds and mid-market sponsors, I have found that mega-fund sponsors are generally more sophisticated on leverage management and covenant negotiation. They have better internal finance expertise and have negotiated more deals. But mega-fund leverage can also be more aggressive because of this sophistication—mega-funds know how to negotiate loose covenants and create technical flexibility that might not exist for mid-market sponsors.

From a lender perspective, lending to mega-fund sponsors involves different considerations than lending to mid-market sponsors. With a mega-fund, you are confident that the sponsor has the resources to support the company through adverse scenarios and can execute operational improvement plans. With a mid-market sponsor, you need to more carefully stress-test the company's ability to support leverage, because the sponsor might lack the operational resources to improve the company if something goes wrong.

PART TEN: SPONSOR-LENDER RELATIONSHIPS AND THE EVOLUTION OF LEVERAGE MARKETS

I want to spend significant time on the relationship between sponsors and lenders, because this relationship is perhaps the most crucial determinant of deal success in leveraged finance. The relationship shapes pricing, documentation, covenant terms, and most importantly, how both parties behave when challenges emerge.

Sponsors select their financing banks based on several criteria. First, pricing and terms. Which banks offer the tightest spreads and the most favorable documentation? Second, relationship. Which banks have financed other deals for this sponsor previously and understand the sponsor's operating and financial management practices? Third, league table ranking. Large sponsors care about their league table standing and which banks have financed their largest deals. Fourth, secondary market performance. How have the banks' deals performed in the secondary market? Do borrowers value the banks' documentation and operational support?

The relationship bank concept is important to understand. A sponsor who has worked with the same bank on multiple deals develops trust and understanding with that bank. The bank understands the sponsor's strategy, understands what covenants and documentation terms the sponsor cares about, and can move quickly on new deals. The sponsor in turn is more likely to hire that bank because of the efficiency of the relationship.

But relationship banking also creates soft dynamics that affect pricing and terms. A bank that has a long relationship with a sponsor and is comfortable with the sponsor's operational approach might be willing to offer tighter pricing than a new bank that does not know the sponsor. The bank might be willing to accept documentation that is less standard, if the bank trusts the sponsor.

I have seen this play out many times. A sponsor who has a strong relationship with one bank will often hire that bank to lead their financing, and the bank will come in at a cheaper price and faster execution than other potential banks. Other banks who are competing will be at a disadvantage because they do not have the relationship.

From a sponsor perspective, relationship banking is beneficial because it creates efficiency and familiarity. From a credit investor perspective, relationship banking can be concerning because loose documentation and aggressive leverage become normalized through repeated relationships.

This brings me to an important evolution in leveraged finance markets over my career: the rise of direct lending and the shift away from traditional sponsor-bank relationships.

For the first twenty years of my career, the traditional model was that sponsors worked with commercial banks (JP Morgan, Credit Suisse, Morgan Stanley, Bank of America, etc.) to arrange the financing for acquisitions. The banks would typically arrange a term loan that they would then syndicate to other banks and institutional investors. The banks would be the arranger and administrative agent, maintaining the relationship with the borrower, operating the credit agreement, and holding some amount of the debt themselves.

Starting around 2012-2013, direct lending emerged as an alternative. Direct lenders—some of which are subsidiaries of PE firms, others independent—bypass the syndication market and directly lend to sponsors. A direct lender might provide $200 million of subordinated debt for a deal that a bank would previously have syndicated. The direct lender has a relationship with the borrower, negotiates documentation directly, and holds the debt to maturity.

This change has significant implications for sponsor behavior. Direct lenders have different economics than banks. Direct lenders typically charge higher pricing (300-400 bps higher for subordinated debt) but accept less liquid terms (less focused on secondary market tradability). Direct lenders are more willing to accept very customized documentation because they plan to hold the debt themselves.

From a sponsor perspective, direct lending offers an advantage in competitive situations. If a sponsor wants to bid aggressively on a deal, the sponsor can layer in direct lending to increase leverage without needing to go through a bank syndication, which might require tighter pricing and more standard terms.

I worked on a competitive auction in 2016 where multiple sponsors were bidding for a platform company. The winning sponsor had structured a financing that included a $300 million term loan from a bank, but also a $100 million subordinated loan from a direct lender. This extra $100 million allowed the sponsor to bid higher enterprise value and win the auction. The direct lender accepted documentation that was more favorable to the sponsor than what a bank would have accepted.

The rise of direct lending has also changed the risk profile of leveraged acquisitions. Direct lending filled a void created by bank deleveraging after the financial crisis, allowing sponsors to leverage companies to higher levels. In some cases, this created better outcomes—companies that deserved to be leveraged could access adequate capital. In other cases, it created excess leverage, because direct lenders' focus on returns encouraged sponsors to take more leverage than was prudent.

Now let me address the shifting dynamics of sponsor-bank relationships over different market cycles. In strong market periods like 2005-2007 and 2015-2018, sponsors have significant power. Multiple banks are competing to arrange deals, wanting to build league table rankings and market share. Banks offer tight spreads and very favorable documentation terms. Covenants are loose and sponsors have maximum flexibility.

In weak market periods like 2008-2010 and 2020, lenders have more power. There is less competition for deals, banks are more concerned about credit quality than league table rankings, and documentation terms are tighter.

I participated in financing for a large platform acquisition in both 2007 (strong market) and 2010 (weak market). The 2007 deal was financed at SOFR + 350 basis points with very loose maintenance leverage covenants (4.5x threshold). The 2010 deal, which was similar in size and quality, was financed at SOFR + 500 basis points with tight maintenance leverage covenants (3.0x threshold).

The same sponsor, with similar credit quality, faced dramatically different financing terms because of market conditions. This illustrates how much sponsor behavior is shaped by credit market conditions. In 2007, the sponsor could take maximum leverage at tight pricing. In 2010, the sponsor had to be more conservative.

One final important evolution has been the development of sponsor advisory services. Many large investment banks now offer "sponsor advisory" services where the bank advises the sponsor on the transaction and the capital structure, rather than just arranging financing.

A sponsor advisory bank will work with the sponsor months before the deal closes, helping the sponsor evaluate different capital structures, stress-test the company's ability to support different leverage levels, and think through covenant language. The sponsor pays the advisory bank a fee for this work. Then, when the deal is ready to be financed, the bank might arrange the financing (leveraging the head start from advisory work) or other banks might arrange the financing.

The advantage of sponsor advisory is that it allows sponsors to optimize their capital structure before committing to it. The disadvantage is that the advisor has financial incentive to push leverage higher to increase the size of the financing and therefore the advisor's transaction fees.

I worked on both sides of sponsor advisory. As an advisor, I sometimes felt pressure to justify higher leverage because higher leverage meant a larger financing and a larger advisory fee. As a credit investor, I sometimes worried that sponsors had been over-advised toward excessive leverage. This is another source of potential misalignment in the ecosystem.

PART ELEVEN: WHAT TWENTY YEARS TEACHES ABOUT WORKING WITH PE

After twenty-three years in leveraged finance, I have developed some observations about sponsor behavior, sponsor quality, and how to build effective relationships with sponsors. Let me close with some reflections on lessons learned.

First, sponsors are not monolithic. There is tremendous variation in sponsor quality, sponsor approach, sponsor ethics, and sponsor behavior. Some sponsors are very conservative on leverage and very disciplined about only doing deals where they genuinely have an edge on operational improvement. Other sponsors are very aggressive on leverage and opportunistic about taking deals where they believe they can extract financial engineering value.

Over time, I have realized that sponsor quality is a meaningful predictor of deal success. Sponsors with strong track records of operational improvement, strong management of leverage, and transparent communication with lenders tend to have better outcomes. Sponsors who are primarily focused on financial engineering, who are aggressive on leverage, and who are not transparent tend to have worse outcomes when credit markets deteriorate.

I can identify sponsors who I would be comfortable lending to at 3.5x leverage, because I trust their operational capabilities and their commitment to supporting the company through challenges. I can also identify sponsors who I would be uncomfortable lending to at 2.5x leverage, because I do not trust their ability to execute their plans or because I believe they will not support the company if conditions deteriorate.

Second, understanding sponsor incentives is crucial. Once you understand that a sponsor's management fee depends on capital deployment, you understand why sponsors are focused on getting deals done quickly. Once you understand that a sponsor's carried interest depends on returns, you understand why sponsors are focused on leverage and financial engineering. Once you understand that a deal partner's compensation depends on transaction success, you understand why deal partners sometimes push leverage higher than is prudent.

This is not to say that sponsors are irrational or unethical. Rather, it is to say that sponsors have clear incentive structures that shape their behavior. As a credit investor or advisor, understanding these incentive structures allows you to anticipate sponsor behavior and to negotiate more effectively.

Third, the best sponsor-lender relationships are built on mutual understanding and trust. Sponsors who understand that lenders have different risk preferences and different objectives will work to create structures that satisfy both parties. Lenders who understand that sponsors are trying to maximize equity returns will work to create structures that allow reasonable leverage while maintaining adequate credit quality.

I have had relationships with PE sponsors that lasted decades, spanning dozens of deals. These relationships were successful because both parties understood each other's motivations and objectives, and because both parties generally acted with integrity and transparency.

Fourth, covenant negotiations are not a zero-sum game. There is a tendency to view covenants as lender tools to constrain sponsors. But well-designed covenants are actually beneficial to both parties. Tight maintenance leverage covenants force sponsors to operate conservatively and to address problems early, before they become severe. This reduces the probability of severe distress and therefore benefits lenders. But it also benefits sponsors by forcing them to stay disciplined and focused on operational improvement.

The best covenants are those that are tight enough to be meaningful but not so tight that they prevent the sponsor from executing reasonable business plans. This requires deep understanding of what the sponsor is trying to do and what flexibility the sponsor needs.

Fifth, understanding sponsor behavior across cycles is important. Sponsors who have been through multiple credit cycles have perspective that sponsors who have only experienced strong markets lack. A sponsor who managed a portfolio company through the 2008-2009 financial crisis has a different perspective on risk and leverage than a sponsor who started their career in 2010 after the crisis had already passed.

Similarly, sponsors who have experienced distressed situations have more realistic views about what can go wrong. A sponsor who has had to inject equity into an underperforming portfolio company, or who has had to negotiate a restructuring with lenders, understands that things can go badly in ways that were not anticipated.

Sixth, the relationship between sponsor and operating partner quality matters significantly. Some sponsors have built exceptional teams of operating partners with deep industry expertise. These sponsors can genuinely improve companies and support higher leverage. Other sponsors have limited operating partner resources or have operating partners without relevant expertise. These sponsors will struggle to support leverage and will likely perform worse.

When evaluating a PE-backed deal, I pay significant attention to the operating partner track record and the quality of operational plans. A sponsor with experienced operating partners who have successfully improved companies in the target industry is a much safer credit than a sponsor without proven operational expertise.

Seventh, transparency and communication are crucial. Sponsors who communicate openly with lenders when something goes wrong, who share information about portfolio company performance, and who are realistic about challenges build trust and maintain the ability to work collaboratively with lenders.

Sponsors who try to hide negative information, who are overly optimistic about recovery scenarios, or who are not transparent about problems create suspicion and make it harder for lenders to support the company when distress emerges.

I have been through situations where early transparency about a problem allowed us to work together to solve it, versus situations where the problem was hidden until it was severe and then all parties had to fight through a contentious restructuring. The transparent situations were always better.

Eighth, long-term relationships are valuable. A sponsor who takes a view toward building long-term relationships with a bank or advisor, rather than playing every deal off against competitors, tends to get better terms and faster execution over time. And more importantly, the sponsor's companies tend to perform better because the advisors are more invested in long-term success.

Similarly, a bank or advisor who focuses on long-term relationships with sponsors tends to be more successful than banks that are just trying to maximize transaction volume.

Ninth, pattern recognition is important. Over twenty years, I have seen certain sponsor patterns repeat. Sponsors who are very focused on short-term financial engineering and quick exits tend to underperform in down cycles. Sponsors who are focused on operational improvement and building durable businesses tend to outperform. Sponsors who are disciplined on leverage tend to have better outcomes than sponsors who are aggressive. Sponsors who treat management well, invest in people, and build strong cultures tend to have better outcomes than sponsors who are purely focused on cutting costs.

These patterns are not universal—there are exceptions—but they are meaningful tendencies.

Finally, I would offer this observation: the leveraged finance industry is at its best when lenders and sponsors work collaboratively toward mutual success. The industry is at its worst when it becomes adversarial and when both parties are trying to extract maximum value at the expense of the other party.

The deals where I have seen the best outcomes are those where the sponsor says to the lender: "Here is what we are trying to do. We need this much leverage to achieve our return targets. This much leverage allows us to execute our operating plan. But we understand that you need downside protection. Let's structure documentation that gives you that protection while giving us adequate flexibility."

And the lender says: "We understand what you are trying to do, and we believe in your operating plan. We are willing to accept this leverage level. But we need covenants that force you to address problems if performance slips. We need information rights that give us visibility into what is happening. We need documentation that protects us if things go badly."

These conversations, when they happen, lead to deals that are financed appropriately and that tend to perform well. When sponsors and lenders instead come to negotiations as adversaries, the deals tend to be either under-financed (which means the sponsor leaves money on the table) or over-financed (which means the lender takes unnecessary risk).

Over my career, I have learned that understanding the private equity perspective—understanding how sponsors think, how they make decisions, and what motivates them—is essential to being successful in leveraged finance. Whether you are a banker, a lawyer, a credit investor, or an advisor, you will be more effective if you understand your counterparty's incentives and objectives.

This understanding is not cynical—it does not mean that sponsors are irrational or unethical. Rather, it means that sponsors are rational actors with clear objectives and constraints. By understanding these objectives and constraints, you can work more effectively with sponsors and can build better deals and better relationships.

The leveraged finance industry at its best is a partnership between debt and equity, where both parties contribute to value creation and both parties benefit. Understanding the PE perspective is the first step toward building that partnership.
