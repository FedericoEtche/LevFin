DISTRESSED DEBT AND SPECIAL SITUATIONS: THE ULTIMATE TEST OF LEVERAGED FINANCE

INTRODUCTION: WHEN EVERYTHING GOES WRONG

I will never forget the first time I walked into a distressed debt situation. It was 2003, and I was a fresh analyst at a major investment bank covering a mid-market chemical company that had loaded up on debt at the worst possible time. The economy was just beginning to recover from the 2001 downturn, but this company—let's call it ChemCo—had borrowed $400 million to fund an acquisition right before their end markets collapsed. Within eighteen months, they were burning through cash at an alarming rate, their leverage had spiraled from 3.0x to nearly 5.5x, and their lenders were beginning to panic.

I was assigned to work with our distressed debt team, and I remember my first conference call with the company's management. The CFO had that particular tone of voice I would later learn to recognize instantly—the tone of someone who understands, with crushing clarity, that his company is about to go through the most traumatic experience of their career. The lenders, for their part, were split. Some wanted to work with management through a difficult period. Others were convinced that management was incompetent and needed to be replaced. And one vulture fund that had accumulated 8% of the term loan was already circling, sensing an opportunity to buy distressed debt at 50 cents on the dollar and potentially control the restructuring.

That situation taught me something fundamental that has guided my entire career: understanding distressed debt is not some specialized niche skill in leveraged finance. Rather, the opposite is true. The way you think about distressed situations—the analysis, the negotiation, the strategic positioning—is the purest expression of leveraged finance itself. It strips away all the marketing, all the leverage-sponsored stories about synergies and strategic fit, and forces you to confront the mathematical and operational reality of what you've built.

In leveraged finance, we spend years building financial models where everything works perfectly. We assume management executes flawlessly. We forecast free cash flow based on orderly market conditions. We assume covenants will be maintained, that refinancings will be available, that the sponsor will eventually exit the investment at a premium to entry. Distressed situations are where all of those assumptions go to die. And in that crucible, you learn more about financial analysis, negotiation strategy, and human nature than you ever could in a decade of normal transactions.

The distressed debt market is, in many ways, the ultimate test of everything you've learned in leveraged finance. It's where the spreadsheet models meet raw economic reality. It's where creditor rights meet bankruptcy law. It's where financial engineering meets the limitations imposed by actual cash generation. Most importantly, it's where I've learned that the best investors in distressed situations are not the ones who are best at financial engineering—they're the ones who best understand human psychology, negotiating strategy, and the law.

The market for distressed debt has been one of the most profitable corners of finance, particularly for those with the temperament and skill set to navigate it successfully. But it's also unforgiving. I've watched brilliant credit analysts get taken apart by seasoned bankruptcy lawyers. I've watched well-capitalized distressed funds chase deals they didn't understand and lose hundreds of millions. I've watched restructurings that seemed certain to result in company failure instead turn into successful turnarounds because of brilliant operational management or a shift in market conditions. And I've watched situations where everyone was making a rational decision from their own perspective, yet the collective outcome was a massive value destruction that benefited almost no one.

This chapter is not an academic treatise on restructuring law or a guide to formal bankruptcy procedures. There are excellent textbooks on those subjects, written by bankruptcy lawyers who are far more qualified than I am to explain the technical details of Chapter 11. Instead, this chapter is a practitioner's guide to understanding distressed debt situations, analyzing their dynamics, identifying opportunities, and executing successful outcomes. It draws on two decades of sitting across the table from distressed management teams, negotiating with creditor groups, analyzing restructuring proposals, and building trading positions in distressed securities.

The cast of characters in any distressed situation is colorful. You have the distressed debt funds—the specialists who deploy capital specifically to buy distressed paper and either push for a favorable restructuring or hold the securities to recovery. You have the opportunistic long-only investors who suddenly find themselves in a distressed situation because of a mistake in their original analysis or a market shift they didn't anticipate. You have the vulture investors who seem to have an almost supernatural ability to smell weakness and position themselves to profit from it. You have the restructuring advisors—the investment bankers, the bankruptcy lawyers, the turnaround specialists—who make their living solving these problems. You have the management teams, some of whom are fighting to save their company and their job, and others who are already planning their departure. And you have the secured lenders—the senior lenders, the second lien lenders, the unsecured creditors—each with their own view of what should happen and how much of the remaining value they should receive.

Understanding distressed situations means understanding all of these players, their motivations, their constraints, and the dynamics between them. It means understanding that there is almost never a "right" answer to how a distressed situation should be resolved—there are only trade-offs, and the resolution that emerges is usually the one that best balances the political economy of the creditor group and the legal constraints imposed by bankruptcy law.

Let me walk you through that journey, starting with the early warning signs that separate companies that will survive downturns from those that will end up in distress.

IDENTIFYING DISTRESSED CREDITS: EARLY WARNING SIGNS

The ability to identify distressed situations before they happen is one of the most valuable skills in credit analysis. And I don't mean the obvious ones—the auto suppliers that everyone knows are in trouble, or the retail chains that are clearly losing market share to e-commerce. I mean the situations where the company still looks reasonably healthy on the surface, but the trajectory is clearly pointing downward, and sophisticated investors are already positioning themselves for what's coming.

I think about this in terms of financial deterioration patterns, market signals, and operational signals. Let me walk through each of these.

Financial deterioration typically follows a predictable pattern. It usually starts with declining EBITDA. This is the canary in the coal mine. EBITDA is the fundamental measure of operating cash generation in leveraged finance, and when it starts declining, everything else follows. I remember one situation with a restaurant group in 2008, just as the recession was deepening. Their EBITDA had been essentially flat for the prior two years, which in a growing company was itself a warning sign. But by the first quarter of 2008, you could see the trajectory changing. Same-store sales were declining, traffic was down, and management's own guidance for the year had become increasingly pessimistic.

What matters in identifying these patterns is not just looking at year-over-year numbers, but understanding the underlying drivers. Are sales declining because of market conditions, or because of company-specific issues? Is gross margin stable, or is it being compressed by either higher input costs or a less favorable product mix? Is the company maintaining operating discipline, or are operating margins declining alongside declining revenues? These are the questions that separate companies that are experiencing a temporary cyclical downturn from companies that are in structural decline.

Once EBITDA starts declining, cash burn follows pretty quickly. The company either has to cut capital expenditures or draw down cash. One of my favorite leading indicators for distress is when a company's capital expenditure suddenly drops below maintenance levels. This usually means management is in conservation mode and is worried about liquidity. Similarly, if the company starts drawing on revolver facilities when it doesn't need to, it's usually a signal that management is worried about future cash availability.

Then comes the leverage spiral. This is when declining EBITDA causes leverage ratios to climb even as absolute debt levels might stay constant. I've seen companies cross from what looked like a comfortable 3.5x leverage ratio to nearly 5.0x leverage in a matter of 18 months, not because debt increased significantly, but because EBITDA declined 30%. Suddenly the covenants that looked safe are no longer safe. The refinancing options that looked available are no longer available. And the company moves from being a steady borrower to being a credit in distress.

The financial deterioration pattern typically accelerates, which is why early identification matters so much. The company that's at 3.5x leverage and declining EBITDA still has time to address the issues—to sell assets, to refinance before things get worse, to implement aggressive cost reduction. But once leverage has climbed to 4.5x or 5.0x and EBITDA is still falling, the window for an out-of-court solution is closing rapidly.

Market signals are the second major category of early warning signs, and they're incredibly useful because they're forward-looking. The market prices in expected distress long before the financial statements show it. Loan prices are the most direct signal. I have a simple rule: if a broadly syndicated loan is trading below 80 cents on the dollar, the market has serious concerns. Not mortal concerns necessarily—companies with loans trading in the 80-85 range can still work through their problems—but serious concerns. Once a loan is trading in the 70-75 range, the market is clearly pricing in significant distress risk. And once you get below 70 cents, you're no longer in the realm of "credit risk"—you're in the realm of "distress valuation," where the price reflects expectations about bankruptcy and recovery.

I remember sitting in a distressed debt trading desk in 2014 and watching the loan price for a particular energy company. The company's fundamentals didn't look that bad—they were still cash flow positive, they had reasonable revenues—but the loan was trading at 75 cents. This was right in the period when people were starting to understand what lower oil prices would mean for the entire energy sector. The lenders who were comfortable holding the loan at par were the unsophisticated long-only guys who hadn't updated their models for the new oil price environment. The sophisticated distressed funds were already accumulating the paper, understanding that the company was headed toward Chapter 11, and positioning themselves accordingly. By the time the company eventually filed, less than two years later, the loan had traded all the way down to 35 cents before recovering somewhat in the restructuring.

Credit default swap spreads tell a similar story. A company's CDS might be trading at 200 basis points—which suggests the market thinks there's maybe a 20% probability of default over five years—while another company with identical fundamentals is trading at 500 basis points. The difference is almost always market structure. The second company probably has smaller lenders who want to reduce credit risk and are selling the CDS. The first company probably has larger, more committed lenders who are willing to hold through the cycle. But the point remains: CDS spreads are incredibly forward-looking. When you see a company's CDS spike from 200 to 500 basis points in a matter of weeks, something has shifted in the market's perception of distress risk.

Bid-ask spreads in the loan market are another useful signal. In normal times, the bid-ask spread on a syndicated loan is maybe 25 basis points—tight, liquid markets. When a credit starts to deteriorate, the bid-ask spreads widen. By the time you're in serious distress territory, you might see bid-ask spreads of 3-5 full points (300-500 basis points), because buyers and sellers have very different views on what the recovery will be.

Operational signals are more subtle, but they matter enormously. Management turnover is a red flag. If a company's CFO or COO leaves suddenly without an obvious successor being named, management might know something that investors don't. Similarly, auditor changes are worth paying attention to. Companies don't normally fire their auditors, so if you see a change in auditors, there's usually something behind it. I once watched a situation where the company fired its long-time auditors and hired a new firm, and within six months they'd announced that they needed to restate two years of financial statements due to accounting errors. The market immediately repriced the credit.

Disclosure gaps are another signal. If a company stops providing guidance, or if they suddenly become evasive in their disclosures, something has shifted. I remember one situation where a company's financial statements started getting progressively vaguer about the status of its most important contracts. This turned out to be because one of their major customers was considering switching vendors, and the company didn't want to disclose that until it was finalized. But to the market, the lack of transparency was interpreted as a sign that management was hiding something worse. The credit spread widened accordingly.

The "zone of insolvency" is a useful concept for thinking about where companies are in their deterioration trajectory. This is the point at which a company's liabilities are reasonably likely to exceed the fair value of its assets. Not necessarily technically insolvent based on balance sheet accounting—balance sheets are notoriously backward-looking and can be quite misleading about economic reality. Rather, genuinely insolvent in the economic sense, meaning that if you liquidated the business, creditors would recover less than they're owed.

The zone of insolvency is important because it has legal implications. Once a company enters the zone of insolvency, the fiduciary duties of the board and management shift. Managers can no longer simply work to maximize equity value—they have to start considering creditor interests. This creates some interesting dynamics, because it's the point at which management and equityholders often start to diverge in their interests, and the board becomes a battleground for control.

How quickly can situations deteriorate? Very quickly. I think about Revlon, which filed for bankruptcy in 2022. Two years before that filing, Revlon looked like a reasonably stable company. Yes, there were questions about whether they could maintain their market position in an increasingly digital beauty market. Yes, they'd had some stumbles with key product launches. But the company was still generating nearly $2 billion in revenue and had a reasonably long operating history. Yet within 24 months, the company's balance sheet had deteriorated to the point where Chapter 11 was inevitable. The combination of slowing sales, supply chain disruptions, elevated debt levels, and increasing competition created a situation that spiraled quite quickly.

Envision Healthcare is another example. I remember analyzing Envision in 2016 or 2017—this was the company that Ares Management and Envision Healthcare had acquired and then merged, and it was supposed to be this dominant player in the urgent care space. The company looked fine on the surface. It had good revenues, it had a growth strategy, it was building something. But there were always questions about whether they could actually achieve the promised synergies and margin expansion. By 2020, the world had changed—COVID had created massive operational disruptions for urgent care providers, patient volumes had collapsed, and Envision had been left with a capital structure that was too aggressive for the new market reality. They eventually did a significant out-of-court restructuring, but they were within spitting distance of a Chapter 11 filing.

What I've learned from watching dozens of these situations is that deterioration typically accelerates. The companies that are going to make it through usually do so by making difficult decisions early and decisively. The ones that try to muddle through—that make small cost cuts and hope things get better—tend to deteriorate much faster. Once you get to the point where you're cutting costs just to maintain liquidity rather than for strategic reasons, you're usually in the process of deteriorating rapidly.

This is why I built what I call a "monitoring framework" for credit portfolios. Once a year, I sit down and review every significant credit position in the portfolio, not just to update the financial statements and rerun the models, but to ask three specific questions: (1) Is the underlying business more or less attractive today than it was a year ago? (2) Are management and the board still aligned on the strategy, or are there signs of internal fractures? (3) If the market turned materially worse, how much time would this company have before they'd be forced to either refinance or restructure? These three questions will almost always identify credits that are moving toward distress before the financial markets do.

THE DISTRESSED DEBT MARKET

Once you've identified a credit that's moving toward distress, the next question is: how does the distressed debt market actually work? Understanding this is essential, because it's a different market with different conventions, different players, and different dynamics than the normal leveraged finance market.

Let me start with the basic mechanics of how distressed debt trades. In normal market conditions, bank loans trade in what's called the "par" market. A bank loan that was underwritten at 100 cents on the dollar trades somewhere close to par—maybe 99 to 101, depending on small interest rate moves and very minor credit changes. The loan is held by its original lenders, banks are making their normal spreads, and life proceeds as expected. The bid-ask spread is tight, usually 25 basis points or less.

But once a credit starts to deteriorate, the loan moves into what we call the "distressed" market. This is a completely different ecosystem. Now the loan might be trading at 80 cents, or 70 cents, or 50 cents. The original lenders—often large banks that don't have specialized distressed trading operations—want out. They don't want to be dealing with a potentially bankrupt company. They don't want to be involved in the restructuring. And they certainly don't want to take the write-down that comes with selling at 70 cents. So they sell to whoever will buy them.

And who buys distressed debt? This is where the cast of characters comes in. First, you have the dedicated distressed funds. These are funds that are specifically capitalized to buy distressed securities. They have the expertise to analyze them, the risk tolerance to take them on, and often the capital and patience to hold them through a restructuring. Many of the best-known distressed funds are just that—firms like Cerberus, Oaktree, Dyal, and others made their names by buying distressed paper in the aftermath of the 2008 financial crisis and subsequent downturns, holding the positions through Chapter 11, and recovering cents on the dollar.

Second, you have opportunistic funds—long-only investors who happen to have capital available and see what they perceive as a cheap opportunity. These might be general credit funds that now find themselves holding distressed positions they didn't intend to, or they might be funds that explicitly include a distressed mandate but aren't specialists in the way that the dedicated funds are. These investors have been much more of a presence in the past ten years than they were before, partly because the cost of capital has been so low and the availability of credit so easy that distressed opportunities have been fewer and farther between.

Third, you have the bank portfolio sales. Sometimes a bank lends money as part of a syndicated facility but doesn't want to hold the paper forever. If the loan is in the distressed market, they might sell their piece to a distressed fund or to another financial institution. These sales are incredibly important for understanding the market, because they often move fairly substantial chunks of debt from unsophisticated holders (the long-term hold investors) to sophisticated holders (the distressed funds).

The Role of LSTA documentation is crucial here. The Loan Syndications and Trading Association has spent decades developing standardized trading conventions for loans, which makes it possible for billions of dollars of loans to trade in the secondary market without the entire transaction having to be negotiated from scratch. Things like the assignment and assumption agreement, the trading documents, the settlement conventions—all of these are standardized. This is what allows a sophisticated distressed investor to move quickly when they see an opportunity, because they can analyze a loan, decide they want to buy it, and close a purchase within days or even hours. Without this standardization, the market would barely exist.

Now let's talk about the bid-ask spread in distress. In normal times, as I mentioned, the bid-ask spread is tight. But in distressed situations, the bid-ask spread widens dramatically. I've seen loan bid-ask spreads of five full points or more in real distress situations. A bid of 45 cents and an ask of 50 cents on the same loan is not unusual. Why such a wide spread? Because buyers and sellers have genuinely different views on what the recovery will be. The seller—maybe a bank that just wants the risk off their books—might be willing to sell at 45 cents because they've already written the position down and they just want liquidity. The buyer—a distressed fund that thinks they can influence the restructuring to get better recovery—might think the position is worth 70 cents to them because of their control and operational expertise.

Claims trading is another aspect of the distressed market that's worth understanding. Once a company files for bankruptcy, the claims against the company—the bank loans, the bonds, the trade payables, the employee claims—all become tradeable. And there's a whole market in these claims, with different rules and conventions than the par loan market. Claims are traded on a "trading at" basis, meaning a claim that was worth par before bankruptcy might be trading at 60 cents "trading at" in the claims market. The settlement mechanics are different too—instead of receiving the cash directly, the buyer of a claim receives whatever the claim recovers in the bankruptcy, whether that's cash, equity in the reorganized company, or some combination.

Claims trading is where a lot of the sophisticated distressed players really make their money. They'll analyze a company's capital structure, figure out where they think the fulcrum security is (more on this later), and then either buy the fulcrum security or positions adjacent to it. The whole point is to either control the restructuring through a blocking position or to get a position that's going to recover the most value.

The par-versus-distressed convention is important to understand because it affects pricing. A bond that's trading at 80 in par dollars (meaning it would cost 80 cents per dollar of principal to buy) might be trading at 95 in distressed-dollar pricing if we're already expecting haircuts. The terminology matters because it can be confusing, and I've seen traders get tripped up on this distinction.

How do you actually value distressed debt? This is where it gets really interesting, because there are multiple valuation approaches and they'll often give you very different answers.

The first approach is recovery analysis. This is pretty straightforward in concept: what will this creditor recover if the company goes through bankruptcy? You need to estimate the enterprise value of the company at emergence from bankruptcy, then waterfall that value through the capital structure to see how much each tranche gets. If the enterprise value is $500 million and the first lien debt is $400 million, then the first lien creditors will recover 100 cents on the dollar from the enterprise value, and the second lien lenders and unsecured creditors will split the remaining $100 million.

The second approach is reorganization value. This is different from enterprise value in that it includes some estimate of what the company might be worth if it can successfully restructure and move forward as a going concern under new ownership. This might be higher than enterprise value if you assume the company can improve its operations, or it might be lower if you assume the company is in structural decline. The key is that reorganization value is forward-looking—it's what you think the business will be worth after the restructuring, not what it's worth today.

The third approach is liquidation analysis. If the company is forced to liquidate, how much can it get for its assets? A manufacturing company might liquidate for 40-50% of its book value of fixed assets, depending on how specialized they are. A retail company might liquidate for much less, depending on lease obligations and the age of its stores. An asset-heavy business like a utility or an infrastructure company might liquidate for a higher percentage of book value. The liquidation analysis gives you a floor on what creditors will recover.

I think about these three approaches in combination. If the recovery analysis suggests recovery of 80%, reorganization value suggests recovery of 75%, and liquidation analysis suggests recovery of 40%, then you probably should be looking at valuations somewhere in the 40-75% range, depending on your confidence in the company's ability to restructure successfully.

THE RESTRUCTURING TOOLKIT: OUT-OF-COURT SOLUTIONS

One of the most important things I learned in my first decade of working with distressed credits is that the best restructurings are the ones that never reach formal bankruptcy court. This is counterintuitive to a lot of people, because they assume that bankruptcy is the automatic solution for distressed companies. But in reality, the most efficient and value-preserving restructurings are often the ones that happen in the negotiating rooms of large law firms, without ever needing to file for bankruptcy.

Why is an out-of-court restructuring better than bankruptcy? Several reasons. First, it's faster. A bankruptcy can take anywhere from six months to several years, depending on how complex the situation is and how contested the various claims are. An out-of-court restructuring can sometimes happen in a matter of weeks or months. Second, out-of-court restructurings preserve business value because they avoid the disruption and uncertainty that comes with a bankruptcy filing. Customers, suppliers, and employees get nervous when a company files for bankruptcy, and you sometimes see people vote with their feet—customers switch suppliers, employees look for other jobs, suppliers demand cash on delivery. Third, out-of-court restructurings usually result in less expensive legal and advisory fees, because you don't have to pay for the full bankruptcy process. And finally, out-of-court restructurings preserve more value for creditors because there's no dilution from bankruptcy costs.

So what are the tools available for out-of-court restructurings?

Amendments and waivers are the most basic tool. When a company is heading toward distress, the first thing that usually happens is that they approach their lenders and ask for relief from their covenants. A covenant waiver is pretty straightforward—the creditors agree to waive the covenant violation that would otherwise trigger a default. In exchange, the creditors usually get something: maybe an increase in the interest rate, maybe some restrictions on the company's ability to pay dividends or make capital expenditures, maybe just the peace of mind that they're aligned with management and are going to work through this problem together.

I've done hundreds of waiver negotiations, and the same pattern shows up every single time. The first conversation with lenders is always difficult, because there's always some denial. "Are you sure this covenant will be violated?" "Is there any way you can manage this?" "What if we adjust the calculation?" But once the company and the lead lender accept that the covenant is going to be violated, then it's just a negotiation about how much relief you're getting and what you're giving in exchange.

Forbearance agreements are a step beyond waivers. A forbearance agreement is a comprehensive agreement between the company and the creditors that outlines what's going to happen over the next period. Typically a forbearance agreement will specify: (1) what covenants are being waived; (2) what additional covenants are being imposed; (3) what the company's financial targets are for the next few quarters; (4) what happens if the company misses those targets; and (5) what the process will be for resolving any future covenant violations or default situations.

Forbearance agreements are really useful because they give the company and the creditors a shared understanding of what success looks like. Instead of just the company trying to stay above water, there's now an explicit agreement about what the company needs to achieve, and what the creditors will do in response if those targets are met or missed. I've seen forbearance agreements create a remarkably collaborative environment, where the company and the lenders are genuinely working together to fix the problems.

Amend and extend transactions take this a step further. If the company has near-term maturities that are unsustainable, they might approach lenders and ask to extend the maturity of the debt in exchange for some change in terms. Maybe they extend the maturity by two years and increase the interest rate by 200 basis points. Or maybe they convert some of the debt to a lower-priority claim in exchange for extending the maturity and getting some capital.

These transactions are called "amend and extend" because the company is amending the terms of the existing debt and extending the maturity. They're incredibly important as a tool for working out of distressed situations, because they buy the company time. Time is the most valuable resource for a distressed company, because it creates the opportunity for the underlying business to improve.

Distressed exchanges are more aggressive versions of amend and extend transactions. In a distressed exchange, instead of just extending the maturity of the existing debt, creditors are exchanging their existing debt for a completely new instrument. This might be a new bond with a longer maturity and a lower coupon. It might be a mix of cash, new debt, and equity. The key point is that creditors are getting something materially different in exchange for giving up their existing claim.

I worked on one distressed exchange where a company had $300 million of bonds due in one year, and they were heading toward default because they couldn't possibly refinance that amount. So they offered bondholders the choice of accepting 70 cents in cash, or exchanging their bonds for a new bond with a longer maturity, lower coupon, and some equity kickers. About 85% of the bondholders took the exchange, because they reasoned that 70 cents in cash was going to be the recovery value in a bankruptcy anyway, so they might as well keep the upside. This bought the company three more years to fix the underlying business.

The liability management exercise, or "LME," is probably the most comprehensive out-of-court restructuring tool that's been developed in the past decade. An LME is basically a comprehensive restructuring of a company's capital structure, done without bankruptcy. The company will propose to creditors a new capital structure, usually with lower debt levels, restructured terms, and sometimes a conversion of debt to equity. The creditors vote on the proposal, and if a sufficient percentage agree (usually defined by the terms of the debt agreements), the deal happens.

What makes the LME such a powerful tool is that it can address fundamental imbalances in the capital structure without requiring a bankruptcy filing. A company that has two or three tranches of debt, all of which are at risk of not being fully recovered, can use an LME to rebalance the capital structure and reduce total debt levels. I remember one situation with a telecom company that had accumulated too much debt over several years of acquisition activity. The company proposed an LME where unsecured creditors would take a haircut of 25% and convert the remainder to new secured bonds with a longer maturity. Secured creditors would see their maturity extended but would maintain their full claim value. This resulted in total debt reduction of about 30%, which put the company back on a sustainable trajectory.

Drop-down transactions are something that become relevant when a company has a subsidiary or a line of business that might be worth more if it's maintained as a separate entity or moved to a different part of the capital structure. The idea is that the parent company "drops down" the asset to a subsidiary, usually one that's lower in the capital structure, where it's outside the reach of the parent's creditors.

I have to be honest here: drop-down transactions are probably the most controversial feature of modern leveraged finance, and they've become a focal point of creditor-on-creditor violence that I'll discuss more later. But the basic concept is straightforward. A company with significant intellectual property might drop that IP down to an unrestricted subsidiary, where it's not collateral for any of the parent company's debt. If the parent company eventually defaults, creditors recover only the operating business, not the valuable IP. The IP stays with the sponsor or with the new management, and creditors are stuck with whatever the core business is worth.

Uptiering transactions are the flip side of this. These are transactions where a company issues new senior debt, with liens that are senior to the existing liens of the incumbent lenders. I remember Serta Simmons, which was acquired by Advent partners in 2012 in a very leveraged transaction. By 2020, the company was struggling with too much debt, and Advent brought in new equity and uptiered new senior debt. This meant that the old first lien lenders' liens were pushed down, and the new money lenders had priors liens on the assets. The existing first lien lenders either had to take a haircut or sell their position, and it created a huge amount of litigation.

The Serta case in particular became a landmark because it showed the court system stepping in to limit the ability of sponsors to stack new senior debt on top of existing debt without the consent of the existing lenders. But it also showed how creative financial engineers can be in trying to preserve sponsorship control and flexibility.

The cooperation agreement is a tool that brings multiple classes of creditors together. When a company is in serious trouble and there's competing views among the creditor classes about what should happen, it's often useful to have all the creditors sign an agreement where they commit to working together. This might be a commitment to negotiate in good faith, a commitment not to accelerate debt or declare defaults unilaterally, or a commitment to work toward a restructuring rather than immediately filing for bankruptcy. These agreements are usually time-limited—maybe 60 or 90 days—and they're designed to stop the situation from deteriorating further while restructuring discussions happen.

When do out-of-court solutions work? Generally speaking, they work best when: (1) the company still has positive free cash flow or can quickly get to positive free cash flow with some restructuring; (2) the underlying business is economically viable and just needs to be deleveraged; (3) the creditor group is relatively concentrated and/or willing to cooperate; and (4) there's enough time for restructuring discussions before something forces a bankruptcy filing.

When do they fail? They fail when: (1) the company is in structural decline and can't be fixed with better financial terms; (2) the creditor group is too fragmented and you can't get enough of them to agree; (3) someone has a blocking position and is demanding something outrageous; or (4) the market situation deteriorates so quickly that there's no time for negotiation.

I remember working on a situation with a regional manufacturing company that seemed like a perfect candidate for an out-of-court restructuring. The company had good assets, reasonable management, and fundamentals that were bad but not catastrophically bad. We negotiated for about four months and had a deal that both the first lien lenders and the unsecured creditors were going to accept. But then the market shifted—oil prices collapsed, which drove down demand for their products—and suddenly the restructuring math didn't work anymore. The company ended up filing for bankruptcy about two weeks before they were supposed to sign the restructuring agreement.

THE SERTA, J. CREW, AND ENVISION PLAYBOOK: CREDITOR-ON-CREDITOR VIOLENCE

If you want to understand the modern distressed debt market and the way that sponsors try to preserve value while creditors fight for recovery, you absolutely have to understand what happened with Serta, J. Crew, and Envision. These cases more or less define the current conversation about what's acceptable in a restructuring and what's not.

Let me start with J. Crew, which filed for bankruptcy in May 2020. J. Crew had been owned by TPG and Leonard Green since 2011, when it was taken private in a leveraged buyout at about $3 billion in enterprise value. Over the subsequent nine years, the company had basically flatlined. No revenue growth, declining store productivity, and a capital structure that was too aggressive for a steady-state mature retailer. By 2020, it was clear that the company wasn't going to be a home run for the sponsors, and it was going to be a very painful exit.

But what made J. Crew famous was what happened right before the bankruptcy filing. The sponsors did what's called a "trap door transfer" of intellectual property. They moved J. Crew's brand, trademarks, and other valuable intellectual property to an unrestricted subsidiary. This subsidiary wasn't collateral for any of J. Crew's debt. So when the company filed for bankruptcy, creditors who thought they had liens on all of the company's assets discovered that they didn't—they had liens on the operating company and its stores, but not on the crown jewel, which was the intellectual property.

This was incredibly controversial. The bankruptcy court ultimately allowed it to stand, but it created a huge backlash in the distressed investing community and in leveraged finance more broadly. The question became: if sponsors can move your best assets out of the reach of creditors right before a default, does your lien actually mean anything?

The J. Crew situation also highlighted the power of the "permitted investments" basket in credit agreements. This is a section of the credit agreement that says the company can make certain kinds of investments without getting lender consent. If the permitted investments basket is written loosely—for example, if it allows investments in "affiliates for any purpose"—then a sponsor can argue that they're simply making an investment in a affiliate, which is expressly permitted. The fact that this investment happens to move valuable assets out of creditor reach is, in the sponsor's view, irrelevant.

This led to a real arms race in credit documentation. Lenders started writing much tighter permitted investments baskets, with explicit restrictions on moving intellectual property. And sponsors started getting more creative about how to structure these transactions. But J. Crew became the symbol of sponsors preserving value at the expense of creditors.

Then came Serta Simmons. Serta is the mattress manufacturer, which was acquired by Advent Partners in 2012 for about $2.3 billion in a very leveraged transaction. By 2020, the company had too much debt and was struggling. So Advent did what looked like a straightforward capital injection: they brought in new equity money and at the same time issued new senior secured debt. This new senior debt was granted first liens on the company's assets, meaning the liens were senior to the liens of the existing first lien lenders.

This immediately created a crisis. The existing first lien lenders, who thought they had first liens on everything, suddenly discovered that they had second liens. The new money lenders had priors. This was the definition of an uptiering transaction, and it was massively controversial.

The lawsuit that followed was one of the more interesting creditor litigation cases of recent years. The excluded lenders—the lenders whose liens were pushed down—argued that the uptiering violated their agreements. The court ultimately sided with them, and held that the sponsor couldn't unilaterally uptier new senior debt without the consent of the existing first lien lenders. This was a big victory for creditors and represented a real constraint on sponsor flexibility.

But even after the Serta litigation, sponsors have continued to find ways to structure transactions that push down existing creditors. It's been less brazen than Serta, but the fundamental dynamic remains: sponsors are trying to preserve value, and creditors are trying to protect themselves, and there's an ongoing dance between them about what's acceptable.

Then there's Envision Healthcare, which is a slightly different story but equally important. Envision had been put together by Ares Management and Envision Management in a complicated transaction. The company was supposed to be this dominant player in urgent care and emergency services. By 2020, COVID had created massive operational challenges, and the company was struggling.

But what made Envision important was the drop-down transactions that happened. The company had moved certain valuable business lines to unrestricted subsidiaries, where they were outside the reach of creditors. This was a less egregious version of the J. Crew situation—the assets that were dropped down weren't completely lost to creditors, but they were no longer pledged as collateral.

These three situations—J. Crew, Serta, and Envision—essentially defined the modern landscape of sponsor creativity and creditor protection. After these situations, credit agreements started including explicit anti-J. Crew provisions, anti-Serta provisions, and drop-down restrictions. Lenders became much more aggressive about fighting transactions that moved assets or liens. And sponsors became much more careful about how they structured these transactions.

The key lesson is this: in modern leveraged finance, the legal documents matter enormously. A credit agreement that's well-drafted will protect creditors from most of these strategies. But a credit agreement that's loosely drafted or that has broad baskets and carve-outs can be used by sponsors to preserve value in ways that creditors never expected. And once you're in distress and filing for bankruptcy, it's too late to fix the documents.

CHAPTER 11 BANKRUPTCY: THE PROCESS

Let me now walk through what happens when a company actually files for bankruptcy. This is the formal process, and it's important to understand it even though, as I said at the beginning, the best restructurings happen before you get to bankruptcy court.

Filing for bankruptcy is surprisingly simple from a procedural perspective. A company files a petition with the bankruptcy court, listing its assets and liabilities, and boom, it's in bankruptcy. This can be done as a voluntary filing—the company files—or an involuntary filing, where creditors force the company into bankruptcy. Involuntary filings are rare, but they happen when creditors believe the company's management is acting against creditor interests and they want to force a controlled process.

The first thing that happens when a company files is the automatic stay. This is a court order that immediately halts all litigation and enforcement actions against the company. Creditors can't foreclose on assets, suppliers can't shut off services, and employees can't sue for unpaid wages. The automatic stay applies to everybody, which is actually a pretty important principle. The idea is that if all the creditors and other parties could all take individual actions, you'd have chaos. Instead, the automatic stay creates a temporary period of calm where the company and its creditors can work out a restructuring.

The automatic stay doesn't mean creditors are helpless. If they need something—for example, they need to get paid for ongoing services—they can ask the bankruptcy court for relief from the stay. And the court will usually grant relief if the creditor can demonstrate that they're not adequately protected. But generally speaking, the automatic stay is a powerful shield for the company, because it stops the company from being dismantled piece by piece while a restructuring is being worked out.

The first 48 hours of a bankruptcy filing are called "first day" motions. These are emergency motions that the company needs to get filed right away in order to keep the business running. These might include motions to approve DIP financing (which we'll talk about shortly), motions to approve procedures for rejecting or assuming contracts, motions to approve paying employee wages, and motions to approve paying certain suppliers for goods and services.

First day motions are usually granted pretty quickly by the bankruptcy court, because the alternative is that the company stops operating and goes out of business, which is bad for everyone. Judges understand this, and they're usually willing to move fast on these motions. By the end of the first day of a bankruptcy filing, you usually have approvals for the things the company needs to keep the lights on.

The plan process is where the actual restructuring happens. Once a company has filed for bankruptcy, it enters an "exclusivity period," which is usually 120 days (though it can be extended). During this period, only the company (or the "debtor") can propose a plan of reorganization. After the exclusivity period expires, creditors can start proposing their own plans.

The plan of reorganization is the document that sets out how the company is going to emerge from bankruptcy. It specifies: (1) how the company's assets are going to be distributed to creditors; (2) what the new capital structure is going to look like; (3) whether equity holders are getting anything or wiped out; and (4) what the treatment is for various claims.

In order for the plan to be confirmed by the bankruptcy court, it has to be accepted by creditors. This involves voting, where creditors vote on whether to accept the plan. Creditors are grouped into "classes," and each class votes separately. The first lien lenders might be one class, the second lien lenders might be another class, unsecured creditors might be a third class, and so on.

In order for the plan to be confirmed, at least one impaired class has to vote to accept it (meaning more than 50% of the dollar amount of claims in that class votes yes, and two-thirds by count), or the judge has to cram down the plan even if no class votes for it. The cram down power is important because it prevents any single class from holding the entire process hostage.

The confirmation process is where the real fight happens in bankruptcies. The company, the official creditors' committee, and various creditor groups all battle over what the reorganization should look like. The key issue is almost always: what is the enterprise value of the reorganized company, and how should that value be distributed among the creditors?

If there's significant disagreement on valuation, the bankruptcy court will hold a trial where experts present competing views on what the company is worth. This is where you get the battles between the company's financial advisors—who are incentivized to show high value so that more creditors get full recovery—and the distressed investors' advisors, who might have a view that the company is worth less because they're trying to justify a lower recovery for certain creditor classes.

The absolute priority rule is important to understand. This rule says that in a bankruptcy, creditors get paid in strict order of their seniority in the capital structure. Secured lenders get paid first, then unsecured creditors, then equity. You can't have a situation where unsecured creditors are getting equity while secured lenders are not getting full recovery. If you want to give equity to unsecured creditors, you first have to give the secured lenders everything they're owed.

This creates an interesting dynamic, because it means that the secured lenders have a certain amount of control over what the reorganized company looks like. If the reorganized company is going to have equity, and the secured lenders are only being recovered partially, then technically the absolute priority rule requires that you wipe out the equity. But in practice, what usually happens is that the parties negotiate some kind of arrangement where the secured lenders agree to less than full recovery in exchange for being able to keep the equity, or in exchange for some other benefit.

The difference between a "free-fall" filing and a "pre-pack" is important. A free-fall filing is where the company files for bankruptcy without having pre-negotiated a plan of reorganization with creditors. The company files, the exclusivity period starts, and the company has 120 days to come up with a plan. This creates a lot of uncertainty, because it's not clear what the restructuring is going to look like or how long the process will take.

A pre-pack, by contrast, is where the company and its creditors negotiate a plan before the filing happens. The company then files for bankruptcy and proposes the pre-negotiated plan to creditors. Since the plan has already been negotiated and accepted by the major creditors, it usually gets confirmed pretty quickly—maybe 30-60 days after filing, rather than six to twelve months.

Pre-packs are almost always better for the company, because they avoid the uncertainty and disruption of a free-fall filing. They also tend to result in better outcomes for creditors, because the distressed funds and other sophisticated investors have already analyzed the situation and agreed on the restructuring.

DIP FINANCING: THE KEY TO CONTROLLING THE RESTRUCTURING

Let me spend some time on DIP financing, because this is often the most powerful tool that determines how a restructuring actually plays out. DIP stands for "debtor-in-possession," and DIP financing is a loan that the company takes out after it files for bankruptcy, to finance operations while the restructuring is happening.

Why is DIP financing so important? Because most companies can't survive a six-month or one-year restructuring without additional capital. They're burning cash, and they don't have the liquidity to make it through. So someone has to provide that capital, and that someone is usually a distressed fund or a specialized lender.

DIP financing is incredibly lucrative for the lender, and it comes with extraordinary rights. A DIP lender typically gets:

First, super-priority claims. This means that if the company doesn't repay the DIP loan, the DIP lender gets paid back from the proceeds of the bankruptcy before everyone else, even before the first lien lenders. This is backed by the power of the bankruptcy court, which essentially guarantees that the DIP lender will be paid.

Second, liens on the company's assets. The DIP lender usually gets liens on the company's assets, and these liens are senior to all existing liens.

Third, fees. DIP lenders typically charge high interest rates—SOFR plus 600 to 900 basis points is not unusual—plus origination fees, upfront fees, and various other fees.

Fourth, most importantly, they get control over the restructuring. The DIP financing agreement typically requires that the company can't file a plan of reorganization without the DIP lender's consent. This gives the DIP lender veto power over the restructuring. The DIP lender can essentially say "we won't consent to any plan unless we get paid back in full," which is an incredibly powerful position to be in.

I remember one situation where a company had received a DIP commitment from a major distressed fund, and the terms included the requirement that any reorganization plan had to result in full recovery for the DIP lender and also full recovery for the first lien lenders. This immediately meant that the restructuring had to be very aggressive in terms of moving value down to second lien lenders and unsecured creditors. The DIP lender essentially controlled the outcome.

Roll-up DIPs are a variation where the DIP lender rolls up some of the company's pre-petition debt into the DIP facility. This means that existing creditors—maybe some of the first lien lenders—have their existing claims converted into DIP claims, with the same super-priority status as the new DIP money. This is useful because it aligns the existing creditors with the DIP lender's interests—they all now have super-priority claims.

The loan-to-own strategy is where a distressed fund comes in with a DIP loan that's specifically designed to enable them to buy the company at the exit of the bankruptcy. The DIP terms are written in such a way that the company can't emerge from bankruptcy without using the distressed fund's money, and the distressed fund then bids for the company in the auction process. This is incredibly controversial, because it's essentially a way for the distressed fund to ensure that they get to own the company, and creditors feel like they've been locked out of the process.

The impact of DIP financing on the restructuring outcome cannot be overstated. The DIP lender is essentially funding the restructuring, which gives them enormous leverage. They can dictate terms to other creditors because those other creditors know that without the DIP lender's money, there's no restructuring at all. I've seen restructurings where the DIP lender got 80% recovery while other creditors got 30%, simply because the DIP lender had a controlling position.

VALUATION IN DISTRESS: THE CENTRAL BATTLEGROUND

In my two decades of working with distressed credits, I've learned that valuation is the single most important issue in any distressed situation. It's not really about the law, or the process, or the mechanics. It's about what the company is worth. Because once you know what the company is worth, the math of the restructuring basically tells itself. You know how much money is available to distribute to creditors, and then it's just a question of distributing it down the capital structure.

This is why valuation fights are so intense in bankruptcies. The company is trying to show that it's worth as much as possible, so that more creditors can get full recovery. The secured lenders are trying to show that it's worth as much as possible, so that they can get full recovery. The distressed investors are trying to show that it's worth as little as possible, so that they can buy the company at a lower recovery for other creditors and capture more upside.

The tools for valuation are the same in distress as they are in normal transactions: DCF analysis, comparable company analysis, and liquidation analysis. But the assumptions you plug into these models are very different in distress.

Let me start with DCF analysis in distress. In normal leveraged finance, you build a DCF by making an assumption about revenue growth, EBITDA margins, capital expenditures, working capital needs, and terminal growth rate. You get a number, and that's the enterprise value.

In distress, the challenge is that historical performance is usually not a good guide to future performance. The company has been deteriorating, so maybe revenues are going down and margins are being compressed. The question becomes: what happens if the company is restructured and gets new management? Does it recover? Does it stabilize? Does it continue to decline?

This is where judgment comes in, and where different analysts will come to very different conclusions. A turnaround specialist who believes the company can fix its operations might build a model where revenues stabilize in two years and EBITDA margins recover to historical levels. A pessimist who believes the company is in structural decline might build a model where revenues continue to decline for the next five years and margins compress further.

I worked on a situation with a restaurant group during the great financial crisis, where the valuation became a huge battleground. The company's revenues had declined from $500 million in 2006 to about $350 million in 2009, and margins had been compressed from 15% EBITDA margins to about 8%. The company needed to be restructured.

The first lien lenders hired a turnaround advisor who built a model showing that with new management and aggressive cost-cutting, the company could stabilize revenues at $350 million and recover margins to 12% within three years. Under this model, the enterprise value came out to about $250 million (8.5x on normalized EBITDA). Given that the first lien debt was $280 million, this meant the first lien lenders would get about 89 cents on the dollar.

But the distressed investor who had accumulated some of the second lien debt hired their own advisor, who built a model showing that the company was in structural decline and would never recover. Revenues would continue to decline to $300 million by year three, and EBITDA margins would remain at 8%. Under this pessimistic model, enterprise value came out to about $180 million (6.0x on normalized EBITDA). Now the first lien lenders were looking at about 64 cents on the dollar.

Obviously, these two models told completely different stories about what would happen in the restructuring. The first model implied that second lien creditors would get something and maybe equity would be worth something. The second model implied that second lien creditors would be wiped out and equity would get nothing.

The bankruptcy court ultimately sided with the turnaround advisor's analysis, reasoning that if the company had experienced new management and aggressive cost control, it could stabilize its business. The company emerged from bankruptcy with the first lien lenders getting about 90 cents and second lien creditors getting some equity. Three years later, the company had indeed recovered to something close to what the turnaround advisor had predicted.

Comparable company analysis is another tool, but it's tricky in distress. The problem is that you're usually comparing a distressed company to non-distressed companies trading in the market. A company trading in the par market at 10x EBITDA is fundamentally different from a company in bankruptcy that's at risk of failing. So the multiples you use have to be adjusted downward to account for the specific risks that the distressed company faces.

I usually build a range. Non-distressed companies in the space might be trading at 10-12x EBITDA. Distressed companies that are expected to survive might trade at 6-8x EBITDA. Companies that are expected to fail might trade at 3-5x EBITDA or lower. The company you're trying to value falls somewhere in this range, depending on how likely you think they are to recover.

Liquidation analysis is the floor in any valuation. If you liquidate the company's assets, how much can you get? For a manufacturing company, fixed assets might liquidate for 40-50% of book value, depending on how specialized they are. Working capital might liquidate for 75-80% of book value. Intellectual property might liquidate for 10-20% of its going-concern value. Once you add all this up, you get a liquidation value, which represents the floor of what creditors can expect.

One of the most powerful tools for thinking about distressed valuations is the "fulcrum security" concept. This is the tranche of debt or claim that's most sensitive to changes in enterprise value. If enterprise value is slightly higher than expected, the fulcrum security gets a lot of value. If enterprise value is slightly lower than expected, the fulcrum security gets almost nothing. The fulcrum security is the lowest-priority claim that's expected to get paid in full in a "base case" scenario.

Why is the fulcrum security important? Because controlling the fulcrum security gives you control over the restructuring. If you own the fulcrum security and you're confident in your enterprise value analysis, you can essentially dictate what happens in the restructuring. This is the core insight of distressed investing.

Let me walk through a worked example to make this concrete. Imagine a company with the following capital structure:

First lien debt: $200 million
Second lien debt: $100 million
Unsecured debt: $150 million
Total debt: $450 million

Now imagine that the company's enterprise value in a restructuring is expected to be somewhere in the range of $400 million to $500 million. Here's what the waterfall looks like at different enterprise values:

At $500 million enterprise value:
First lien gets: $200 million (100% recovery)
Second lien gets: $100 million (100% recovery)
Unsecured gets: $150 million (100% recovery)
Equity gets: $50 million

At $450 million enterprise value:
First lien gets: $200 million (100% recovery)
Second lien gets: $100 million (100% recovery)
Unsecured gets: $150 million (100% recovery)
Equity gets: $0 million

At $400 million enterprise value:
First lien gets: $200 million (100% recovery)
Second lien gets: $100 million (100% recovery)
Unsecured gets: $100 million (67% recovery)
Equity gets: $0 million

At $350 million enterprise value:
First lien gets: $200 million (100% recovery)
Second lien gets: $100 million (100% recovery)
Unsecured gets: $50 million (33% recovery)
Equity gets: $0 million

At $300 million enterprise value:
First lien gets: $200 million (100% recovery)
Second lien gets: $100 million (100% recovery)
Unsecured gets: $0 million (0% recovery)
Equity gets: $0 million

In this example, the unsecured debt is the fulcrum security. At enterprise values above $350 million, unsecured creditors are getting paid. At enterprise values below $350 million, they're not. They're the most sensitive tranche to changes in enterprise value.

This analysis is incredibly powerful, because it tells you exactly where the fight is going to be. If you own unsecured debt in this company, your focus should be on accumulating enough unsecured debt to have some control over the restructuring process. You want to be able to influence the valuation assumptions, because small changes in enterprise value translate into big changes in your recovery.

If you own second lien debt, you don't care as much about the exact enterprise value as long as it's above $300 million. You know you're going to get 100% recovery. Your focus should be on making sure the company doesn't liquidate and the enterprise value doesn't fall below $300 million. And you want to make sure that the first lien lenders don't get to grab any value that should rightfully be yours.

If you're an equity investor, you don't have much influence at all unless you think there's a possibility that enterprise value will be above $450 million, which is what it would take for equity to get anything.

CREDITOR STRATEGIES IN RESTRUCTURING

Understanding the incentives of different creditor classes is essential to understanding how restructurings play out. Different creditors have different priorities and different leverage, and the restructuring outcome reflects how successfully these different groups can negotiate with each other.

The cooperative strategy is where creditors decide to work together. This happens when the creditors agree that the best outcome for all of them is to cooperate and negotiate a restructuring rather than fight it out in bankruptcy. The advantage of cooperation is that it can result in a faster resolution, lower professional fees, and generally a better outcome for everyone because the business isn't getting disrupted by the uncertainty of bankruptcy.

In cooperative scenarios, creditors often form what's called an "ad hoc group" where representatives from different creditor classes meet to negotiate. The first lien lenders might have one view on what should happen, the second lien lenders might have another view, and unsecured creditors might have a third view. But if they can find common ground, they can sometimes negotiate a deal that all three groups can live with.

The aggressive strategy is the opposite. This is where creditors believe that their interests are fundamentally incompatible with other creditors' interests, and they decide to fight for their interests rather than cooperate. In aggressive scenarios, creditors accumulate blocking positions—they buy enough of a particular tranche of debt to have veto power over any restructuring that doesn't protect their interests.

I worked on one situation where a second lien lender accumulated enough of the unsecured debt to have a blocking position, and then used that position to negotiate a deal where they would be protected at the expense of the unsecured creditors who hadn't been smart about their positioning. The second lien lender essentially said, "The only plan I'm going to vote for is one where I get full recovery and the other unsecured creditors take a haircut." Since they had a blocking position, they could enforce this.

The official creditors' committee is another important player. In a Chapter 11 bankruptcy, the court appoints an official committee of unsecured creditors to represent the interests of all unsecured creditors. The committee usually has representatives from the largest unsecured creditors—maybe a large trade creditor, a large bondholder, and a couple of smaller creditors. The committee can hire advisors, review the company's operations, and propose alternatives to the company's plan.

The steering committee is a different animal. This is usually a committee formed by the largest creditors—often first lien lenders, second lien lenders, and some of the larger unsecured creditors—who meet regularly to discuss the restructuring. The steering committee doesn't have any formal legal power, but it's usually where the real negotiations happen. The company will present options to the steering committee, the creditors will fight it out, and eventually a consensus emerges about what should happen.

Inter-creditor warfare is common, and it reflects the fundamental conflict of interest that exists between different creditors. The first lien lenders want to maximize their recovery, which means they want enterprise value allocated to them first. They don't care much about second lien lenders or unsecured creditors. The second lien lenders want to maximize their recovery, which means they want enough enterprise value allocated to them after the first lien lenders are paid. And unsecured creditors want whatever they can get after everyone senior to them is paid.

This conflict gets even more intense when there are multiple tranches of debt with overlapping claims on the same assets. One of the most important fights in modern restructuring is over "make-whole" and "no-call" provisions. These are provisions in bond indentures that say the issuer has to pay bondholders a specified amount even if the bonds are prepaid or the company goes through a change of control.

These provisions are hugely valuable to bondholders in normal times—they provide price protection—but they become incredibly contentious in restructurings. A make-whole provision might say that if the company defaults or goes through a change of control, the bondholder is entitled to the greater of par or the present value of all future cash flows at a specified spread. In a restructuring, if the company wants to refinance or restructure the bonds, the company might have to pay substantial make-whole premiums.

I've seen situations where make-whole provisions ate up 10-20% of the enterprise value. This creates huge tension, because the company and the other creditors are arguing that the make-whole provisions are too generous and shouldn't be honored in their entirety. But the bondholders are arguing that the make-whole provisions are part of their contract and should be honored.

One real example of creditor strategy is what happened with the 2008 auto bankruptcies. When General Motors and Chrysler went through bankruptcy, there was intense negotiation about how much the unsecured creditors would have to give up. The United Auto Workers union had a significant claim as unsecured creditors, and they negotiated aggressively to preserve value. Meanwhile, the secured lenders—mostly the auto finance companies—knew they were going to get full recovery and weren't too worried about the details. The outcome reflected the UAW's negotiating strength and willingness to fight for their interests.

THE ADLER GROUP CASE STUDY: RESTRUCTURING ACROSS BORDERS

One of the most fascinating distressed situations I've been involved in was following the workout and eventual restructuring of a major German real estate developer. While I can't name it for confidentiality reasons, the situation illustrates a lot of important lessons about how distressed situations play out in different jurisdictions.

This company—let's call it Adler Group—had borrowed heavily to acquire real estate portfolios across Germany. The company had multiple tranches of debt—some bank debt, some bonds issued into the market, some structured financing arrangements. The company also had operations in multiple countries, which added significant complexity.

What made the situation interesting was that Adler Group was using what's called a "Scheme of Arrangement" in the United Kingdom, and also dealing with German restructuring law through the StaRUG process (Strukturbereinigungsgesetz), which is Germany's new restructuring law that came into effect in 2021.

The capital structure complexity was extreme. The first lien debt was held by a mix of German banks and international financial institutions. There were bonds issued to public investors. There was mezzanine debt from financial sponsors. And there were trade payables and other operational liabilities.

The intercreditor dynamics were particularly intense because the different creditors had completely different views on what should happen. The secured lenders thought the real estate portfolio was worth enough to result in significant recovery for them. The distressed investors who had accumulated junior debt thought the portfolio was worth much less. The sponsors wanted to preserve their equity investment and bring in new capital to stabilize the situation. The trade creditors just wanted to get paid.

What was particularly interesting about the Adler Group situation was how the cross-border structure affected the outcome. The company had the option to file under German law (which would have used the StaRUG process) or to use the UK Scheme of Arrangement route. Using the Scheme of Arrangement meant that a supermajority of creditors could agree to a restructuring that would bind all creditors, even those who voted no. Using the German process meant more traditional debtor-friendly bankruptcy procedures.

The company ultimately chose the Scheme of Arrangement route, which meant the restructuring could be imposed on dissenting creditors if enough of them agreed. This is a huge advantage compared to the US Chapter 11 system, where you need separate votes from each class of creditors.

The lessons from Adler Group are multiple. First, the jurisdiction matters enormously. A company that's operating across multiple countries can often choose which country's restructuring law to use, and that choice will have huge implications for how the restructuring plays out. Second, leverage in restructuring comes from creditor coordination and blocking positions. The distressed investors who had accumulated positions could dictate terms. Third, real estate values are crucial in determining outcomes—disputes about valuation of the real estate portfolios drove huge wedges between creditor groups.

EUROPEAN RESTRUCTURING FRAMEWORKS

Since I've been discussing the Adler Group and cross-border restructurings, let me spend a moment on the different restructuring frameworks available in Europe. For many years, London was the center of the restructuring universe because of the flexibility of the UK Scheme of Arrangement. But this has been changing with the introduction of new restructuring procedures.

The English Scheme of Arrangement, under Part 26 of the Companies House Act 2006, allows a company to propose a restructuring of its capital structure that will bind creditors if approved by a supermajority. The advantage of the Scheme is that you don't need to file for formal insolvency—the company can use a Scheme even if it's not yet technically insolvent. And you don't need the individual consent of each creditor class—once the creditors vote and the court approves, the Scheme is binding on all creditors, including dissenters.

Part 26A, which was introduced more recently, streamlined the Scheme process even further and is designed for small companies or companies undergoing small restructurings. These are powerful tools, and they're one of the reasons London has been so attractive for restructuring for so long.

The EU Restructuring Directive, which came into effect in 2021, introduced a new early restructuring framework that many member states have implemented. The framework allows a company that's not yet formally insolvent to propose a restructuring to its creditors. The theory is that allowing restructuring before formal insolvency happens will preserve more value by avoiding the disruption of a full bankruptcy.

Germany's StaRUG, which I mentioned earlier, is an example of this new framework. The StaRUG allows a company to propose a restructuring plan that can bind creditors if approved by a supermajority. It's based on the US Chapter 11 system but with a more streamlined process.

The Dutch WHOA (Wettelijke Homologatie OndernemingsOmleidingsplan voor Schuldenaren) is another version of this framework. It allows a company to propose a restructuring plan that can be approved by creditors and crammed down if necessary.

What's interesting about these new frameworks is that they're trying to create a middle ground between the US Chapter 11 system (which is very debtor-friendly and allows the company to propose a plan that can cram down creditors) and the traditional insolvency frameworks (which are more creditor-protective and give creditors more power).

How do these European frameworks compare to Chapter 11? In some ways, they're more creditor-protective. The threshold for approval is typically higher—maybe 75% of creditors rather than 50% plus one class. In some ways, they're more flexible. The company doesn't have to file for formal insolvency; it can restructure while still operating normally.

But the key difference is that in all of these frameworks, once you have creditor approval, the restructuring is binding on all creditors. This is where the US system is different. In the US, you can cram down an unwilling class of creditors, but the absolute priority rule has to be satisfied. In the European systems, you might be able to cram down a plan even if the absolute priority rule isn't satisfied, depending on how the procedure is structured.

Cross-border jurisdiction shopping is a real phenomenon. A company operating across multiple countries can often choose which country's law to use for the restructuring. This creates a race to the bottom, where the "best" jurisdiction for a company to file in is the one that's most favorable to the company's existing management and sponsors.

For many years, London was the place everyone wanted to file because of the flexibility of the Scheme of Arrangement. But with the rise of the new European frameworks, the jurisdiction landscape is shifting. Some companies are now choosing to file under German law (StaRUG) or Dutch law (WHOA) because these procedures might be more streamlined or more favorable in particular situations.

DISTRESSED INVESTING RETURNS AND STRATEGIES

Now let me shift gears and talk about the actual returns that distressed investors can achieve and the strategies they use to generate those returns.

Historical returns of distressed debt strategies have been quite attractive. During the 2008-2009 financial crisis, distressed debt investors who had capital available to deploy and were willing to buy at very low prices generated returns of 50-100%+ by buying debt at 30-50 cents on the dollar and having that debt recover to 80-100 cents. But it's important to note that these extraordinary returns happened during a once-in-a-decade crisis when prices were at historic lows.

In more normal times, distressed debt strategies generate returns in the 12-20% range, which is still very attractive compared to investment-grade credit. But it's important to understand that these returns come with significant volatility and active risk—you're not just buying and holding; you're actively participating in the restructuring process.

Fulcrum investing is the core strategy that most sophisticated distressed investors use. The idea is to identify the fulcrum security, buy it at a discount to what you think it's worth based on your valuation analysis, and then either hold it through the restructuring to capture the recovery or actively participate in the restructuring to influence the outcome in your favor.

The power of fulcrum investing is that you're getting asymmetric risk-reward. If the company recovers better than expected, your position is valuable. If the company recovers worse than expected, you have some protection because you're in a priority position relative to equity. The fulcrum security is the best risk-reward at any given point in time, which is why sophisticated distressed investors spend so much time and energy trying to identify it.

Loan-to-own strategies are more aggressive. The idea here is that a distressed fund comes in with financing that allows the company to continue operating, and on the exit of the restructuring, the distressed fund gets to own the company. This is controversial because it essentially locks other creditors out of the process, but it can be incredibly profitable for the distressed fund if they can buy the company at a reasonable valuation and operate it successfully.

Liquidation plays are where you buy a position that you think is worth more in liquidation than it's trading for in the market. This might be a company with valuable assets that are being valued at distressed multiples. You buy the position and either push for a liquidation or a sale of the company, realizing the value of the assets.

The "basis trade" is a more sophisticated strategy where you buy the cash bonds or loans at a discount and simultaneously buy CDS protection on the company. The idea is that if the company recovers better than the CDS is pricing, you make money on the bonds/loans. If the company defaults worse than expected, the CDS protects you. It's a way to make money on your assessment of recovery while hedging tail risk.

Distressed-for-control strategies are where the distressed investor buys enough of a particular tranche of debt to have veto power over the restructuring. They then use that control to negotiate a restructuring that heavily favors their position. This can be incredibly profitable, but it's also controversial because it raises questions about whether the distressed investor is extracting unfair value at the expense of other creditors.

Non-control strategies are the opposite. The distressed investor buys a position in a distressed company but doesn't try to control the restructuring. They're essentially betting on their analysis of recovery and hoping the restructuring process works out in their favor. This is less profitable than control strategies but also less risky, because you're not locked in a battle with other creditors.

How do long-only fund managers handle distressed positions differently from dedicated distressed funds? Long-only managers are usually not set up to actively participate in restructurings. They're not hiring bankruptcy lawyers, they're not sitting on steering committees, they're not negotiating with other creditors. So when they accidentally get a position in a distressed company, they're either stuck holding it and hoping for recovery, or they're selling it to a distressed specialist at a discount.

This is actually one of the reasons why there can be such attractive opportunities for distressed specialists. The long-only manager doesn't have the expertise or the bandwidth to participate in the restructuring, so they're willing to sell at a discount to someone who does.

WHAT TWENTY YEARS TEACHES ABOUT DISTRESSED SITUATIONS

After two decades of working with distressed companies, I've learned some lessons that I think are broadly true and worth sharing. These aren't mathematical formulas or doctrines that you'll find in textbooks. They're more like rules of thumb that I've developed by observing how distressed situations actually play out.

The first lesson is that there's an emotional component to distressed situations that shouldn't be underestimated. When a company is heading toward distress, people's livelihoods are at stake. Management is worried about their jobs. Employees are worried about their paychecks. Creditors are worried about their investments. This emotional intensity affects how people make decisions. People do things in distressed situations that they would never do in normal situations. They hold blocking positions when it's not in their economic interest. They refuse reasonable compromises because of anger or desperation.

Understanding this emotional component is important because it means that sometimes the most rational financial outcome is not the one that actually happens. The outcome that happens is the one that reflects the political economy of the stakeholder group. So part of being good at distressed investing is understanding not just the math but also the human dynamics.

The second lesson is that speed matters more than perfection. In most distressed situations, it's better to get a restructuring done quickly, even if it's not perfect, than to wait for perfect information. This is because the longer the restructuring takes, the more value gets destroyed. Every day a company spends in bankruptcy is a day where business is disrupted, customers are at risk of leaving, and cash is being burned. So there's a strong incentive to get a restructuring done quickly.

This means that in negotiations, the party that can move the fastest often wins. If I can say "I'll vote for your plan if you give me X," and I can do it quickly while other creditors are still analyzing, I've gained leverage. Conversely, if I'm stuck in a process where we're having extensive valuation disputes and that process takes six months, I've lost leverage because other creditors have incentive to just push through their plan.

The third lesson is that restructuring outcomes are usually determined before the filing. By the time a company actually files for Chapter 11, the most important decisions have usually already been made. Is the company going to continue as a going concern, or is it going to be liquidated? Are the sponsors going to be able to hold their equity, or are they getting wiped out? Which distressed funds are going to be important players in the restructuring, and which creditors are going to be pushed to the sidelines? Usually, these questions are answered in the weeks or months before the filing, not in the bankruptcy process itself.

This is why pre-negotiation is so important. The company and the major creditors are going to negotiate, and the outcome of that negotiation is going to determine what the bankruptcy looks like. If a company can negotiate a supportive letter from the first lien lenders before filing, it's in a much stronger position than if it files without having worked with the lenders first.

The fourth lesson is that the patterns repeat across every cycle. I've watched distressed situations play out in the 2002-2004 period, the 2008-2009 financial crisis, the 2015-2016 energy downturn, the 2020 COVID crisis, and the 2022-2023 period. And the patterns are remarkably consistent. Companies that borrowed aggressively and borrowed short got into trouble when the market turned. Sponsors that loaded companies up with too much leverage got hurt. Creditors that diversified across multiple names and wasn't over-exposed to any single credit fared much better than those that weren't.

This suggests that building skill in distressed situations is less about memorizing the unique features of any particular crisis and more about understanding the generic patterns that repeat across crises. The companies that get into trouble are typically those that borrowed aggressively when times were good and don't have the flexibility to adjust when times get tough. The capital structures that cause the most pain are those where multiple creditor classes all have expectations of getting paid that can't be satisfied at the same time. The restructurings that work are those where there's sufficient consensus among the major creditors about what needs to happen.

The fifth lesson is that building a career in distressed investing requires specific skills and temperament. The skills are obvious: you need to understand financial analysis, you need to understand bankruptcy law (or at least work closely with bankruptcy lawyers who do), and you need to understand credit analysis and how to identify undervalued opportunities.

But the temperament is equally important. Distressed investing can be stressful and contentious. You're dealing with situations where people are angry, where large amounts of money are at stake, and where there's genuine conflict between different stakeholders. You need to be able to stay calm in these situations. You need to be able to handle the complexity without getting overwhelmed. You need to be willing to make a decision based on incomplete information, knowing that you might be wrong. And you need to be able to live with losing positions, while still maintaining the discipline to avoid big catastrophic losses.

I've seen brilliant financial analysts who couldn't handle the emotional and interpersonal demands of distressed investing. And I've seen people with less technical skill who succeeded because they had the right temperament and could navigate the human dynamics effectively.

CONCLUSION

Working in distressed debt and special situations has been, without question, the most rewarding part of my career in leveraged finance. I've made more money in distressed situations than in any other area. I've learned more about how companies actually work, about negotiation, about human nature, and about financial markets than I ever would have learned staying in the normal leveraged finance world. And I've had the chance to participate in some incredible restructurings where I was genuinely helping solve very difficult problems.

The distressed debt market is where leveraged finance reveals itself most completely. All the assumptions we make about companies being able to access capital, about markets providing liquidity, about structured finance working as designed—all of that goes away in distress. What you're left with is economic reality. A company either generates enough cash to pay its creditors, or it doesn't. A capital structure either makes sense, or it doesn't. Creditors either cooperate, or they fight.

Understanding distressed situations makes you a better credit analyst, a better negotiator, and a better investor, even if you never intend to specialize in distressed debt. Because the way you think about what could go wrong, the way you analyze risks, the way you negotiate—all of that becomes better when you understand how situations actually deteriorate and how they're resolved.

The distressed debt market has also become more important in leveraged finance over the past decade. With more debt in the system, with more competition for returns, and with the cost of capital being so low for so long, there have been fewer and fewer true distressed opportunities. But when they come, they're often even more valuable than they were before, because there's been less practice and people are often caught unprepared. This suggests that distressed skills will continue to be in high demand and highly rewarded.

For anyone building a career in leveraged finance, I'd strongly encourage spending some time understanding distressed situations. It will make you a better analyst, a better investor, and a more complete financier.
