CHAPTER 8: TAX CONSIDERATIONS IN LEVERAGED FINANCE

When people ask me what has changed most fundamentally in leveraged finance over my twenty-plus years in this business, I don't hesitate: the tax landscape. Early in my career, in the late 1990s and early 2000s, tax optimization was almost an afterthought—a elegant flourish to an otherwise straightforward LBO structure. Today, tax considerations drive the entire architecture of a transaction. In some cases, they can mean the difference between a deal that creates genuine returns and one that merely shuffles cash across borders. This chapter reflects that evolution, drawing from hundreds of transactions I've worked on across Europe and beyond, alongside the mistakes I've witnessed—and sometimes made myself.

The fundamental challenge in leveraged finance taxation is this: the very mechanism that makes private equity attractive—the use of leverage to amplify returns—sits at the intersection of multiple tax regimes. Each jurisdiction wants its bite. Regulators worry about base erosion. Sponsors want certainty. Target companies need to service debt. And somewhere in the middle, tax advisors like myself must find the path that's aggressive enough to save substantial cash, yet conservative enough to survive audit. It's a perpetual negotiation between greed and prudence.

Let me start with the most consequential development of the past fifteen years: the assault on interest deductibility.

INTEREST DEDUCTIBILITY LIMITS: THE DEATH OF SIMPLE LEVERAGE

When I began my career, you could load a company with debt and deduct virtually every penny of interest expense. The economic rationale seemed sensible: debt is a legitimate financing cost, and deductibility of financing costs is standard. But in the post-2008 era, particularly after the 2012 OECD BEPS initiative and its evolution, regulators globally decided that interest deductibility had become a primary tool for profit shifting. The narrative changed from "fair treatment of debt financing" to "multinational corporations are hollowing out tax bases through excessive debt push-downs."

The European Union's Anti-Tax Avoidance Directive (ATAD), which came into force in 2019, was the watershed moment for me and my peers across Europe. I remember sitting in our Brussels office in early 2018 when our tax group first explained the implications. The ATAD introduced a fundamental challenge to the way we had structured every substantial LBO for nearly two decades.

The core ATAD provision—and this is crucial—limits net interest deductions to 30 percent of the group's tax EBITDA. Not of individual company EBITDA. Not of interest paid. Of group-wide, consolidated tax EBITDA. This creates an entirely new calculation layer. For a leveraged company earning €100 million in tax EBITDA, with the group carrying €200 million in net interest expense, only €30 million of that interest can typically be deducted. The other €170 million gets carried forward indefinitely—but it carries forward, not backward, and interest carryforwards can only be used in future periods.

What does this mean in practice? Consider a mid-market LBO I worked on in 2019—a €500 million acquisition of a Belgian industrial company. The traditional pre-ATAD structure would have been straightforward: push acquisition debt down into the target via intra-group loans, load the company with 4.5-5x leverage, and let the tax shield from interest deductions fund much of the interest expense. Under that framework, we'd deduct €80-90 million in annual interest from taxable income, saving roughly €25-28 million annually in tax (at ~32-35% blended rates). The post-ATAD world? Completely different.

The Belgium operations alone earned €150 million in EBITDA. The group EBITDA was €180 million (including some international sources). At 30 percent of €180 million, the net interest deduction was capped at €54 million. The company's actual interest expense was €75 million. That €21 million excess got carried forward, but provided no current-year relief. The after-tax cost of debt immediately rose from roughly 3.5 percent (after tax shield) to something closer to 4.8 percent. This might seem small, but across a leveraged portfolio, it reshapes returns.

What fascinated me about the ATAD—and this is where tax policy becomes almost interesting—is the "escape clause." If net interest expense is below €3 million in absolute terms, the limitation doesn't apply. This created a flood of transactions between €3 million and €5 million of net interest expense designed to stay just under the threshold. But more importantly, the ATAD includes equity add-backs in some EU states and allows carryforwards. The directive itself says member states can implement more or less restrictive versions within parameters. This created an unintended benefit for sophisticated sponsors: jurisdiction shopping became more valuable than ever. A company that could generate €180 million EBITDA in a low-tax EU state could deduct up to €54 million, whereas the same company in a standalone jurisdiction might hit the limitation at €30 million. Suddenly, the location of earnings became as important as the amount of debt.

The UK's approach has been more nuanced, which is typical of British tax policy. Pre-COVID, the UK had adopted a "fixed ratio rule"—essentially similar to ATAD—but with key differences. The UK version allowed groups to claim an "equity escape" if net interest expense was higher than 30 percent of tax EBITDA but lower than the group's net interest/EBITDA ratio at the holding company level. This was genuinely brilliant from a design perspective, because it protected existing groups from being immediately caught by more restrictive rules than they'd previously faced. I worked on several post-2019 restructures for UK groups to optimize under this framework, timing the transition to maximize the exemption period. But that's being phased out. The UK is moving toward Pillar Two minimum taxation, which I'll address later, and the old fixed ratio rules are being quietly buried.

The United States never adopted anything quite like ATAD at the federal level. Instead, Congress in 2017 simply passed Section 163(j), limiting net business interest deductions to 30 percent of adjusted taxable income (ATI). American tax policy being what it is, ATI is more complex than EBITDA—it includes depreciation and amortization adjustments—but the intent is similar. The practical impact, though, is somewhat different. Section 163(j) applies to businesses with more than $25 million in gross receipts, so mid-market and larger LBOs feel it. But the U.S. has separate small business exceptions, and for pass-through entities (partnerships, S-corps), there's the Section 163(j) "excess business interest" limitation, which is a different rule entirely. I've spent countless hours in analysis of whether a transaction should be structured as a C-corporation or a partnership precisely because of this divergence. A pass-through structure might preserve losses in a way that a C-corp doesn't, allowing interest deductions to shelter non-interest income from other sources.

What's happened in practice with these rules—and this is where my practitioner perspective matters—is that they've compressed leverage multiples globally. Pre-2015, a well-structured European LBO at the mid-market level (€200-500 million EBITDA) could sustain 5-5.5x leverage comfortably. Post-ATAD, 4.5x is more realistic for fully underwritten deals. In the US, similar compression occurred. This has profound implications for sponsor returns. If you're modeling a leveraged IRR on a 20% exit multiple change, and you're forced to reduce leverage from 5.0x to 4.5x, your equity return drops by roughly 5-7 percentage points before any operational improvements. That's not trivial.

But here's where it gets interesting from a structuring perspective. The rules apply to net interest expense. This creates an incentive to minimize interest payments at the operating company level—to push interest up through the capital structure rather than concentrating it where tax rates are highest. It also creates an incentive to maximize EBITDA, which means capitalizing more costs as intangible assets or fixed assets rather than expensing them. Some sponsors have become almost maniacal about aggressive depreciation and amortization schedules, capitalizing costs that traditionally were expensed. Whether these survive audit is another question.

TAX-EFFICIENT LBO STRUCTURING: THE ART OF THE LEGALLY AGGRESSIVE

Let me be direct: tax-efficient structuring isn't about finding loopholes. It's about understanding that every tax rule has boundaries, and within those boundaries, there's enormous legitimate variation. A tax-efficient structure and a tax-avoidant structure often look identical on paper. The difference is in documentation, substance, commercial rationale, and defensibility.

I'll illustrate with a structure I've used dozens of times: the multi-layer holding company approach, which remains one of the most powerful tools for managing tax incidence across a leveraged group.

A typical mid-sized LBO might look like this at inception: Newco (acquisition vehicle) acquires Target for €500 million. Newco is capitalized with €200 million in equity from the sponsor and €300 million in debt—a 3:1 leverage multiple. The debt is raised from a combination of bank lenders and institutional investors. Straightforward, right?

Now, let's add tax efficiency. Instead of Newco directly owning Target, we interpose a holding company structure. Newco (no longer the operating entity, now the investment vehicle) owns 99% of Holdco 1. Holdco 1 owns 100% of Holdco 2. Holdco 2 owns 100% of the operating Target. The debt is incurred at the level of Newco or Holdco 1, and is pushed down through the structure via intra-group loans.

Why does this matter? Multiple reasons. First, it creates flexibility in the eventual exit. If in year 5 the sponsor wants to recapitalize, refinance, or execute a partial exit, the multi-layer structure allows substantial reorganizations without unwinding the underlying operating company. Tax neutrality in restructurings is valuable. Second, and more immediately important, it creates optimization opportunities for how interest expense is allocated across entities in different jurisdictions. If Holdco 1 is in Luxembourg and Holdco 2 is in the Netherlands, the interest expense deductibility in each jurisdiction depends on local rules. A multi-layer structure allows you to design the interest allocation to maximize overall group deductions within those rules.

But before I go further, let me address the elephant in the room: aren't these structures precisely what regulators are attacking? The answer is nuanced. The structures themselves aren't illegal or even aggressive in many cases. The problem arises when the structure is "aggressive" with respect to transfer pricing or intercompany interest rates, or when it seems designed purely for tax avoidance with no commercial rationale.

This is where substance-over-form doctrine comes in. I'll never forget a discussion with a UK tax inspector in 2012—a remarkably thoughtful woman named Sarah—who said something I've never forgotten: "We don't care if you structure for tax efficiency. We care whether the structure exists independent of tax. If you removed the tax benefit, would this structure still make sense?" That insight has shaped my approach ever since. When I recommend a multi-layer holding company structure, I'm simultaneously thinking about the non-tax commercial rationale. And genuinely, there is one: multi-layer structures do provide operational flexibility, allow for cleaner exits, and enable separation of risk between levels.

The key to defending these structures is documentation. I cannot overstate this. The difference between a structure that survives audit and one that gets demolished is often nothing more than a board resolution saying, "We are implementing a holding company structure to provide operational flexibility in managing the group and to optimize capital structure management." That simple statement becomes the foundation for arguing substance. Without it, you're trying to defend a pure tax play.

Let me move to the specific European structures that have been the backbone of LBO tax planning for decades.

LUXEMBOURG HOLDING COMPANIES: THE ORIGINAL STRUCTURE AND ITS EVOLUTION

Luxembourg deserves its own section because it has been, for thirty years, the holding company jurisdiction of choice in European leverage finance. Understanding why, and what has changed, is crucial to understanding modern LBO structuring.

The original appeal of Luxembourg was almost embarrassingly simple: it taxed the profits of holding companies at corporate rates (about 29-30% combined national and municipal tax), which was fine, but through various mechanisms (intellectual property boxes, the imputation system, and plain old deductions for financing costs), it was possible to engineer structures where an intermediate holding company paid minimal tax while still being a legitimate, functional entity in the group.

One classic structure, which I've seen hundreds of times, works like this: A pan-European acquisition is made through a Luxembourg Société Anonyme (S.A.). The S.A. is capitalized with equity from the sponsor and debt from the financing markets. The S.A. then acquires operating subsidiaries in Germany, France, the UK, Italy, and so on. These operating subs pay dividends to the Luxembourg parent. Under Luxembourg law, and more importantly under European Union Parent-Subsidiary Directive, dividends received by a Luxembourg parent from its EU operating subsidiaries were (and are) entirely exempt from taxation. Similarly, if the Luxembourg parent sells an equity stake in an operating subsidiary to another group entity or realizes a gain, it could use the "substantial interest exemption" to avoid tax on those gains.

The power of this structure was that it allowed you to house leverage at the top level, where you could deduct unlimited interest expense against dividends flowing up from operating companies. The operating companies in higher-tax jurisdictions (Germany at 30%, France at 28%, etc.) would be relieved of leverage through intercompany loans, allowing their profits to be distributed upward to the tax-efficient holding company, where those profits would be immediately passed to creditors as interest deductions. It was elegant, legal, and ultimately extremely effective.

But—and this is critical to my narrative—the structure relied on specific aspects of EU law and Luxembourg tax law that have been substantially eroded. First, the IP box regime (which I'll discuss more) has been tightened. Second, and more importantly, the OECD BEPS initiative and subsequent EU legislation (particularly the Interest Limitation Directive) have constrained how aggressively interest can be deducted, and the EU's Anti-Tax Avoidance Directive specifically targets interest deduction limitations at the group level rather than the entity level.

Here's what I mean in practice. A transaction we worked on in 2007—a €1.2 billion pan-European industrial company acquisition—was structured with a Luxembourg holding company carrying €700 million in debt. The operating company in Germany, despite significant EBITDA, carried essentially no debt because all leverage was at the holding company level, with intercompany loans pushing the cost of capital down. The interest deduction shield at the holding company level protected the group from significant taxation on the operating profits flowing up as intercompany reimbursements. The effective tax rate on the entire group was approximately 18-19%.

A comparable transaction in 2021 couldn't achieve that structure. Even using Luxembourg, the same €1.2 billion transaction couldn't load nearly as much leverage at a top-level holding company because: (a) the ATAD interest limitation rules apply globally to the group, capping the deduction; (b) the Luxembourg parent would have to satisfy transfer pricing rules requiring intercompany interest rates to be "at arm's length," and post-BEPS, arm's-length rates are scrutinized more carefully; (c) most importantly, the substance-over-form doctrine and GAAR provisions in the ATAD explicitly target structures designed primarily for tax avoidance.

What's happened to Luxembourg structuring, then? It hasn't disappeared—it can't, because it genuinely does serve functions beyond tax avoidance. But it's been constrained. Modern Luxembourg holding companies in LBO structures are more likely to be used for: (1) cash pooling and treasury management in multicurrency groups; (2) intellectual property holding and licensing structures (though the IP box has become less generous); (3) exit optionality and refinancing flexibility; and (4) coordinating financing activities across an EU group in a way that's tax-neutral rather than tax-minimizing.

The big question I get asked constantly is: Is Luxembourg dead as a structuring jurisdiction? The answer is no, but it requires more defensibility and less reliance on pure tax arbitrage than before. You can still use Luxembourg, but you need a genuine business purpose. That's actually made our job harder in some ways—more creative, certainly, but harder to justify.

THE NETHERLANDS: HOLDCOS, COOPERATIVES, AND THE DUTCH TRADITION

If Luxembourg is the European holding company jurisdiction of tradition, the Netherlands is the jurisdiction of innovation—and of recent controversy.

The Netherlands has offered sophisticated LBO structuring tools for decades, including structures that are genuinely unique. Most notably, the Dutch cooperative (coöperatief) structure, which operates under fundamentally different tax and legal rules than a standard corporate form, has been used for highly efficient internal financing and dividend distribution.

Let me explain how this works, because it's a beautiful example of using form to achieve tax substance. A Dutch cooperative is a legal entity under Dutch law, like a corporation, but with different governance rights. Historically, Dutch tax law treated dividends paid to members of a cooperative very favorably. The combination of a standard operating structure with a cooperative subsidiary allowed dividend income to flow through the cooperative on a favorable tax basis, with the cooperative able to take deductions for certain distributions to members that a standard corporation couldn't.

I worked on a transaction in 2009 where a pan-Benelux acquisition was structured with the holding company as a Dutch cooperative, with operating subsidiaries in Belgium and Luxembourg as members of the cooperative. The structure allowed operating profits to flow through the cooperative to investors with significant tax efficiency—not quite elimination, but substantial reduction. Under modern Dutch tax rules and EU anti-avoidance rules, that specific structure would likely not be available. The Netherlands, recognizing it was under pressure from the EU and OECD, reformed these rules.

But the Netherlands remains a crucial jurisdiction for LBO structuring for more defensible reasons. Dutch law has excellent facilities for group tax consolidation (fiscal unity), which allow a Dutch holding company and its subsidiaries to form a single fiscal unity and file a consolidated tax return. This creates flexibility in allocating losses and income across entities. More importantly, the Netherlands has an expansive participation exemption—when a Dutch company holds more than 5% of another company's shares and has held them for more than a year, dividends received are exempt from taxation. This is not unique to the Netherlands, but the Dutch system is particularly well-developed.

In modern LBO structures, the Netherlands is often used as an intermediate holding company in a way that's entirely defensible. A European acquisition might be held through a Dutch cooperative or Dutch corporate entity, which owns operating subsidiaries in other jurisdictions. The Dutch entity benefits from the participation exemption on dividends and can coordinate intra-group financing through loan facilities. The structure serves real functions—it allows for dividend streaming that's legitimate under EU rules, and it provides operational flexibility for refinancing or restructuring.

The controversy around Dutch structures has largely centered on intellectual property licensing structures and transfer pricing—areas that have become far more scrutinized post-BEPS. But straightforward Dutch holdco structures in LBOs remain viable.

IRELAND: TAX RESIDENT AMBIGUITY AND THE MODERN APPEAL

Ireland deserves special attention in any discussion of LBO structuring, though its role has evolved significantly.

For decades, Ireland was attractive primarily because of its low corporate tax rate (12.5%, significantly below most EU neighbors). That low rate still matters, but it's increasingly being constrained by OECD Pillar Two minimum taxation (which I'll address later). However, Ireland's current importance in LBO structuring stems more from a different feature: it has historically been possible to structure Irish entities in ways that were not tax-resident in Ireland.

I know that sounds arcane, but it's actually fundamental. An Irish-incorporated company might not be tax-resident in Ireland if it was incorporated before January 1, 2020 and certain conditions apply—or if it satisfies the "Place of Effective Management" test, which could situate it elsewhere for tax purposes. More recently, Ireland introduced the "statutory residence" rule. This created ambiguity that, for a time, was highly valuable for structuring.

A transaction I worked on in 2012 involved an Irish acquisition vehicle incorporated in Dublin but managed and controlled from Luxembourg. Under Irish tax law, the entity was not tax-resident in Ireland (because management and control were in Luxembourg). Under Luxembourg law, it was tax-resident in Luxembourg. This "falling between jurisdictions" created opportunities for tax efficiency that were difficult to replicate elsewhere.

But—and this is a major but—Ireland has substantially tightened these rules. The statutory residence rule and increased OECD scrutiny have made these ambiguous structures significantly riskier. Modern Ireland structures in LBOs are more likely to be straightforward: an Irish operating subsidiary (where genuine operations occur) held through a European holding company, benefiting from Ireland's 12.5% rate. The days of using Ireland primarily for tax residence ambiguity are largely behind us.

However, Ireland's emergence as a data center and cloud computing hub has created a new LBO dynamic. Several of our more recent deals involve Irish entities as genuine operating hubs, with IT and cloud infrastructure concentrated there. In those cases, the Irish structure is commercially driven, not tax-driven.

TRANSFER PRICING IN LEVERAGED ACQUISITIONS: THE CRITICAL DIMENSION

Transfer pricing might be the single most important—and most dangerous—dimension of LBO structuring. It's where technical expertise, regulatory scrutiny, and actual audit risk converge.

Transfer pricing, in the context of leveraged acquisitions, has two primary manifestations: first, the pricing of intercompany loans (particularly those used to push acquisition debt down into operating companies); and second, the pricing of management fees, royalties, or service charges flowing between entities in different jurisdictions.

Let me start with intercompany loan pricing. When a holding company lends to an operating subsidiary to fund acquisition consideration, that loan must have an interest rate. If it doesn't—or if the rate is dramatically below market—tax authorities will argue that the excess interest that "should" be paid but isn't is a hidden dividend or transfer of value. This creates taxable income in the lending entity and reduces deductions in the borrowing entity.

The standard for intercompany interest rates is "arm's length"—the rate that would be charged between unrelated parties in comparable circumstances. This sounds simple until you start actually determining it. What's the credit quality of the borrower? What's the subordination hierarchy? What's the tenor of the loan? Is it floating or fixed? What's the risk-free rate environment?

Post-BEPS, regulators have become exceptionally aggressive in challenging intercompany interest rates. The OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines now include an entire section on financial transactions, and the standards for documentation are stringent. Tax authorities now routinely challenge interest rates that, fifteen years ago, would have been accepted without question.

I'll give you an example from practice. In 2018, we structured an acquisition where an operating subsidiary in Germany borrowed from its holding company in Luxembourg. The interest rate was set at 2.8%, based on a comparable company analysis of market rates for similar credits at the time. When the German tax authorities (Bundeszentralamt für Steuern, the BZSt) audited the transaction three years later, they argued that given the subordination of the loan and the sector volatility, a comparable rate would have been 3.5-4.0%. The difference—0.7-1.2%—was disallowed, creating €14-20 million in additional taxable income in Germany. We fought it, and ultimately settled at 3.1%, but that settlement cost hundreds of thousands in professional fees and years of uncertainty.

This experience taught me that transfer pricing documentation is not optional—it's foundational. Every intercompany loan needs contemporaneous transfer pricing documentation: a functional analysis of the borrower and lender, a market analysis of comparable rates, and clear economic reasoning for why the chosen rate is arm's length.

The second major transfer pricing issue in LBOs concerns management fees. Holding companies often charge management fees to operating subsidiaries for services rendered—strategic guidance, financial management, board-level oversight, etc. These fees can be a tool for extracting earnings from higher-tax jurisdictions to lower-tax ones.

Here's where the legal and ethical lines blur. A management fee of 2-3% of EBITDA might be defensible if you can document that the holding company actually provides valuable services. But a management fee of 8-10% with minimal documentation? That's audit bait. I've seen deals where a €300 million EBITDA operating company pays €20 million annual management fees to a holding company, with nothing but a vague description of "strategic oversight" to justify it. Those deals don't survive serious audit.

The modern approach, which I recommend, is to be conservative with management fees. If your holding company genuinely provides treasury, HR, IT, and strategic services, charge for them—but document the value. If it's purely a pass-through entity, charge a minimal fee (if any) and don't create audit risk.

WITHHOLDING TAX ON INTEREST PAYMENTS: TREATY NETWORKS AND BACK-TO-BACK STRUCTURES

Withholding taxes on interest payments between related parties represent another layer of complexity in LBO structuring. Most countries impose a withholding tax on interest payments to non-resident entities. In the EU, withholding tax rates on interest range from 15-35%, depending on the jurisdiction. This can substantially increase the cost of debt.

However—and this is crucial—bilateral tax treaties typically reduce or eliminate withholding taxes on interest payments between related parties. The network of tax treaties allows an interest payment from, say, a German operating company to a Luxembourg holding company to be made without withholding tax, because the US-Germany treaty eliminates withholding on interest.

But what if a treaty doesn't exist or doesn't cover the payment? This is where "back-to-back" structures become valuable. A back-to-back loan structure involves multiple entities between the ultimate lender and the ultimate borrower, arranged so that each intermediate interest payment is covered by a treaty.

For example: A syndicated loan is made to Newco (Luxembourg). Newco makes an interest payment to the bank lenders (no withholding—paid to non-resident creditors). Newco then makes an intercompany loan to Holdco (Netherlands). The interest payment from Newco to Holdco is covered by the Luxembourg-Netherlands treaty (no withholding). Holdco then makes an intercompany loan to the German operating company. The interest payment from Germany to the Netherlands is covered by a treaty (minimal or no withholding).

By structuring the debt through multiple entities, each with a favorable treaty relationship, you can eliminate withholding tax entirely, even when direct debt between source and lender might incur withholding.

The challenge is that anti-avoidance rules now scrutinize these structures aggressively. The EU Interest Limitation Directive includes provisions specifically targeting "back-to-back loan arrangements," and OECD BEPS Action 4 is similarly targeted. The modern rule is that if a back-to-back loan is recognized as existing primarily to avoid withholding tax, it can be disregarded.

But—and this is important—a back-to-back structure that has legitimate commercial rationale (like pooling debt, managing currency exposure, or coordinating financing) remains defensible. The line is between using the structure for tax efficiency (defensible) and using it purely for tax avoidance (increasingly indefensible).

In my practice, I recommend back-to-back structures only when they have dual economic rationale. If you're using multiple entities in the capital structure anyway for operational reasons, the interest payments flowing through those entities naturally create back-to-back economics. But designing a structure purely to minimize withholding tax is increasingly dangerous.

DEBT PUSH-DOWN: THE MECHANICS AND THE RISK

Debt push-down is arguably the most fundamental tax lever in leveraged finance. It's also perhaps the most scrutinized.

Here's how it works in principle: A sponsor raises acquisition financing at the holding company level. Rather than leaving the acquisition debt at the top of the structure, the sponsor uses the holding company to make downstream loans to its subsidiaries, which use those loans to distribute cash to the parent (either as dividends or as equity returns). This "pushes down" the debt burden—and critically, the interest deductions—to the operating company level.

Why would you do this? Because it allows the operating company to deduct interest expense and reduce its tax liability in the jurisdiction where it operates. If a German operating company earns €100 million in EBITDA and would normally pay 30% tax (€30 million), but you push down debt that allows it to deduct €40 million in interest, the taxable income drops to €60 million and tax drops to €18 million. That's a tax savings of €12 million annually.

The classic push-down structure works like this: Newco (holding company) raises €300 million in debt. Newco makes an intercompany loan to Sub (operating company) for €300 million. Sub uses the proceeds to make a dividend payment or equity distribution to Newco, which uses the cash to pay down acquisition debt or distribute to investors. The net effect: Sub now carries the debt burden (through intercompany obligations) while Newco has received cash. Sub deducts the interest; Newco accrues interest income (which is matched by actual interest payments to external creditors).

From a tax perspective, this is almost magic—you've moved the deduction to the entity in the lowest-tax jurisdiction, maximizing the group's tax efficiency.

But modern tax authorities are exceptionally suspicious of debt push-downs. They argue that they're "abuse of law" or "artificial arrangements" designed to artificially shift tax bases. The EU's GAAR (General Anti-Abuse Rule) provisions in the ATAD specifically target these structures if they lack genuine commercial substance.

The modern approach to defensibility is documentation. A debt push-down must have genuine economic reason to exist. Common justifications include: (a) coordination of group financing—the operating company needs to fund capital expenditures and working capital; (b) operational management—centralizing financing decisions at the holding company level; (c) exit flexibility—allowing the holding company to access the operating company's cash without unwinding the equity structure.

None of these are dispositive, but they provide a foundation for arguing that the push-down existed for reasons beyond tax avoidance. I always recommend that our clients document the business rationale for push-down structures contemporaneously—board resolutions, committee minutes, CFO communications explaining why this structure is being implemented from a financial management perspective.

THIN CAPITALIZATION AND DEBT-TO-EQUITY RULES ACROSS EUROPEAN JURISDICTIONS

Different European jurisdictions have different rules governing how much debt a company can carry relative to its equity. These "thin capitalization" or "debt-to-equity" rules are supplementary to interest limitation rules and create their own constraints.

Germany has one of the most notorious—a strict "4:1 debt-to-equity ratio rule." If a company's net interest expense exceeds 30% of its tax EBITDA (the ATAD rule), it triggers Germany's "interest barrier rule," which further limits deductions. But even before hitting the ATAD threshold, if a German company has leverage above 4:1 (debt divided by equity), it may face restrictions. This has been revised somewhat under recent updates to German law, but the principle remains.

France has a similar approach, with an "8x" rule (net financial debt divided by EBITDA), though this is more of a practical threshold than a hard legal rule.

The UK, historically, had no explicit thin capitalization rule, which made it popular for highly leveraged structures. But post-ATAD harmonization, the UK is moving toward similar rules.

The practical implication is that to achieve the 4.5-5.0x leverage multiples common in modern LBOs, sponsors must often accept meaningfully lower leverage in certain jurisdictions. If the operating company is in Germany and equity is constrained by sponsor commitments, achieving 5x leverage might violate the 4:1 debt-to-equity rule.

This has led to creative structures where leverage is stacked at the holding company level (not subject to the same thin capitalization rules) with more modest leverage at operating companies. But these structures are exactly what anti-avoidance rules target.

The modern solution, which I recommend, is to accept that leverage will be modulated by jurisdiction. A multicountry group might carry 3x leverage in Germany, 4.5x in France, and 5x in Luxembourg. By varying leverage by jurisdiction, you optimize within each legal constraint while maximizing overall group leverage. It requires more complex modeling and transaction documentation, but it's increasingly how deals are structured.

TAX CONSOLIDATION AND FISCAL UNITY: ADMINISTRATIVE EFFICIENCY AND TAX OPTIMIZATION

Several European jurisdictions allow a holding company and its subsidiaries to form a "fiscal unity" or "tax consolidated group," where the group files a single consolidated tax return. Germany's Organschaftshaft, France's Régime d'Intégration Fiscale, and the Netherlands' Fiscal Unity are examples.

The primary benefit of fiscal unity is administrative efficiency: instead of each entity filing separate returns and calculating separate tax liabilities, the group does one consolidated calculation. Losses in one entity offset profits in another in real-time, which can be valuable for managing group tax liability during the high-leverage period of an LBO.

But there are costs. Fiscal unity typically requires the holding company to own 100% of subsidiaries (or very high ownership thresholds, like 95%), which can constrain capital structure flexibility. It also makes subsequent sales of subsidiaries more complicated, because exiting fiscal unity requires unwinding the group structure.

For LBO purposes, fiscal unity can be valuable in the years immediately post-acquisition, when the operating company might not be sufficiently profitable to utilize interest deductions. By consolidating with the holding company, losses can be offset against holding company income. But as the company stabilizes and debt pay-down occurs, fiscal unity becomes less valuable and more constraining.

I've structured deals both with and without fiscal unity, and the decision turns on specific facts of the transaction. If the operating company is likely to have losses in years 1-3 due to interest deductions exceeding EBITDA, fiscal unity is likely beneficial. If the company is profitable and the holding company is carrying minimal other income, fiscal unity adds complexity without much benefit.

HYBRID INSTRUMENTS AND THEIR TAX TREATMENT

Hybrid instruments—financial instruments that have characteristics of both debt and equity—have historically been a tool for tax optimization in LBOs. A preferred share that combines elements of equity (ownership) and debt (mandatory redemption, fixed payments) could potentially be treated as debt for tax purposes while being equity from a legal perspective.

Post-BEPS, hybrid instrument structuring has become substantially more restricted. The OECD's BEPS Action 4 and the EU's Interest Limitation Directive both specifically target hybrid structures. The principle now is that if a financial instrument is characterized as debt for tax purposes, it must have substantive characteristics of debt.

Modern LBOs typically avoid explicit hybrid instruments. Instead, they use straightforward combinations of term debt, mezzanine debt, and equity. A typical structure might include: first lien bank debt (traditional senior secured), second lien debt (subordinated, higher interest), mezzanine debt (typically PIK toggling to cash-paying), and preferred equity.

Each tier has clear debt or equity characteristics, simplifying tax treatment and reducing audit risk.

VAT CONSIDERATIONS IN LEVERAGED FINANCE FEES

Value Added Tax (VAT) treatment of leveraged finance transactions has become increasingly important, particularly in Europe where VAT rates of 15-27% can be material costs.

Financing fees and advisory fees in LBO transactions may or may not be subject to VAT, depending on the nature of the service and the jurisdiction. Financial services in most EU states are exempt from VAT—a bank providing a term loan does not charge VAT on the interest or fees. But advisory fees (paid to sponsors, advisors, arrangers) may be subject to VAT depending on the nature of the service.

This creates a peculiar situation: a sponsor that raises debt and uses proceeds for an acquisition might not incur VAT on the financing costs, but might incur VAT on advisory fees related to the acquisition. This VAT cannot always be recovered (depending on the sponsor's VAT status and the nature of its business).

In cross-border transactions, VAT treatment becomes even more complex. Services provided by a non-EU advisor to an EU buyer may be treated differently than services provided by an EU advisor. I've seen deals where the VAT treatment of advisory fees shifted based on where advisors were located and how the services were characterized.

The modern approach to VAT planning in LBOs is relatively simple: work with VAT specialists to characterize services appropriately and structure them to minimize VAT incidence. This might mean organizing advisory services in specific jurisdictions, timing service delivery to align with VAT period, or using specific contractual characterizations to ensure VAT exemption.

OECD BEPS AND ITS FUNDAMENTAL RESTRUCTURING OF LBO TAX PLANNING

The OECD Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (BEPS) initiative, launched in 2012, fundamentally altered the landscape for LBO tax planning. BEPS was the regulatory response to the reality that multinational corporations were shifting enormous profits to low-tax jurisdictions through sophisticated planning. The initiative produced 15 Actions, many directly relevant to leveraged finance.

The most impactful for LBO practitioners:

Action 4 targeted interest deductions and other financial policy rules. This led to the interest limitation rules I discussed earlier.

Action 13 required country-by-country reporting (CbCR), where multinational groups must report their allocation of profits, employees, revenues, and taxes across jurisdictions. For large LBO groups, CbCR creates an audit roadmap—if a group's reported profits and taxes don't align with expected patterns, it invites scrutiny.

Action 5 addressed harmful tax practices, including IP boxes and patent boxes. This didn't eliminate the structures, but substantially tightened them.

Action 14 was the Transfer Pricing Aspects of Financial Transactions, which created much stricter standards for pricing intercompany loans and other financial arrangements.

The cumulative impact is that what was acceptable tax planning in 2005 is indefensible in 2025. Structures that relied on arbitrage between jurisdictions (shifting profits to low-tax locations) have been substantially curtailed.

But BEPS also clarified that tax planning isn't inherently wrong. Tax planning that has genuine business substance is acceptable. This has created an incentive for sponsors to think more carefully about business rationale. Modern LBOs are structured with both tax efficiency and operational benefits in mind.

MINIMUM TAX, PILLAR TWO, AND THE FUTURE OF TAX PLANNING

The most consequential development in LBO taxation since BEPS is the OECD's move toward global minimum taxation. Pillar Two of the OECD Inclusive Framework created an international agreement that large multinational enterprises (those with more than €750 million in annual revenues) would be subject to a minimum 15% tax globally.

This is a fundamental shift. For decades, tax planning has focused on shifting profits to low-tax jurisdictions. Pillar Two makes that significantly less valuable. If you shift profits to Ireland (12.5% tax), a top-up tax of 2.5% will be levied at the parent company level, bringing the total to 15%.

For large LBOs—particularly those with large acquisition targets—Pillar Two creates a new layer of complexity. The tax planning that drove structures for the previous 20 years becomes materially less valuable.

However, Pillar Two isn't a complete game-changer. First, it applies a 15% minimum, not a 25% or 30% minimum. For groups that operate primarily in moderate-tax jurisdictions (Germany at 30%, France at 28%), Pillar Two is less impactful. Second, Pillar Two has transitional relief provisions and excludes certain categories of income. The mechanics are complex.

What Pillar Two does do is reduce the incentive to compress tax liability to the absolute minimum through aggressive structuring. If you're going to pay 15% anyway (due to Pillar Two), the marginal benefit of getting from 12.5% to 8% is significantly reduced.

This is pushing tax planning philosophy toward what I'd call "sustainable" structuring—structures that are designed to be commercially defensible and to survive audit, with tax efficiency as a secondary benefit rather than the primary driver.

ANTI-AVOIDANCE RULES: GAARS AND EMERGING CONSTRAINTS

The proliferation of General Anti-Avoidance Rules (GAARs) across European jurisdictions has made tax planning materially more constrained than it was even five years ago.

A GAAR typically allows tax authorities to disallow tax benefits if an arrangement is deemed to be "abusive" or primarily designed to obtain tax benefits. The standard varies by jurisdiction. Some GAARs are quite strict (the UK's GAAR has a high threshold for disallowance), while others are more flexible.

The EU's GAAR, which came into force with the ATAD, is relatively broad. It disallows arrangements that, having regard to all relevant facts and circumstances, are not genuine considering their essential purpose. If an arrangement is found to be a tax avoidance transaction, the tax authority can disallow the benefits.

What makes GAARs particularly dangerous for LBO structuring is that they shift the burden of proof. In traditional tax audits, the tax authority must prove that a structure violates specific rules. With a GAAR, the structure merely has to be deemed "abusive"—a lower evidentiary bar.

This means that even if a structure technically complies with interest limitation rules, transfer pricing rules, and thin capitalization rules, it could still be disallowed if deemed to lack genuine commercial purpose.

The practical response is to be conservative. Structures that work on a purely technical level but appear designed primarily for tax avoidance are increasingly risky. Structures that have genuine business rationale and happen to offer tax efficiency are increasingly defensible.

TAX DUE DILIGENCE IN LEVERAGED ACQUISITIONS: RISKS AND PREPARATION

Tax due diligence in an LBO context is fundamentally different from tax due diligence in a strategic acquisition. In a strategic acquisition, the buyer often cares about historic tax liabilities and risks. In an LBO, the sponsor is equally concerned with future tax optimization and structuring risk.

A comprehensive LBO tax due diligence should cover:

1. Historic tax compliance and audit exposure. Has the target filed timely tax returns? Are there any open audits or disputes? What's the statute of limitations exposure?

2. Transfer pricing documentation. If the target has intercompany transactions, are those properly documented? Are interest rates on intercompany loans defensible?

3. Hybrid instrument and debt characterization. Are there any financial instruments that might be characterized as hybrid? Could they be reclassified by tax authorities?

4. Intellectual property. Does the target own IP that could be optimized? Are there existing IP licensing arrangements that could be improved?

5. Financing structuring risk. What's the optimal leverage for the target given its jurisdiction and profitability profile? What's the tax cost of the optimal capital structure?

6. Treaty access. What tax treaties could be beneficial for the structure? Are there treaty access risks (e.g., could the structure be viewed as treaty shopping)?

7. Cash movement and distribution cascades. How will cash be distributed to sponsors? Are there withholding tax implications?

I always insist on thorough tax due diligence before commitment. The cost—typically €200k-500k for a mid-market deal—is trivial compared to the risk of post-acquisition tax liabilities or audit disputes.

RECENT TRENDS AND PRACTICAL STRUCTURING IMPLICATIONS

Beyond the major regulatory developments I've discussed, several recent trends are reshaping LBO tax practice:

First, there's an increasing focus on tax certainty. Sponsors are increasingly seeking advance rulings from tax authorities (APAs, Advance Pricing Agreements) for intercompany transactions, rather than relying on transfer pricing policies that might be challenged. This adds time and cost to transactions, but substantially reduces audit risk.

Second, there's a shift toward "default tax efficiency"—structures that are efficient by virtue of their commercial design, rather than relying on aggressive interpretations of tax rules. This means using proper transfer pricing methodologies, defensible intercompany rates, and commercial business structures.

Third, environmental, social, and governance (ESG) considerations are increasingly intersecting with tax planning. Some sponsors are consciously reducing tax optimization in specific jurisdictions to improve tax transparency and ESG ratings. This is partly driven by investor pressure and partly by recognition that aggressive tax minimization creates reputational risk.

Fourth, there's an emergence of "tax documentation risk." Tax authorities increasingly demand evidence not just of compliance, but of proper business rationale. Sponsors that can't produce contemporaneous board resolutions, risk assessments, and CFO communications explaining why structures were chosen are at disadvantage in audits.

Finally, there's an increasing use of tax indemnification in acquisition agreements. Sponsors are pushing representations and warranties regarding tax compliance, with indemnification for post-closing tax liabilities. This is creating incentive for more conservative structures—if you're indemnifying the buyer against tax risk, you're less likely to implement aggressive structures.

WHAT TWENTY YEARS TEACHES: REFLECTIONS AND PRINCIPLES

I'm going to conclude this chapter with some reflections on what two decades of working in this space has taught me, because the technical details, important as they are, point toward deeper principles about tax planning in leveraged finance.

First, sustainability beats optimization. Early in my career, I was drawn to the most aggressive structures—the ones that minimized tax to the absolute lowest possible level. These structures often worked, at least until they didn't. They would survive audits in some cases, and get demolished in others. The uncertainty itself was costly. Over time, I've come to believe that a structure that's 80% as tax-efficient as the most aggressive option, but that's defensible and has genuine commercial rationale, is superior. It costs less to document, is less likely to be challenged, and if it is challenged, is more likely to be defended successfully.

Second, transfer pricing and intercompany pricing are the critical bottleneck. More LBO structures get undone by transfer pricing challenges than by any other mechanism. Interest rates that are off by a quarter percent, management fees that seem excessive, intellectual property royalties that don't align with comparable practices—these are what tax authorities focus on. Get transfer pricing right, and you've solved 60% of your tax audit risk.

Third, substance matters more than form. Regulators have shifted from asking "Does this structure comply with the literal language of the tax rules?" to "Would this structure exist if it didn't provide tax benefits?" The second question is much harder to answer. Structures that make genuine operational or financial sense are defensible. Structures that are pure tax engineering are increasingly indefensible.

Fourth, documentation is an asset, not a checkbox. I've seen structures survive audits that were marginally within the rules, purely because of excellent documentation. And I've seen structures get demolished that were technically compliant, but with poor documentation. Board resolutions, committee minutes, CFO communications, transfer pricing studies—these aren't just compliance exercises. They're the foundation for defending your structure.

Fifth, tax authorities are getting smarter. The inspectors I'm dealing with today are more sophisticated than those I dealt with ten years ago. They understand transfer pricing methodology, they understand how structures work, and they understand the commercial economics of transactions. You cannot rely on tax authorities not understanding what you're doing. You have to assume they understand and design structures that are defensible under that assumption.

Sixth, tax planning is no longer separable from business strategy. The days when you could optimize the structure purely for tax without regard to operational impact are over. Modern LBO structuring requires simultaneous optimization for tax, operations, financial reporting, and compliance. The structure that's most tax-efficient might be operationally awkward or might create financial reporting complexity. The optimal structure is the one that balances all these considerations.

Finally—and I say this carefully—there's an increasing recognition that some level of tax is not just acceptable but appropriate. This isn't to say that sponsors should ignore tax optimization. Rather, it's to recognize that a structure that reduces tax liability from 25% to 20% might be worth pursuing, but a structure that tries to reduce it to 8% probably isn't. The incremental benefit of the second level of optimization often isn't worth the risk and documentation burden.

I've been fortunate to work through the entire arc of tax planning evolution in leveraged finance, from the relatively unregulated environment of the 1990s through the BEPS revolution and now into the Pillar Two era. What's clear to me is that the field is maturing. The days of using leverage and complex structures primarily to minimize tax are over. What remains is genuine tax optimization within defensible structures—and that's probably healthier for everyone involved.

The sponsors who succeed in the next phase of leveraged finance will be those who recognize that tax is a constraint to optimize within, not the primary driver of structuring decisions. Build a good company, structure it fairly, and the tax benefits will follow. That's not as exciting as a multi-layer Luxembourg-Netherlands-Ireland structure designed purely to minimize group tax rate. But it's a lot more sustainable.
