EVENTS OF DEFAULT: A DEEP DIVE
A Leveraged Finance Practitioner's Guide

INTRODUCTION: WHY EVENTS OF DEFAULT MATTER MORE THAN YOU THINK

I remember the exact moment when I truly understood the weight of an Event of Default clause. It was 1997, and I was a twenty-four-year-old analyst at a boutique credit shop in Manhattan, sitting in a basement conference room of a restructuring advisor's office. The company in question was a mid-cap specialty chemicals manufacturer that had blown through its capital expenditure basket by about thirty-seven million dollars in the third quarter. Not catastrophic in absolute terms, but enough to trigger what the credit agreement called a "financial covenant default." I watched the senior lender—a weathered managing director in his fifties who had seen the S&L crisis and lived to tell about it—lean back in his chair and simply say: "We have the nuclear button now."

That phrase stuck with me. The nuclear button. Because that is precisely what an Event of Default clause is in a credit agreement. It is not merely a technical violation of terms. It is the mechanism by which lenders transform from passive capital providers into active stakeholders with the power to reshape, restructure, or eliminate the equity value of a business in a single decisive motion. Press that button—declare all amounts due and payable, accelerate the debt, march into bankruptcy court, seize collateral—and the entire capital structure of a company can reorganize in a matter of weeks.

Over the past twenty-three years since that meeting in that basement, I have watched Events of Default clauses do far more damage to borrowers than people in the early stages of their careers typically appreciate. The young analyst team gets assigned to read the credit agreement and often skims past the EOD section with something approaching boredom. "Okay, so the company defaults if they don't pay. Fine. What else?" But the magic, the danger, and the opportunity in EOD clauses lies in the depth and breadth of what can trigger them, how they interact with other parts of the capital structure, and what remedies become available once they are triggered.

This chapter is written from the vantage point of someone who has read thousands of credit agreements, negotiated dozens of amendment and restatement agreements that fundamentally rewrote what constitutes an Event of Default, advised lender groups during the precarious moments when they had to decide whether to invoke their nuclear power, and helped borrowers navigate the minefield of trying to stay on the right side of fifteen different EOD provisions simultaneously. It is also written with the humbling recognition that I have gotten some calls wrong, missed some nuance that cost clients real money, and learned hard lessons about when a junior colleague's careful reading of a definition section caught something that would have meant the difference between a restructuring and a bankruptcy.

The reason Events of Default matter more than most practitioners initially assume is that they establish the boundary between credit as a normal contractual obligation and credit as a conditional property right. So long as a company is not in default, the lenders are contractually bound to fund their commitments (subject to certain conditions), maintain their negotiated pricing discipline, and work within the constraints of the credit agreement. But the moment an Event of Default occurs and is declared, the power dynamic shifts fundamentally. The lenders are no longer obligated to fund incremental capital. They can demand higher pricing. They can require management changes. They can push toward a restructuring. They can foreclose on collateral. They can sit in the driver's seat of a Chapter 11 filing. Everything changes. And that change is triggered by the EOD clauses.

I have seen lenders use the mere threat of declaring a default—a default that technically existed for weeks but had not yet been formally declared—as leverage in negotiating a comprehensive amendment. I have seen borrowers burn through millions of dollars in restructuring advisor fees trying to cure a default that, while technically curable, had created such a loss of confidence among lenders that the relationship had fundamentally broken down. And I have seen companies emerge from situations where every reasonable observer thought they were insolvent because the lenders decided, for strategic reasons, not to accelerate. The EOD clause is the mechanism through which all of that leverage works.

What Twenty Years Teaches: The first thing that a career in this business teaches you is that the boring, technical, deeply detailed sections of a credit agreement are where the actual risks live. This is counterintuitive for young professionals, who often assume that the juicy action happens in the covenants sections—the leverage ratios, the interest coverage maintenance, the capital expenditure baskets. Those matter, certainly. But I have seen far more deals go sideways due to a provision buried in the definition of "Event of Default" about what happens to the classification of debt if a material adverse effect occurs, or how the "continuing directors" test in the change of control section gets triggered, than because of a company missing an interest coverage ratio by one tenth.

The second lesson is that EOD clauses are where lenders encode their expectations about how a borrower will behave in stressed circumstances. The presence or absence of certain EOD provisions, the breadth of grace periods, the materiality thresholds on various clauses, the consent rights that are required before remedies can be exercised—all of these decisions reflect the lender's view of what risk they are taking and what leverage they will need if things go wrong. Understanding the lender's expectations is understanding the EOD section.

TAXONOMY OF EVENTS OF DEFAULT: THE COMPLETE LANDSCAPE

Over my career, I have worked with credit agreements that contained anywhere from nine to forty-three separate Events of Default. The variation reflects the risk profile of the borrower, the sophistication of the lender, the stage of the economic cycle when the deal was agreed, and the leverage at which it was syndicated. But certain categories of defaults appear with remarkable consistency. These categories form a useful taxonomy for thinking about the landscape of potential defaults.

Payment Defaults: The Most Straightforward, The Most Dangerous

The mechanics of a payment default seem absurdly simple: the borrower fails to pay when due, and therefore breaches the credit agreement. But the simplicity is deceptive. Payment defaults are the most binary of all defaults—there is no subjectivity, no judgment call, no materiality qualifier. You either paid the money or you did not. And this binary nature makes payment defaults both the most straightforward to identify and, paradoxically, the most dangerous to trigger inadvertently.

A standard payment default clause reads something like this: "The failure of the Borrower to pay principal, interest, and fees when due and payable shall constitute an Event of Default." This appears to offer absolute clarity. But between the moment that a payment should have been made and the moment that a lender declares a default based on non-payment, there are several crucial mechanical steps that I have seen generate enormous disputes.

First, there is the grace period. For interest payments on loans, the typical grace period is five business days. This means that if an interest payment is due on a Monday, the borrower technically has until the following Monday to pay without triggering a default, assuming they give adequate notice of their intention to exercise the grace period. For principal payments, the grace period is often shorter—three business days—or nonexistent, depending on the negotiation. For fees, such as annual administrative agent fees or commitment fees, there is often no grace period at all, though the payment is typically made as part of an annual closing.

The grace period serves several practical purposes. First, it accounts for the mechanical delays inherent in the banking system. A wire transfer instruction given on Monday at 2:00 PM may not clear until Tuesday morning, depending on the time zone and the complexity of the routing. Second, it provides a de facto cure period that allows a borrower who has experienced a genuine, temporary liquidity problem to get current without triggering a default. Third, it provides psychological breathing room that can be valuable in stressed situations. I have personally negotiated amendments where the mere extension of a payment grace period from three to five business days was the difference between a friendly restructuring and a contested default and acceleration.

But here is where the complications start. During the financial crisis, I worked with a borrower that had wired funds for an interest payment at 3:45 PM on a Friday, thinking that the payment would clear by end of business. Instead, the wire arrived in the lender's account on Monday morning—after the close of business Friday and thus, technically, after the grace period had expired, even though the borrower had acted in good faith. The issue was not merely academic. Three other lenders in the syndicate had immediately circulated a notice of default, claiming that the failure to pay interest by Friday end of business constituted an Event of Default. It required a conference call with counsel, a review of the exact language of the payment obligation (which did not explicitly specify that the wire had to be received by close of business), and ultimately a waiver amendment to clear the matter up.

The point is that payment defaults, while seemingly binary, are subject to disputes about timing, mechanics, and what counts as "due and payable" in the context of practical banking realities. I have learned to draft payment default clauses that account for these realities, often specifying that funds must be received by the administrative agent's designated account by a certain time on the due date, or that the failure to wire funds by 2:00 PM New York time on the due date shall constitute a default, understanding that this gives the borrower a clear, bright-line rule to follow.

Beyond interest and principal, payment defaults can also encompass the failure to pay taxes, insurance premiums, or other charges that the borrower may be obligated to pay on behalf of itself or that have been allocated to it by the credit agreement. These are less common in leveraged facilities but emerge regularly in acquisition finance structures where the borrower is acquiring a company and taking on certain earn-out or contingent purchase price obligations. I negotiated a credit agreement for a lower-middle-market acquisition where the purchase price included a three-year earnout. The credit agreement required the borrower to set aside cash for the earnout obligation quarterly, and the failure to deposit the required amount into an escrow account constituted a payment default. This seemed straightforward until the acquired company's business imploded and the borrower attempted to assert that the earnout clause in the purchase agreement was breached and therefore the earnout itself was not due. The lenders, however, pointed to the credit agreement language and argued that regardless of whether the earnout was ultimately payable under the M&A agreement, the borrower's failure to escrow the cash when required constituted a payment default under the loan agreement. That particular dispute consumed six months and required an intercreditor and subordination agreement to resolve.

The mechanics of cure for payment defaults are also important. Technically, a payment default can be cured by paying the full amount due, plus accrued interest on the unpaid amount and, in some credit agreements, a default interest rate that accrues at a premium over the contract rate. However, in many credit agreements, once a default has been declared, the entire outstanding principal amount becomes immediately due and payable. This creates a Catch-22: the borrower cannot actually cure the payment default because the lender has immediately transformed it into an acceleration of all debt. This is precisely why the interaction between the payment default clause and the acceleration remedy is so crucial.

What Twenty Years Teaches: After reading thousands of credit agreements, I can tell you that the payment default section is where sloppy drafting causes the most practical problems. The lenders' counsel will often cut and paste language from a prior deal without adapting it to the specific facts of the current transaction. I have seen credit agreements where the payment default clause specified payment to the "agent's account," but the agent's account information was never provided to the borrower. I have seen language requiring payment in "immediately available funds" without defining what that means in a context where different banking hubs might have different same-day payment capabilities. The key lesson is that even though payment defaults seem obvious and uncomplicated, the boundary between a good payment default clause and one that will generate disputes in a stressed situation comes down to clarity about timing, location, and mechanism.

Cross-Default and Cross-Acceleration: The Critical Distinction and Its Cascade Effects

If payment defaults are the foundation of the EOD taxonomy, cross-default provisions are the glue that binds together a complex capital structure. Cross-default provisions essentially say: if the borrower defaults under another material debt obligation, then that default also constitutes an Event of Default under this credit agreement. The idea is that defaults do not exist in isolation. If a company has defaulted on one set of debt, the lenders providing other debt have a legitimate concern that their own debt is now at heightened risk.

The mechanics of cross-default can vary significantly, and these variations matter a great deal. The most important variable is the threshold amount. A typical cross-default provision might read: "If the Borrower or any Material Subsidiary shall be in default under the terms of any Material Indebtedness in an amount exceeding $25,000,000, then the Borrower shall be deemed to be in default under this Agreement."

Let me unpack that language. First, there is the threshold amount: $25 million in this example. This is where the real negotiation happens. Lenders want a threshold that is material enough to actually trigger their concern, but not so immaterial that every minor covenant breach in a complex capital structure immediately spreads to their facility. Borrowers want a threshold that is high enough that they have some cushion between a problem in one part of their capital structure and a default spreading to all their debt. I have negotiated these thresholds for everything from $5 million at the lower end (for smaller, less complex companies) to $150 million at the upper end (for large, multi-jurisdictional companies with multiple tranches of debt).

The threshold amount is not merely a technical detail. During the 2008 financial crisis, I worked with a company that had $400 million in total debt, split among a first-lien term loan ($150 million), a second-lien term loan ($150 million), and a mezzanine facility ($100 million). The cross-default threshold in the first-lien agreement was $50 million. When the company's operating performance deteriorated, it missed a covenant in its subordinated debt facility, creating an Event of Default in the mezzanine facility. However, the amount involved was only $30 million of notional debt value (the issue related to a covenant breach, not an actual missed payment, so the "amount" was somewhat subjective). The first-lien lenders debated for two weeks whether this constituted a cross-default under their facility. The technical answer was that no principal amount had been accelerated in the mezzanine facility; therefore, the threshold had not been breached. But the practical reality was that the company was now in default in one part of its capital structure, and the confidence of first-lien lenders was shaken. This debate ultimately led to a comprehensive workout that might have been avoided if the cross-default threshold had been defined with greater precision.

The second key variable in a cross-default clause is what constitutes "default" for purposes of triggering the cross-default. Does it only apply if the borrower has actually failed to make a payment due, or does it also apply if the borrower has committed a covenant breach that constitutes a default under the other agreement? Most sophisticated credit agreements have a tiered approach: a cross-default will be triggered if there is a payment default under Material Indebtedness of any amount, but a covenant or other non-payment default will only trigger cross-default if the amount in default exceeds the threshold. This reflects the insight that payment defaults are rarer and more serious than covenant defaults, so they merit protection even at smaller amounts.

Third, there is usually language about what happens between the time a default occurs in the other debt facility and the time it gets reported to the lenders under the credit agreement. Many cross-default clauses include a grace period or notice requirement. The language might read: "Provided, that no Event of Default shall be deemed to have occurred as a result of such cross-default unless the Borrower shall not have cured or caused to be cured such Material Indebtedness default within 30 days of written notice from the required lenders hereunder." This is a crucial protection for borrowers, because it prevents the automatic, instantaneous cascade of defaults across all debt facilities. It gives the borrower a chance to cure in the downstream facility even after the upstream lender has noticed the problem.

I have worked on many restructurings where this cure grace period was the linchpin of keeping the company from tipping into involuntary bankruptcy. In one case involving a mid-market retail company, a material supplier decided to accelerate a payment under a supply agreement, claiming that the retailer had missed a payment milestone. This technically constituted a "Material Indebtedness" default under the cross-default language in the credit agreement. However, the credit agreement also specified that the borrower had 30 days to cure the cross-default. During that 30-day period, the supply agreement was successfully renegotiated, the payment obligation was restructured, and the threatened cross-default was averted. Without that grace period language, the entire capital structure would have unraveled.

Now, here is where it gets more complicated. Cross-default provisions can cascade. Imagine a capital structure with a first-lien loan, a second-lien loan, and a mezzanine loan. Each of these three facilities will typically have cross-default language that references the other two. If a payment default occurs in the mezzanine facility (say, a missed interest payment), that immediately triggers a cross-default under the second-lien facility (assuming it exceeds the threshold). The triggering of the second-lien cross-default then immediately triggers a cross-default under the first-lien facility. Now all three tranches are in default simultaneously, and the company is facing acceleration across its entire capital structure. I have watched this domino effect unfold in real time, and it is both terrifying and fascinating from a technical perspective.

The other critical distinction in this space is between cross-default and cross-acceleration. These are not the same thing, and the distinction matters enormously in a restructuring context. Cross-default means that a default in one facility technically constitutes a default in another facility. However, cross-acceleration—which is a more lender-friendly provision—means that if the other indebtedness is accelerated, then the lenders under this facility have the automatic right to accelerate their own indebtedness as well. Cross-acceleration is more favorable to lenders because it protects them in a situation where the first-lien lenders accelerate and begin moving toward a bankruptcy filing, which would wipe out the second-lien and subordinated lenders. With a cross-acceleration clause, the subordinated lenders can race the first-lien lenders to court and file their own case to protect their position.

However, cross-acceleration clauses are also more negotiated in modern leveraged finance deals. Sponsors and borrowers have pushed back on automatic cross-acceleration, arguing that they need the flexibility to work things out in one part of the capital structure without having acceleration automatically spread to other parts. The result is that many modern credit agreements distinguish between cross-default and cross-acceleration, allowing for the former while limiting the latter to specific circumstances.

I worked on a J. Crew-type situation about ten years ago, where a large specialty retailer with a complex, multi-tranched capital structure was experiencing operational stress. The first-lien lenders began accelerating their debt and moving toward a structured bankruptcy, but the second-lien and subordinated lenders wanted to preserve optionality. The question was whether they had the right to accelerate alongside the first-lien lenders or whether they had to wait for the first-lien lenders to act. The credit agreement language determined the answer to that question, and it made the difference between the subordinated lenders having a seat at the restructuring table and being locked out entirely.

What Twenty Years Teaches: Cross-default and cross-acceleration provisions are where I see the most creative drafting and the most contentious negotiations in leveraged finance. Lenders want them to be tight, capturing as much downside risk as possible. Borrowers want as much flexibility as possible. The result is that these provisions have evolved significantly over my career. In the early 2000s, cross-default language was often very broad, applying to virtually any Material Indebtedness. Post-GFC, there was a movement toward tighter, more carefully defined cross-default triggers. In the hot credit market of 2005-2007, there was enormous pressure from lenders to capture every conceivable default cascade. In 2009-2010, there was enormous pressure from borrowers to build in circuit-breakers and carve-outs that would prevent the instantaneous spread of defaults across the capital structure. The lesson is that the specific language of the cross-default provision is a direct read on how confident the market was at the time a deal was signed.

Representation and Warranty Breaches: The Hidden Landmine

Most young analysts see the representations and warranties section of a credit agreement as a kind of informational appendix—statements of fact about the borrower that the lender relies on at closing. But representations and warranties become far more dangerous when the credit agreement includes language that makes a material breach of any representation or warranty constitute an Event of Default. When that language is added, the reps and warranties section transforms from a static statement of fact into a dynamic constraint on the borrower's continued compliance with the credit agreement.

A typical R&W default clause might read: "If any material representation or warranty made by the Borrower herein shall prove to be false, incomplete, inaccurate, or misleading, and such falsity, incompleteness, inaccuracy, or misleadingness could reasonably be expected to result in a Material Adverse Effect, then the Borrower shall be in default hereunder."

This language is extremely broad. It does not require that the representation or warranty was false at the time it was made; it only requires that it proves to be false subsequently. And the inclusion of the materiality qualifier—"material" and "could reasonably be expected to result in a Material Adverse Effect"—creates ambiguity that can be exploited by lenders in a stressed situation.

I have seen lenders use the R&W default clause as leverage in restructuring negotiations, claiming that representations about the borrower's business (made months or years earlier) have proven false. For example, a representation might state that "all Contracts are in full force and effect and there are no material breaches thereof." If, months later, the company discovers that one of its key customers has breached a supply contract—something that was not known at closing—does the original representation now prove to be false? Technically, one could argue yes. And if a lender is motivated to renegotiate the credit agreement or push toward a restructuring, they can cite this technical falsity as an EOD.

I worked on a situation involving a manufacturer that had acquired a smaller company as part of a leveraged buyout. At closing, the borrower represented that it had disclosed all pending litigation. Eight months later, a former employee of the acquired company filed a wrongful termination lawsuit. Technically, this lawsuit was not "pending" at closing because it had not been filed. But the lender, looking to push the borrower toward a comprehensive amendment that included higher pricing and tighter covenants, argued that the representation about "all pending litigation" was now false because the employee's conduct (which had violated the company's policies) was known at closing, and the lender should have been informed about the potential risk. The dispute consumed two months and ultimately required a waiver amendment to resolve.

The mechanism through which R&W defaults are triggered varies. Some credit agreements require the lender to send a notice of default and give the borrower a cure period (typically 30 days) to "cure" the representation and warranty breach. But here is the practical problem: you cannot "cure" a representation and warranty that was false at the time it was made. You can remediate a condition (fix a compliance problem, settle a lawsuit, restructure a contract), but you cannot retroactively make a false statement true. This is why some credit agreements instead include language that allows the lender to waive the R&W default if the borrower takes prompt remedial action, or language that provides a basket amount below which R&W breaches are deemed immaterial.

The reps and warranties that get the most intense negotiation are those that relate to:

First, financial statements and financial condition. Lenders want representations that financial statements have been prepared in accordance with GAAP, that there are no undisclosed liabilities, and that the financial condition of the company is accurately reflected. If the company's actual financial condition turns out to be materially worse than represented, this can trigger an R&W default. I have seen situations where a company's inventory was actually obsolete and had to be written down, or where there were unrecorded liabilities for pension funding, and the lender claimed an R&W default based on the inaccuracy of the financial statements.

Second, litigation and contingent liabilities. Lenders want representations that there is no pending or threatened litigation that could reasonably be expected to result in a Material Adverse Effect. If the company subsequently becomes involved in material litigation, the question is whether the lender had an obligation to be informed about the condition or risk that led to the litigation.

Third, environmental and regulatory compliance. Lenders want representations that the company is in compliance with all environmental laws and regulations. If, post-closing, the company discovers that it is subject to environmental cleanup obligations that were not previously disclosed, this can trigger an R&W default.

Fourth, material customer and supplier relationships. Lenders often want representations about the company's largest customers and suppliers, including that there are no material breaches and no material risks. If a large customer is lost post-closing, the question is whether the loss indicates that there was a pre-existing problem that should have been represented.

The most sophisticated credit agreements include "sandbagging" language, which says whether the lender "sandbagged" on the representations—meaning, whether the lender had knowledge of a potential R&W breach at closing but closed anyway without raising the issue. Some agreements provide that sandbagging is not permitted (i.e., a lender who knew about a potential issue cannot later claim an R&W default), while others permit sandbagging (i.e., a lender can claim an R&W default even if they had knowledge of the condition at closing). The difference in these provisions can be enormous in a restructuring context.

What Twenty Years Teaches: Representations and warranties default clauses are where I see the most contentious disputes between lenders and borrowers in stressed situations. The reason is that the borrower made thousands of statements at closing, most of them drafted by the lender's counsel, and many of them containing language broad enough to encompass almost any adverse development post-closing. When a lender is looking to renegotiate a credit agreement or push a restructuring, the R&W default clause becomes a convenient tool for creating a technical default that can be pointed to as justification for more stringent terms. My advice to junior practitioners is to read the representations and warranties section very carefully when reviewing a credit agreement, paying close attention to the specific language and the scope of what is being represented. And for borrowers, I have consistently argued that R&W default clauses should include clear materiality standards and should provide the borrower with a reasonable cure period to remediate breaches.

Covenant Defaults: Financial, Maintenance, and Incurrence

The covenant section of a credit agreement is where lenders impose ongoing constraints on the borrower's business operations and financial condition. Covenant defaults occur when the borrower violates these constraints. However, the mechanisms and remedies differ significantly depending on the type of covenant breached.

Financial covenants are typically measured on a periodic basis—quarterly or annually—and relate to the borrower's leverage ratio, interest coverage ratio, debt service coverage ratio, or other metrics derived from the company's financial statements. A typical financial covenant might state: "The Borrower shall maintain, as of the end of each fiscal quarter, a Total Leverage Ratio not to exceed 4.50x, calculated as [specific formula]."

The mechanics of a financial covenant default require that the company's actual leverage ratio exceed the specified maximum at the end of the measurement period. The key technical question is: when exactly does the default occur? Is it the moment the financial statements are released (which might be 45 days after the end of the quarter)? Or is it the moment the covenant test is performed (which might be 15 days later)? This timing question matters because it affects when the lender's right to declare a default arises and when the borrower's cure rights begin.

Most credit agreements require the borrower to certify its compliance with financial covenants within a certain number of days after quarter-end—typically 45 days for quarterly financials and 90 days for annual financials. If the borrower fails to deliver the financial statements on time, this can itself constitute a covenant default (failure to deliver financial statements), which is separate from the substantive covenant test. I have personally negotiated situations where a company was not in breach of its financial leverage ratio, but was nonetheless in default because it failed to deliver its financial statements on time due to a fire in the controller's office or some other temporary disruption.

The grace period for financial covenant defaults is typically zero—meaning that if a company fails a financial covenant test, it is immediately in default. However, most credit agreements include a cure mechanism: if the company fails the test but is otherwise in compliance, the lenders will typically grant a short cure period (often just a few business days) to allow the borrower to prepay some debt and bring itself back into compliance. I have negotiated amendment and restatement agreements where the ability to cure a failed financial covenant was the entirety of the negotiation. The company would miss a leverage ratio by a small amount, offer to pay down some debt, and everyone would move on. But if the lenders were unmotivated to work with the borrower, they could simply wait out the cure period and declare a default.

Maintenance covenants are different from financial covenants in that they relate to ongoing conditions and operational metrics rather than financial ratios. These include covenants such as:

- Maintaining insurance. The borrower must maintain property, casualty, and liability insurance in specified amounts with named additional insured and loss payee provisions.

- Maintaining compliance with law. The borrower must comply with all applicable laws and regulations, including environmental laws, labor laws, and regulatory requirements.

- Maintaining licenses and permits. The borrower must maintain all licenses, franchises, and permits necessary to conduct business.

- Maintaining properties. The borrower must maintain its properties in good condition and repair (with customary wear and tear excepted).

- Maintaining key relationships. The borrower must not allow its relationships with key customers or suppliers to be materially impaired.

These maintenance covenants are typically measured on a "best efforts" or "commercially reasonable efforts" standard, and breach often requires some showing that the company has failed to take reasonable steps to maintain the required condition. This is much more subjective than a financial covenant, and disputes about whether a maintenance covenant has been breached are more common than disputes about whether a leverage ratio has been exceeded.

Incurrence covenants are restrictions on the borrower's ability to take certain actions, such as incurring additional debt, making acquisitions, paying dividends, or making capital expenditures above certain baskets. An incurrence covenant might state: "The Borrower shall not incur any additional Indebtedness except: (a) the refinancing indebtedness, (b) Permitted Liens securing amounts not to exceed $25 million, (c) intercompany indebtedness, and (d) other indebtedness if, on a pro forma basis, the Total Leverage Ratio would not exceed 4.00x."

Incurrence covenants work differently than financial or maintenance covenants because they do not wait for periodic testing; they are tested at the time the incurrence occurs. If a borrower wishes to incur debt, it must calculate on a pro forma basis whether the resulting leverage ratio would exceed the maximum, and if it would, the borrower simply cannot do the transaction without amending the credit agreement.

The interesting thing about incurrence covenants is that a violation does not occur simply by making the incurrence; the violation occurs by making the incurrence that would cause the specified metric to exceed the threshold. So if a company attempts to incur debt that would cause its leverage ratio to exceed the maximum, the incurrence itself violates the credit agreement. However, the consequence of the violation is not the same as a violation of a financial covenant. If a company fails a leverage covenant test, it is in default and the lenders have all their acceleration and remedy rights. But if a company attempts to incur debt and that incurrence would cause a leverage violation, the result is typically that the incurrence is prohibited and does not close. The credit agreement essentially prevents the transaction from occurring rather than allowing it to occur and then imposing a default.

However, if the company goes ahead and incurs the debt anyway (in violation of the incurrence covenant), then it has committed an affirmative breach, and that breach may constitute an Event of Default. I have seen situations where a company, desperate for liquidity during a restructuring, has incurred subordinated debt in violation of its credit agreement's incurrence covenant, betting that the senior lenders would be unwilling to accelerate because doing so would push the company into bankruptcy. That bet sometimes pays off and sometimes does not, depending on the lenders' motivation and the relative value of negotiation versus acceleration.

There is also an important distinction between covenant defaults that give rise to a default under the credit agreement immediately and covenant defaults that only trigger a default if they are not cured within a specified cure period. For financial and incurrence covenants, the default typically arises immediately upon the breach (though there may be a grace period for notification and calculation). For maintenance covenants and certain other covenant violations, there is often a cure period of 10, 30, or 60 days, depending on the nature of the covenant and the difficulty of cure.

The equity cure mechanism deserves special attention because it is how private equity sponsors have historically managed around financial covenant breaches. Most credit agreements contain language that says something like: "Notwithstanding anything to the contrary, the Borrower shall be permitted to cure any failure to comply with a financial covenant by contributing equity capital to the Borrower within [typically 5-10] business days after the end of the measurement period."

Here is how the equity cure works in practice. Suppose a company fails its leverage covenant test at the end of the quarter, with an actual leverage ratio of 4.55x when the maximum was 4.50x. If the company can contribute equity sufficient to pay down $10 million of debt, the pro forma leverage ratio would be 4.45x, and the covenant would be cured. The PE sponsor writes a check for the subordinated equity capital, which the borrower uses to repay debt, and the covenant breach is cured. For many deals with aggressive leverage assumptions, this equity cure mechanism is critical to managing around the covenant quarterly volatility that invariably occurs.

However, there are several limits on the equity cure mechanism that I have negotiated over the years. First, the contribution must be in "equity capital," which typically excludes shareholder loans or other quasi-equity instruments. Second, the contribution cannot be made more than twice per calendar year, or in the aggregate cannot exceed a certain percentage of the annual EBITDA. Third, if the equity cure is used, the measurement period is extended forward by a few days, but the company still needs to be in compliance by the extended date. Fourth, if the company fails to maintain compliance even after the equity cure, the lenders can declare a default. In the depths of the 2008 financial crisis, I saw lenders get much more aggressive about limiting equity cure rights, arguing that they did not want PE sponsors using equity as a way to indefinitely defer inevitable bankruptcies.

What Twenty Years Teaches: Covenant defaults are where the rubber meets the road in leveraged finance. A company can be technically in default on a covenant while remaining a viable business, and a lender's decision about whether to declare the default and exercise remedies is often a judgment call about the business trajectory and the relative value of working things out versus acceleration. I have watched covenant defaults be granted extensive waivers during the COVID-19 pandemic, where lenders recognized that covenant breaches were not reflective of underlying business problems but rather of temporary disruptions. And I have watched covenant defaults be treated as irreversible signs of trouble in other situations, where even a single covenant breach triggered a cascade of amendment negotiations and ultimately a restructuring. The lesson is that a covenant default is not a mechanical thing; it is a judgment call that lenders make based on the broader business and market context.

Judgment Defaults: When the Courts Get Involved

A judgment default clause creates an Event of Default when the borrower becomes subject to a judgment, decree, or order of a court or governmental authority in an amount exceeding a specified threshold. A typical clause reads: "If a final judgment, order, or decree shall be rendered against the Borrower in an amount exceeding $[25,000,000] (individually or in the aggregate) and such judgment shall remain unsatisfied and unstayed for a period of [30-60] days, then the Borrower shall be in default hereunder."

The mechanics are important. First, the judgment must be "final," which typically means that all appeal rights have been exhausted (or the time for appeal has expired). A judgment that is pending appeal does not constitute an EOD. Second, the judgment must exceed the specified threshold, which is often higher than the threshold for covenant defaults because a company might be involved in multiple pieces of litigation that, in the aggregate, could result in large judgments. Third, the borrower must have failed to satisfy the judgment within the specified period (typically 30 to 60 days), giving the borrower time to appeal, post bond, or negotiate a settlement.

The question of what counts as a "judgment" can be surprisingly complex. Does it include only judgments rendered by courts, or does it also include arbitration awards? Most modern credit agreements specify that it includes arbitration awards. Does it include judgments rendered in foreign jurisdictions? This is often negotiated, with some agreements specifying that only judgments from US courts count, and others including judgments from certain foreign jurisdictions if they are enforceable in the United States.

One of the more nuanced questions in judgment default clauses relates to the "materiality" qualifier. Some agreements specify that the judgment must be "not subject to appeal," while others specify that the judgment must be "non-dischargeable in bankruptcy." These qualifiers are important because they distinguish between judgments that the borrower is actively contesting and judgments that are truly final.

I worked on a case involving a company that was subject to a $40 million judgment from a patent infringement lawsuit. The patent holder obtained the judgment, but the company immediately appealed, posting bond for the amount while the appeal was pending. Under the credit agreement's judgment default clause, the judgment was not "final" because appeal rights were being exercised, and therefore no EOD was triggered. However, the judgment was final in the sense that the trial court had entered its order. The lenders debated for weeks whether the appeal bond satisfied the conditions for staying the judgment, and whether the failure to satisfy the judgment "unsatisfied" meant literally unpaid or meant non-discharged through appeal. The dispute was ultimately resolved through an amendment that clarified that a judgment that is under appeal and for which bond has been posted is not an EOD.

Judgment default clauses often include carve-outs for certain types of judgments that the borrower is required to carry insurance for. For example, a judgment arising from a normal course workers' compensation claim or a liability claim for which insurance is required to be maintained might not constitute an EOD, on the theory that the insurance company will pay the judgment. However, if the borrower fails to maintain the required insurance and then is subject to a judgment that insurance would have covered, this could constitute an EOD.

The interaction between judgment defaults and the bankruptcy code is important. Under the bankruptcy code, most judgments are dischargeable in bankruptcy—meaning that if the company files for bankruptcy, the judgment creditor generally becomes an unsecured creditor for the amount of the judgment and shares pro rata in the distribution to unsecured creditors. However, some judgments (such as those for punitive damages or judgments arising from fraud) may be non-dischargeable. This is why some credit agreements specify that the judgment default only applies to non-dischargeable judgments or judgments that would remain a liability even if the company filed for bankruptcy.

What Twenty Years Teaches: Judgment default clauses are actually quite important and often underestimated. Over my career, I have seen several situations where a major judgment in a litigation matter became a catalyst for restructuring discussions. The difference between a judgment that is "final" (and thus triggers an EOD) and a judgment that is under appeal (and thus does not) is a critical legal question that can trigger material consequences. My advice is to always have in-house counsel actively monitoring pending litigation and to engage with the lenders proactively if a material judgment is about to become final. Many lenders will grant a waiver for a judgment EOD if they are informed in advance and given visibility into the company's plan for managing the liability.

ERISA Defaults: A Specialized Category with High Stakes

ERISA defaults exist because lenders have legitimate concerns about pension and benefits obligations that can suddenly become liabilities. A typical ERISA default clause might read: "If (i) any Plan shall have an Unfunded Benefit Liability in excess of $[50,000,000], or (ii) a reportable event requiring notice under ERISA Section 4043 shall occur, or (iii) a notice of intent to terminate any Plan shall be provided, then the Borrower shall be in default hereunder, provided that no default shall be deemed to occur if the Borrower or any of its ERISA Affiliates shall have posted sufficient security or obtained a waiver from the Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation."

The reason lenders care about ERISA is that if a company has substantial unfunded pension liabilities, those liabilities can be triggered and become payment obligations when the company enters bankruptcy or attempts to terminate the pension plan. The PBGC, a quasi-governmental corporation, insures pension benefits, but the insurance is capped, and excess unfunded liabilities become liabilities of the company. In a bankruptcy, an underfunded pension plan can suddenly convert from a contingent liability into a material cash payment obligation.

ERISA defaults are most relevant for larger, established companies with mature pension plans. They are less relevant for smaller companies that have only 401(k) plans without defined benefit features. For larger companies, ERISA defaults are a genuine concern that lenders take seriously.

I worked on a leveraged buyout of a mid-cap manufacturing company that had a substantial pension plan with significant unfunded liabilities. At the time of the LBO, the pension plan had an unfunded liability of approximately $120 million. The credit agreement included an ERISA default clause that made any increase in unfunded liability of more than $10 million an EOD. During the third year of the holding period, the company's investment performance declined and interest rates fell, causing the unfunded liability to increase by $35 million. Technically, this constituted an ERISA default. However, the PE sponsor negotiated a waiver with the lenders by posting a letter of credit equal to a portion of the excess unfunded liability, essentially providing security for the lenders' exposure. The situation was ultimately resolved without an acceleration, but it illustrates how ERISA defaults can become material to leveraged buyout situations.

The specific triggers for ERISA defaults vary. Some agreements focus on the absolute amount of unfunded liability, while others focus on changes in unfunded liability or on specific triggering events such as a "reportable event" under ERISA or a notice of intent to terminate the plan. Some agreements carve out the impact of changes in interest rates or equity market performance on the valuation of unfunded liability, recognizing that some fluctuations in unfunded liability are not reflective of problems with the company's business or pension funding policy.

What Twenty Years Teaches: ERISA defaults are important primarily for larger, more mature companies. But they are often negotiated without sufficient attention being paid to the actual mechanics of ERISA and how pension liability is valued and measured. I have seen credit agreements that contain ERISA default thresholds that are technically breached at closing but that the lenders waive because they did not realize the facts at the time they signed. The lesson is that for any leveraged buyout involving a company with a defined benefit pension plan, the credit agreement's ERISA default language needs to be carefully reviewed, and consideration should be given to whether the company's pension liability is likely to increase during the period the debt is outstanding.

Change of Control Defaults: The Most Heavily Negotiated Category

If payment defaults are the foundation of the EOD taxonomy, change of control defaults are the most heavily negotiated. This is because change of control is where lenders and borrowers have fundamentally different interests. Lenders fear that a change of control could result in a weaker company or a sponsor with less financial resources, making the debt riskier. Borrowers and sponsors view the ability to sell the company or effect a change in control as a fundamental property right that should not be restricted without good reason.

A typical change of control provision reads: "An Event of Default shall occur if (i) any person or group acquires beneficial ownership of more than [35-50]% of the outstanding equity of the Borrower, or (ii) the Continuing Directors are less than a majority of the Board of Directors, or (iii) the Borrower ceases to own, directly or indirectly, substantially all of the assets of [Material Subsidiaries]."

Let me parse this carefully. First, there is the equity threshold—the percentage of beneficial ownership that, if exceeded, constitutes a change of control. This is negotiated heavily. A 35% threshold means that a sponsor or new owner could acquire just over 35% and trigger a change of control. A 50% threshold means that a new owner needs to acquire a majority of equity. The choice reflects the lender's comfort level with changes in majority ownership. In the era of pre-financial crisis easy credit (2005-2007), I saw change of control thresholds as high as 60% in competitive deals. In stressed periods, lenders have pushed the threshold down to 25-30% because they want maximum protection against any reduction in sponsor ownership.

Second, there is the "Continuing Directors" provision. This is a mechanism that allows the original board of directors to control whether a change of control has occurred. The language typically says that a change of control occurs if more than a certain percentage of the board members are directors who were not "continuing directors" at the time the credit agreement was signed. "Continuing directors" are defined as directors who were directors at the time of closing and who are reappointed or remain directors. The idea is that if the original board of directors remains in control and has consented to all decisions, then even if majority equity has changed hands, it is not a "true" change of control because the original board retains veto power over major decisions.

The continuing directors mechanism has an interesting history in M&A practice. In the early 2000s, it became a tool for hostile takeover defense. A company with a strong board could resist a hostile bidder by ensuring that the company's credit agreements required that the majority of continuing directors consent to any change in control. If the takeover proceeded over the board's objection, it would constitute a change of control default, and the debt would be accelerated, making the acquisition more expensive and therefore less attractive to the hostile bidder.

Third, there is typically carve-out language for permitted transfers, which often includes:

- Transfers to permitted holders (typically institutional investors or the original sponsor)
- IPO transactions, where equity is sold to public shareholders
- Transfers in connection with a refinancing, where the same sponsor retains control
- Transfers by will or the laws of descent and distribution

The IPO exception is interesting because it reflects the reality that lenders generally accept that a company can go public without triggering a change of control default, recognizing that the equity is being dispersed to many shareholders and that the company is being subjected to public company governance requirements.

I have personally negotiated change of control language extensively, and I can tell you that the breadth of the carve-outs often determines the true flexibility that a company and sponsor have. In one deal involving a lower-middle-market business services company, the change of control provision had a 40% equity threshold but also included language that permitted transfers to the sponsor's affiliates and permitted transfers to financial buyers that maintained continuity of management. This relatively broad carve-out language gave the sponsor the flexibility to sell the company to a financial buyer or to effect a dividend recap that brought in new equity capital without triggering a change of control default. In another deal involving a larger company with a more conservative lender group, the change of control language was much more restrictive, permitting transfers only to the original sponsor or in an IPO, which significantly limited the sponsor's exit options.

The interaction between change of control and put rights is also important. In many credit agreements, a change of control triggers not an automatic default but instead a put right, giving lenders the option to require the borrower to prepay their loans at 101% or 102% of par, plus accrued interest. The put right is a mechanism that compensates lenders for the uncertainty and risk associated with a change of ownership without outright accelerating the debt. If the new owner is sufficiently strong and the business is attractive, the lenders can simply exercise the put right, get their capital back, and move on. If the new owner is weaker or the business is deteriorating, the lenders might not exercise the put right because they would rather work with the company than get par back at a time when the company is stressed.

I worked on a transformation acquisition where a large private equity firm acquired a mid-cap industrial company from a PE sponsor that had held it for five years. The original credit agreement included a change of control put right exercisable at 102% of par if there was a change in sponsor ownership. When the acquisition was announced, the lenders had to make a determination about whether to exercise their put right. The acquiring sponsor was stronger financially and had a good track record, so the first-lien lenders decided not to exercise the put right, instead staying with the company and the new owner. However, some of the second-lien and mezzanine lenders, concerned about the transition to a new owner, did exercise the put right and got paid off at acquisition closing. This is a classic example of how a change of control put right can serve as a tool for lenders to make differential judgments about risk.

What Twenty Years Teaches: Change of control provisions are where I see the most evolution in credit agreements over the course of my career. In the 1990s and early 2000s, lenders were very restrictive about change of control, essentially trying to lock the sponsor in with the original debt. By the mid-2000s, in a competitive market, change of control provisions had become much more permissive, with broad carve-outs for IPOs, refinancings, and transfers to permitted holders. Post-financial crisis, lenders became more restrictive again. And in recent years, there has been a gradual movement back toward more flexibility, with sponsors pushing for broader carve-outs. The lesson is that a company's ability to effect a sale or change of ownership is fundamentally determined by the breadth of the change of control carve-outs in the credit agreement. If you are a borrower or sponsor, you should negotiate for change of control language that gives you the flexibility you believe you will need. If you are a lender, you should recognize that very restrictive change of control language may make your debt harder to refinance or more expensive when it eventually needs to be refinanced.

INSOLVENCY AND BANKRUPTCY EVENTS OF DEFAULT: THE NUCLEAR OPTION

The final major category of EOD is insolvency, bankruptcy, and related events. These are the true nuclear buttons that lenders have, because they give lenders the right to put a company into bankruptcy.

A typical bankruptcy/insolvency default clause reads: "It shall be an Event of Default if (i) the Borrower shall be unable to pay its debts as they become due, or (ii) the Borrower shall make an assignment for the benefit of creditors, or (iii) a custodian or receiver shall be appointed for the Borrower or any substantial part of its properties, or (iv) the Borrower shall file a voluntary petition in bankruptcy or shall be adjudicated bankrupt or insolvent, or (v) there shall be filed against the Borrower an involuntary petition under the Bankruptcy Code, which petition shall remain undismissed for 60 days, or (vi) the Borrower shall file any answer or other pleading admitting or failing to contest the material allegations of any bankruptcy petition filed against it, or (vii) the Borrower shall take any action in furtherance of, or consent to, any bankruptcy petition."

This language is dense and covers multiple bases. Let me unpack each element.

First, insolvency: "unable to pay its debts as they become due." This is not about having negative net worth (which is the balance sheet test of insolvency); it is about having insufficient liquidity to pay obligations as they come due (the cash flow test of insolvency). In bankruptcy law, this is called "equitable insolvency" and it is a lower threshold than technical insolvency. A company could be technically solvent (with assets exceeding liabilities on a balance sheet basis) but equitably insolvent (lacking the liquidity to pay its debts as they come due). An EOD for equitable insolvency gives lenders the right to intervene when a company is running out of liquidity, even if it is technically not insolvent.

Second, assignment for benefit of creditors: this is a state law remedial tool where a borrower assigns substantially all of its assets to a trustee for the benefit of its creditors. This is much rarer than bankruptcy but does exist in some states, and lenders want to be protected against it.

Third, appointment of a custodian or receiver. This could occur in connection with a court proceeding or in connection with state law receivership. It does not require a bankruptcy filing; it just requires that a court has appointed someone to take over management of the company's assets.

Fourth, voluntary bankruptcy filing. This is straightforward: if the borrower files a voluntary petition under the Bankruptcy Code, it is an EOD. However, this may seem circular—if the company has already filed for bankruptcy, why does the lender need an EOD? The answer is that the EOD declaration can trigger consequences before bankruptcy (such as acceleration of debt, which will then be dealt with in the bankruptcy as a general unsecured claim, along with the other obligations of the company). But for senior secured lenders, the acceleration that occurs immediately upon a bankruptcy filing can be important to establishing priority of claim.

Fifth, involuntary bankruptcy petition. This is where an outside creditor (often a lender, but sometimes a supplier or other creditor) files a bankruptcy petition against the company. Here is where the timing matters: the EOD is not triggered merely by the filing of an involuntary petition; it is triggered if the involuntary petition "remains undismissed for 60 days." This is an important grace period because an involuntary petition can often be dismissed quickly if the company can cure the underlying problem or negotiate with the petitioning creditors. The 60-day period gives the company time to accomplish a cure or negotiation.

The 60-day involuntary bankruptcy period is something I have thought about extensively over my career. The reason for the grace period is that involuntary bankruptcies are somewhat extreme remedies, and sometimes a company that has a payment problem can resolve that problem within 60 days without needing to resort to bankruptcy. However, from a practical standpoint, a company that has an involuntary petition filed against it is in serious distress, and the mere filing of such a petition is a signal that the company's creditworthiness is at risk. I have personally advised lenders to file involuntary bankruptcy petitions for companies that were not paying their obligations, and I have seen those petitions be dismissed within 60 days when the company found alternative financing or when the underlying payment obligation was negotiated. The 60-day period is therefore a real grace period that sometimes works out and sometimes does not.

Sixth, admission or failure to contest. This is a provision that makes it an EOD if the borrower admits to the material allegations of a bankruptcy petition or fails to contest them. The idea is that the borrower cannot indirectly consent to or admit to bankruptcy without triggering an EOD.

Seventh, voluntary action in furtherance of bankruptcy. This covers situations where the borrower does not file but takes actions consistent with a bankruptcy filing—such as hiring a bankruptcy advisor, preparing a bankruptcy filing, or engaging with a court about potential bankruptcy procedures.

The language around voluntary versus involuntary bankruptcy is important because it reflects how the lenders view the severity of each type of filing. A voluntary filing (where the company itself initiates the bankruptcy) is treated as an immediate and absolute EOD because the company has essentially given up and is seeking bankruptcy protection. An involuntary filing (where outside creditors initiate) is treated with a 60-day grace period because it is theoretically possible that the filing could be dismissed and the company could work its way out of the problem.

In practice, the distinction between voluntary and involuntary bankruptcy filings is often less clear than the language might suggest. A company facing distress might engage in negotiations with its primary lenders and, if those negotiations break down, might ultimately file for bankruptcy. The question of whether the filing was "voluntary" (initiated by the company) or "involuntary" (initiated by creditors who lost patience) sometimes turns on negotiation dynamics rather than strict legal form.

I worked on a complex situation involving a specialty chemicals company with multi-tranched debt that was experiencing deteriorating EBITDA. The first-lien lenders were willing to work with the company and the sponsor on a restructuring, but the second-lien lenders, concerned about their recovery, were less flexible. In the course of negotiations, the second-lien lenders threatened to file an involuntary bankruptcy petition if the first-lien lenders did not agree to a comprehensive restructuring that gave the second-lien lenders more favorable treatment. This threat of involuntary bankruptcy effectively put pressure on the first-lien lenders to move negotiations forward, and ultimately a comprehensive restructuring was agreed before the involuntary petition was actually filed. This is a classic example of how bankruptcy EOD provisions can be used as leverage in restructuring discussions.

What Twenty Years Teaches: Bankruptcy and insolvency defaults are where the power dynamic between lenders and borrowers is most stark. A lender can trigger a bankruptcy by filing an involuntary petition if the borrower is not paying its debts. This power is so significant that it is sometimes used as implicit leverage: the lender does not actually file, but the threat of filing shapes the borrower's behavior and negotiating posture. From a borrower's perspective, understanding how bankruptcy EOD provisions work and what triggering events are in their control versus outside their control is crucial to managing the situation. And from a lender's perspective, recognizing that the ability to file an involuntary bankruptcy is a significant power tool is important to understanding how restructurings work in stressed situations.

GRACE PERIODS AND CURE RIGHTS: THE DEVIL IN THE DETAILS

The distinction between notice and cure is fundamental to how EOD clauses actually operate in practice. Many junior practitioners assume that once a condition exists that technically constitutes an EOD, the lender immediately has all of its remedy rights. But this is not how it works. Most credit agreements include grace periods and notice requirements that mean that a borrower might have a technical EOD without the lender having exercised any remedies.

Here is a typical sequence: (1) a covenant is breached, (2) the lender issues written notice of the breach to the borrower, (3) the borrower has a specified cure period (often 10 to 60 days, depending on the type of covenant), (4) if the borrower cures the breach within the cure period, no EOD occurs, (5) if the borrower does not cure within the cure period, the breach has ripened into an EOD, and (6) the lender then has the right to declare the EOD and exercise remedies.

The notice requirement is important because it prevents the automatic cascading of defaults and gives the borrower an opportunity to address a breach before the lender escalates to the default stage. I have seen situations where a company was technically in breach of a covenant for a short period but cured before receiving formal notice from the lender, and the question of whether a "default" had occurred was disputed because the lender had not yet sent a written notice.

The cure period is where much of the negotiation happens around EOD clauses. For financial covenants, the typical cure period is short—often just 3 to 5 business days for the company to deliver financial statements confirming that a previously failed covenant is now met. For maintenance covenants, the cure period might be 30 or 60 days, giving the company time to take remedial action. For other types of defaults, the cure period might vary.

The equity cure mechanism, which I mentioned earlier, is a specific variation on the cure right. With an equity cure, the borrower can cure a financial covenant breach by having the sponsor contribute equity capital that is used to pay down debt, improving the relevant financial ratio. Most credit agreements limit the number of equity cures that can be used in a given time period (typically two per year or a certain number per the life of the loan) and also limit the aggregate amount that can be contributed through equity cure (often capped at a percentage of annual EBITDA or at a fixed dollar amount).

The equity cure mechanism became much more restrictive post-financial crisis. In the easy credit era of 2005-2007, equity cure was frequently available and relatively unconstrained, on the theory that if the sponsor was willing to pump in equity, that was a positive sign that the company was being actively supported. But in 2008-2010, as many equity cure contributions failed to prevent underlying business deterioration, lenders became much more skeptical of equity cure and more willing to limit or eliminate it. I have negotiated credit agreements in recent years where equity cure has been completely eliminated, on the lender's theory that if the company is struggling with leverage, it should restructure the debt rather than have the sponsor keep throwing good money after bad.

Standstill periods and forbearance agreements are mechanisms through which lenders can agree to waive or defer the exercise of their rights with respect to an EOD. A forbearance agreement might say, "The Lenders agree that notwithstanding the existence of an Event of Default, they will not declare the default or exercise remedies for a period of 90 days, on the condition that the Borrower provides weekly cash flow updates and engages in good faith restructuring discussions."

Forbearance agreements are common during stressed periods and are often a mechanism through which lenders buy time for a restructuring to be negotiated while preserving their rights to declare the default and exercise remedies if the restructuring discussions do not progress. I have negotiated many forbearance agreements, and I have seen them work both as effective bridges to a successful restructuring and as prolonged exercises in hope that ultimately led nowhere.

What Twenty Years Teaches: The mechanics of grace periods, notice, and cure rights are where I see young practitioners most often make mistakes in evaluating EOD status. A company might be in technical default for weeks before the lender formally declares it, and the mere existence of a technical default does not necessarily mean the relationship has broken down. Conversely, a lender might declare a default and exercise all its remedies immediately, even if technically the company might have had a cure period, if the lender believes that the company is in such severe distress that waiting for the cure period would be imprudent. Understanding these mechanics is crucial to evaluating the actual risk profile of an EOD situation.

CROSS-DEFAULT AND CROSS-ACCELERATION: REAL-WORLD CASCADE SCENARIOS

I mentioned cross-default and cross-acceleration earlier in the taxonomy section, but the real-world mechanics of how cascades work deserve deeper exploration. Let me walk through some actual situations I have experienced.

The Caesars Entertainment restructuring of 2014-2015 is a good example of how cross-default and cross-acceleration dynamics operate in a large, multi-tranched capital structure. Caesars had been taken private in a massive LBO in 2008 at the height of the credit bubble, loaded with debt at very high leverage. When the financial crisis hit and the company's operating performance deteriorated, the multiple tranches of debt—first-lien loans, second-lien loans, and subordinated notes—began to stress. When it became clear that Caesars would not be able to satisfy its debt obligations through normal operating cash flow, the question became: which tranche would default first?

The dynamics of cross-default in this situation were complex. The first-lien lenders wanted to preserve their position and had the most secured claim on the assets. If they accelerated, they would be positioned to foreclose on substantially all of the company's assets and potentially recover most of their principal. The second-lien lenders had a junior position and knew that if the first-lien lenders accelerated, they would be subordinated to the first-lien recovery. The subordinated noteholders had the lowest position and had minimal hope of recovery in any restructuring scenario.

The way the capital structure was set up, there were cross-default provisions that would cascade acceleration through the tranches. When the company became unable to service its debt, all the lenders had the right to declare a cross-default and accelerate. However, the lenders' incentives were not aligned. The first-lien lenders, as the most senior creditors, had the most to lose from a chaotic restructuring and the most to gain from an orderly process. The subordinated lenders, as the lowest-ranked creditors, had less to lose from acceleration and more to gain from aggressive action that might improve their recovery through a faster restructuring.

What ultimately happened in the Caesars restructuring was that the company, its sponsors, and its lenders negotiated a comprehensive agreement—a "360-day plan," as it was called—that essentially restructured the capital structure by:

1. Converting second-lien debt into new first-lien debt
2. Extending maturities and reducing interest rates
3. Diluting the equity holders significantly but allowing the sponsor to retain some upside
4. Creating a new governance structure with lender representation

This restructuring effectively averted cross-default cascades by negotiating a comprehensive solution that addressed all tranches simultaneously, rather than allowing each tranche to enforce its rights separately, which would have led to a destructive cascade.

Another instructive example is the iHeart Media restructuring of 2014-2016. iHeart had also been taken private in a massive LBO and was loaded with debt. When operating performance deteriorated, the question of cross-default became acute. The first-lien lenders wanted to preserve the going concern value of the business (since as a media company with long-term contracts, liquidation value was likely to be much lower than going concern value). The second-lien lenders and subordinated noteholders wanted to avoid a situation where they would be wiped out in a restructuring.

The iHeart situation is interesting because it illustrates how cross-default provisions interact with subordination agreements and intercreditor agreements. The second-lien lenders, in their intercreditor agreement with the first-lien lenders, had agreed that they would not enforce their cross-default rights unilaterally. Instead, the first-lien lenders had control over what happened to the company. This meant that even though a cross-default technically existed in the second-lien facility, the second-lien lenders could not unilaterally declare the default and accelerate.

However, what the second-lien lenders could do was participate in the negotiation and restructuring process. And indeed, the iHeart restructuring ultimately involved negotiations among the first-lien, second-lien, and subordinated noteholders, with each group agreeing to accept less favorable terms in exchange for the company surviving as a going concern.

From my perspective, these large restructurings—Caesars, iHeart, and others like them—illustrate how cross-default provisions can create incentives for either destructive competition among creditors or for negotiated solutions. If the cross-default provisions are too aggressive and allow junior creditors to unilaterally accelerate, you get a race to the bottom where each creditor tries to protect itself without regard to the collective good. If the cross-default provisions are mitigated by subordination and intercreditor agreements that give the senior creditors control, you get a more orderly process where all the creditors work toward a solution that preserves the going concern value of the business.

What Twenty Years Teaches: The real-world lesson from these large restructurings is that cross-default is not just a mechanical provision; it is a control mechanism embedded in the capital structure. The breadth of cross-default provisions and the presence of subordination agreements and intercreditor agreements fundamentally shape how leverage resolves during stress. And the people negotiating these structures at the time of signing should recognize that their cross-default language is essentially determining who will have the power to make decisions in a future restructuring scenario.

THE BANKRUPTCY EVENT OF DEFAULT: MECHANICS AND STRATEGIC CONSIDERATIONS

I have touched on bankruptcy defaults, but the strategic considerations around how bankruptcy defaults operate deserve deeper exploration because they affect how lenders and borrowers think about bankruptcy risk.

The first distinction is between a voluntary bankruptcy filing (initiated by the company) and an involuntary bankruptcy filing (initiated by outside creditors). In the credit agreement, a voluntary filing is typically an immediate and automatic Event of Default. There is no cure period. The moment the company files a voluntary petition, it has breached the credit agreement, and all of the lender's acceleration and remedy rights are triggered.

An involuntary bankruptcy filing is treated differently. Most credit agreements provide that an involuntary petition must remain "undismissed" for a specified period (typically 60 days) before it constitutes an EOD. This grace period reflects the reality that involuntary petitions can sometimes be dismissed, either because the creditors filing the petition withdraw, or because the company negotiates an immediate cure with the petitioning creditors.

The practical distinction between a 60-day grace period and an immediate default is significant. In a situation where a company is stressed but not yet in bankruptcy, the existence of a 60-day grace period means that the petitioning creditors and the company have 60 days to work out a resolution (payment, settlement, or other arrangement) without triggering an immediate EOD in all the company's other debt facilities. If the resolution is accomplished within 60 days, the involuntary petition is dismissed, and no cross-default is triggered in the other facilities.

However, if the resolution is not accomplished within 60 days, the involuntary petition remains on file, and it becomes an EOD in all the facilities that reference involuntary bankruptcy filings. At that point, if the company has not filed a voluntary petition on its own (with the lenders' coordination), all of the lenders gain the right to accelerate simultaneously.

I have seen situations where the 60-day involuntary bankruptcy period became a critical negotiation window. For example, I worked with a distributor company that had a payment dispute with one of its major suppliers. The supplier, frustrated by months of non-payment and unsuccessful negotiation, filed an involuntary bankruptcy petition. This filing immediately created an EOD (by the involuntary bankruptcy provision) in the credit agreement, and technically, all of the lenders had the right to accelerate. However, because the involuntary petition could be dismissed within 60 days, the lenders and the company agreed to a forbearance period. During this period, negotiations occurred between the company, the supplier, and the company's lenders. Ultimately, the supplier agreed to accept a partial payment and a restructured payment plan, the involuntary petition was withdrawn, and the company was able to continue as a going concern.

The automatic stay provision in the Bankruptcy Code is also relevant to EOD mechanics. When a company files for bankruptcy (voluntary or involuntary, Chapter 7 or Chapter 11), the automatic stay prevents creditors from continuing enforcement actions outside of the bankruptcy. This means that if a lender has accelerated a loan and the company then files for bankruptcy, the lender cannot proceed with collection activities; instead, the lender must present its claim in the bankruptcy.

From the perspective of lenders, this creates a strategic consideration. If a company is in default and likely to file for bankruptcy, the lender might want to declare the default and accelerate immediately, before the bankruptcy filing, in order to have the acceleration on record. Once the company files, the automatic stay applies, but the acceleration is treated as a claim in the bankruptcy.

Conversely, from the perspective of borrowers and sponsors, the automatic stay is a powerful tool. If a lender has accelerated and is moving toward remedies (such as foreclosing on collateral), a bankruptcy filing triggers the automatic stay and halts the enforcement activity. This is why, in many restructuring scenarios, the threat of bankruptcy becomes a negotiating point: if the lenders are being unreasonable, the company can threaten to file for bankruptcy and trigger the automatic stay, forcing the lenders to come to the negotiating table.

I worked on a situation where a company had defaulted on its debt and the lenders had accelerated and were preparing to foreclose on substantially all of the company's assets. The sponsor and company management met with their restructuring advisor, who advised that the optimal strategy was to file for Chapter 11 bankruptcy before the lenders could complete the foreclosure. Filing for bankruptcy would trigger the automatic stay, would give the company a chance to negotiate a plan of reorganization with its creditors, and would prevent the lenders from liquidating the assets in a manner that might be disadvantageous to the business as a going concern. The threat of a bankruptcy filing was sufficient to bring the lenders back to the negotiating table, and a comprehensive restructuring was ultimately agreed without a bankruptcy filing.

What Twenty Years Teaches: The intersection of EOD provisions, bankruptcy law, and strategic considerations is one of the most complex and important areas of leveraged finance. Understanding not just the mechanical trigger for an involuntary bankruptcy EOD (60 days undismissed) but also the strategic considerations around when lenders will file involuntary petitions, when borrowers will file voluntary petitions, and how the automatic stay affects everyone's incentives is crucial to understanding how restructurings actually work.

REMEDIES UPON EVENT OF DEFAULT

Once an Event of Default has been declared, the lenders have several remedies available to them. Understanding these remedies is crucial because they shape the practical leverage that lenders have and the choices available to borrowers in a stressed situation.

The primary remedy is acceleration—the declaration that all outstanding principal, accrued interest, and unpaid fees are immediately due and payable. Acceleration transforms the credit agreement from a document with scheduled payment obligations extending into the future into a document requiring immediate, full repayment.

The mechanics of acceleration depend on the credit agreement language. Some agreements provide for automatic acceleration upon the occurrence of certain Events of Default (such as bankruptcy or involuntary insolvency). Other agreements provide for discretionary acceleration, meaning that the lenders have the option to accelerate but are not required to do so.

The threshold for declaring acceleration is also important. Most credit agreements provide that acceleration is at the election of "the Required Lenders," which is typically defined as lenders representing at least 50.1% or 66.67% of the outstanding principal amount. This means that a single lender, even if it represents a significant portion of the facility, cannot unilaterally declare acceleration. Instead, a majority (or supermajority) of lenders must agree.

The reason for the majority requirement is to prevent any single lender from having the ability to blow up the facility. If one lender could unilaterally accelerate, then in any situation where that lender's interests diverged from the other lenders' interests, that lender could force an acceleration, which could push the company into bankruptcy or force a restructuring, even if the other lenders preferred to work with the company.

However, the majority requirement also creates potential for deadlock or strategic behavior. If some lenders want to accelerate and others do not, negotiations must occur to resolve the disagreement. I have been involved in situations where the holdout lenders who did not want to accelerate were able to extract significant concessions (such as higher fees, faster amortization, or better recovery terms) in exchange for supporting acceleration or, conversely, in exchange for agreeing not to block the lenders' desire to work toward a restructuring.

Beyond acceleration, the remedies available to lenders upon an EOD include:

Exercise of remedies against collateral: If the debt is secured, the lenders have the right to foreclose on the collateral. This might involve taking control of bank accounts, seizing inventory, taking possession of equipment, or exercising control over receivables. The mechanics of how collateral remedies are exercised depend on the type of collateral and the state law governing the collateral.

Setoff rights: If the borrower is also a customer or depositor at the lending bank, the bank might have the right to setoff amounts owed as a deposit against amounts due on the loan. For example, if a company has a $5 million outstanding loan and also has a $2 million deposit in the bank's operating account, the bank might setoff the $2 million deposit against the loan, reducing the amount owing to $3 million.

Control of the company: Some credit agreements give lenders the right to assume control of the company's bank accounts, cash management, and working capital in the event of a default. This might involve the lenders exercising control over which invoices get paid and which do not, essentially allowing the lenders to function as operating creditors with the right to direct the flow of cash.

These remedies are all theoretically available upon an EOD. However, in practice, the exercise of remedies is often constrained by practical, legal, and strategic considerations.

From a practical standpoint, if a company is in default, the lenders might not immediately exercise all available remedies because doing so might destroy the value of the company. For example, seizing all of the company's inventory might damage customer relationships or supplier relationships, reducing the ultimate recovery. Controlling the company's cash might prevent it from making necessary operational expenditures. And accelerating the debt might force a bankruptcy filing, which might be more expensive and disruptive than a negotiated restructuring.

From a legal standpoint, there are constraints on remedies. If the company has filed for bankruptcy, the automatic stay prevents lenders from exercising remedies (except within the bankruptcy process). If there are other creditors with higher priority claims, those creditors might have their own claims on the collateral. And if the company is a regulated entity (such as a bank, insurance company, or utility), regulatory law might constrain what remedies are available.

From a strategic standpoint, lenders often use the threat of exercising remedies as leverage to force restructuring negotiations. Rather than actually foreclosing on collateral or seizing bank accounts (which might destroy value and harm recovery), lenders might threaten these actions in order to pressure the company toward a comprehensive amendment that includes higher pricing, tighter covenants, and increased lender control. I have been in many situations where the implicit threat of remedies has been more powerful than the actual exercise of remedies would have been.

What Twenty Years Teaches: Over two decades, I have learned that the actual exercise of remedies is much rarer than the threatened exercise of remedies. Sophisticated lenders recognize that the real value of their remedies lies in the leverage they provide, not in actually using them. The most successful restructurings I have been involved with have been those where the lenders have used the credible threat of remedies to motivate all parties toward a negotiated solution, rather than actually accelerating and exercising remedies in a destructive manner.

STRATEGIC USE AND ABUSE OF EVENT OF DEFAULT PROVISIONS

This brings me to one of the most important parts of my career lesson: understanding that EOD clauses are not merely technical provisions for responding to actual business failure. They are strategic tools that lenders use to generate leverage, that borrowers use to manage their exposure, and that restructuring professionals use to navigate toward solutions.

Lenders use EOD clauses strategically in several ways. First, they use the threat of declaring a default as leverage in amendment negotiations. If a company is approaching a covenant breach, the lenders might approach the company and suggest that rather than waiting for the breach to occur and then waiving the default, the company should immediately agree to an amendment that provides more favorable terms to the lenders (higher pricing, additional fees, tighter covenants, additional collateral, or a combination of these). The threat is implicit: if you do not agree to the amendment, we will wait for the covenant breach to occur and then demand these terms as the price of our waiver.

I have negotiated dozens of amendments where the lender's leverage was based entirely on the company's impending breach of a covenant. The company would come to me and ask whether paying down debt to avoid the breach would be better or whether accepting an amendment with higher pricing would be better. The answer depends on the specific numbers, but the key point is that the lenders are using the EOD clause as leverage.

Second, lenders sometimes intentionally allow defaults to occur in order to create the ability to renegotiate. I worked with a lender group that, when approaching a refinancing, deliberately withheld covenant relief that it could have provided, in order to allow the company to experience a covenant breach and thus create "forced" negotiations around the refinancing. This is somewhat aggressive behavior, but it is a real strategy that some lenders employ.

Third, lenders use EOD clauses to establish a legal basis for increasing the interest rate or creating "default interest." When a company is in default, many credit agreements allow the lender to increase the interest rate (typically by 200-500 basis points) as default interest, to compensate for the increased risk. This default interest can be a substantial cost to the company and creates additional incentive to cure the default.

Borrowers, conversely, use strategies to manage around EOD provisions. First, they actively manage covenants to avoid reaching trigger levels. This might involve prepaying debt to improve ratios, timing asset sales to boost cash flow, or strategically timing acquisitions or divestitures to manage around covenant breaches.

Second, borrowers use the equity cure mechanism (as I discussed earlier) to cure covenant breaches by having sponsors contribute equity capital to pay down debt.

Third, borrowers and their restructuring advisors work to negotiate forbearance agreements or covenant waivers before a breach actually occurs. If management can see that a covenant breach is coming in the next quarter, a good restructuring strategy is to engage with the lenders proactively, present a plan for how the company will return to compliance (whether through operational improvements, asset sales, or other actions), and ask for a waiver or modification of the covenant.

Fourth, borrowers sometimes strategically take actions that allow them to avoid an EOD by technically complying with a different definition or provision. For example, if a cross-default clause has a $50 million threshold, and the company is approaching default on another facility, the company might negotiate a settlement of the other facility at $45 million specifically to stay below the cross-default threshold.

One interesting development over my career has been the emergence of "covenant holidays." This term, popularized during the COVID-19 pandemic, refers to temporary suspensions of financial covenant testing. During 2020, when the pandemic created unprecedented uncertainty about near-term business performance, lenders and borrowers entered into amendments that suspended financial covenant testing for two or four quarters, allowing companies to preserve liquidity by not having to maintain specific leverage or coverage ratios while the pandemic was ongoing.

The covenant holiday concept is interesting because it inverts the typical dynamic of EOD provisions. Rather than using EOD provisions to enforce tight constraints on the borrower, the parties mutually agree to suspend the constraints temporarily in recognition of abnormal circumstances. This represents a more sophisticated understanding of how leverage works: leverage is not just about technical defaults, but about the relationship between lenders and borrowers and their shared interest in the success of the business.

I also want to address the "J. Crew problem," which has become a shorthand in my mind for how EOD provisions can interact with other aspects of capital structure to create problematic dynamics. J. Crew's bankruptcy in 2020 was precipitated by a perfect storm of factors, but one key element was the interaction between leverage, operating challenges, and the equity cure mechanism.

J. Crew had been taken private in an LBO and accumulated substantial leverage. As retail trends shifted and the company's performance deteriorated, the company began approaching leverage covenant breaches. The PE sponsor, committed to the asset and concerned about losing its investment, used the equity cure mechanism repeatedly to fund debt paydowns that allowed the company to stay below covenant thresholds. However, these equity contributions did not address the underlying business problems—declining sales, shift to e-commerce, etc. What ultimately happened was that the sponsor ran out of willingness to fund additional equity cures, the company finally breached covenants that could not be cured, and the company entered bankruptcy. The lesson from J. Crew is that equity cure can become a mechanism for deferring inevitable problems rather than for solving temporary issues.

Another real-world example I want to address is the Serta Simmons "uptiering" transaction. Serta Simmons went through a series of refinancings that involved complex transactions designed to improve the company's capital structure and maintain leverage at acceptable levels. These transactions involved the use of uptiering—the idea that if you refinance the company's debt with new term loans at higher leverage, the existing lenders can exit with par, and the new lenders take the higher leverage risk. These transactions were viewed as creative ways to manage around covenant breaches and refinancing risk, but they also involved the inherent risk that if the new uptiered debt did not perform well, the company would be loaded with even more leverage than before.

What Twenty Years Teaches: Understanding how EOD provisions can be used strategically is crucial to being a good leveraged finance professional. The naive view is that EOD provisions are mechanical tools that lenders use only when things go truly wrong. But the sophisticated view is that EOD provisions are strategic tools that lenders use throughout the life of a facility to negotiate better terms, enforce discipline, and preserve optionality. This does not mean that lenders are always acting in bad faith or that the strategic use of EOD is always harmful to borrowers. Sometimes lenders using EOD provisions strategically leads to productive negotiations that improve the terms of the facility and increase the probability of success. Other times, it leads to adversarial relationships that ultimately damage the company. The key is understanding the dynamics and having the judgment to know when to deploy them and when to use other tools.

LITIGATION AND DISPUTES AROUND EVENTS OF DEFAULT

Despite the specificity of EOD provisions, disputes about whether an EOD has actually occurred are surprisingly common. These disputes tend to fall into several categories.

The first category is disputes about the objective facts. Did the company actually make the payment when due? Did the company actually comply with the covenant test? The most straightforward disputes are about observable facts. However, even these can be contentious. I have been involved in disputes about whether a wire transfer was actually received by the lender's account, about the exact calculation of leverage ratio (when rounding or timing of closing adjustments can affect the result), and about whether the company had actually made certain material representations.

The second category is disputes about the interpretation of the EOD language. For example, what exactly constitutes a "Material Adverse Effect"? What does "best efforts" mean in the context of maintaining customer relationships? When the credit agreement refers to "Material Indebtedness," does that include contingent liabilities and accrued but not yet due obligations?

I worked on a case where the credit agreement's cross-default clause referred to defaults under "Material Indebtedness." The company had an off-balance-sheet financing arrangement that was structured as an operating lease rather than as debt. When the leasing company claimed that the company had violated the lease, the lender claimed this constituted a cross-default under the Material Indebtedness provision. The company argued that an operating lease was not "indebtedness." The resolution of this dispute, which took several months and involved extensive legal briefing, determined whether a default existed in the credit facility.

The third category is disputes about whether the lender has properly exercised its rights. Most credit agreements provide that the lender must send written notice of a default before exercising remedies. What happens if the lender fails to send proper notice? Can the borrower claim that the default was never properly declared? Can the borrower assert that the lender's failure to provide notice waived the default?

These questions have generated case law that is not always entirely consistent. Some courts have held that if a lender fails to comply with notice requirements in the credit agreement, that failure prevents the lender from exercising remedies. Other courts have held that while failure to provide notice might breach the lender's contractual obligations, it does not prevent the lender from exercising remedies under the facility.

The fourth category is disputes about remedies. If an EOD has been properly declared, can the lender choose which remedies to exercise? Can the lender accelerate without first attempting a workout? Can the lender foreclose on collateral without notice? The answer to these questions typically depends on what the credit agreement says about remedies.

One area where disputes have become more prominent is the "no action" clause. Many credit agreements include language that says something like: "No failure or delay by the Lenders in exercising any right under this Agreement shall operate as a waiver thereof, nor shall any single or partial exercise preclude any other or further exercise or the exercise of any other right." This language is designed to prevent a lender from inadvertently waiving rights through inaction or partial exercise.

However, borrowers have sometimes argued that a lender's prolonged inaction in declaring a default (even though the default technically existed) constitutes an implied waiver of the default. The "no action" language is designed to prevent this argument, but it does not always work. I have seen situations where a lender waited weeks or months to declare a default, and a court concluded that despite the "no action" language, the lender's long delay constituted a waiver.

Another area of dispute is the "phantom default"—a situation where the lender claims a default exists but the borrower disputes that the conditions for the default have actually been met. These disputes typically arise around representations and warranties defaults, where the lender claims that a representation has proven false and therefore an EOD exists, but the borrower disagrees about whether the representation was actually false or about whether the falsity is material enough to constitute an EOD.

I worked on a case where the lender claimed that the borrower's representation about the status of pending litigation was false because a settlement amount had increased from what was disclosed at closing. The lender argued that this constituted a representation breach because the material settlement obligation had expanded. The borrower argued that the increase in settlement amount was not a falsity of the original representation but was instead a normal evolution of settlement negotiations, and that the representation had been accurate as of the closing date. The dispute required the hiring of litigation counsel and consumed several months before being resolved through settlement and waiver.

What Twenty Years Teaches: Disputes about whether an EOD has actually occurred are common enough that every leverage finance professional should develop comfort with the possibility that EOD language will be disputed. The moral of the story is that EOD language should be drafted as clearly and specifically as possible, with defined terms that are understood by both lenders and borrowers. Ambiguous language in an EOD clause is dangerous because it creates room for interpretation and dispute, especially in stressed situations where the lenders and borrowers have divergent interests.

CONCLUSION: WHAT TWENTY YEARS TEACHES ABOUT EVENTS OF DEFAULT

Over twenty-three years in leveraged finance, I have seen the Events of Default section of credit agreements evolve significantly. In the early 1990s, when I started in this business, EOD language was often quite broad and lender-friendly, giving lenders maximum flexibility to declare defaults and exercise remedies. Post-financial crisis, there was a movement toward more borrower-friendly language that included clearer materiality thresholds and more robust cure periods. During the hot credit markets of 2004-2007, lenders pushed hard for broad and aggressive EOD language. In 2008-2010, borrowers pushed back. In recent years, we have seen a kind of equilibrium, with EOD language being relatively balanced between lender and borrower interests.

But the most important lesson I have learned is that the specific language of EOD provisions matters less than the relationships and trust between lenders and borrowers. In situations where there is genuine trust and communication, technical defaults are often waived or modified without destroying value or requiring restructuring. In situations where there is adversarial relationships and poor communication, technical defaults can trigger cascades of remedy exercises and push companies into bankruptcy.

The second lesson is that the junior people who read the EOD section carefully and understand the nuances and interactions with other provisions are providing enormous value. I have watched junior analysts catch issues in cross-default language that senior folks missed because the junior analyst actually read the definitions section carefully. I have watched young practitioners negotiate covenant language that ended up being far more protective to their clients than the old standard language that everyone was used to.

The third lesson is that EOD provisions are strategic tools, not merely technical provisions for when things go wrong. Understanding how to use them strategically (as a lender) or how to avoid triggering them strategically (as a borrower) is crucial to being an effective leverage finance professional.

And the final lesson is that the most successful leverage finance professionals are those who understand that leverage works through relationships and trust, not through the force of legal language. The best credit agreements are those that both lenders and borrowers genuinely want to follow, not those that lenders are constantly threatening to invoke or borrowers are constantly trying to evade. Building that alignment—where lenders and borrowers have shared interests in the success of the business and in the appropriate management of leverage—is the real skill.

Events of Default clauses will always be part of leverage finance. They serve an important purpose, creating the legal foundation for how lenders will respond if things go wrong. But how that legal foundation gets used depends far more on the quality of the relationships and the sophistication of the parties involved than on the specific language in the credit agreement.
