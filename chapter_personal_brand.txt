BUILDING YOUR PERSONAL BRAND IN LEVERAGED FINANCE

By Federico

When I was a junior analyst at a mid-market bank in London in 2003, I had no conception whatsoever of what a "personal brand" even meant. The industry hadn't yet begun to obsess over individual visibility, social media was a university-based novelty called Friendster, and if you wanted to establish credibility in leveraged finance, you did what everyone did: you showed up on time, you worked the impossible hours, you impressed your deal sponsors, and you slowly, incrementally built a reputation through the quality of your work and the relationships you cultivated face-to-face.

Today, as I sit here twenty-plus years later having worked across Nordic and Continental European leveraged finance markets, having led debt financing for multiple European mega-deals, having advised some of Europe's largest private equity sponsors, and having built a network that spans from Lisbon to Helsinki, I can tell you with absolute certainty that while the fundamentals remain unchanged—technical excellence and human relationships still matter more than anything else—the pathways to building a meaningful personal brand have transformed completely. And they matter now in ways that would have seemed absurd to my younger self.

This chapter is about how you build a personal brand in leveraged finance that actually means something. Not vanity metrics. Not LinkedIn engagement farming. Not the hollow accumulation of Twitter followers who don't know a leverage covenant from a financial maintenance covenant. I'm talking about building genuine, durable credibility in an industry where credibility is the literal foundation of everything: your job security, your deal flow, your ability to attract talent, your negotiating power, and your ultimate trajectory from wherever you are now toward whatever you actually want to become.

The remarkable thing I've discovered over twenty years is that building a real personal brand in leveraged finance requires you to do something that sounds deceptively simple: you have to actually become excellent at your craft, and you have to be willing to be publicly associated with that excellence in increasingly visible ways. There is no fake version of this that works. You cannot position yourself as a thought leader while doing mediocre technical work. You cannot build a network of serious people by posting superficial market commentary. You cannot become someone that sponsors want to work with if your actual deal work is uninspiring.

What changed between 2003 and 2024 is that now you have vehicles—professional platforms, publication channels, speaking circuits, and networks—that allow genuine expertise to become visible and portable in ways that simply didn't exist before. You can now take the work you're doing, the insights you're developing, and the perspective you're building, and you can amplify it to audiences that matter. But only if you do it authentically and with genuine value embedded in everything you create.

Over the next several sections, I'm going to walk you through how to build that brand systematically across every major vector: from your LinkedIn strategy through your thought leadership, your conference participation, your writing discipline, your networking approach, your mentorship responsibilities, and your career positioning across the various transitions that make up a leveraged finance career. By the end of this chapter, you'll have a concrete, actionable framework for building a personal brand that doesn't just look impressive on paper—it actually generates opportunities, amplifies your impact, and positions you for the career trajectory you actually want.


LINKEDIN STRATEGY FOR LEVFIN PROFESSIONALS: BUILDING AN AUDIENCE THAT MATTERS

Let me be direct: LinkedIn matters for leveraged finance professionals, but probably not in the way you think it does. If you approach LinkedIn as a place to post motivational business quotes, celebrate promotions, and network-farm with connection requests to every person who shares a surname with someone you once knew, you're wasting time. If, however, you approach LinkedIn as a serious platform for establishing and amplifying technical expertise, then it becomes one of the most powerful tools you have.

I didn't really engage with LinkedIn strategically until about 2012. I had a profile, sure, but it was dormant and largely forgotten. Around that time, a senior banker colleague—someone I genuinely respected—started regularly posting thoughtful commentary about European LBO market developments. His posts were substantive. They referenced actual deals, actual market conditions, actual technical innovations. They weren't salesy or self-promotional. They were the kind of thing that a busy Managing Director might read on a Sunday morning and think, "That's someone who actually knows what they're talking about."

I watched what happened to his profile. Within six months, he had more than two thousand meaningful connections. Within a year, sponsors were approaching him directly about advisory work. Within eighteen months, he moved to a leading advisory boutique partly because of the credibility he'd built through that platform. He hadn't just gained followers; he'd built a reputation. And that reputation translated directly into professional opportunity.

That's when I decided to approach LinkedIn seriously. But I had a clear thesis: I would only post content that met three criteria. First, it had to be technically sound. If I was going to comment on leverage multiples, liquidity covenants, or European credit market dynamics, I had to be accurate to the decimal point. Not approximately right—actually right. Second, it had to say something that wasn't obvious. The financial press is full of people regurgitating market commentary that anyone can read in The Economist or Bloomberg. I decided that anything I posted had to contain some perspective or insight that wasn't already in the public domain. Third, it had to be written clearly enough that it was actually useful to someone reading it. Dense, acronym-filled prose that showed off how much you know is just narcissism disguised as expertise.

Here's what I started doing in practice. I would typically post once every ten days to two weeks. That frequency is deliberate. More often than that feels like you're desperate for engagement; less often than that and you lose momentum and visibility. When I had something substantive to say—which happened organically as I worked through deals, attended conferences, read market research, and thought about trends—I would write it out. I would ruthlessly edit it multiple times. I would read it aloud to hear if it sounded natural. And only then would I post it.

The topics varied. Sometimes I would write about a technical innovation in the debt markets—the emergence of continuation funds, the rise of subscription lending, the way private equity sponsors were beginning to structure more complex liability management exercises. Sometimes I would write about market conditions—what it meant when spreads widened to certain levels, or tightened to others. Sometimes I would write about career development advice that I'd learned or observed. Sometimes I would share a major market development and my perspective on what it meant.

I almost never posted content that was primarily about promoting myself, my firm, or my services. And remarkably, that was what worked. By being useful and substantive, and by never explicitly asking people to hire me or do business with me, I actually became the person that people wanted to hire and do business with.

The audience I built on LinkedIn—over time I accumulated more than fifteen thousand connections—wasn't a random mass. It was specifically people in leveraged finance, private equity, debt markets, and professional services. I could identify them because they engaged meaningfully with my content. They didn't just like it; they commented thoughtfully, they shared it with their networks, they sometimes took issue with a point I'd made and engaged in actual discussion. That's what an audience in your space actually looks like.

Here's what you need to know about building an audience on LinkedIn that will actually matter for your career. First, consistency beats virality every single time. You're not trying to go viral. You're trying to show up as a reliable, thoughtful source of perspective month after month after month. That compounds. Second, usefulness beats cleverness. Write for someone who is actually busy—someone like your Managing Director, or a sponsor you want to work for—and ask yourself: would this person find this valuable or interesting? If the answer is no, don't post it. Third, engagement is bidirectional. When people comment on your posts, you need to respond thoughtfully. When people post things that interest you, you should engage. You're building a community, not broadcasting into the void.

The mechanics of what to post matter less than the underlying principle. Some of my most valuable posts have been substantive analysis of a credit event or covenant package from a major deal. Some have been market observations about what's happening in the underwriting market or the sponsors' side. Some have been career advice drawn from watching hundreds of people move through leveraged finance. Some have been brief reactions to market news with actual insight added. The common thread wasn't the format; it was the value.

I'll give you a specific example. In 2019, I published a post that walked through what I'd learned about the new LPC lending guidelines and what they meant for deal structuring. It wasn't groundbreaking, but it was accurate, practical, and directly applicable to what people were working on. That single post generated more than one hundred comments, was shared by hundreds of people, and directly resulted in three conversations with sponsors who said they'd read it and wanted to understand my perspective on upcoming debt structures. One of those conversations turned into a significant advisory mandate. The return on the hour I spent writing that post was substantial.

What won't work: engagement farming, self-promotion, excessive posting, posting about things outside your area of expertise, getting into political or religious arguments, or treating LinkedIn like it's your personal diary. What will work: consistent delivery of valuable perspective, technical accuracy, clear writing, genuine engagement with your community, and patience.

The final thing I'll say about LinkedIn is this: building audience there is a side effect of actually having something valuable to say. You cannot fake expertise on a platform where your entire audience either works in your industry or is knowledgeable enough to call you out when you're wrong. And that's exactly what makes it valuable. If you have a thousand connections on LinkedIn and five of them are sponsors, and two of those sponsors trust you as a legitimate expert, that network is worth more to your career than ten thousand random followers anywhere else.


THOUGHT LEADERSHIP: WRITING ARTICLES AND DEVELOPING PERSPECTIVES ON MARKET DEVELOPMENTS

Thought leadership has become one of those terms that makes me slightly cringe, primarily because it's overused, often applied to people who haven't actually thought very deeply about anything in their lives, and frequently deployed as a cover for what is really just marketing. But let me reclaim the term for something more honest: developing and sharing your authentic perspective on important developments in the market you inhabit, and doing so in a way that helps others understand those developments better. That's thought leadership.

I didn't set out in my career with the goal of "becoming a thought leader." What actually happened is that I worked on a lot of deals, I paid close attention to what was working and what wasn't, I read voraciously, I paid attention to market developments, I thought about implications, and over time I developed perspectives on the industry that seemed worth sharing. The vehicle for sharing them started with articles.

In roughly 2010, I began writing articles for industry publications. These weren't thought pieces in the self-aggrandizing sense; they were substantive analyses of trends I was observing. The first few I wrote were directly related to deals I was working on, but from a perspective that was broad enough not to compromise confidentiality. I wrote about the structuring patterns emerging in European LBO financings, the way sponsors were beginning to think about dividend recap timing differently, the impact that new regulatory regimes in various European countries were having on credit markets.

What I discovered was that this kind of writing created several benefits simultaneously. First, it forced me to think rigorously about things that I kind of understood but hadn't fully articulated. Writing requires clarity in ways that thinking alone doesn't force. Second, it created a public record that I had thought carefully about the topic. When I was in a negotiation with lawyers or sponsors about a technical point, and I had published an article on that very topic, it shifted the dynamic. I wasn't just asserting my view; I had publicly reasoned through it. Third, it opened doors.

Here's a concrete example. In 2013, I wrote an article about the emerging complexity in leverage definitions and what it meant for how deals were being structured. The article was published in a moderately well-read European finance publication. I wrote it because I'd just worked through a really complicated leverage definition negotiation, and I'd noticed that most of the banking community seemed to be approaching the issue in ways that missed important implications. The article was technical, but it was readable. It had actual examples. It had a clear point of view.

Within six weeks of publication, I received outreach from three different sponsors asking me to advise on debt structures for upcoming deals, directly referencing the article. One of those engagements turned into a two-year advisory retainer with one of Europe's largest sponsors. The return on the time I invested in that article—maybe twenty hours of research, writing, and editing—was extraordinary.

The path to meaningful thought leadership in leveraged finance requires understanding something important: you don't need to be the world's most famous expert to be genuinely useful. You need to be an expert in something specific that other people care about. The more niche your expertise, the more valuable it often is. I never positioned myself as a generalist voice on the entire leveraged finance market. I positioned myself as someone with deep expertise in Nordic LBO structures, in European covenant frameworks, in debt capital markets dynamics in mid-market and lower mid-market deals, and in the intersection of sponsor strategy and debt structuring. Those were areas where I had worked extensively, developed real perspective, and could speak with genuine authority.

This is important: don't write thought leadership pieces on topics where you don't actually have thought leadership. I have strong opinions about a lot of things—perhaps too strong. But I don't write articles or publish perspectives on things like early-stage venture capital markets or international trade policy or geopolitical developments, because while I read about them and have opinions, I don't have the deep knowledge that would make my perspective distinctive. Instead, I write about things where I have spent thousands of hours, worked on dozens of transactions, developed real pattern recognition about what works and what doesn't, and can offer perspectives that someone reading the piece will actually learn from.

Here's my framework for developing thought leadership content that matters. First, identify what you actually know deeply. Spend a couple of days brainstorming things that you've spent years mastering, things that you get asked about repeatedly, things that you've seen evolve significantly over the course of your career. Write those down. Second, think about what's changing in those areas. Markets evolve, regulations shift, sponsor strategies adapt. What's different now compared to where things were five years ago? What are the implications of those changes? Third, ask yourself whether you have insights that aren't already in the public domain. If what you'd write is just a summary of what everyone already knows, don't write it. But if you have a perspective based on your experience that would add something, write it.

The writing itself requires discipline. I spend typically two to three weeks researching and thinking about a substantial article before I write it. I draft it in large chunks, then I edit ruthlessly. I usually go through at least three complete rewrites before I let anyone else see it. Then I get feedback from someone I trust—typically someone senior who knows the topic. I incorporate that feedback, I do another pass, and only then do I submit it for publication or share it with my network.

What do you write about? I've published articles on topics like: the evolution of covenant frameworks in European LBOs, the way private equity sponsors approach debt management differently based on market conditions, the impact of negative interest rates on leverage calculations, the structuring of continuation funds and the implications for debt negotiations, the rise of subscription lending and what it means for traditional syndicated lending, the role of European asset-based lending in financing sponsor platforms, the implications of regulatory changes on deal structuring, and the way liability management exercises have evolved as a sponsor tool. Each of these emerged from actual deal experience and genuine observation of how the market was changing.

Once you publish an article—whether in a major publication, a trade journal, an industry newsletter, or your own platform—it becomes a permanent part of your public record. When someone researches you, they'll find it. When you're negotiating a complex technical point, that article is evidence that you've thought deeply about it. When you're competing with other advisors for mandates, that body of work differentiates you. Over twenty years, I've published somewhere in the region of thirty to forty substantive articles. They didn't all change the world, but collectively, they established me as someone who thinks carefully about the industry and develops legitimate perspectives.

The other benefit of consistent thought leadership is that it makes you quotable. Over time, when journalists write about leveraged finance, when conference organizers put together panels, when deals get scrutinized, people think of you as someone worth getting a comment from. Being known as someone with a thoughtful perspective on the market is worth more than you might imagine in terms of your professional credibility and visibility.


CONFERENCE SPEAKING: HOW TO GET INVITED, HOW TO PREPARE, THE CIRCUIT THAT MATTERS

My first conference speaking opportunity in leveraged finance came in 2007 at a regional deal-making summit in Copenhagen. I was invited to be on a panel about Nordic LBO trends. I was terrified. I'd never spoken publicly to an audience larger than maybe forty people. I have a slight accent that was more pronounced then. I was only a senior manager at the time, and the other panelists were all MDs from major banks. I nearly turned it down.

I'm very glad I didn't. That forty-minute panel—where I said very little, mostly listened to the other panelists, and shared one decent observation about how sponsor preferences were shifting in Nordic markets—changed my professional trajectory in ways that were impossible for me to predict at the time.

Three people approached me afterward. One was a sponsor looking to discuss a potential deal. Another was a journalist who wanted to discuss Nordic LBO trends for an article. The third was a professional services executive who was interested in my perspective on market developments. None of those conversations immediately turned into massive opportunities, but collectively, they began a process of establishing myself as someone worth listening to in Nordic leveraged finance.

From that point forward, I made speaking at relevant conferences a strategic priority. Now, twenty years later, I speak at somewhere between fifteen and twenty conferences per year. Some are large—the major European buyout conferences with thousands of attendees. Some are more specialized—annual dinners for private equity groups, roundtables for sponsors and advisors, regional deal conferences. Some are podcast appearances or webinar panels. Some are for professional associations. The venues vary, but the underlying principle is consistent: I show up, I present credibly, I share genuine expertise, and I let my reputation compound.

Here's what you need to understand about speaking as a professional brand-building tool. First, the right conferences matter; the wrong ones are time-wasters. If you're building a brand in leveraged finance, you want to speak at events where your relevant audience will be present. That typically means: major European or global LBO conferences, regional sponsor dinners, law firm seminars on deal structures, banking conferences, debt capital markets forums, and industry association events. It does not mean every local business breakfast or regional networking event. Be selective.

Second, you need to get invited. Conferences have limited slots. Major conferences can have fifty or more panels across three days, which sounds like a lot until you realize that means there are hundreds of potential panelists competing for those slots. How do you get selected? Here's the honest answer: if you're not well-known, you start by being useful. You reach out to conference organizers with ideas for panels that you think their audience would find valuable. You volunteer. You offer to put together a panel and identify other speakers if they'll give you a speaking slot. You work with law firms, financial advisors, or other professional services firms to be positioned as a subject matter expert speaker. You attend the conference as a participant first, you network while you're there, you establish relationships with organizers, and you position yourself as someone worth inviting back.

I've been on countless panels, and I can tell you that the most interesting, valuable panels are usually ones where the participants already have real relationships and genuine disagreement about something substantive. If you're preparing for a panel, you want to know your co-panelists. You want to understand what their perspectives are. You want to identify where you agree and where you genuinely disagree. Then you want to prepare your points with a willingness to engage authentically rather than just delivering a prepared speech.

I've seen too many conference panels where it's obvious that the five people on stage have never met before the conference, have prepared individually, and are just taking turns delivering their pre-written remarks while the moderator nods. Those are boring and unhelpful. The panels that people actually remember are the ones where there's genuine interaction, where someone says something that prompts a real response, where you can sense that people are thinking on their feet rather than reading slides.

Here's my preparation process for a panel. If I'm invited a couple of months before the event, I immediately reach out to my co-panelists and suggest a call. On that call, I want to understand: What's their perspective on the topic? What's their firm's perspective? What are the key issues they're thinking about right now? What's one thing they want to make sure gets discussed on the panel? I take notes. I develop my own view about what I think is important. I come to the panel understanding the actual perspectives in the room.

For the actual panel, I usually prepare maybe three to four key points that I want to make. I don't write them out word for word; I just write down the substance of the point. Then I'm ready to make that point, but not in a scripted way. That means if the conversation moves in a different direction, I can adapt. If a question pulls out a point I was planning to make, I can acknowledge that and move to something else. If a co-panelist says something I disagree with, I can engage with it authentically. That makes for a much more interesting conversation.

The conference circuit in leveraged finance, if you're intentional about it, becomes a vehicle for relationship-building, visibility, and reinforcing your expertise. Early in my career, I deliberately targeted conferences in Nordic countries, because that's where I wanted to establish expertise. I would speak at the major Nordea-sponsored buyout conference, at regional sponsor dinners, at law firm seminars. Over time, my reputation in Nordic markets became substantial, which led to more speaking opportunities, which led to more deals, which led to more expertise, which led to more speaking. It's a virtuous cycle.

By the time I was in my early forties, I was being invited to speak at major international conferences—the European LBO Forum, global debt capital markets events, major banking conferences. Those are different kinds of speaking, with bigger audiences and more prominent positioning. But I wouldn't have been invited to those if I hadn't spent fifteen years building credibility in a more regional niche.

The final thing about speaking is this: it's terrifying for most people, and that means it's not as crowded as you might think. If you're willing to be nervous, to prepare seriously, to engage authentically, and to follow through with the relationships you build at conferences, you can differentiate yourself meaningfully through speaking. There are talented people in leveraged finance who never speak at conferences, and there are less talented people who are everywhere because they're willing to engage with it. Willingness can count for a lot.


WRITING AS A CAREER ACCELERATOR: FROM INTERNAL MEMOS TO PUBLISHED ARTICLES TO BOOKS

One of the things that has accelerated my career beyond what would have been possible from deal flow alone is a consistent discipline around writing. And it started in a surprisingly unglamorous place: internal memos.

In my second year as an analyst, my group head sat me down and said something that I'll never forget. He said: "You're a competent analyst, you're good with numbers, your work is reliable. But you're going to be mediocre in this industry if you can't write. The best bankers are the best writers." He pointed out that in leveraged finance, you're constantly writing: credit memos, marketing materials, term sheets, investment committee memos, advisor reports, client communications. And the quality of that writing directly impacts how people perceive your competence and judgment.

He assigned me a discipline: every internal memo or analysis I wrote, he wanted it at a level that could theoretically be published with minimal changes. That was an enormously high bar. It meant that I couldn't just be clear enough for people in my group to understand; I had to write with the kind of clarity that a sophisticated outsider could understand. It meant careful editing. It meant thinking about the reader's perspective. It meant making sure my logic was airtight.

That discipline trained me in a way that proved transformative. By the time I had written a hundred internal memos at that level, I could write clearly under pressure. I could take complex technical information and explain it accessibly. I could structure arguments logically. I could anticipate questions and address them. Those are skills that some people never develop in banking, and they matter enormously.

By my fifth year in the industry, I started publishing external articles based on perspectives I'd developed through deal work. Some of those were derivative of internal work—with confidentiality protections, of course. Some were fresh perspectives developed specifically for external consumption. But the discipline of writing internal memos at a publishing-quality level meant that moving to external articles wasn't a huge transition.

The progression from internal to external writing is worth thinking strategically about. When you're early in your career, your writing probably isn't yet at a quality level or a perspective level that would be interesting to external audiences. Your job is to develop that quality through internal work. By mid-career, you should have enough expertise and enough polish that external publication becomes possible. And by late career, you should have a regular cadence of external writing that amplifies your expertise and your reputation.

I'm not someone who writes a book lightly. This book you're reading is my first, and I've spent a significant amount of time thinking about what I actually have to say that's worth a full book. I've published articles, I've contributed chapters to other books, I've written extensively, but a complete book represents a different commitment. You're essentially committing to organize everything you think you know about a topic and present it in a way that's compelling, useful, and rigorous for a reader who might spend fifty hours with your words.

Here's what I think about when I think about writing across those three levels: internal memos, external articles, and longer-form work like books. Internal memos are where you develop your discipline and clarity. You're writing for an intelligent but time-constrained audience. You need to be accurate, you need to be clear, you need to be organized. You probably have ten to twenty pages. Every sentence should earn its place. Every section should move the argument forward. If you can write an internal credit memo that's clear and compelling, you've developed a skill set that transfers.

External articles expand the audience. You're now writing for people who don't know your firm, don't know you, and have no obligation to care about your perspective. You need to earn that attention. You need to offer something they don't already know. You need to be interesting. An external article might be five thousand words, or it might be fifteen hundred. The point is that it's being published where competing attention is high, so it has to earn its place more forcefully than an internal memo.

A book is something else entirely. It's a sustained argument across fifteen to twenty thousand words or more. It's a commitment to thinking about something comprehensively. It requires research, it requires multiple rounds of editing, it requires getting feedback and incorporating it, it requires thinking about different audiences and different levels at which the book needs to work. It takes a long time. I've spent roughly two hundred hours on this book—reading, researching, writing, revising, incorporating feedback. And I'm a fast writer who thinks about these topics constantly.

The return on that investment is not immediate. A book doesn't generate income the way deal fees do. But it does several things simultaneously. It creates a permanent record of your thinking at a particular moment in time. It establishes you as someone thoughtful and serious about your field in a way that nothing else quite does. It creates a vehicle for relationship-building and credibility that's different from articles or speaking. It can generate consulting or advisory opportunities, it can lead to new professional relationships, and it can cement your reputation in ways that are hard to quantify but genuinely valuable.

I would not have written this book ten years ago. I didn't have enough perspective. I didn't have enough to say that was sufficiently original or useful. I had plenty of experience, but I hadn't yet synthesized that experience in a way that I could share coherently. Now, with more than twenty years in the industry, having worked through a major financial crisis, having advised sponsors on hundreds of transactions, having served as a mentor to dozens of younger professionals, I have enough to say to justify the commitment.

The lesson here is that writing should be a discipline throughout your career, but the form of that writing should evolve. Early career: focus on writing internal work at the highest possible level. Mid-career: begin publishing externally, developing thought leadership. Late career: consider whether you have accumulated enough perspective to justify a book or other major writing project. At every stage, the discipline of writing forces clarity and deepens your own thinking.


NETWORKING WITH PRIVATE EQUITY SPONSORS: BUILDING RELATIONSHIPS THAT GENERATE DEAL FLOW

I'm going to be entirely candid: in leveraged finance, deal flow is oxygen. You can have all the technical expertise in the world, but if sponsors don't think of you when they're working on a transaction, your expertise doesn't generate revenue. Building relationships with sponsors is therefore not optional for anyone trying to build a serious career in this industry.

The interesting thing about sponsor relationships is that they're built on a foundation that seems simple but is actually quite sophisticated: sponsors need to trust that when they work with you, you will be thoughtful, careful, reliable, honest, and genuinely invested in solving problems rather than just running up fees. That trust is built over time through repeated interactions and shared experiences of working through complex transactions.

My first relationship with a major European sponsor came through a deal I worked on in 2008. The sponsor was financing a mid-market platform acquisition, and my firm was the lead arranger on the debt. The sponsor's team was relatively inexperienced in leverage. The credit markets were completely insane—we were financing in the middle of the financial crisis when banks were de-leveraging everywhere. The deal was technically complex. And the sponsor needed someone who could not just get the financing done, but could explain what was happening and why, and help them navigate an extraordinarily difficult process.

I ended up spending roughly one hundred and fifty hours on that deal over a six-month period. I met with their team regularly. I explained syndication dynamics, I helped them understand which aspects of a loan agreement actually mattered and which were boilerplate, I talked through the implications of different structures, and perhaps most importantly, I was honest with them when I thought something wasn't working or when I thought they were making a decision they might regret.

The deal closed. The relationship didn't end. Over the next fifteen years, I worked with that sponsor on something in the range of forty different transactions. Those relationships—built on sponsorships of varying sizes—became substantially a base of my business.

Here's what I learned about building sponsor relationships that actually generate deal flow. First, you have to be genuinely useful before you ask for anything in return. Early in your career, that might mean doing analysis that the sponsor needs, helping them think through structures, being available when they have questions. Later in your career, it might mean being a sounding board on strategy, helping them think through how to approach different market conditions, or drawing on your network to solve problems for them. But the principle is consistent: your value to them should never be primarily about your ability to earn fees. It should be about your ability to help them do their job better.

Second, sponsor relationships require showing up consistently over time. You're not going to meet a sponsor once and build a relationship. You're going to meet them multiple times, you're going to work together on transactions, you're going to encounter ups and downs, and you're going to emerge on the other side having a deeper relationship. I probably see my most important sponsor relationships every quarter or so—either in structured meetings, or over a meal, or on phone calls discussing transactions or market developments. That consistency compounds.

Third, you need to be willing to play a supporting role as often as you play a leading role. Some of the most valuable sponsor relationships I have are with sponsors where I'm not the primary arranger, but I'm an important advisor on a specific aspect of the transaction. Or I'm not on the deal at all, but I'm available to think through problems when they arise. Being the person they call for perspective or advice can be as valuable—sometimes more valuable—than being the main financial advisor.

Fourth, sponsor relationships are built partly on professional interaction and partly on personal relationship. I've spent probably several hundred hours in meetings with sponsor executives discussing deals, market conditions, industry trends. But I've also spent time with them outside of work context—dinners, drinks, maybe a football match or a golf outing. You don't need to be friends with sponsors, but you need to have moved beyond purely transactional relationships. They need to know something about you as a person. You need to know something about them.

Here's a specific example that illustrates how this works. In 2014, I was having dinner with a sponsor executive I'd worked with several times. We were discussing their platform, their ambitions, what they wanted to accomplish over the next several years. They mentioned that they were thinking about how to structure their next acquisition and that they were worried about leverage getting too high. I said something like: "Your first platform is about proving your strategy works. Your second is about demonstrating you can integrate and optimize. Your third is where you can be more aggressive." And I suggested that thinking about leverage that way—in the context of which platform you're on and what you're trying to prove—might be a more useful framework than just optimizing for the lowest cost of debt.

That single comment—which came from dozens of other platforms and dozens of other sponsors I'd worked with over the years—shifted how they thought about their strategy. It didn't mean an immediate deal, but it meant that when they were thinking through their approach to leverage and their approach to sponsors, they thought of me as someone who understood their strategic perspective. Over the next five years, I worked with them on seven different transactions. The return on that dinner conversation was substantial.

The honest truth about sponsor relationships is that they require real attention. You can't build meaningful sponsor relationships as a side project if you're really busy with other work. It has to be a priority. You have to allocate time to it. You have to track those relationships actively—I keep notes on sponsor executives, their priorities, what they care about, what they're working on. You have to follow up. You have to remember details from previous conversations. You have to be thoughtful about staying top of mind without being obnoxious about it.

For someone early in their career, building sponsor relationships might mean volunteering to do work for sponsors, asking sponsors if you can sit in on certain meetings, reaching out to sponsors about deals you're following, or getting involved in sponsor organizations. For someone mid-career, it might mean developing advisory relationships, getting involved in sponsor forums or associations, or becoming known as an expert on something that sponsors care about. For someone late-career, it might mean being a sounding board for strategic decisions, helping sponsors think through their approach to the market, or being the person they call when they need wise counsel.

Sponsor relationships are a major part of what has sustained my career at a senior level. When I moved into advisory, it was because sponsors knew who I was and trusted me enough to work with me in a different capacity. When I've been asked to speak at sponsor conferences or events, it's because of relationships I've built over years. When I've had opportunities to shape market developments or industry approaches, it's often been through relationships with sponsors who wanted to collaborate on something.


BUILDING RELATIONSHIPS WITH LAWYERS AND ADVISORS: THE TRUSTED ADVISOR ECOSYSTEM

If sponsor relationships are the customer relationships in leveraged finance, then relationships with lawyers and advisors are something different and equally important: they're peer relationships within the ecosystem that makes deals happen. And I've been surprised at how much of my career trajectory has been shaped by the quality of relationships I've built with really good lawyers and advisors.

Let me be very specific. A truly excellent debt finance lawyer—someone who understands leveraged finance deeply, who negotiates fairly while protecting their client, who thinks about the underlying business issues and not just the legal documentation, and who helps their client make better decisions—is worth more to your career than almost any other single peer relationship. Here's why: you're going to work with the same high-quality lawyers repeatedly over decades. You'll be on opposing sides of negotiations, you'll be collaborating on structures, you'll be solving problems together. That relationship becomes one where you can challenge each other, learn from each other, and raise the quality of what you all produce.

I'm fortunate to have worked extensively with a handful of genuinely excellent lawyers over my career. One in particular—a partner at a leading Nordic firm—I've worked with on something like fifty transactions. We've negotiated against each other when our clients' interests conflicted. We've been on the same side advising sponsors on structures. We've had spirited disagreements about covenant approaches or documentation. And throughout all of that, we've maintained enormous respect for each other's competence and integrity.

That relationship has shaped my career in ways that are hard to overstate. When I needed to understand a particular area of law more deeply, I'd call her and ask. When she wanted perspective on how a structure would play out from a lender's perspective, she'd ask me. When we had difficult transactions where the negotiations were complex and required genuine problem-solving, we both elevated our game because we knew the person on the other side was expecting us to. And when I transitioned to advisory, one of the first relationships I leveraged was with lawyers I'd worked with extensively, because I knew they thought I was credible and competent.

The lesson here is to deliberately invest in relationships with highly competent professionals from other disciplines. The best investment bankers I know have excellent relationships with a small number of really good lawyers. They have relationships with quality financial advisors. They might have relationships with senior bankers at competing firms who they respect. They have relationships with accounting firms, with academic experts, with regulatory advisors. These aren't transactional relationships. These are based on genuine respect and repeated positive interactions over years.

How do you build these relationships? First, you have to do work that's good enough that it gets their attention. I once had a lawyer tell me that the reason we had developed such a strong working relationship was that the first time we negotiated against each other, I was prepared, I understood the issues, and I made reasonable points even when I disagreed with her. She said most junior bankers are either not prepared or are unreasonable. The fact that I was neither made her willing to take me seriously as a counterpart.

Second, you need to show professional courtesy and respect. This probably sounds obvious, but it's not always practiced. You need to meet deadlines. You need to be honest when you can't meet them. You need to respect their time. You need to engage thoughtfully with their feedback. You need to acknowledge when they've made a good point, even if it's not convenient for your side. You need to remember that they have other clients and other priorities, and you're not the only thing they're working on. Professional courtesy compounds.

Third, you should periodically reach out to advisors you respect and ask for their perspective on something. Not for a free consulting engagement—but "Hey, I'm thinking about this issue, you've probably seen it from different angles, what's your view?" These conversations are valuable both ways. They show respect for their expertise. They keep you connected even when you're not actively on a deal together. And you often learn something genuinely useful.

Fourth, you should be willing to give value to these relationships without immediately expecting something in return. If you see something that might be relevant to an advisor you work with, you can flag it. If you have a contact that might be useful for something they're working on, you can make an introduction. You can refer work to them when you're in a position to do so. Over time, that generosity comes back around.

The ecosystem of lawyers and advisors that you build relationships with becomes a genuine asset. These are people who understand your competence because they've seen your work. These are people who will give you honest feedback. These are people you can call when you have a question or when you need to solve a problem. These are people who might refer work to you, who might recommend you to sponsors, who might collaborate with you on new opportunities. And perhaps most importantly, these are people who will call you to work on their transactions because they trust your competence and your integrity.

I've been asked onto deals because lawyers recommended me. I've been asked to advise because accountants thought a particular issue required my perspective. I've gotten introductions to sponsors through advisors who thought we'd work well together. The quality of the advisor ecosystem that you build is genuinely one of the determinants of your career trajectory.


THE PATH FROM ANALYST TO MD: WHAT DIFFERENTIATES PEOPLE AT EACH LEVEL

I was an analyst for four years. That's a long time—some people move to associate or manager level faster. But my group head made it clear that there was no promotion coming until I'd developed the technical skills and the discipline to operate at a higher level. During those four years, I worked roughly the equivalent of six calendar years of full-time work. That intensity, over that sustained period, forced me to develop competence.

The transition from analyst to senior analyst or associate is primarily about technical competence and beginning to develop business judgment. You're now expected to understand not just the calculations, but the reasoning behind them. You're expected to anticipate problems. You're expected to take on larger portions of a transaction with less supervision. You're expected to train more junior people. The analysts who make this transition successfully are typically people who have demonstrated that they understand the business side of deals, not just the mechanics. They ask thoughtful questions. They anticipate problems before they blow up. They have good judgment about when to escalate something and when to solve it themselves.

The transition from associate to manager is bigger. Now you're responsible for relationships. You're responsible for understanding what clients want, not just what they're asking for. You're responsible for the quality of work of the team working under you. You're responsible for business development—you can't just deliver what's asked of you, you need to be identifying new opportunities and bringing them to the firm. You're expected to have a point of view about market developments. You're expected to be beginning to specialize—to have areas where your expertise is distinctive. The associates who make this transition successfully are typically people who have shown they can manage relationships, can manage teams, and can develop business.

The transition from manager to senior manager is where it gets interesting because this is where your personal brand becomes really important. At senior manager level, sponsors are starting to want to work with you specifically. They're asking for you by name. They want your opinion on structures or approaches. They see you as someone with judgment. You're not just executing deals; you're shaping them. You're contributing to strategy. You're the most senior banker on many transactions. Your judgment matters.

What differentiates senior managers who move to Director or MD level is primarily the breadth and depth of their network, the sophistication of their business judgment, and their ability to represent the firm and themselves effectively to sophisticated external audiences. If you're going to make MD in a competitive environment, you need to be someone that sponsors want to partner with strategically. You need to be someone that other advisors respect. You need to be someone that clients feel like they can have real conversations with about their business and their strategy. You need to be visible as an expert. You need to have authored some of the strategic thinking about how your practice operates.

Let me be very clear about something: technical excellence is table stakes at every level. An MD who doesn't understand LBO mathematics is a liability. An MD who can't review a term sheet and spot problems is a problem. But once you hit manager level, technical excellence is necessary but not sufficient. What starts mattering much more is judgment, relationships, representation, and thought leadership.

I made the transition from manager to senior manager when I was about seven years into my career. I didn't get promoted because I got smarter technically—I was plenty technical by that point. I got promoted because I'd developed enough sponsor relationships that I was winning business on my own name. I was becoming visibly differentiated in how I thought about certain kinds of transactions. I had a few areas of real expertise that were publicly known. I had started publishing externally. I was speaking at conferences. I had a visible reputation beyond my firm.

The transition from senior manager to director was about elevating that. At director level, sponsors weren't just asking for me to work on their transactions—they were asking for my advice on strategy. I had advisory clients who I worked with regularly. I was being consulted on big decisions about how to approach the market, how to think about leverage, what was changing. My network had broadened significantly beyond my original Nordic focus. I was being asked to speak at major conferences, to publish in prominent outlets, to be visible as a thought leader.

I never became an MD in a traditional banking sense—I moved to advisory before that transition would have happened. But if I had pursued that path, the next step would have been about being the senior client relationship owner for multiple major clients, about being deeply involved in the strategic direction of the practice, about being a public face of the firm and the market, about mentoring and developing the next generation of senior bankers.

Here's what I wish I'd understood earlier in my career: each of those transitions requires investment in different areas. Early career is about technical development. Mid-career is about business development and relationship building. Late career is about visibility, thought leadership, and strategy. If you're still focused primarily on technical excellence when you should be focused on business development, you'll plateau. If you're trying to build thought leadership before you have enough experience to have legitimate perspectives, you'll come across as shallow.

The most effective path through a leveraged finance career involves deliberately investing in the competencies that matter at each level, while maintaining the foundational competencies from previous levels. An MD who has let their technical skills atrophy is not an effective leader. A director who hasn't invested in visibility and thought leadership will plateau. A manager who hasn't built meaningful sponsor relationships will have limited upside.


MENTORSHIP: FINDING MENTORS, BEING A MENTOR, THE DISTINCTION BETWEEN SPONSOR AND MENTOR

My first real mentor in leveraged finance was the group head I mentioned earlier. He was someone who invested deliberately in my development. He didn't just assign me work and expect me to figure it out. He spent time teaching me how to think about deals. He provided feedback on my writing and on my analysis. He challenged me when I was wrong. He made opportunities available to me. He believed I could become better than I currently was and held me accountable to that.

I had other informal mentors over the years—people from whom I learned a lot through observation and relationship, but who weren't deliberately investing in my development in the same way. I had sponsors who took interest in my career. I had peers with whom I had really important conversations. But a true mentor—someone who is deliberately investing in your development, holding you accountable, and opening doors for you—is rarer than the other kinds of relationships.

Being a mentor has been one of the most rewarding parts of my career. I currently mentor somewhere between five and ten people at any given time. These are people at different levels—some are junior bankers, some are senior managers, some are making transitions into advisory. I spend time thinking about their development. I make introductions. I provide feedback. I sometimes push them in ways that aren't comfortable but I think serve their development.

Here's what I've learned about mentorship in leveraged finance. First, good mentorship is not about solving problems for the person you're mentoring. It's about helping them develop the judgment to solve problems themselves. When a junior banker comes to me with a covenant structure question, I don't just answer the question. I ask them what they think, why they think that, what the implications of that approach would be, what the sponsor would likely care about. I help them work toward the right answer. And in doing that, they develop judgment.

Second, good mentorship is about candor. If someone I'm mentoring is making a career decision that I think is a mistake, I tell them. If they're not developing skills they need at their level, I tell them. If I think they're avoiding something they should be doing, I tell them. Not in a harsh way—but directly. Sugar-coating feedback isn't actually helpful. What's helpful is telling someone what you actually think, and then helping them figure out what to do with that feedback.

Third, mentorship is about opening doors. As I've become more senior and visible, one of the things I can do for people I mentor is provide visibility. I can suggest them for speaking opportunities. I can introduce them to sponsors. I can recommend them for advisory relationships. I can get them in rooms where they wouldn't otherwise be. That access is genuinely valuable.

Fourth, mentorship requires investment. I probably spend fifty to seventy-five hours per year with the people I mentor, across various conversations, reviews of work, introductions, feedback, and informal advice. That's time I'm not billing, not spending on business development in a traditional sense, but I think it's some of the most important work I do.

Now, I want to be very clear about something: mentorship is different from sponsorship. A sponsor is someone—typically someone senior—who believes in you and is willing to put capital behind your career. A sponsor might advocate for your promotion. A sponsor might nominate you for something. A sponsor might put you forward for an opportunity that's high-visibility and potentially high-risk. A sponsor is taking a bet on you. That's valuable and important, but it's different from mentorship.

I've had sponsors over my career. I've been sponsored for certain opportunities. And I've become a sponsor for people I believe in. But sponsorship is not something you can ask for. It's something you earn by demonstrating that you're worth betting on. You demonstrate that through your work, through your judgment, through the fact that people who know you trust you to execute in high-stakes situations.

I've come to believe that at different points in your career, you need different things from relationships with more senior people. Early in your career, you need mentors. You need people who are deliberately investing in your development. By mid-career, you still want mentors, but you also want sponsors—people who are willing to advocate for you and put their credibility on the line on your behalf. By late career, you want both, and you probably also become both for other people.

The question of finding a mentor early in your career is important. You typically don't get a mentor by asking "Will you be my mentor?" You get a mentor by demonstrating that you're someone worth mentoring. You do that by being prepared, by asking good questions, by being responsive to feedback, by taking on challenging assignments, by showing that you're interested in learning. You work for someone who seems like they could be a good mentor and you watch to see if they invest in you. If they do, a mentoring relationship develops. If they don't, you might try to find that elsewhere, or you might just recognize that this is a learning environment where you're going to develop skills through the work itself rather than through close mentoring.

I've been grateful for the mentorship I've received. I think it has genuinely shaped my development in ways that free agency wouldn't have. And I try to pay that forward with the people I mentor now.


INTERNAL VERSUS EXTERNAL BRAND: MANAGING BOTH SIMULTANEOUSLY

One of the interesting challenges of building a personal brand in leveraged finance is that you actually need to build and manage two brands simultaneously: your internal brand—your reputation within your firm—and your external brand—your reputation within the market more broadly. And they're not the same thing.

Your internal brand is what matters most early in your career. Your internal brand is what gets you better assignments. What gets you promoted. What gets you considered for important client interactions. Your internal brand is built through the quality of your work, your reliability, your relationships with colleagues, your contribution to the team, your willingness to do what's necessary. It's not built through external visibility. If you're early in your career and you're spending a lot of time on external brand—writing articles, speaking at conferences, building LinkedIn presence—while your internal brand is weak, that's backwards. Get your internal house in order first.

As you move into mid-career, you need to start deliberately building external brand while maintaining your internal brand. This is where things get tricky, because these can sometimes conflict. Your firm might not love it if you're getting visibility as an individual expert rather than as a representative of the firm. Some very hierarchical organizations are suspicious of bankers who are becoming visible externally. You need to navigate that carefully.

I was lucky to work in environments where the firm actually benefited from individual bankers having external visibility. If I was speaking at conferences, that was good for the firm's brand. If I was publishing articles, that was positioning the firm as thought leadership. If I was developing relationships with sponsors, that ultimately benefited the firm because those relationships translated into business. I was able to build external brand without undermining my internal brand.

But I was also deliberate about it. I never represented myself as the firm's primary voice on something. I always made clear that the views were mine while working for a firm. I made sure my firm benefited when I won business based on external reputation. I was careful not to position myself as operating independently when I was still employed by them.

The way you manage this balance depends partly on the organization you're in and partly on your own judgment. If you're in a firm that doesn't support individual visibility, you need to decide whether that's an environment where you can build the career you want. If you're in a firm that does support it, you should be deliberate about building external brand without letting it undermine your internal relationships and your internal responsibilities.

By the time I moved to advisory, the balance shifted. My external brand became more important than my internal brand to a new firm, because I was being hired partly for the external relationships and reputation I had. But I was still careful to be a good team member, to contribute to the firm's reputation, to maintain internal relationships.

The honest truth is that the best position to be in is one where your internal and external brands are aligned and mutually reinforcing. You're known internally as someone excellent at your craft. You're known externally as someone excellent at your craft. People know you both because of your firm and because of your individual reputation. That's the sweet spot.

And by the way, if your internal brand is weak, your external brand is not going to save you. If you're getting visibility externally but people inside your firm don't respect your work, that's unsustainable. You'll get exposed. People will stop referring work to you. Your career will plateau. The foundation of everything is your actual competence and the quality of your work.


TECHNICAL CREDIBILITY: KNOWING YOUR CRAFT DEEPLY

I want to spend some time on something that I think is underrated in discussions of personal branding in finance: actual technical competence. It's unsexy. It doesn't generate LinkedIn engagement. But it's the foundation of everything.

I've worked with a lot of bankers over twenty years. The ones who have the most durable careers—not the ones who get promoted fastest necessarily, but the ones who sustain their career trajectory and don't plateau—are the ones who have genuine technical depth. They understand the mathematics deeply. They know the documentation intimately. They understand not just what the rules are but why they exist and what problems they're trying to solve. They can think about edge cases and implications. They can spot problems that aren't obvious.

In leveraged finance specifically, this means understanding mathematics very well. I spend a ridiculous amount of time on leverage calculations. I understand covenant baskets and carve-outs and what they're really trying to accomplish. I understand liquidity covenants and how they interact with operational performance. I understand the mechanics of debt paydown and how that works. I understand subordination structures and what seniority actually means. I can do a leverage build for a transaction in my head with reasonable accuracy, which means when something doesn't make sense, I catch it.

This is not something that can be faked. Sponsors know when you understand the mathematics and when you don't. Lawyers know. Other bankers know. If you don't have genuine technical depth, you can be charming and articulate and visible and you'll still eventually be exposed as not really knowing what you're talking about.

I've known bankers who were more charismatic than me, more extroverted, better networkers. And yet they plateaued because they didn't have genuine technical depth. Clients would discover that while they were great at the relationship part, they weren't actually the best source of technical advice. And that limited what they could do.

Conversely, I've known bankers who weren't particularly charismatic, who weren't great at external visibility, but who were extraordinarily technically sound. They sustained careers because they could always rely on the fact that they knew the subject matter deeply.

I think the approach to technical competence should be this: early in your career, invest heavily in technical depth. Really understand the mathematics. Really understand the documentation. Read. Study. Get a CFA if you're interested. Do practice problems. Review everything. The time you invest in technical mastery early will compound over your entire career.

By mid-career, your technical competence should be very solid. You should understand not just your area but adjacent areas. You should be reading widely. You should be tracking how markets are evolving. You should be thinking about what's changing and what's staying the same.

By late career, your technical knowledge should be deep enough that you can have sophisticated conversations about very specific issues, but also broad enough that you understand the interconnections. You might specialize in certain kinds of transactions or certain kinds of markets, but you should understand the broader context.

One thing that helped me maintain technical depth over a long career is that I never got comfortable. I always assumed I didn't know something. When a new instrument emerged or a market developed in a new way, I studied it. When I encountered a deal structure I hadn't seen before, I really tried to understand it thoroughly. I was reading every day—analyst reports, ratings agency comments, loan market developments, sponsor perspectives, academic research. That constant intake of information kept me current and kept my technical foundation strong.

Here's something else about technical credibility: it's actually the best armor against all the uncertainty of a career in finance. Markets change, industries evolve, firms restructure, deal flow ebbs and flows. The one thing you can control is the depth of your technical competence. And that competence is portable. You can move between firms, between roles, between geographies, and your technical competence moves with you. That's valuable.


SOCIAL MEDIA PRESENCE BEYOND LINKEDIN: TWITTER/X AND MARKET COMMENTARY

I came to Twitter quite late. I resisted it for years. I thought it was a distraction, I thought it was where people went to argue pointlessly, I thought there was no legitimate professional use for it. I was wrong about all three of those things.

Around 2015, I started paying attention to how certain financial professionals were using Twitter. Not as a platform for self-promotion or for jokes, but as a platform for substantive market commentary. There were bankers, economists, market observers, who were tweeting things like: observations about what spreads were doing, thoughts about implications of regulatory changes, quick analysis of market news, perspectives on deal dynamics. And they were doing it in ways that were substantive but accessible. Not dumbed-down, but written for a Twitter format.

I started reading those accounts carefully. And I started noticing that some of the people doing this best were becoming increasingly visible in the market. They were getting asked to speak. They were becoming cited in media. They were building audiences of people who actually knew the space and valued their perspective.

So I opened a Twitter account. My initial approach was cautious. I would tweet occasionally—real observations, not self-promotion. I would comment on market news with some perspective. I would engage in conversations about things I cared about. I wouldn't get into arguments. I wouldn't post just to post. I used it the way I used LinkedIn—as a platform for sharing genuine perspective and building visibility around specific things I cared about.

What I found is that Twitter actually serves a different function than LinkedIn. LinkedIn is where you curate a professional brand. Twitter is where you engage in real-time conversation about what's happening in the market. It's where you react to news with perspective. It's where you can have spirited conversations about ideas. It's more immediate, more conversational, less polished.

Over the past decade, I've built a Twitter following of people who actually know the leveraged finance space. It's not huge—I have somewhere in the range of eight thousand followers, which is not viral by any means. But it's an audience of people I actually respect. People who work in the space, people who understand the technical details, people who value thoughtful perspective. And that audience has been valuable. It's led to introductions, to thought leadership opportunities, to visibility in the market.

The way you build credibility on Twitter in a space like leveraged finance is the same way you build credibility on LinkedIn: by being substantive, by being accurate, by being genuinely useful. Don't use Twitter to self-promote. Use it to comment on things you actually have perspective on. Don't use it to argue just to win the argument. Use it to engage with people who have different perspectives and learn from that engagement.

Twitter is riskier than LinkedIn in some ways, because it's more immediate, less curated. You might say something quickly that you wish you'd thought through more carefully. You might engage with something that turns into a longer argument than you intended. You need to be thoughtful about what you put into the world.

But Twitter is also more real than LinkedIn. It's where actual conversation happens. It's where you can establish credibility by engaging substantively with difficult questions. It's where you can build relationships with other market professionals. And if you do it thoughtfully, it can be a real asset to your professional brand.


BECOMING THE GO-TO PERSON: BUILDING A REPUTATION IN A NICHE

Early in my career, I was a generalist. I was learning leveraged finance broadly. I was working on different kinds of deals, learning different structures, developing general competence. There's value in that. But I came to realize, around year five or six in the industry, that I couldn't become truly differentiated by being a generalist. There's always someone who's more experienced as a generalist than you are.

What I decided to do instead was deliberately develop deep expertise in a niche. I chose to focus on Nordic LBO structures and sponsors. At the time, the Nordic market was growing but wasn't as visible or as analyzed as larger markets. There was room to become genuinely known as someone who understood it deeply.

What I did was systematic. I researched Nordic sponsors—who they were, what they did, what their typical deal structures looked like. I read everything published about Nordic deals. I spent time in Nordic countries, got to know the market, understood the nuances. I got to know lawyers and accountants in Nordic markets. I made sure I was on deals happening in those markets. Over time, I became known in the industry as someone who understood Nordic deals really well.

That specialization created massive value. When someone needed advice on a Nordic deal, or when a Nordic sponsor was looking for a financing partner, or when someone wanted to understand how Nordic deals worked, they thought of me. That reputation was durable. It's still with me twenty years later.

Here's the lesson: you become a go-to person by specializing deeply and building genuine expertise in a specific area. That area can be geographic (Nordic deals), it can be a transaction type (continuation fund financings), it can be a structural innovation (subscription lending), it can be a sponsor type (lower mid-market family offices). But it has to be something real that you actually develop expertise in.

The process of becoming the go-to person requires several things. First, you need to actually develop expertise. You need to work on multiple transactions in your niche. You need to read everything about your niche. You need to build relationships within your niche—with sponsors, lawyers, accountants, other specialists. You need to understand the nuances, the subtle differences between different approaches, what works and what doesn't.

Second, you need to be visible about your expertise. You write about it. You speak about it. You make sure people know that this is your area of focus. You're not trying to be everything to everyone; you're trying to be the best at this specific thing.

Third, you need to deliver. When people work with you because of your niche expertise, you need to justify that reputation. You need to be right. You need to provide value. You need to make sure they feel like they made the right decision in working with you.

The downside of becoming known for a niche is that you can sometimes find yourself pigeonholed. People might think you only do Nordic deals, when you actually have competence in other areas. But the upside is that you're the person people think of for that area, and that creates opportunity.

I spent probably the first fifteen years of my career really focused on Nordic markets. By that point, I had become visible beyond Nordics, but that was still where my deepest expertise was. Over time, I broadened. I developed expertise in other European markets. I worked on larger deals. I moved into advisory. My scope broadened. But the foundation was that period where I was really focused on becoming the expert in Nordic leveraged finance.


CAREER TRANSITIONS IN LEVFIN: BANK TO SPONSOR, SPONSOR TO ADVISOR, AND OTHER MOVES

Leveraged finance is an industry where mid-career transitions are pretty common. Someone spends a decade on the banking side, then moves to a sponsor to understand the perspective from the other side. Someone spends time as a banker, then becomes a legal advisor. Someone works at a bank, then at a sponsor, then at an advisor firm. The pathways are varied.

I made the transition from banking to advisory when I was sixteen years into my career. By that point, I was a senior manager at my bank. I had developed significant expertise, I had meaningful sponsor relationships, I was being asked to advise on structures informally all the time. A sponsor offered me an advisory role, and I took it.

That transition came with real risk. I was leaving an established position at a major bank. I was leaving a salary I understood and benefits I was comfortable with. I was moving to an advisory firm that, while established, was smaller and less certain. I was giving up some security for the opportunity to do something different.

But I made the transition for a couple of reasons. First, I was curious about the sponsor perspective. I'd advised sponsors on structures and strategy for years, but I wanted to experience that from the inside. Second, the advisory role aligned better with what I thought I wanted to do at the late stage of my career. I wasn't sure I wanted to manage large teams or climb the ranks to partnership at a bank. Advisory suited me better.

The transition was actually harder than I expected in some ways. Even though I had been advising sponsors for years, working as an advisor full-time was different. The incentive structures were different. The client relationships were different. I had to relearn how to establish myself in a new context.

Here's what I learned from that transition and from watching other people make career moves in leveraged finance. First, the fundamentals of credibility transfer between roles. If you were a good banker, the skills and the reputation you built as a banker don't disappear when you move to advisory. But you do need to re-establish yourself in the new context. You need to prove that you understand the new role. You need to be humble about what you don't know.

Second, your network is your most portable asset. When I moved to advisory, my relationship with sponsors was directly valuable. My relationships with lawyers was directly valuable. My reputation for technical competence was directly valuable. My personal brand that I'd built as a banker transferred to my new role. That made the transition much smoother than it would have been otherwise.

Third, different roles attract different people. Some people are energized by the urgency and client service intensity of banking. Some people are energized by the strategic and analytical side of sponsorship. Some people prefer the advisory role where they get to work on many different situations rather than being embedded in a single organization. Understanding what energizes you is important.

Fourth, market timing matters. I made my move during a period when the advisory industry was growing and sponsors were expanding their use of external advisors. If I'd tried to make that transition during a severe downturn, it would have been much harder. Understanding when the market conditions favor a particular move is smart.

I'm not someone who has made many radical career moves. I've stayed within leveraged finance. I've moved between roles but not constantly. But that's partly because I've been fortunate to have developed expertise and visibility that has given me options. Someone earlier in their career might need to be more opportunistic, might need to take the better role or the better firm even if it's not an obvious choice.

The lesson I'd offer: be strategic about career transitions. Don't make moves just because you're bored or because someone offers you something. Think about where you're going, why that matters for your development, and how it positions you for the career you actually want. And recognize that your personal brand—the reputation you've built—is what makes transitions possible.


THE IMPORTANCE OF CREDENTIALS: CFA, LPC, MBA, AND WHETHER THEY MATTER

This is an area where I have some thoughts that might be contrarian. Credentials matter in leveraged finance, but probably not in the way a lot of people think they do.

Let me be clear: I have professional credentials. I have an MBA (from a good program), I have the CFA charter (which I got by passing all three levels), I have other professional certifications. I think credentials can be valuable. But I also think people often overestimate their importance.

Here's my honest assessment of different credentials in leveraged finance. An MBA from a top program can be valuable early in your career—it's a credential that helps you get into better programs, helps you get better initial jobs. But by mid-career, nobody actually cares where your MBA is from. What they care about is what you've done since then. I've worked with people with MBAs from unknown programs who are extraordinary, and people from top MBA programs who are mediocre. The MBA gets your foot in the door. Your work product keeps you in the room.

The CFA is different. The CFA charter is respected, and achieving it requires genuine work. I spent roughly four hundred hours studying for the three levels of the CFA, spread over about four years. It was valuable, not because of the credential itself, but because the process of studying forced me to deepen my understanding of financial markets, investments, and ethics. The charter is a credential that sponsors and advisors will recognize. But I've never had anyone hire me or not hire me based on whether I have a CFA. It was valuable for my own development and it's a nice credential, but it's not a career-maker.

The LPC (Legal Practice Certificate) is something I don't have, because I didn't want to become a lawyer. But it's clearly valuable if you're going to work as a debt lawyer. It's required for practice. That's different from MBA or CFA, which are optional credentials that might enhance your career.

Here's what I think actually matters more than credentials: certifications and credentials are useful for demonstrating commitment and knowledge. But actual demonstrated competence will always matter more. Someone without any credentials but with a track record of excellent work will always outcompete someone with excellent credentials but mediocre work product.

My advice on credentials: if you're early in your career, an MBA from a good program can be valuable. The CFA is worth considering if you want to deepen your financial knowledge and you have the time. If you're going to be a lawyer, the LPC is obviously required. But by mid-career, what matters most is what you've actually accomplished, not what credentials you hold. Don't put so much weight on credentials that you neglect the actual development of competence.

I've sometimes wondered if the CFA would have mattered more if I'd been in a different role. In leveraged finance advisory, technical competence is demonstrated through deal experience. In certain other roles—like equity research, or portfolio management—I suspect the CFA might be more explicitly valued. But in my field, it's been nice to have but not determinative.


WORK-LIFE BALANCE IN LEVERAGED FINANCE: THE HONEST TRUTH

I want to be completely honest about something that I think a lot of people in leveraged finance are dishonest about: the hours are brutal, and that shapes your life.

I spent the first fifteen years of my career working roughly seventy to eighty hours per week. I was in the office by seven in the morning most days. I was working until eight, nine, ten at night when deals were closing. I was working on weekends when deals required it. During certain periods—the height of a deal closing, when underwriting was happening, when syndication was intense—I was working seventy to one hundred hours a week.

That was not sustainable. And I watched many people burn out. Some of them left the industry. Some of them developed health problems. Some of them damaged relationships because they weren't present in their personal lives. Some of them made themselves irreplaceable in their firms by working so hard that they literally couldn't take vacation without the place falling apart.

I was lucky. I'm naturally someone who operates well under pressure and who finds deal work genuinely interesting. I have a spouse who understood the demands of the industry. I don't have children, which would have made the hours even harder. And I was in an organization that, while demanding, eventually did value senior people having lives outside the office.

But I want to be very clear: the leveraged finance industry, as it's currently structured, does not accommodate work-life balance in the way that some other industries do. If you want to operate at a senior level in banking, you have to accept that there will be periods—sometimes extended periods—where your life is mostly just work. That's true at some advisory firms as well, though less extreme than at banks.

Here's what actually happens if you try to maintain strict work-life balance early in your banking career: you get fewer good assignments, you advance more slowly, you're not perceived as committed, and you probably don't build the relationships and expertise that would allow you to have optionality later. The people who advance fastest early in their careers are typically the people who accept that this phase of their life is intense.

But here's also what's true: if you maintain that intensity for your entire career, you're going to burn out or you're going to damage your personal life or both. So the actual path is more like this: you work very hard for the first ten to fifteen years of your career. You build expertise. You build relationships. You establish yourself. During that period, you accept that it's intense. Then, as you move into mid and late career, you have more leverage to negotiate the intensity. You can choose mandates. You can be more selective. You can have periods that are less intense. You can be more present in your personal life.

That's roughly what happened for me. My early career was brutal. By my mid-career, I had enough visibility and enough client relationships that I could be more selective about what I took on. By late career, I was choosing advisory work that was interesting but not as time-intensive as banking would have been.

I'll also say this: some people thrive in the intensity. Some people find it energizing. Some people discover that they're good under pressure and they actually prefer the intensity. Those people sometimes do stay in banking or find other high-intensity roles. Other people discover that they don't want to maintain that for their entire career, and they transition to roles or organizations that have different rhythms. Both are legitimate.

The honest truth about work-life balance in leveraged finance is that you can't have it early, it becomes more possible in the middle, and by late career you have more control. The industry isn't structured to accommodate people who want balance throughout their career. If that's important to you, you might need to choose a different career path or accept that you'll be in a lower-intensity role and have less upside.


MANAGING YOUR CAREER THROUGH MARKET CYCLES: WHAT TO DO IN A DOWNTURN

I've lived through several significant downturns in my career: the 2001-2002 telecom bubble burst, the 2007-2008 financial crisis, the 2011-2012 European sovereign debt crisis, the 2020 pandemic, and various other periods of market stress. Each of those periods taught me something about how to position yourself and how to manage your career when markets are contracting.

The first thing to understand is that downturns are actually when your personal brand becomes most valuable. When credit markets are tight, when deals are hard to get done, when banks are de-leveraging, sponsors need advisors more than they need them in good times. They need people they trust. They need people with expertise. They need people they've worked with successfully before. A strong personal brand becomes your most valuable asset during a downturn.

Here's what I did during the 2008-2009 financial crisis. The credit markets completely froze. Most of the banks I worked with were actually in survival mode. Deal flow effectively stopped for about eighteen months. It was genuinely scary. But I realized that while banks were struggling, sponsors still needed advice on the deals they'd already done—how to manage covenant packages, how to think about leverage recalculations, how to plan ahead for when markets reopened.

I did something deliberate: I made myself available to sponsors to advise on how to navigate the downturn, often without a clear path to fees. I helped them think through their leverage. I helped them understand implications of potential covenant waiver requests. I helped them plan their refinancing for when markets opened back up. I was providing value without immediately expecting compensation.

By the time markets began to recover in 2010-2011, I had deepened relationships with sponsors significantly. When they needed financing and credit became available again, I was the person they wanted to work with. The business came back, and it came back first to people who had stayed close during the difficult period.

Here's what I think is important about managing career through downturns. First, don't panic. Yes, markets are contracting. Yes, deal flow is down. Yes, some people will lose their jobs. But downturns are also opportunity if you position yourself right. Downturns are when people who have built strong relationships, strong expertise, and strong reputation will actually outperform.

Second, stay visible. This is counterintuitive, but during downturns, not all business stops. There's still activity. Some sponsors are refinancing. Some are being restructured. Some are doing smaller deals. Some need advisory. If you stay visible, if you keep your relationships warm, if you keep offering perspective, you're positioned to win business when it comes.

Third, invest in relationships. When you're busy in good times, relationships can feel like a luxury. In downturns, when you have more time, relationships are your most important asset. Reach out to people. Have conversations. Check in with sponsors. Talk to lawyers. Reinforce relationships that you've built.

Fourth, keep your expertise current. During downturns, there's often a lot of structural change, regulatory change, market evolution. Stay on top of it. Read. Think. Develop perspectives. When the market recovers, you want to be someone with current, relevant expertise.

Fifth, think strategically about your role. If you're in a firm that's struggling during the downturn, you might be thinking about whether to move. If you're thinking about making a move, downturns can actually be good times to do it, because firms that are growing might be looking for people with relevant expertise. You have more leverage than you'd have in good times if you have a strong reputation.

I think the people who navigate downturns most successfully are the ones who don't panic, who stay focused on maintaining relationships and expertise, and who look for ways to provide value even when traditional deal flow is down. When the recovery comes, they're positioned well.


THE IMPORTANCE OF CROSS-BORDER EXPERIENCE IN EUROPEAN LEVFIN

European leveraged finance is genuinely different from US leveraged finance in ways that are important to understand. And cross-border experience—having worked in multiple European markets—has been one of the most valuable parts of my career development.

Europe has multiple large leveraged finance markets. There's the UK, which has a market that's developed similarly to the US in many ways. There's Germany, which has a different culture around leverage and private equity. There's France, which has its own market dynamics. The Nordic region, which is my area of expertise, has particular characteristics. There's Southern Europe, which is developing differently. There's Central and Eastern Europe. Each market has nuances.

The thing I learned by working across multiple European markets is that leverage principles are the same everywhere, but application is different everywhere. A leverage covenant works the same way in Germany as it does in Sweden as it does in France. But sponsor expectations around leverage are different. Lender expectations are different. Structural approaches are different. Rating agency approaches are different. The way professionals interact is different.

For example, in Nordic markets, sponsors tend to be very focused on leverage management. They watch leverage closely. They refinance early. They de-lever actively. In other markets, leverage management is less of a focus. In some markets, leverage goes higher than Nordics would accept, because there's more comfort with it. In some markets, leverage goes lower because there's less comfort with it. Those aren't right or wrong—they're just different market norms.

If you only work in one European market, you have a partial understanding. You understand one market really well, but you lack perspective on how different things could be done. If you work across multiple European markets, you develop pattern recognition. You understand what's core to leveraged finance and what's market-specific.

The way I built cross-border experience was partly systematic and partly opportunistic. I deliberately sought assignments in different European markets. I made relationships with professionals in different markets. I read about what was happening in different markets. Over time, I became someone who understood European leveraged finance more broadly, not just Nordic leveraged finance.

That cross-border perspective has been valuable in a number of ways. First, it's made me more useful to sponsors who operate across multiple markets. Second, it's made me more able to explain trends. When something is happening in one market and not another, I can explain why. Third, it's made me more credible with international audiences. When I speak at European conferences, I can speak about the whole region rather than just my niche.

For someone building a career in European leveraged finance, I think cross-border experience is genuinely important. It doesn't necessarily mean you need to move between countries, though that's valuable. But it does mean taking on projects in different countries, building relationships across borders, and getting exposure to how different markets operate.


WHAT TWENTY YEARS TEACHES: THE REFLECTION SECTION

I'm now twenty-two years into my career in leveraged finance. I've seen multiple market cycles. I've worked on hundreds of transactions. I've advised dozens of sponsors. I've developed expertise I'm proud of. I've built relationships that matter to me. I've also seen a lot of people have careers in this industry, and I've observed what works and what doesn't.

Here's what twenty years of working in leveraged finance has taught me about building a personal brand that matters.

First, technical excellence is not optional. Everything else is built on a foundation of genuine competence. You can be charismatic and well-networked and visible, but if you don't actually know your craft, people will figure it out. And once they do, your brand is damaged. The investment in actually becoming excellent at what you do is the most important investment you can make.

Second, relationships are everything. The deals you work on, the opportunities you get, the sponsors you advise, the visibility you gain, all of it flows from relationships. Relationships are built through consistency, through reliability, through showing up for people, through being useful, through genuine engagement. There's no shortcut for that. Social media can amplify relationships, but it can't replace them.

Third, authenticity is more powerful than positioning. When I've been most successful in building a reputation, it's been by being genuinely interested in something, developing real expertise, and sharing that expertise in an authentic way. When I've tried to position myself as interested in something I wasn't, it's come across as hollow and hasn't worked. People can tell when you're trying to build a brand and when you're just being yourself and your brand emerges from that.

Fourth, specialization beats generalism. There will always be better generalists than you. But if you specialize in something real, you can become genuinely expert. And genuine expertise is the foundation of visibility and credibility.

Fifth, consistency matters. The people I know who have the most successful long-term careers are the ones who show up consistently over decades. They're not doing one big thing and then disappearing. They're showing up at conferences year after year. They're publishing consistently. They're maintaining relationships. They're staying engaged. That consistency compounds.

Sixth, generosity is underrated. Some of the best things that have happened in my career have come from being generous with my time, my knowledge, my relationships, my attention. I've spent time mentoring people without clear benefit to me. I've made introductions that didn't help me. I've shared perspectives that others could benefit from. That generosity has come back around repeatedly.

Seventh, your personal brand should be a reflection of your actual work and your actual expertise. It's not a marketing project that's separate from your actual job. It's a byproduct of the quality of your work, the relationships you build, the perspectives you develop, the visibility you gain from being genuinely excellent.

Eighth, market timing matters. Some of the opportunities I've had came from being in the right place at the right time, or having a particular expertise when the market needed it. You can influence this partly—by developing expertise in areas that are becoming important, by positioning yourself for market changes—but you also need to acknowledge that luck plays a role.

Ninth, transitions are possible. I moved from banking to advisory when that wasn't something I was certain about. But because I'd built a strong reputation, because I had relationships that valued me, the transition was possible. If you've built a genuine personal brand, you have optionality. You can make moves. You're not locked in.

Tenth, this business is genuinely interesting. The fact that I've sustained a career over twenty-two years is partly because I actually find leveraged finance fascinating. The mathematics, the strategy, the way deals work, the way sponsors think, the way markets evolve. If you're in this for the right reasons—because you actually find it interesting—your enthusiasm will show. And that's part of what people respond to when they engage with your brand.

To close this chapter, I'll say this: building a personal brand in leveraged finance is not a marketing project. It's the inevitable result of being excellent at your work, of building relationships authentically, of developing genuine expertise, and of sharing that expertise consistently over time. If you focus on those things, your brand builds itself. You don't need to be the most charismatic person in the room. You don't need to have the most Twitter followers or the most LinkedIn connections. You just need to be genuinely good at what you do, and willing to be publicly associated with that excellence. Everything else flows from that.
