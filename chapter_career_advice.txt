CAREER ADVICE — WHAT TWENTY YEARS TEACHES

I wish someone had written this chapter for me when I was twenty-five years old, sitting in a Goldman Sachs analyst training program, convinced that I had no idea what I was doing and that everyone around me was smarter, more prepared, and somehow born understanding leveraged buyouts. I wish I'd known then what I know now: that most of that program was also just learning on the job, that the people ahead of me felt roughly the same blend of confidence and fraud in roughly the same proportions, and that a career in leveraged finance is less like climbing a clearly marked mountain and more like wandering through a dense forest where you occasionally look up and realize you've made meaningful progress without always knowing exactly how you got there.

Most career advice in leveraged finance books is terrible. It's either viciously practical in a way that strips out all context—"here's how to model an LBO"—or it's aggressively motivational in a way that bears almost no resemblance to the actual experience of working in this industry. What I've tried to do here is be genuinely honest. That means acknowledging that this career is often exhausting, sometimes demoralizing, occasionally brilliant, frequently tedious, and surprisingly capable of being genuinely meaningful if you approach it with the right mindset. It means talking about the people who get left behind, the trades we all make between money and time and dignity, and the surprising fact that the best people I've met in this industry are not always the ones at the top of the compensation ladder.

This isn't a field guide to breaking into credit investing. There are other parts of this book for that, and honestly, the technical skills are teachable; they're just work. This is about the part that nobody really teaches you: how to build a career in leveraged finance that is sustainable, satisfying, and aligned with who you actually are rather than who you thought you were supposed to become.

## THE FIRST STEPS INTO A WORLD YOU DON'T UNDERSTAND YET

I got into leveraged finance almost by accident. I was coming out of college with an economics degree, decent grades, and absolutely no idea what I wanted to do with my life. I knew I liked solving problems, I knew I was decent with numbers, and I knew that I wanted to make more money than my parents had, which was perhaps not the most noble motivation but is certainly honest. An internship at a boutique investment bank turned into a full-time analyst offer, and I took it because it seemed like the next logical step and because I was terrified of the alternative: uncertainty.

The traditional path into leveraged finance—the analyst program at a bulge bracket bank—is real but it's also increasingly not the only path. When I started, if you wanted to be in credit investing, you went to Goldman, Morgan Stanley, or Citi, or you went to a few highly specialized boutiques. Now there are dozens of legitimate entry points. You can go into direct lending platforms, credit funds, law firms that specialize in leveraged finance, and various advisory firms. The path matters less than I initially thought it would. What matters is that you get into an environment where you're learning from intelligent people who actually care about credit, and that you're building real skills rather than just checking boxes on a resume.

When people ask me about breaking into this field, I tell them the truth: hiring managers are looking for three things, and almost everything else is noise. First, they want evidence that you can do the analytical work. This doesn't mean you need to have modeled an LBO in college—almost nobody has—but you need to demonstrate that you can take apart a financial statement, ask intelligent questions about the underlying assumptions, and spot when something doesn't add up. In interviews, this means doing the actual work when they give you a case study, not trying to bs your way through it. They can tell the difference.

Second, they want to see that you'll show up and be reliable. This sounds basic, but it's shockingly differentiating. In a field where the work is project-based and often compressed, someone who delivers on commitments, who thinks about quality, and who doesn't require constant supervision is worth enormous amounts. I remember hiring an analyst years ago who wasn't the smartest person in the room, but she delivered everything on time with zero defects, asked clarifying questions before doing the work rather than building something wrong, and basically made my life easier every single day. She got promoted significantly faster than several people with higher GPAs from better schools.

Third, they want some evidence that you're not going to be a catastrophic human being to work with. They want to hear about times you've worked in teams, how you handled conflict, what you learned from failure. The brutal honesty here is that the final piece is about fit—not just intellectual fit, but whether you're someone the team actually wants to spend fifty to seventy hours a week with.

The interview process for leveraged finance is partly about the traditional case study—here's a company, here are the financials, model me an LBO—and partly about everything else. The case study is real, and yes, you need to be able to do it. I've interviewed hundreds of people and there's a clear difference between people who understand the mechanics and people who have just memorized the template. The template people fall apart the moment anything in the case is different from what they've seen before. The people who understand it can adapt, they can ask smart questions, and they can build something useful.

But honestly, the case study is often the easier part of the interview. The harder part—which nobody really teaches you—is managing the five other conversations that day. It's the question from the senior person where they're not really testing your knowledge, they're testing whether you'll defend your work or just cave immediately when someone challenges you. It's the conversation with the operations person where they're assessing whether you're the kind of person who respects the infrastructure of the organization or whether you're going to be dismissive about things that don't seem directly related to "the work." It's the quiet moment with the eventual boss where you're trying to figure out if this is someone who will actually develop you or just burn you up on project work.

Networking in this field is often described as essential, and it is, but most of the advice about networking is actively harmful. Most people approach it as a transaction: I'll have coffee with this person and extract value. I'll build a relationship with this potential client. I'll attend the conference and work the room. This doesn't work, or at least it doesn't work well. What actually works is being genuinely interested in people, staying in touch with people for reasons that aren't immediately about you, and being the person who is helpful, not just helpful-seeking.

I made a list once of the twenty people who had most materially impacted my career. Some of them were obvious mentors, but many of them were peers I met at conferences, colleagues who worked at other firms, lawyers I met on deals, and people I genuinely just liked and stayed in touch with. With very few exceptions, I never asked them for anything. I showed up when they asked for input. I made introductions when I could. I had lunch with them when we were in the same city. And because I had done those things, when I needed something—advice on whether to take a particular job, an introduction to someone in the industry, feedback on a decision I was wrestling with—I could ask without it feeling like an extraction.

The importance of a good mentor in year one cannot be overstated. This is someone, ideally, who has been where you are, who isn't directly evaluating you for the partnership, and who genuinely cares about your development. I had a managing director who took me under his wing in my first year. He wasn't my direct boss. He barely knew me initially. But he had heard that I was working on something and he pulled me into a deal meeting to listen. Then he explained, afterward, what had actually happened and why it mattered. And he kept doing that—pulling me into meetings, explaining the context, asking me what I thought. This wasn't altruism on his part. He was investing in talent he thought had potential, which is a very reasonable thing to do. But it completely changed my trajectory because someone with real credibility was showing me that I could think about complex situations and that my perspective mattered, even when I was spectacularly junior.

Finding that person is sometimes luck—they need to exist at your firm and they need to be generous with their time—but you can create conditions where it's more likely. You can ask people explicitly: would you be willing to grab coffee once a month and let me ask you questions? Most good people will say yes, because everyone likes feeling like they're being useful. You can be thoughtful about who you ask. You probably don't want your direct manager as your mentor, because there's an evaluation conflict. You want someone who is further along in their career, who has done things you want to do, and who has enough security in their position that they can be honest with you about what's hard and what's not.

Getting into leveraged finance was, for me, about making a series of small decisions. I took the analyst role because it seemed like a good opportunity at the time. I worked hard and delivered quality work, which opened the next door. I had good luck with some of the deals I worked on early—they went well, I learned a lot, I met interesting people. I had bad luck with some other ones, but by that point I'd developed enough capability that I could learn from the failures rather than being destroyed by them. I don't think there's a blueprint for it. I think there's a willingness to show up, to try to do good work, to be open to luck when it comes, and to be thoughtful about the next decision rather than just assuming there's only one possible path.

## THE FIRST THREE YEARS: LEARNING TO BUILD IN THE DARK

Your first year in leveraged finance is largely about experiencing acute panic on a consistent basis while trying to hide the fact that you are experiencing acute panic. On day one, you know almost nothing. You don't know what an EBITDA adjustment is. You don't know why people care about leverage ratios. You don't know how to read documentation. You don't know what questions to ask. You're going to sit in meetings where senior people are talking about things that sound like they might be English but don't quite fit together in ways that make sense. You're going to be asked to build a model and you're going to have absolutely no idea if what you're building is correct or garbage.

What actually happens, if you stick with it, is that somewhere around month four or five, you'll realize that you've built maybe fifteen LBO models now. You've read documentation on a dozen deals. You've sat through sixty meetings about credit analysis. And while you still don't know everything, you've stopped being completely lost. There's still enormous amounts you don't understand, but the not understanding has narrowed. It's not "what is leverage?" anymore; it's "why do these two credit funds evaluate leverage ratio step-down provisions differently?"

The learning curve is steep. I have watched very smart people wash out in year one because they were waiting to understand everything before they felt confident. They would spend six hours on a model trying to figure out if it was right, rather than building it in four hours and then asking for feedback. They didn't understand that in this industry, done and reviewed is better than perfect and late. They didn't understand that part of the learning is learning by doing something wrong, having someone correct you, and then knowing better next time.

The technical skills matter. You need to be able to build an LBO model that actually works. You need to understand how to read a credit agreement and pull out the covenants that matter. You need to be able to do financial analysis in a way that's both rigorous and fast. You need to understand what questions to ask about a company's business model, its competitive positioning, and its potential futures. These are all teachable. None of them are actually hard. They're just work.

What nobody really teaches you, though, is the soft side. In year one, you're going to make mistakes. You're going to send an email that's poorly worded. You're going to take three days to do something that should have taken one because you didn't understand the requirement. You're going to build a model and have your boss completely re-do it because you fundamentally misunderstood the capital structure. These things will happen. The difference between people who make it and people who don't is often how they respond.

Making a mistake and then trying to hide it, or making a mistake and then making excuses, or making a mistake and then overexplaining it—all of those strategies make your life harder and mark you as someone who isn't reliable. Making a mistake and then saying "I don't think I understood the requirement. Can we spend five minutes so I make sure I do this correctly next time?" marks you as someone who is worth investing in. It sounds simple but it's shockingly differentiating. I've seen senior people take chances on developing junior people largely because those people had the maturity to own mistakes and learn from them.

The hours reality is worth talking about. My first year, I routinely left the office at midnight. Several times a year, I slept at the office. Weekends were not reliably free. My friends who went to law school had marginally worse hours. My friends who went into banking had marginally better hours. Most jobs I'm aware of were not like this. I'm not going to pretend this is fine, or normal, or that everyone loves it, because that would be dishonest. It's not fine and it's not normal. It's also real, and if you're considering a career in leveraged finance, you need to know that you're signing up for a period—hopefully a temporary one—where your life is substantially consumed by work.

What I would say, though, is that it's not eternally that way. The junior years are brutal in terms of hours, at least at most firms. But by the time you're an associate or a senior associate, the hours are more variable. You have periods where you're slammed and periods where you're not. By the time you're a VP or higher, you have substantially more control over your time, which is one of the things you're actually being compensated for. The question is whether you can survive the junior years to get there, and that's as much about mindset as capability.

The learning tax—the mistakes you're going to make—is higher when you're junior. I spent a week building a model that was based on completely wrong assumptions about the working capital requirements. I built a financial projection that didn't tie to anything. I misread a ratio on a source document and made a recommendation based on bad data. These were all painful. And then I learned from them and I didn't make them again. If you can develop enough self-awareness to recognize what you're doing wrong and adapt, you can survive this phase. If you can't, you wash out.

One of the harder lessons is learning when to push back and when to just execute. In year one, you probably don't push back much. You're building the foundational knowledge and frankly, you're in no position to challenge senior people on substantive issues—you don't know enough. But somewhere around year two or three, you start developing actual judgment. You start to have questions about whether something the team is doing makes sense. And you have to calibrate: is this something I should be quiet about because I'm probably wrong or don't fully understand? Is this something I should ask a clarifying question about? Or is this something I should actually push back on?

I had a deal where we were assuming a pretty aggressive revenue growth scenario, and I had questions about whether the underlying drivers made sense given the market dynamics. I was still pretty junior. I asked my boss about it. He listened, thought about it, and said "You're probably right to be skeptical, but here's how we're thinking about it and here's why we think it's reasonable." That was incredibly valuable. I learned that raising the question was good. He wasn't offended. He appreciated the thinking, even though he still went with his original assessment. I also learned that ultimately, it's his judgment call, and my job is to make sure he has good information.

Building your reputation in those first three years is actually simple: be reliable, do quality work, and be someone people want to work with. I think a lot of junior people believe that they need to be the smartest person in the room, or the one who has the clever insight, or the one who works the hardest. Some of that helps. But what actually compounds is reliability. Be the person who does what they say they're going to do. Be the person who builds models without errors. Be the person who asks clarifying questions. Be the person who's easy to work with. These things seem small but they compound over time. By year three, you're not known for any one brilliant insight. You're known as someone you can trust, and that is actually the more valuable reputation.

## THE TRANSITION: FROM EXECUTING TO THINKING

Somewhere between year three and year five, if you're on a reasonable path, you start to shift from being someone who executes other people's ideas to someone who is beginning to have ideas of your own. You start to lead small parts of deals. You start to be in the room when the team is deciding what to work on, not just executing once the decision is made. You might be called an associate or a senior analyst or a VP depending on the firm, but the transition is real regardless of the title.

This is where the job starts to feel different. You're still putting in substantial hours, but now some of those hours are spent thinking about strategy rather than just doing modeling. You're responsible for maintaining quality but you're also responsible for delegating some of the work. You're beginning to interact with clients, not as a spectator but as someone who is representing the firm.

The shift from executor to manager is harder than I anticipated. When you're a junior analyst, your job is very clear: build this model, analyze this document, answer this question. When you become someone who is managing other people's work, the job becomes much less clear. You need to delegate effectively, which means you need to trust that other people will do good work, which means you need to not micromanage, which is terrifyingly hard when it's ultimately your name on the work. You need to be able to communicate what you want in a way that's clear enough that people can execute it without you having to stand over them. You need to be confident enough to push back when something isn't right, but not so controlling that people feel like they're being treated like children.

I remember the first deal I effectively led as an associate. It was a medium-sized LBO at a firm where I was responsible for the credit analysis and the financial model. I had two analysts working for me. I was absolutely panicking underneath the surface because I was certain that one of them would make a mistake that would blow up the deal, or that I would miss something that was important, or that everyone would realize that I had no idea what I was doing. What actually happened is that I checked the analysts' work carefully, caught a few things, gave them feedback, and they fixed it. The first version of the model had some issues but I caught them and we refined it. And somehow, we produced good analysis that the team trusted.

The thing I learned from that experience is that leadership in this industry isn't about perfection. It's about having your hands on the work well enough to know when something is wrong, being able to articulate why it's wrong, and then trusting that people will improve it. And it's about not being paralyzed by the fear that you'll miss something. You will miss something sometimes. You catch most of it and you learn from the things you miss.

Client interaction is a totally different skill from the technical work, and I think a lot of people underestimate how important it is to develop it early. When you're junior, you're probably invisible to clients. You're building a model or doing analysis, but the client doesn't really know you exist. As you move up, you start to represent the firm in front of the client. You're the person presenting analysis. You're the person responding to questions. You're the person the client has a relationship with.

This is fundamentally about communication and trust. Clients need to trust that you understand the situation, that you're being honest with them about the risks and opportunities, and that you're not just telling them what they want to hear. They also need to find you credible. You can be credible because of your technical knowledge, but more importantly, you're credible because you think deeply about their business, you ask intelligent questions, and you're forthcoming when you don't know something.

I had a situation where I was presenting analysis to a client and the client asked me a question I didn't know the answer to. My instinct was to stall, to say something vague, to try to figure out what they wanted to hear. Instead, I said "I'm not sure, but I'll look into that and get back to you by tomorrow." The client was satisfied with that. What I learned was that admitting knowledge gaps is actually more credible than pretending to have all the answers. The fact that I followed up and delivered was what mattered.

The question of whether to do an MBA somewhere between year three and year five comes up for a lot of people. I can give you a simple answer: it depends on where you want to go. If you want to stay in credit investing at a firm like the one you're at, it doesn't matter much. It might help you move up slightly faster and it certainly doesn't hurt. If you want to move to the buyside as a portfolio manager, especially at a place that's in the upper tier, it probably helps, though it's not required. If you want to move into private equity or hedge funds, it's less clear. If you want to do something entirely different—go in-house, move into advisory, do something outside of finance—it's probably useful.

What I would say is that the opportunity cost is real. You're paying a lot of money and you're taking a year or two away from your career. You're not accumulating experience in the industry. You're in school. For people who need the credential to make a jump, it makes sense. For people who are doing fine where they are, I'm less convinced. I took an executive MBA while I was working, which was absolutely brutal but probably made more sense than leaving for a full-time program would have been.

The move to the buyside is one of the major inflection points in a leveraged finance career. A lot of people make it. Some people make it and then move back. The appeal is pretty clear: better compensation, more autonomy, a role that's more directly about investing rather than advising. The question is when and how to make that move. I know people who made it after three years and never looked back. I know people who tried it and came back to the sellside. I know people who waited until year ten and then made the jump.

What I can tell you is that the buyside and the sellside are quite different environments. On the sellside, you're measured by client satisfaction, deal volume, and revenue generation. You're managing relationships with lots of clients. You're working on whatever comes in the door, which means you're building broad expertise. On the buyside, you're measured by investment returns. You're managing fewer relationships, often with your own capital and that of your partners. You're working on investments where you've made a choice about what to pursue. It's deeper in some ways and shallower in others.

The move is probably easier when you're moderately senior—a VP or senior associate—rather than when you're very junior or very senior. Very junior, you probably don't have enough skills yet to be useful at a fund. Very senior, you probably have a good situation where you are and the move requires you to prove yourself again. In the middle, you have enough skills to be useful but you're junior enough that moving is part of your natural career progression. The mechanics of making the move are usually about having a relationship with someone at a fund who thinks you'd be valuable. This is where those relationships I talked about earlier actually matter.

## THE MID-CAREER INFLECTION POINT: THE FORK IN THE ROAD

Somewhere between year seven and year twelve, you're going to face a significant fork in the road. You're probably at a point where you could stay where you are and continue moving up within the organization. You've built a track record, you're producing good work, you're known in the industry. And you're probably wondering if you should stay or if you should try something different. This decision is often not made on purely rational grounds, and I think that's okay.

The different paths you could take include: staying on the sellside and moving toward senior roles (director, MD track); moving to the buyside as a portfolio manager or senior analyst; moving to advisory work at a restructuring firm or strategic advisory; or moving in-house to work for a company, a portfolio company, or an operating platform. There are other options too—starting something yourself, moving into public markets, teaching—but those are the main ones.

The compensation reality is one factor in this decision, though I think people weight it too heavily. At the sellside, MD compensation at a bulge bracket can be $1-5 million, depending on the firm and how much revenue you're actually generating. At a more specialized firm, it's probably $500,000 to $2 million. On the buyside, if you're successful as a portfolio manager with carry, you could make significantly more, but you could also make less if things don't go well. In advisory, you're probably in the $500,000 to $2 million range. In-house, you're probably in the $200,000 to $500,000 range unless you have a really senior role.

What I've learned about compensation is that past a certain point—and that point is lower than most people think—more money doesn't actually make you happier. The difference between making $200,000 and making $500,000 is meaningful. It affects your financial security, your ability to buy a home, to travel, to take risks. The difference between making $500,000 and making $2 million is less meaningful. It's still real money and it matters, but it doesn't dramatically change your life in the way that earlier jumps do. The difference between making $2 million and making $5 million is mostly about ego and comparative position. I say this as someone who has made it to the higher end of the compensation spectrum: the incremental value of each additional dollar diminishes significantly.

This is why I think the "golden handcuffs" problem is so prevalent in this industry. People stay in situations they've grown to resent because the compensation is very high and they've structured their lives around that compensation. I've seen fifty-year-olds who are genuinely unhappy but can't imagine leaving because their mortgage and their kids' school tuitions and their lifestyle are all predicated on a $2 million year. It's a trap. I've also seen people make major moves—leaving to start a fund, leaving to go inhouse, taking a significant pay cut to work somewhere they actually wanted to work—and being much happier for it.

The framework I'd recommend for evaluating whether to make a move is to think about it as an investment decision. What are the expected returns? Not just financial returns, though those matter, but returns in terms of learning, autonomy, impact, and enjoyment. What are the risks? What's the downside if it doesn't work out? Can you afford that downside? What's the optionality—if you make this move, does it open up other opportunities or does it close them down?

When I was considering moving from the sellside to a buyside role, I did this analysis. The expected financial return was higher but uncertain because of the carry structure and the performance of the fund. The expected learning return was very high because I would be making investment decisions rather than advising on them. The expected autonomy return was high. The risk was that the fund might not perform well, in which case I might be in a position where I needed to find another job in a market where I was a bit out of touch. But I calculated that I could afford that risk. I had enough savings. I was in a position where I could take a year or two to land somewhere if needed. So I made the move.

Building your network as a senior professional is different from the networking you did when you were junior. You're not trying to get in the door; you're trying to expand your sphere of influence and create opportunities. You're probably going to conferences. You're probably writing. You're probably speaking at industry events. You're probably on boards of industry organizations. These things all do matter for building a network, but they matter in ways that are different than you might think.

What I've found is that the most valuable part of being a senior professional is being a resource for people. You have enough knowledge and enough connections that you can be useful. Someone wants to make a transition and they ask for advice. Someone is thinking about starting a fund and they want to talk through the idea. Someone has a deal they're trying to understand and they want to get your perspective. Being generous with that—actually spending time thinking about people's problems and trying to be helpful—is how you build and maintain a network. It's not transactional. It's not about getting something. It's about having enough security and success that you can afford to be generous.

Managing your career like an investment is a useful metaphor. You're trying to build an asset that compounds. Early in your career, you're investing heavily in building skills and a reputation. You're not trying to maximize short-term returns; you're trying to maximize long-term value. As you move up, you're trying to protect that asset and grow it. You're thinking about risks—concentration risk if you only know one type of deal, for example. You're thinking about diversification—maybe you develop skills in multiple areas rather than being a specialist in just one. You're thinking about exit value—what is your human capital worth if you need to leave this particular position?

This is the point in your career where work-life balance actually becomes something you can influence rather than just something that happens to you. You have more control over your time. You can negotiate. You can choose projects. You can set boundaries. What I've found is that the people who are happiest in their mid-career are the ones who actually use that leverage to make choices that make their lives better rather than just accumulating more money and more prestige. The people who are most stressed are the ones who maximize everything and end up with no margin in their lives.

## THE SENIOR YEARS: WHAT IT MEANS TO LEAD

By the time you've been in this industry for twelve years or more, you've accumulated a certain amount of credibility and authority, whether you wanted to or not. You're probably in a position where you're making decisions about what the team works on. You're probably responsible for developing talent. You're probably a name that matters in the industry. And you're probably wrestling with questions that are different from the ones you wrestled with earlier in your career.

What does it mean to be a senior practitioner? It's partly about the technical skills—you know credit analysis in a deep way, you understand the market, you can identify risks that other people miss. But it's also significantly about judgment, relationships, and the ability to think strategically about complex situations. A senior person isn't just someone who is better at the technical work; they're someone who understands the context in which the work is happening and can make decisions that account for that context.

Revenue generation versus investment acumen are two different measures of success, and which one matters more depends on where you are. If you're at a sellside firm, revenue generation matters enormously. The firm needs you to bring in clients, manage relationships, and produce deals. If you're on the buyside, investment acumen matters more. The firm needs you to make good calls on what to invest in and what to avoid. Most senior people succeed when they have both, but they usually lean into one or the other. I've seen incredibly smart investors who struggle with client management, and I've seen great rainmakers who are less good at the actual investment decision-making. The smartest firms figure out how to have both and structure roles accordingly.

Managing teams and developing talent is probably the thing that matters most to a senior person, and it's also the thing that people are least prepared for. The job of a senior person isn't just to make good investment decisions; it's to make good investment decisions and make sure that there are other people coming along who can do what you do. If you're not doing that, you're just delaying the day when the firm has to figure out how to replace you.

What I've learned about developing talent is that it requires spending time you think you don't have, being willing to slow down in the near term to build capability in the long term, and being honest about what people are good at and what they're not. I've made the mistake of trying to develop people in areas where they don't have aptitude, thinking that with enough effort they could get there. What I've learned is that you're better off taking someone who has natural aptitude and helping them refine it than you are trying to build aptitude where it doesn't exist. Some people are naturally good analysts. Some people are naturally good at client interaction. Some people are naturally comfortable with ambiguity. You can improve all of these things, but you can't create them from nothing.

The role of relationships at a senior level is probably more important than it is at any other point in your career. By the time you're senior, your value is partly your judgment but it's also significantly the relationships you have. You have relationships with clients who trust you and bring you deals. You have relationships with other professionals in the industry who will take your call, who will do a favor for you. You have relationships with people who work for you or have worked for you and who you've helped develop. These relationships are valuable in multiple ways: they're directly valuable because they mean you have deal flow and information; they're valuable because they're a reflection of your reputation; and they're valuable because they make the work enjoyable.

What the industry looks like from the top is quite different from what it looks like from the ground. When you're junior, you think the people at the top have it all figured out. They have perfect confidence and perfect information. The reality is that even at the senior level, you're making decisions with incomplete information. You're uncertain. You're trying to figure out what you don't know. What you have that junior people don't have is experience with previous situations that were similar, a better intuition for what questions to ask, and enough security that you're less crippled by the uncertainty.

Staying technically relevant while being strategic is a tension that senior people have to manage. You can't be in the model-building business anymore if you're truly senior. You don't have time. But you also can't be completely disconnected from the technical work because then you lose credibility and you miss things. What I've found works is staying in the model-building business enough to understand how decisions should be made, but not doing the work yourself. I'll look at a deal and I'll review the model and I'll know immediately whether the analysis is sound or whether there are questions, because I understand the mechanics intimately. But I'm not spending fifty hours a week building models. That's what the junior people are for.

The competitive dynamics among peers at a senior level can be surprisingly challenging. You came up with these people. You know them well. In many cases, you've worked together directly. And now you're competing with them for resources, for recognition, for deals, for advancement. This can be more stressful than competing with people you don't know because the relationships are real. I've seen friendships damaged by competition at the senior level, and I've seen people maintain friendships while competing effectively because they have a mature perspective about it.

What I think the healthiest senior cultures have is an understanding that there is competition but there is also genuine collaboration. People are competing but they're also sharing information, referring deals to each other, supporting each other's development of junior people. It's not zero-sum. You can have high standards and high competitiveness while also being collegial and generous. The people I respect most at senior levels are the ones who can do both simultaneously.

## TECHNICAL SKILLS THAT NEVER STOP MATTERING

Throughout a career in leveraged finance, certain technical skills matter more than others, and they matter differently depending on where you are in the progression. But there are a few that never stop being relevant.

Credit analysis is at the top of the list. This is the skill of looking at a business and understanding whether it can service its debt, what could go wrong, what the downside scenarios look like. A junior analyst does credit analysis. A senior professional does credit analysis. A partner at a fund does credit analysis. The mechanics change. The tools change. But the fundamental skill of being able to analyze credit quality is evergreen. I think this is partly because credit investing is fundamentally about preserving capital, which is never not important.

What credit analysis means changes across your career, though. When you're junior, it's about reading financial statements accurately, understanding what leverage ratios mean, knowing what questions to ask. As you get more senior, it's about pattern recognition—this company is starting to show some of the same dynamics I saw in another company ten years ago that ended badly. It's about understanding the broader context: what's happening in the supply chain, what's happening to the customer base, what's happening in labor markets. It's about having opinions about what the sustainable leverage is for a particular industry. The skills are cumulative, but they're not just more of the same; they're qualitatively different.

Financial modeling as a skill will never stop being relevant, though what it means to be good at it changes. In year one, being good at modeling means building a model that works, where all the calculations are correct and the outputs make sense. By year ten, being good at modeling means understanding which assumptions matter most, being able to build a model quickly that answers a specific question, and being able to sense-check whether the outputs are reasonable. I'm still building models sometimes when I need to think through a specific question, even though that's not my primary job anymore. The skill has just compressed. I can do in an hour what used to take four hours.

Documentation expertise is one of the rarest and most valuable skills in this industry. The people who truly understand credit agreements, indentures, and other transaction documents are in short supply and they command premium compensation. A lot of people think of documentation as a lawyering thing, but the best investors understand the documents intimately. You know which covenants actually matter. You know which provisions are standard and which are custom. You know what could go wrong. This is something you develop over time by working on documents, paying attention to what actually ends up mattering, and learning from other people.

Restructuring knowledge is probably the most counter-cyclical skill. When the market is booming, restructuring feels less important. When the market seizes up, restructuring becomes incredibly valuable. If you go through a cycle or two in this industry, you'll probably go through a period where your firm is looking at distressed situations or where you're on the defensive in your portfolio. Understanding how restructuring works—the legal framework, the strategic options available, the incentives of different parties—is valuable in those moments. I've seen junior people who didn't live through a downturn be genuinely lost when it came because they'd never had to think about what happens when a company gets into trouble. The people who had seen at least one cycle had frameworks for understanding the situation.

Market intuition is another skill that develops over time and is hard to teach. This is the ability to read flows, sentiment, and technical dynamics in a way that informs your investment thesis. It's understanding that even if you think a credit is sound, if the market is dysfunctional and pricing it in a way that doesn't reflect fundamentals, you might want to be cautious. It's understanding when a particular sector is getting too much attention and when it's being ignored. It's understanding when leverage is pricing in growth that the company probably can't achieve. This is partly instinct and partly pattern recognition. I think it's developed through paying attention to what's actually happening in the market over a long period of time, and through conversations with other people who are paying attention.

Writing is the most undervalued skill in finance. The ability to write clearly and persuasively makes an enormous difference in your career, and I think people underestimate this. Your analysis is only useful if other people can understand it. Your recommendations are only valuable if you can articulate why you're making them. Your thoughts on the market are only worth something if you can communicate them effectively. And yet, most people don't invest in improving their writing. They think it's something you either have or you don't. You can absolutely improve your writing. You do it by writing a lot, by reading how other smart people write, and by being willing to cut out the parts that are weak and re-work them.

Public speaking and presentation skills matter more at the senior level but they matter throughout. Being able to walk into a room and communicate something clearly, answer questions, and handle disagreement effectively is a skill that compounds. I've been afraid of public speaking throughout my career and I've pushed myself to do it anyway. It's gotten easier. I'm probably better at it than I was, though I'd still describe myself as someone who gets nervous before presentations. What I've learned is that being nervous is fine. What matters is that you prepare, you know what you're going to say, and you stay focused on communicating the information rather than worrying about what people think of you.

Keeping learning and staying current is a discipline. The world of credit investing has changed substantially in my twenty years in it. The instruments have evolved. The risks have changed. The participants have changed. The amount of technology has changed. If you're not constantly reading, talking to people, thinking about what's changing, and updating your mental models, you get stale. You miss shifts. You miss signals. The senior people who are still sharp are the ones who treat staying current as part of their job, not something they'll do in their free time.

## THE EMOTIONAL AND PSYCHOLOGICAL DIMENSION

I think career advice in leveraged finance almost always ignores the emotional and psychological dimension, which is a major oversight. This job does things to you. It teaches you certain ways of thinking. It exposes you to stress. It shapes your identity. I don't think any of that is inherently bad, but it's worth acknowledging.

Impostor syndrome is real in this industry and it doesn't go away. I know people at extremely senior levels who still, occasionally, think that they're not as smart as everyone else, that they've just been lucky, and that someday everyone will figure out that they don't actually know what they're doing. I have these thoughts sometimes. I'm reasonably confident in my ability to analyze credit, but I still get imposter syndrome on different dimensions. When I'm in a room with very quantitatively sophisticated people, I feel less smart on the quant side. When I'm with people who are better writers, I feel less capable as a communicator.

What I think you need to do with impostor syndrome is acknowledge it and then move forward anyway. You're never going to feel completely certain. You're never going to be completely sure that you're the best person for the decision. You make the best judgment you can with the information you have, and you accept that you might be wrong. This is, actually, what experienced decision-making is. It's not perfect confidence. It's good judgment with humility about your limitations.

Dealing with failure is something everyone has to do but nobody really talks about. I've been wrong about credit. I've recommended passing on companies that turned out to be great investments. I've invested in companies that didn't work out. I've lost money on deals. I've lost clients. I've had junior people leave because I wasn't good at developing them. These things are genuinely painful when they happen and they're particularly painful early in your career because each one feels like it matters enormously.

What I've learned is that failure is information. If you're in this business and you've never been wrong about anything, you're probably not making enough decisions. You're being too conservative. The people who take the most risk and make the best returns are also the people who have failures. They just learn from them. I've tried to get better at this—at actually analyzing what went wrong rather than just feeling bad about it. Did I miss a signal? Did I make a wrong assumption? Did I get unlucky? Usually it's some combination. And then I try to figure out what I'll do differently next time.

The stress reality is worth addressing directly. Credit investing is stressful. You're making decisions about how to deploy capital. You're living with those decisions. You're exposed to market moves. You're exposed to company-specific risk. You're exposed to economic cycles. During periods of market dislocation—2008, 2020, and other moments—the stress is acute. People lose sleep. People's relationships are affected. People's health suffers.

I don't think the solution is to pretend this isn't true. I think the solution is to develop practices that help you manage the stress. Some people do this through exercise, which is probably the most evidence-supported stress management tool. Some do it through meditation or other mindfulness practices. Some do it through hobbies that take their mind off work. Some do it through talking to people they trust about what they're struggling with. What I've found is that if you don't actively manage stress, it compounds. You get more irritable. You sleep worse. Your decision-making gets worse. Your relationships get worse. It's a downward spiral.

Mental health in leveraged finance is something that needs to be taken seriously and often isn't. The culture in much of this industry is one where you don't talk about struggling. You just power through. And yes, there are people who are genuinely energized by the challenge and who love the intensity. There are also people who are struggling and not saying anything because they think they're supposed to just be fine with it. I've known people who went through significant depression while working in this industry. I've known people with anxiety disorders. I've known people in recovery from substance abuse issues that were exacerbated by the stress and culture of the industry.

If you're struggling with mental health, the first thing to do is get help. Talk to someone. Find a therapist. Talk to your doctor. Don't try to just power through it. The second thing to do is find an environment where you can actually be yourself. If you're at a firm where you can't talk about struggling, where the culture is one of relentless toughness, find a different firm. There are firms with more sane cultures. There are buyside roles that are less demanding. There are ways to be in this industry without destroying your mental health.

Building resilience through difficult markets is something that time teaches you. The first significant market dislocation you go through is genuinely scary. It feels like the end of the world. You don't know if the market will recover. You don't know if your firm will survive. You don't know what will happen to your investments. It's terrifying. Then you realize the market does recover. It takes time, sometimes a long time, but it recovers. Your firm probably survives. Some of your investments work out and some don't. But the cycle completes. And that gives you confidence for the next time.

The comparison trap is something I think particularly affects people in leveraged finance because the industry is relatively small and everyone knows what everyone else is making or what opportunities people have gotten. I've struggled with this at various points in my career. Someone I know gets promoted to MD before I do. Someone I came up with at a different firm raises a billion-dollar fund while I'm still at my firm. Someone leaves and joins a prestigious platform. And I feel like I should have done it first, like somehow I'm falling behind. This is not a productive way to think about your career, but it's a seductive one because there's always someone doing something more impressive than you.

What I've tried to do, with mixed success, is remember that I don't actually know other people's situations. Maybe that person who got promoted early is now stuck in a role they hate. Maybe the person who raised a fund is under enormous stress because the fund isn't performing. Maybe the person at the prestigious platform makes less money because the salary didn't compensate for the higher-cost location. I don't know their full situation, so comparing myself to them on the one or two dimensions I know about is not very informative. And more importantly, my career is my career. The success of my career should be measured by my own goals and values, not by what other people are doing.

Finding meaning in financial work is something that becomes important as you move up in your career. When you're junior, your meaning is probably just "I'm learning cool stuff" or "I'm making money." When you're more senior, at least for many people, just accumulating capital stops being meaningful. What becomes meaningful is having an impact—on your team, on your portfolio companies, on the market, on your community. Some people find meaning in the intellectual challenge. Some find it in the investment returns. Some find it in the teams they build. Some find it in being a resource for other people. But the meaning has to be there or the work becomes hollow.

The relationship between work and identity is something I've wrestled with a lot. I've been in leveraged finance for twenty years. It's a big part of who I am. But I'm also aware that it's not my entire identity. I have other interests, other relationships, other dimensions to my life. And I'm aware that if my identity is entirely wrapped up in my work, and then something happens to my work—I get fired, I retire, I move into a different role—I lose my sense of self. I try to maintain a sense of identity that's bigger than my job, though I'm not sure I always succeed at it.

## RELATIONSHIPS AND NETWORKING

I've emphasized this a few times already but it bears emphasis again: relationships are the single most valuable asset you can build in a career in leveraged finance. Not relationships in the transactional sense—relationships where you're trying to extract value. Real relationships where you actually care about the other person and where there's mutual interest in each other's success.

I have a theory that the most valuable relationships in this industry are with people who are scattered across firms and geographies. Early in my career, I worked closely with certain analysts and associates. We went through difficult periods together. We collaborated on deals. We learned from each other. Some of those people ended up at different firms. Some moved to the buyside. Some went into law. Some went in-house. Now, twenty years later, I have this network of intelligent people across the industry. That network is valuable in ways that are sometimes direct—a client connection, a deal opportunity, an introduction to someone I need to meet—and sometimes just about having people to talk to about the work and the challenges.

The best advice I ever got about networking came from someone early in my career who said: "Be the person people want to know." That's it. Don't go to networking events trying to extract value from people. Show up at industry events and actually engage with people. Be interested in what they do. Offer value when you can. Stay in touch with people over time. Remember things about their lives. Send them a relevant article if you see one. Introduce them to people who might be valuable for them to know. Do these things for the right reasons—because you genuinely want to be helpful—and not because you're calculating what you'll get back.

The mentor-mentee relationship is worth spending more time on because I think a lot of people get it wrong. A good mentor relationship is not about the mentor telling the mentee what to do. It's about the mentor asking good questions and helping the mentee think through situations. It's about the mentor opening doors and making introductions. It's about the mentor being honest about what they see in the mentee—their strengths and the areas where they need to develop.

Finding mentors involves being vulnerable enough to ask for help. I think a lot of people don't do this because they think it will make them look weak or unprepared. In my experience, people who are worth having as mentors respect the maturity it takes to say "I need help thinking about this" rather than pretending to have everything figured out. The ask should be specific. Don't ask someone to be your mentor without being clear about what you're looking for. "Could we grab coffee once a month? I'd like to get your perspective on decisions I'm facing" is much better than "would you mentor me?"

Being a mentor is also an important part of a senior career. I think a lot of senior people don't realize how valuable they could be if they invested in developing other people. I've been shocked, at times, to realize that a specific piece of advice I gave someone—something I didn't think was that significant—actually changed their trajectory. That's tremendously rewarding. It's also good for the industry. If everyone who gets to a senior level brings other people with them, the talent pipeline improves. If people just extract value and don't give back, the talent pipeline gets weaker.

Cross-functional relationships are something that builds over time and is valuable in ways that are sometimes overlooked. You work closely with lawyers on documentation. You work with operations people on portfolio management. You work with compliance people on regulatory issues. These aren't people you probably think of as part of your "network," but they're essential. The lawyers who understand your business model and who have worked with you on deals end up knowing a lot about what you do and how you think. When you need something complex done, you call them because they understand the context. The operations people who have dealt with your portfolio and helped you through difficult periods end up being strategic partners. These relationships matter.

Industry conferences and events are worth attending but you have to be strategic about which ones. There are probably a dozen major conferences in leveraged finance every year. You can't go to all of them. I think it's worth going to two or three where the quality of attendees is high and where you'll run into people you actually know and want to spend time with. It's worth focusing on conversations over "networking." If I'm at a conference and I see someone I know and respect, I'm going to spend an hour having lunch with them rather than trying to meet ten new people for five minutes each.

LinkedIn and social media have created new ways to stay in touch and to build visibility. I think for a lot of people in this industry, LinkedIn was initially seen as something for people looking for jobs. Now it's more mainstream. What I've found is that it's useful for staying in touch with people and for making sure that people know what you're thinking about and what you're working on. Posting thoughtful comments on posts, sharing relevant ideas, occasionally writing something that represents your thinking—these things keep you visible and keep you connected. The key is to do it in a way that feels authentic. If you're just posting random corporate-speak, nobody cares. If you're actually sharing your perspective, people engage.

How relationships compound over time is one of the most underrated phenomena in career building. When I was junior, I didn't think much about relationships compounding. I just thought about getting the work done. But looking back, the people I stayed in touch with even when we weren't working together directly ended up being incredibly valuable. Someone I worked with as an analyst and then didn't see for five years ended up being the person who brought me the opportunity that changed my career. Someone I helped at a conference ten years ago ended up being in a position to do me a significant favor. These things compound. You do good work and you maintain relationships and you stay in touch. And over time, that becomes increasingly valuable.

## MONEY AND COMPENSATION

Let me be straightforward about compensation because a lot of career advice in this space dances around it.

The compensation structure for leveraged finance is genuinely attractive, particularly as you move up. A junior analyst at a good firm makes somewhere between $100,000 and $200,000 in base plus bonus. An associate makes $200,000 to $500,000. A VP makes $300,000 to $1 million. A senior VP or director makes $500,000 to $2 million. An MD at a big firm could make anywhere from $1 million to $5 million or more if they're generating significant revenue.

On the buyside, if you're a senior analyst or junior PM at a credit fund, you might make $250,000 to $750,000 in salary plus potentially bonus and carry. If you're a PM with carry, your total compensation could be substantially higher—$1 million to $10 million or more depending on the fund's performance and the size of your carry.

These are genuinely large numbers, particularly relative to most other industries. This is one of the reasons people are attracted to the field.

What I think is important to understand is how compensation is used as a retention tool in this industry. You get a decent salary, but the real compensation comes through bonuses that are paid at the discretion of the firm and potentially through equity or carry that vest over time. This creates a situation where you're very much tied to the firm, because leaving means giving up unvested compensation. It's not necessarily nefarious—the firm has invested in your development and they want to retain people—but it's definitely a feature of the business model.

How to negotiate compensation effectively is something that more junior people should think about earlier than many do. Most compensation conversations are annual and they follow a bonus. You should go in with data about market compensation—what people at your level at other firms are making. You should be specific about what you're asking for and why. You should understand that compensation isn't just salary; it's the whole package. Sometimes the firm can't move on salary but they can move on bonus or on other benefits. Sometimes they can't move on overall cash but they can move on vesting schedules or other terms.

I think it's also worth saying that negotiation is probably easier from a position of strength. If you're someone the firm really values, if you have other options, if you're not desperate—that's when you have leverage. If you're asking for a raise while also making clear that you're happy to leave, you're in a much better position than if you're asking for a raise while being terrified of losing your job.

The equity or cash trade-off at different career stages is real. As a junior person, you want cash because you need to live and you don't know if you'll be here long. As someone more senior, equity and carry become more interesting because they can compound substantially. The question is whether the equity or carry is actually going to be valuable. If you're at a firm that's not performing well, equity is not valuable. If you're at a firm that's performing really well, equity can be worth significantly more than the cash salary you're giving up.

When money should drive decisions and when it shouldn't is worth thinking about carefully. Money should drive decisions when you're trying to reach a particular financial goal—buying a home, paying off debt, achieving financial security. Money shouldn't drive decisions when it's just about accumulation for its own sake, or when it's going to make you significantly unhappy.

I've watched people make decisions entirely based on compensation—staying at a firm they're unhappy at because the compensation is high, moving to another role primarily because it pays more—and I've seen those decisions work out and not work out. The times I've seen them not work out are usually because the person discovers that money alone doesn't make the job tolerable. They're still unhappy. They're just unhappy and making more money.

The diminishing marginal utility of income is a real phenomenon, and most people don't realize it until they've already structured their entire life around achieving a high compensation number. At some point, earning another million dollars a year doesn't actually change your life in any meaningful way. You still have the same number of hours in a day. You still can't be in two places at once. You still have the same basic relationships. What you have is more money, which is great, but it's not as great as the marginal utility you'd get from that money at a lower income level.

## THE INDUSTRY'S EVOLUTION AND YOUR CAREER

Leveraged finance has changed substantially in my twenty years in it, and those changes matter for how you think about your career.

When I started, the main players in leveraged finance were investment banks, a handful of large buyside firms, and some boutique shops. Now there are hundreds of firms. Private credit has exploded. Direct lending has become a massive asset class. The number of platforms, the number of sponsors, the amount of capital available to deploy has all increased dramatically.

The rise of direct lending is the biggest structural shift I've seen. Direct lending—where you're lending directly to companies rather than buying leveraged loans in the secondary market—has become a huge business. It's created new career paths. It's changed what skills matter. It's changed the economics of deals. If you're building a career now, understanding direct lending is probably more important than it was for me at the same career stage.

Technology's impact on credit investing has been profound and it's accelerating. When I started, we were building models in Excel and the state of the art for data management was a shared drive. Now there's sophisticated analytics, machine learning applications to credit analysis, data visualization tools that didn't exist. The people who are going to succeed going forward are people who can work effectively with technology—not necessarily people who are building the technology, but people who understand what it can do and how to use it.

ESG and sustainability are becoming increasingly important, though I think the trend is more substantial on the investing side than it is on the portfolio company side. As an investor, you're probably being asked questions about ESG in your investment process. ESG risk is becoming a part of credit analysis. Whether this is a genuine shift toward sustainability or a passing fad is a more complex question, but what's clear is that you need to understand it and have a perspective on it.

Regulation's growing influence has been steady throughout my career. The regulatory environment after 2008 changed a lot of things. The Dodd-Frank Act created new requirements. Regulation of private credit is increasing. As you move through your career, regulatory expertise becomes valuable. It's not something you need to be an expert in, but understanding the regulatory framework matters.

Globalization has changed the nature of the business. When I started, most of the deals were domestic (assuming you were in the US). Now there's a global capital market. You might be competing with firms in Europe or Asia. You might be investing in companies in multiple countries. Geographic arbitrage used to be a real thing; now it's less so because capital flows freely.

What the next twenty years might look like is genuinely uncertain, which is probably healthy. Artificial intelligence is likely to have impacts on this industry that we can't fully predict. Changes in the regulatory environment could materially shift the business model. Market structure shifts could create opportunities or challenges. My best guess is that the fundamentals of credit investing don't change—you're still analyzing companies, still trying to understand whether they can service their debt, still taking risks for potential returns. But the context in which you're doing that work is definitely going to evolve.

How to position yourself for an evolving market is about being flexible and staying current. If you build skills in one area and the industry shifts, you want to be able to adapt. This is why I think having broad exposure early in your career matters. You learn about different types of deals, different geographies, different industries. You're not locked into one particular approach. And as the industry changes, you can adapt your skills to what matters in the new environment.

## DIVERSITY AND INCLUSION

I'm going to be honest about diversity in leveraged finance because most people aren't willing to be.

The current state of diversity is that this remains a predominantly male, predominantly white industry, particularly in senior roles. Walk into a room of MDs at most firms and you'll see that the vast majority are men and the vast majority are white. This is improving, but it's improving slowly. The number of women has increased. The number of people of color has increased. But the rates of increase are not particularly fast and the glass ceiling is still very much in place for women and for people of color.

What the industry does well is hire smart people from different backgrounds. There are wonderful people of all backgrounds in leveraged finance. What the industry does poorly is retain them, develop them, and advance them at the same rates as white men. I think this is partly structural and partly cultural. The structural part is that a lot of recruitment and advancement happens through networks, and networks are often homogenous. The cultural part is that the industry has certain unspoken norms about what success looks like, how you should behave, how you should communicate, and those norms tend to advantage people who are already dominant in the industry.

The business case for diverse teams in credit analysis is pretty straightforward. When you're making investment decisions, you're trying to assess risk and opportunity. Your ability to do that is better when you have diverse perspectives. A team that is all similar tends to have similar blind spots. A team that is diverse is more likely to catch things that others miss. From a pure business perspective, diverse teams make better investment decisions. This is not just nice-to-have; it's a competitive advantage.

Practical steps for improving inclusion are many and most firms know what they are. The question is whether they actually implement them. Recruiting from diverse sources requires active effort. Mentoring and development of people from underrepresented groups requires active effort. Creating a culture where people from different backgrounds feel included requires active effort and it requires senior people who are willing to push back on biases when they see them. A lot of firms do some of these things. Very few do all of them systematically.

Personal reflections on working in a historically homogenous industry: I've benefited from the default status quo. I'm a man, I'm white, I come from a middle-class background. I've never had to fight against biases in hiring or promotion. I've never had to worry that I was getting less opportunity because of who I am. This is an advantage that I've had. I'm increasingly aware that I've had this advantage and that not everyone has. Part of being a senior person is using whatever advantage you have to help create space for people who haven't had the same advantages.

How to be an ally and advocate is something that I think senior people need to take seriously. It starts with self-awareness—recognizing that there are dynamics at play that you might not be seeing because you've benefited from the status quo. It continues with action—actively recruiting from diverse sources, mentoring people from underrepresented groups, speaking up when you see bias, advocating for people who aren't in the room. It means taking risks on people who might not fit the traditional mold, because some of the best people I've worked with were people who weren't the "typical" leveraged finance person.

## WHAT TWENTY YEARS ACTUALLY TEACHES

After twenty years, what lessons actually stick? What wisdom actually transfers?

The first is that patience is more valuable than I would have guessed when I started. I was in a tremendous hurry. I wanted to be promoted quickly. I wanted to make partner. I wanted to have made it. What I've learned is that the people who end up being most successful over long careers are often not the ones who are in the biggest hurry. They're the ones who take their time, who build deep expertise, who think about where they want to be in ten years rather than just the next promotion. Patience doesn't mean passivity—you're still working hard and pursuing opportunities. It means not panicking if you're not on the fastest possible trajectory.

The importance of intellectual humility compounds over time. When you know a lot about something, it's easy to become confident that you know more than you do. The best investors I know are comfortable saying "I don't know" and "I was wrong about that." They're curious about perspectives that differ from their own. They're willing to be convinced by good arguments. They're not attached to being right. This intellectual humility is probably the biggest predictor of long-term success in investing.

How to know when you've found the right role is something you get better at over time. Early in your career, you might take any role because you're grateful to have it. Over time, you get pickier. You know what kind of work energizes you. You know what kind of team environment you need. You know what kind of compensation structure matters to you. When you find a role where all of those things align, it feels different. You don't mind working hard because you're working on things you care about. The time disappears. You're engaged.

The difference between success and fulfillment is real and they're not always the same thing. I know very successful people who are not fulfilled. They made MD. They're making a lot of money. And they're not happy. I know people who are moderately successful by industry standards who are deeply fulfilled because they're doing work that matters to them, with people they like, in an environment that treats them well. Success is usually visible to the outside world. Fulfillment is usually only visible to you. The question worth asking yourself is which one actually matters to you.

Advice I'd give my twenty-five-year-old self: First, you're not as behind as you think you are. Everyone around you also feels kind of lost. Second, be generous with people. Help them. Share what you know. This comes back to you in ways you don't expect. Third, don't take everything so seriously. This is a job. It's an interesting job and there's interesting money involved, but it's a job. You're not solving for cancer. You're analyzing whether a leveraged buyout is a good investment. It matters but it doesn't matter that much. Fourth, build skills that transfer. Credit analysis is valuable anywhere you end up. Writing is valuable anywhere you end up. Understanding complex systems is valuable anywhere. The specific knowledge of how a particular firm does things is less valuable. Fifth, your relationships are more important than your compensation. Invest in them.

Why the best people in this industry share what they know is something I've learned through observation. The people who are most successful and most fulfilled are usually the ones who are most generous with their time and their knowledge. They write. They speak at conferences. They mentor. They share perspectives. This isn't because they're altruistic—they are, to some extent—but it's also because sharing what you know keeps you sharp. It forces you to articulate your thinking. It keeps you engaged with the industry even as you move up. And it signals that you're someone who has confidence in your own thinking. Insecure people hoard knowledge. Secure people share it.

Final reflections on why I wrote this book and this chapter: I wrote it because I remember what it felt like to be twenty-five and confused about how to have a career in this industry. I remember feeling like everyone else knew something I didn't. I remember thinking the advice I was getting was either too technical or too motivational and not enough about the actual experience of being a human being with a career in leveraged finance. I wrote it because I've lived through different market cycles and different career stages and I've learned things that I think are worth sharing. I wrote it because the more senior you get, the more you realize that what matters is passing things forward. The industry is better off when people who have been successful help other people think about their careers more clearly. This chapter is my attempt to do that.

The truth of a career in leveraged finance is that it can be genuinely rewarding. It can also be genuinely difficult. It's rarely boring. The money is real and meaningful. The intellectual challenge is real. The stress can be significant. The hours can be long. And if you navigate it well—if you make choices aligned with your values, if you invest in relationships, if you stay curious and humble, if you remember that this is a means to a life rather than the entirety of a life—it can be a career that you look back on with real satisfaction.

My career has given me the privilege of working with extraordinary people. It's given me the opportunity to learn deeply about companies, industries, and markets. It's given me financial security that has allowed me to take risks and make choices that are aligned with what matters to me. It's given me a network of intelligent, interesting people around the world that I can call on and who can call on me. It's also cost me time that I didn't spend with people I love. It's created stress that has affected my health. It's sometimes felt empty in ways that accumulating capital and making good investments couldn't fill.

The challenge is holding all of that as true simultaneously. This job is genuinely valuable. And sometimes it's a trap. You're probably capable of more than you think. And you're probably also doing things that don't need to be done as intensely as the industry does them. The best career in leveraged finance is one where you're aware of all of that, where you make conscious choices about what you're optimizing for, and where you're willing to change course if what you thought you wanted turns out to be different from what actually makes you happy.

That's what twenty years teaches: the importance of paying attention to your own experience, the willingness to adapt, and the understanding that a successful career is ultimately the one that feels successful to you, not the one that looks successful to everyone else.
