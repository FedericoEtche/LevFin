# Chapter 11: The Change of Control Covenant

## Introduction

The Change of Control covenant stands as a fundamental protection in leveraged finance, yet its traditional function has been systematically dismantled and repurposed by sponsor-friendly innovations. Historically a crucial safeguard, it provided investors with a clear exit right when a company's ownership and strategic direction were subject to dramatic shift.

The covenant's core purpose is to shield investors from credit risks associated with a new, unknown owner and management team—risks that were not underwritten at the time of initial investment. By compelling the issuer to make a repurchase offer, the covenant forces a refinancing or, at minimum, provides creditors the option to be repaid.

However, the modern iteration has been reshaped into a key battleground. The rise of sophisticated exceptions—collectively known as "portability"—represents a fundamental realignment of risk from sponsors to creditors. These provisions allow a company's debt to remain in place following an acquisition, prioritizing a sponsor's desire for seamless, cost-effective exit over the creditor's right to reassess their investment under new stewardship.

---

## 11.1 The Core Mechanism: The Bondholder Put Right

Contrary to common misconception, the occurrence of a Change of Control event does not trigger automatic redemption or an Event of Default. Instead, it compels the issuer to make a formal "Change of Control Offer" to bondholders.

### The Put Right

This mechanism grants investors the "put right"—the right, but not the obligation—to require the issuer to repurchase their bonds. The standard repurchase price is **101% of principal amount**, plus accrued and unpaid interest.

This "101 put" is a cornerstone of the covenant's protective function.

### The Bondholder's Decision

The decision to tender remains with the bondholder. An investor may choose *not* to exercise their put right for strategic reasons:

- Bonds trading above 101% in the secondary market
- Comfort with credit profile and strategic direction of new ownership
- Expectation that new owner will improve the business
- Desire to maintain exposure to the credit

The put right is an *option*, not an obligation.

## 11.2 Defining the Trigger: Beneficial Ownership and Permitted Holders

### The Primary Trigger

A Change of Control is typically deemed to occur when any "person" or "group" becomes the **Beneficial Owner** of **more than 50% of total voting power** of the company's Voting Stock.

For precision, this is often defined by referencing Sections 13(d) and 14(d) of the U.S. Exchange Act—the beneficial ownership reporting rules that govern public company disclosure.

### Permitted Holders

This defined term carves out a specific group of owners—typically incumbent financial sponsors, founders, or key management at time of debt issuance—from the CoC calculation.

A transfer of beneficial ownership *among* these pre-approved Permitted Holders does not trigger the covenant. This clarifies the covenant's intent: it is not meant to be triggered by internal reorganizations or transfers between existing sponsors, but to protect creditors from sale of the business to an *external* third party.

### Secondary Triggers

Beyond the ownership threshold, Change of Control provisions often include additional triggers:

**Board Composition:** A majority of board seats becoming occupied by persons not nominated by existing shareholders or the incumbent board ("continuing directors" requirement).

**Sale of Substantially All Assets:** A sale or transfer of all or substantially all assets of the company and its subsidiaries, taken as a whole.

**Merger/Consolidation:** Certain mergers or consolidations where existing shareholders cease to hold majority voting power.

## 11.3 The Erosion of Protection: The Rise of Portability

"Portability" has fundamentally subverted the traditional purpose of the Change of Control covenant. It refers to provisions that allow a company's debt to remain in place—to be "portable"—following an acquisition by a new owner, thereby denying investors their traditional put right at 101.

This represents a significant shift in balance of power, prioritizing sponsor exit flexibility over creditor reassessment rights.

### Leverage-Based Portability

The most common portability feature in European high-yield bonds is leverage-based portability. This provision stipulates that an event which would otherwise constitute a Change of Control will *not* be deemed as such if the issuer satisfies a specified pro forma leverage ratio test after giving effect to the transaction.

**The Transformation:** This converts a hard, structural protection into a flexible, financial one. It subordinates qualitative credit judgment—the identity, track record, and strategy of the new owner—to a single, often manipulable, quantitative metric.

**The Manipulation Risk:** The pro forma leverage ratio can be engineered to meet the test through aggressive EBITDA add-backs for anticipated cost savings and synergies. Creditors are compelled to accept a new risk profile so long as the numerical test is met.

### The Free Option

Portability functions as a "free option" for the sponsor on a future sale—paid for by creditors in the form of increased, uncompensated risk. The sponsor gains exit flexibility; creditors receive nothing in exchange for bearing the risk of new ownership.

## 11.4 Market Trends: The Systematic Assault on Protection

Analysis of European leveraged loan terms between 2019 and 2021 reveals a multi-pronged assault on creditor protections, with aggressive and systematic trends toward more sponsor-friendly portability provisions.

### Flexible Leverage Testing

The proportion of deals allowing "flexible testing" of the leverage ratio doubled from 33.3% in 2019 to 66.6% in 2021. This transforms the test from a single, high hurdle into a series of lower hurdles—dramatically increasing the sponsor's chances of executing a sale without triggering the put right.

### Equity Cheque Requirement

In 2021, a full 50% of deals reviewed did not require a minimum equity investment from the incoming acquirer. In 2019, only one-third of deals lacked this requirement.

The removal of this condition eliminates a key signal of a new sponsor's commitment and "skin in the game"—leaving creditors exposed to potentially undercapitalized buyers.

### Event of Default Blocker

The traditional condition that no Event of Default be continuing for portability to apply has vanished from the market. In 2021, no deals contained a "no EoD" blocker, and two-thirds contained only a limited "no material EoD" blocker.

This is particularly alarming: sponsors can now exit a business even as it is actively defaulting on obligations, leaving creditors to deal with fallout under new ownership.

### Usage Caps

In 2021, 50% of deals had no cap on the number of times portability could be utilized. In stark contrast, in 2019, 100% of deals reviewed included a "one time only" usage cap.

This transforms portability from a single, exceptional exit opportunity into a standing feature—allowing multiple ownership changes without creditor consent and compounding long-term risk.

### Sunset Periods

The average "sunset" period—during which the portability feature can be exercised—increased from 18 months in 2019 to 22 months in 2021. This longer window grants sponsors greater strategic patience, allowing them to time exits for maximum advantage while extending uncertainty for creditors.

## 11.5 Other Loopholes and Exceptions

Beyond leverage-based tests, several other common exceptions further weaken the Change of Control covenant.

### Successor Parent Exception

This provision ostensibly permits internal corporate reorganizations—such as insertion of a new holding company—without inadvertently triggering a CoC.

**The Loophole:** Its common formulation creates significant vulnerability. Sponsors can draft this exception broadly enough to permit another company owned by the same sponsors to acquire the issuer—facilitating portfolio company mergers or transfers without triggering the creditor put right.

### Public Holding Company Exception

This exception prevents a CoC trigger if the company becomes a wholly-owned subsidiary of a publicly-traded Parent Entity, provided no single person or group controls the new parent.

**The Risk:** A sale to a widely-held public company constitutes a material change in ownership and strategy, yet the CoC covenant would not be triggered—denying creditors an exit.

### IPO Exception

Many covenants provide that an initial public offering does not constitute a Change of Control, even if it results in the sponsor selling down below 50% ownership. The rationale is that public markets provide liquidity for bondholders—but the credit risk profile may fundamentally change.

### Ratings-Based Triggers (The "Double Trigger")

Some Change of Control provisions include a "double trigger" requiring both:
1. A change in ownership, AND
2. A ratings downgrade within a specified period

If ratings are maintained, no put right arises—even though ownership has changed. This effectively outsources creditor protection to rating agencies.

## 11.6 High-Yield Bonds vs. Leveraged Loans

A fundamental difference exists in how Change of Control provisions can be amended in European high-yield bonds compared to leveraged loans.

### European High-Yield Bonds

Amending a "sacred right" such as the Change of Control provision is typically an "all or nothing" endeavor. Such change requires consent from a high supermajority of bondholders—often 90% of outstanding principal. If this threshold is met, the amendment binds *all* holders, effectively "dragging" non-consenting investors into new terms.

### U.S. High-Yield Bonds

In contrast, amendments to sacred rights typically require consent of each "affected holder"—granting individual investors a powerful veto that their European counterparts lack.

### European Leveraged Loans

These agreements often feature "structural adjustment" provisions allowing changes to key terms with only consent of participating lenders. Lenders who do not consent are not bound by the change and remain in the original loan tranche.

This difference in amendment mechanics means issuers face a much higher bar to alter CoC terms in the European bond market.

## 11.7 Interaction with Events of Default

It is critical to distinguish between the Change of Control event itself and a resulting Event of Default.

### The Sequence

1. **CoC Event Occurs:** Ownership threshold crossed
2. **Offer Obligation Triggered:** Issuer must make repurchase offer within specified period (typically 30-60 days)
3. **Bondholders Decide:** Accept or reject the offer
4. **Potential Default:** *Failure* to make the offer, or to secure necessary waivers, can constitute an Event of Default

The occurrence of a CoC triggers the obligation to make an offer. It is the *failure* to make that offer that can subsequently constitute an Event of Default—potentially leading to acceleration of the debt.

### Casino Guichard-Perrachon

In its 2023 disclosures, Casino anticipated that a proposed investment by EP Global could necessitate a waiver from secured creditors to prevent a change of control from triggering default. The company publicly considered entering a formal conciliation process to provide a framework for these negotiations.

This demonstrates a company seeking to prevent default by obtaining necessary creditor consents ahead of a transaction that could otherwise breach the covenant.

## 11.8 Strategic Implications

### For Creditors

**Primary Risk:** Being involuntarily locked into an investment under a new, unknown, and potentially more aggressive or highly leveraged owner—without ability to exit at a premium. The credit profile underwritten at issuance can be fundamentally and adversely altered.

**Due Diligence Focus:**
- Identify all portability exceptions: leverage-based tests, Successor Parent, Public Holding Company loopholes
- Scrutinize leverage test specifics, including potential for manipulation via EBITDA add-backs
- Confirm presence or absence of investor-friendly conditions: usage caps, no-EoD blockers
- Model impact of a new owner adding leverage at parent level or via structurally senior debt at non-guarantor subsidiaries

### For Sponsors

**Primary Objective:** Portability provides critical flexibility for future sale. It eliminates friction, cost, and market risk associated with mandatory debt refinancing that would otherwise be required upon exit—maximizing returns.

**Negotiating Stance:**
- Negotiate widest and most permissive portability carve-outs during documentation
- Ensure leverage tests are achievable and can be tested on flexible dates
- Resist restrictive conditions: equity cheque requirements, usage caps, broad default blockers
- Maximize universe of potential future buyers

---

## 11.9 The Broader Context: A Microcosm of Market Shift

The degradation of the Change of Control covenant is emblematic of the broader "covenant-lite" era defining leveraged finance. This shift results from influential financial sponsors successfully driving for more documentary flexibility to manage portfolio companies and execute investment strategies.

This evolution mirrors the broader convergence of leveraged loan and high-yield bond terms—where sponsor-friendly features from one market are increasingly adopted in the other, leading to what many now term "high yields in disguise" in the loan market.

The modern Change of Control covenant, with its expansive portability carve-outs, is no longer primarily a tool for credit protection. It has been repurposed into a mechanism to facilitate M&A and sponsor exits.

## Summary: The Diligence Framework

**Trigger Analysis:**
- What ownership threshold triggers the covenant? (50% is standard)
- Who are the Permitted Holders? How broadly defined?
- Are there secondary triggers (board composition, asset sales, mergers)?
- Is there a "double trigger" requiring ratings downgrade?

**Portability Analysis:**
- Is there leverage-based portability? What is the test level?
- Can the test be satisfied using pro forma synergies and add-backs?
- Are there usage caps? (One-time only vs. unlimited)
- Is there an EoD blocker? (Full, limited to "material," or none)
- What is the sunset period?

**Exception Analysis:**
- How broad is the Successor Parent exception?
- Is there a Public Holding Company exception?
- Does an IPO qualify as a Change of Control?

**Amendment Analysis:**
- What consent threshold is required to amend CoC provisions?
- Is this a "sacred right" requiring supermajority?
- Can non-consenting holders be dragged into amendments?

**Put Right Mechanics:**
- What is the repurchase price? (101% is standard)
- What is the offer period?
- What happens if offer is not fully subscribed?
