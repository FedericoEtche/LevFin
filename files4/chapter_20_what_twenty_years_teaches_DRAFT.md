# Chapter 20: What Twenty Years Teaches

*This chapter is different from the others. The preceding pages gave you frameworks, mechanics, definitions. This one is personal. Take what's useful; discard what isn't.*

---

## The Real Math

Here's something nobody tells you at the start: this career is roughly 90% frustration and 10% joy.

That's not cynicism. It's arithmetic. The deals that fall apart. The analysis that goes nowhere. The internal politics that block good work. The markets that turn against you. The weekends lost. The calls at midnight. The sense that no matter how much you do, someone wants more.

And then — occasionally — something works. A deal closes. A piece of analysis proves right. Someone important notices. You feel, briefly, that you've made it.

That feeling lasts a few days. Then the treadmill restarts.

I've watched people enter this industry expecting the 10% to be the norm. It isn't. The question isn't whether you can tolerate frustration — everyone thinks they can. The question is whether you can tolerate it for years, compounding, while watching the highlight reels of people who seem to have it easier.

Some can. Many can't. Both are legitimate answers.

---

## Where I Started

I come from a place where wearing a suit meant you'd made it.

My father came home every evening in an impeccable suit, hair slicked back perfectly. I don't know if he loved his work. I don't know if it fulfilled him. But I knew what it *represented*: he had crossed over to the other side. The side where you weren't struggling.

Then I saw Wall Street — the original, the 1987 one — and I wanted to be Gordon Gekko. That's a cliché. I know it's a cliché. Every finance memoir has some version of this origin story. But clichés become clichés because they're true for enough people, and it was true for me.

The suit, the tie, the skyline, the deals. That was the image of success I carried into this career.

It took years to understand that the image and the reality have very little to do with each other.

---

## The Path Without Shortcuts

I had no connections. No pedigree. No family friends who could make a call. No alumni network that opened doors.

I was also — I am also — socially awkward. Not charmingly eccentric. Just awkward. The kind of person who doesn't know how to work a room, who says the wrong thing at dinners, who finds small talk physically exhausting.

In an industry built on relationships, this is not an advantage.

I didn't come up through a bulge bracket bank. I didn't have the credential that makes people assume competence before you've proven it. Everything I learned, I learned by doing it — often by doing it wrong first.

Trial and error. Emphasis on the error.

This path has one benefit: you understand things deeply because you had to. Nobody handed you the playbook. You wrote it yourself, in the dark, getting things wrong until you got them right.

It has many costs. The obvious ones are career progression, compensation, access. The less obvious one is what it does to you internally — the constant sense of having to prove something, of being one mistake away from exposure, of not quite belonging in rooms you've earned the right to enter.

---

## The Dark Territory

I need to say something about mental health, because nobody in leveraged finance books talks about it, and they should.

There were periods when I wasn't okay.

Not dramatically unwell. Not hospitalized. Just... not okay. The weight of the hours, the pressure, the uncertainty about whether any of it was worth it. Stretching myself into territory that, looking back, I shouldn't have entered without help.

I pushed through things I probably shouldn't have pushed through. I told myself it was temporary, that it would ease up after this deal, this quarter, this year. It didn't ease up. The demands just shifted shape.

If you recognize yourself in this — if you're reading this at 2am, running on caffeine and anxiety, telling yourself it's fine — I want to say something clearly: get help earlier than I did.

This industry will take everything you're willing to give. It has no internal mechanism to say "enough." That mechanism has to come from you, and sometimes you need support to build it.

There's a macho culture that treats endurance as virtue. Wearing exhaustion as a badge. I participated in that culture. I'm not proud of it.

The people I respect most now are not the ones who sacrificed everything. They're the ones who figured out how to be excellent without losing themselves. I'm still figuring that out.

---

## The Paradox of Technical Mastery

I built my career on technical skills. Understanding the mechanics. Reading documentation that others skimmed. Building models that actually worked. Knowing the *why* behind the *what*.

This book is a product of that orientation. Hundreds of pages on covenants, definitions, structures — the craft of understanding how leveraged finance actually works.

And here's the uncomfortable truth: technical skills are necessary but increasingly insufficient.

When I started, deep technical knowledge was rare. It was a differentiator. You could build a career on being the person who actually understood the documents.

Now? Technical knowledge is more accessible than ever. AI tools are flattening the advantage. A junior analyst with the right prompts can produce analysis that took me years to learn how to do.

What remains scarce is something else: judgment, relationships, the ability to influence, presence in a room. The soft stuff. The stuff I was never naturally good at.

I don't regret my path. The understanding I built is real, and it's mine. This book exists because of it.

But if I'm being honest about what *moves careers* — especially into senior positions — it's not the technical mastery. It's the other thing.

## The Advice I Didn't Follow

Here's what I'd tell my younger self, the version who had technical skills but not yet judgment:

**Never eat alone.**

Every meal is an opportunity to build a relationship. I ate a lot of meals alone, at my desk, because I thought the work was what mattered. The work matters. But the relationships compound in ways the work doesn't.

**Think of yourself as a brand.**

Not in a cringe, self-promotional way. In a strategic way. What do people think of when they think of you? What are you known for? What makes people seek you out rather than someone else?

I spent years being the person who chased opportunities. The better position is being the person opportunities chase.

**Technical skills are replaceable. Impact on people isn't.**

Unless you're von Neumann — and you're not, neither am I — there's someone who can do the technical work as well as you. Maybe better. What's harder to replace is the effect you have on people. The trust you build. The sense that working with you makes things better.

I'm giving you advice about things that don't come naturally to me. That's intentional. I learned these lessons late and incompletely. Maybe you can learn them earlier.

---

## The Holy Grail

So why did I do it this way? Why spend twenty years pursuing technical mastery when the returns to that investment were diminishing?

Because I couldn't help it.

Some people are motivated by deals, by money, by status. Those things matter to me — I'm not above them. But they're not what gets me up in the morning.

What gets me up is understanding. The holy grail, for me, has always been the same: the ultimate truth. The fundamentals. The *why* behind the *how*.

I wanted to understand leveraged finance — really understand it, not just execute transactions within it. I wanted to know how the machine worked, not just how to operate the controls.

This book is an attempt to capture that understanding before it dissipates. Before the market evolves again. Before I forget the things that took years to learn.

I think I've gotten close. Not all the way — the grail remains slightly out of reach; it always does. But close enough that writing it down feels worthwhile.

---

## The Treadmill Doesn't Stop

One more thing.

I've worked with senior people, successful people, people whose names you'd recognize. Almost none of them feel like they've arrived. Almost all of them are still chasing something — the next deal, the next title, the next validation.

The treadmill doesn't stop. There's no point where you finally feel like you've made it and can relax. If you're waiting for that feeling, you'll wait forever.

What I've learned to do — imperfectly, inconsistently — is find meaning in the work itself. Not in the destination, because there is no destination. In the practice. In the craft. In the understanding.

There's always someone doing better than you. There's also someone doing worse — much, much worse. The comparison game has no winner.

The only sustainable approach I've found is to define success internally. Did I understand something today that I didn't understand yesterday? Did I do work I'm proud of? Did I treat people well?

Some days the answer is yes. Many days it isn't. That's the job.

---

## What Remains

After twenty years, what do I actually know?

I know how covenants work. I know how deals are structured. I know what documentation protects and what it doesn't. I know the games that sponsors play and the counter-moves available to creditors.

That's what the rest of this book is about. The technical knowledge. The frameworks. The mechanics.

But I also know some things that don't fit in frameworks:

- That the people who last are not always the smartest
- That relationships matter more than analysis in the long run
- That the industry will take whatever you give it and ask for more
- That success feels different from the inside than it looks from the outside
- That understanding something deeply is its own reward, even when the market doesn't value it
- That you can survive the dark periods, but you shouldn't go through them alone

I don't know if this chapter was useful. It's different from the others — less structured, more personal. Maybe it belongs in this book; maybe it doesn't.

But it's honest. And after twenty years, that's worth something.

---

*The technical knowledge in this book will become outdated. Markets evolve, documentation changes, new structures emerge. What I hope lasts is the way of thinking — the orientation toward understanding, the skepticism toward easy answers, the recognition that behind every covenant and every basis point are human beings making decisions under uncertainty.*

*Good luck.*
