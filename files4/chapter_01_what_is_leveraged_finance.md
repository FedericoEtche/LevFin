# Chapter 1: What Is Leveraged Finance?

## 1.1 The Milken Revolution

Leveraged finance can be a broad and confusing subject. It embodies a significant number of different topics that can be challenging even on a standalone basis. Capital structures, credit agreements, indentures, covenants, intercreditor arrangements — each could fill its own textbook.

But strip away the complexity and, at its core, leveraged finance is just debt. Corporate debt. Specifically, corporate debt provided to companies with a credit rating below investment grade.

That's it. That's the definition. Everything else is detail.

To understand how we got here, though, you need a bit of history. Because it wasn't always obvious that below-investment-grade companies should be able to borrow money at all.

Before the 1980s, the bond market was a genteel place. Blue-chip corporations issued bonds. Governments issued bonds. If your company had a credit rating below BBB- from Standard & Poor's or Moody's, the capital markets were effectively closed to you. You could negotiate with your bank for a loan. You could ask your shareholders for more equity. Or you could do without.

The implicit assumption was that companies with lower credit ratings were simply not creditworthy. They were "junk." Why would anyone lend them money?

Michael Milken had a different idea.

Milken was a banker at Drexel Burnham Lambert who noticed something the market had overlooked: below-investment-grade companies weren't necessarily *bad* credits. They were just *different* credits, requiring different analysis and different pricing. If you understood the risks and charged appropriately for them, these bonds could offer attractive risk-adjusted returns. The higher yields compensated for the higher default rates. The math worked.

There's an entire bibliography on Milken — the genius, the excesses, the eventual fall, the conviction, the pardon. You can spend weeks going down that rabbit hole if you're interested. But for our purposes, what matters is this: he created a market where none existed. He proved that capital could flow to companies previously considered unworthy of it.

The bonds he pioneered were called "junk bonds" — bonds, yes, but with that "junk" label sitting rather too prominently on the nose. It wasn't great marketing. But the market didn't care about the name. Investors hungry for yield piled in. Companies previously locked out of capital markets suddenly had options. Milken became, for a time, the most important banker in America.

Over time, the terminology softened. "Junk bonds" became "high-yield bonds." Same product, better branding. Then, as loans emerged as an alternative to bonds for these same borrowers, someone coined the umbrella term "leveraged finance."

And here we are.

## 1.2 A Working Definition

So what exactly counts as leveraged finance?

**Leveraged finance is corporate debt — whether bonds or loans — provided to companies with credit ratings below investment grade.**

In practice, this means companies rated between BB+ and B- by Standard & Poor's or Moody's. (Fitch exists too, but for most market purposes, you need at least one of S&P or Moody's in the picture.)

Here's how the ratings stack up:

| S&P Rating | Moody's Rating | What It Means |
|------------|----------------|---------------|
| BBB- and above | Baa3 and above | Investment Grade |
| BB+ | Ba1 | Top of leveraged finance |
| BB | Ba2 | Leveraged finance |
| BB- | Ba3 | Leveraged finance |
| B+ | B1 | Leveraged finance |
| B | B2 | Leveraged finance |
| B- | B3 | Bottom of typical leveraged finance |
| CCC+ and below | Caa1 and below | Distressed territory |

The BB+ to B- range is the heartland. Below B-, you're typically in distressed territory — companies where the question isn't "will they pay on time?" but "will they pay at all?" Above BB+, you're approaching investment grade, where different rules, different investors, and different pricing conventions apply.

One thing worth noting: a company's rating isn't permanent. Companies get upgraded. Companies get downgraded. A BB credit can become investment grade if performance improves. An investment-grade credit can become leveraged finance if performance deteriorates. The line between the two worlds is crossed regularly, in both directions.

## 1.3 The Instruments

A few words about what you'll actually encounter in the leveraged finance market.

Within this space, you'll find a whole zoo of debt instruments that show up in company capital structures. We'll go deeper into each in later chapters, but here's a quick orientation:

**Senior Secured Debt** sits at the top of the capital structure, with first claim on assets:

- **Leveraged loans** (often called "Term Loan B" or just "TLB") — floating-rate debt, typically held by CLOs and credit funds
- **Senior secured notes** — high-yield bonds with collateral backing
- **Senior secured floating rate notes** — same thing, but with a floating coupon instead of fixed

**Subordinated Debt** ranks junior to senior secured, compensated with higher yields for the additional risk:

- **Second lien loans and notes** — secured, but behind the first lien in the waterfall
- **Senior notes** — confusingly named, since they're typically unsecured and "senior" only to equity
- **Senior subordinated notes** — explicitly junior to senior debt
- **Mezzanine** — hybrid instruments sitting between senior debt and equity
- **Holdco PIK notes** — debt issued at a holding company level, often paying interest "in kind" (more debt) rather than cash

**Unitranche Debt** is a more recent innovation — a blended single-tranche facility that combines senior and subordinated characteristics, typically provided by direct lenders rather than the syndicated market.

Don't worry if these all blur together right now. The distinctions between secured and unsecured, between first lien and second lien, between contractual subordination and structural subordination — these will become clear as we work through the mechanics. For now, just understand that "leveraged finance" encompasses a spectrum of instruments, each with its own risk profile, pricing, and position in the capital structure.

## 1.4 A Note on Scope

One more distinction before we proceed: the difference between the **mid-market** and **traditional leveraged finance**.

The mid-market involves smaller companies, smaller deal sizes, and often direct lending rather than syndicated transactions. Documentation tends to be simpler. Investor bases are more concentrated. Relationships are more bilateral. A mid-market deal might involve a single direct lender providing a €100 million unitranche facility to a company with €20 million of EBITDA.

Traditional leveraged finance — the focus of this book — involves larger transactions. Broadly syndicated loans. Publicly traded high-yield bonds. Institutional investor bases including CLOs, credit funds, and asset managers. Deal sizes run into the hundreds of millions or billions. Documentation is standardised enough to allow liquid secondary trading, yet bespoke enough to require careful analysis.

Why focus on traditional leveraged finance? Because it's the first mover when it comes to innovation.

The structures, the covenant technology, the creative (and sometimes destructive) developments that reshape the market — these emerge first in the large-cap space before filtering down to smaller deals. If you want to understand where the market is going, you watch what's happening in traditional leveraged finance.

In later chapters, you'll understand why the word "innovation" deserves those implicit quotation marks. Not all innovation serves all stakeholders equally.

## 1.5 The Eternal Question: How Much Debt?

How do market participants determine the right amount and type of debt for a given company?

This question consumed my early career. Every deal seemed different. I kept searching for a pattern, a formula, some algorithm that could be mechanically applied to determine exactly the right debt instrument, its size, its terms.

Here's the first thing I'll tell you — and perhaps the most important conclusion you'll draw from this entire book:

**There are no absolute truths.**

What worked for one company may not work for another. What worked for a company last year may not work for that same company today. The "optimal" capital structure that emerges from a beautifully constructed financial model will rarely coincide with what the company actually ends up issuing.

Debt structuring is a craft. You need to consider what the financial model says, yes. But you also need to understand market conditions. Market trends. Which investors have capital to deploy right now. What instruments those investors are actually looking for. What terms they'll accept.

The answer to "how much debt?" is never purely analytical. It's a negotiation between what a company needs and what the market will provide.

## 1.6 The Fruit Market

Let me try an analogy. It's a bit abstract, but bear with me.

Imagine a fresh fruit market. Many vendors operate here. Some sell all types of fruit. Some sell only bananas. Some sell only apples. But here's the key distinction: some vendors will sell you just a few pieces of fruit — like a corner shop — while others only deal in bulk. A wholesaler's business model requires large orders. They won't sell you five bananas.

Now picture a family shopper who needs five bananas and five apples. They need to find a vendor who (a) sells both bananas and apples, and (b) will sell small quantities. If they approach the wholesaler, they'll be turned away.

Next, picture a restaurant owner who needs fruit to run the business for a day. The corner shop doesn't have enough. They need the vendor with trucks and wooden crates stacked high.

Finally, picture the owner of a supermarket chain. They walk past all the fruit stands and end up at a desk with no fruit in sight — just someone with a laptop, taking orders for scheduled delivery.

This is how capital markets work.

Depending on how much money you need and what you need it for, you have to find the right vendor. If I need $100,000 to buy a house, I go to a bank, provide documentation, and wait a couple of weeks. If I want to buy something for $500, I swipe my credit card and get instant access. But if Meta needs $10 billion to build a data centre, Mark Zuckerberg makes phone calls, holds meetings with investors and bankers, and assesses what pools of liquidity might be available for Meta's purposes at that moment. The timeline is considerably longer than my credit card swipe.

Leveraged finance is one part of this capital markets ecosystem — the part devoted to companies with credit ratings in the BB+ to B- range. When these companies need money, they knock on the doors of a specific set of vendors who are willing and accustomed to provide debt to these borrowers, at a certain price and under certain conditions.

## 1.7 What Makes Leveraged Finance Different?

So what distinguishes leveraged finance from, say, investment-grade debt?

One word: **leverage**.

Consider a person earning $100,000 per month who wants to borrow $10,000 with no existing debt. This person probably has a pre-approved loan sitting in their banking app. A few clicks, no questions asked.

Now imagine that same person asking for $2 million. That's almost twice their annual gross income. The bank will have questions. They'll want documentation.

Now imagine them asking for $10 million. The institution considering this loan will conduct detailed analysis. They'll build a spreadsheet, run scenarios, stress-test assumptions. Can this person actually repay? Under what conditions? What happens if their income drops? They'll probably want security — collateral, covenants, restrictions on what the borrower can do.

This last scenario captures leveraged finance. You're not necessarily lending enormous sums in absolute terms. What matters is **the amount borrowed relative to the borrower's size and cash flow**.

A company raising €500 million might be conservatively financed or aggressively leveraged — it depends entirely on whether that company generates €50 million or €500 million of EBITDA. The ratio is what matters.

And because the ratios are higher in leveraged finance — because lenders are taking more risk relative to the borrower's capacity — they demand more protections. More analysis. More covenants. More security. More control.

For institutions lending money in this market, sleeping well at night requires taking all necessary steps to achieve some degree of certainty that the money will be repaid, one way or another.

What "one way or another" means in practice is a theme we'll return to throughout this book.

## 1.8 The Cast of Characters

Leveraged finance transactions involve a recurring cast of participants. Here's who you'll meet:

**Private Equity Sponsors**

The most prominent users of leveraged finance are private equity firms — the "sponsors." They raise capital from institutional investors (pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, endowments, family offices) and use it to buy companies. The purchase price is funded with a combination of equity (typically 30-50%) and debt (the rest). That debt is leveraged finance.

The names are familiar: Blackstone, KKR, Apollo, Carlyle, CVC, EQT, Permira. Plus hundreds of smaller firms across the mid-market. Their business model depends on leverage — debt magnifies equity returns when things go well. It also magnifies losses when they don't.

**Corporate Issuers**

Not everything involves private equity. Many companies access leveraged loans and high-yield bonds directly, without a sponsor, to fund acquisitions, refinance existing debt, or finance capital expenditure. These might be family businesses, publicly traded companies that happen to have sub-investment-grade ratings, or former sponsor-backed companies that have since been sold or listed.

**Arranging Banks**

Investment banks sit at the centre of the market. They advise on structures, underwrite the debt (temporarily taking it onto their own balance sheets), and syndicate it to institutional investors. The major European arrangers include JP Morgan, Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Bank of America, Deutsche Bank, Barclays, BNP Paribas, and others.

Arrangers earn fees for this service. Their willingness to underwrite — to commit capital before knowing exactly who will buy — is what makes the market function. When arrangers pull back, as they did in 2008 and briefly in 2022, the market seizes up.

**Institutional Investors**

The buyers of leveraged loans and high-yield bonds are predominantly institutional:

- **CLOs (Collateralised Loan Obligations)**: Structured vehicles that buy leveraged loans, package them into portfolios, and issue tranches of securities with different risk profiles. CLOs are the single largest buyer of leveraged loans — often 60-70% of the market. Their constraints and preferences shape how loans are structured.

- **Credit Funds**: Dedicated funds investing in leveraged credit, often managed by alternative asset managers.

- **Mutual Funds and ETFs**: Retail-accessible vehicles offering high-yield exposure.

- **Insurance Companies and Pension Funds**: Long-term investors seeking yield.

- **Hedge Funds**: Active traders pursuing relative value, event-driven, or distressed strategies.

**The Supporting Cast**

Various intermediaries keep the machinery running:

- **Administrative Agents**: Banks that process drawdowns, track compliance, and manage the lender syndicate.
- **Trustees**: Independent fiduciaries representing bondholders. Unlike agents, trustees can't negotiate — they act only at bondholder direction.
- **Legal Counsel**: Every party has lawyers. Sometimes multiple sets.
- **Rating Agencies**: S&P, Moody's, and Fitch assign the ratings that define who's in and who's out.

## 1.9 A Word on LBOs

The leveraged buyout — the LBO — is the canonical leveraged finance transaction. Understanding its basic mechanics contextualises everything else.

**How It Works**

A private equity sponsor acquires a company using a combination of equity (typically 30-50% of the purchase price) and debt (the remainder). The debt is raised at the acquired company's level, secured against its assets, and repaid from its cash flows.

The logic is simple: debt reduces the equity cheque and amplifies returns.

If a sponsor buys a company for €1 billion using €400 million of equity and €600 million of debt, and later sells it for €1.5 billion after repaying €200 million of debt, the equity has grown from €400 million to €700 million — a 75% return.

Had they used all equity, the same sale would be a 50% return.

Of course, leverage cuts both ways. If the company's value falls to €800 million, the equity is worth only €200 million — a 50% loss. With all equity, the loss would have been 20%.

**The Typical Structure**

A standard LBO capital structure might include:

- **Revolving Credit Facility (RCF)**: Committed but undrawn, for working capital and liquidity. Senior secured, provided by banks.
- **Term Loan B (TLB)**: The core senior secured debt. Floating rate, 6-7 year maturity, syndicated to institutional investors.
- **Senior Secured Notes**: High-yield bonds that may sit alongside or replace the TLB. Fixed rate, longer tenor.
- **Subordinated Debt**: Second lien, mezzanine, or holdco PIK — additional leverage at higher cost, junior to the senior layer.
- **Equity**: The sponsor's investment, plus any management rollover.

**Why It Matters**

The LBO structure shapes everything in leveraged finance documentation. Covenants exist to protect lenders from sponsor behaviour that might benefit equity at creditors' expense — excessive dividends, asset stripping, additional leverage.

The tension between sponsor flexibility and creditor protection is the central drama of every negotiation. When we examine covenants in later chapters, we're examining the rules designed to manage this tension.

Understanding the LBO is essential context for understanding why those rules exist and why they're so heavily contested.

## 1.10 What Lies Ahead

This book moves from foundational concepts to technical detail.

**Part I: Foundations** establishes the building blocks. You're in it now. The chapters that follow examine capital structures — how the layers of debt and equity fit together. We'll explore EBITDA, that deceptively simple metric underpinning virtually every calculation in the market. And we'll tackle debt sizing — the art of determining how much leverage a company can bear.

**Part II: The Instruments** looks closely at loans and bonds. They serve similar economic purposes, but their structures, documentation, and behaviour in distress differ in ways that matter enormously.

**Part III: Covenants** is the technical heart of the book. Covenants are the rules of engagement — the contractual provisions governing what a borrower can and cannot do with creditors' money at risk. We'll work through each major category: restricted payments, debt and liens, asset sales, change of control, guarantees and collateral. We'll examine how these covenants interact, where the loopholes hide, and how they perform when companies hit trouble.

**Part IV: Amendment Mechanics** addresses what happens when someone wants to change the rules. Voting thresholds, sacred rights, consent solicitations, the differences between loans and bonds — these mechanics determine who controls the documentation and under what circumstances.

**Part V: When Things Go Wrong** covers restructuring. The amend-and-extend transactions, the liability management exercises, the formal insolvency processes. This is where documentation gets tested. Where the protections you thought you had either hold or crumble. Where "one way or another" reveals its meaning.
