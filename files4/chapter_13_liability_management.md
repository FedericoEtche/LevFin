# Chapter 13: Liability Management Transactions

*Or: The Polished Term for Creditor-on-Creditor Violence*

## Introduction

Let us be direct about what we're discussing.

"Liability Management Transaction" is the sanitized term that appears in press releases, restructuring advisor pitches, and investor presentations. It sounds clinical, almost administrative—as if the company is simply tidying up its balance sheet.

In practice, an LMT is an exercise in **creditor-on-creditor violence**: a transaction where a distressed borrower utilizes existing baskets, carve-outs, and voting thresholds to prioritize new capital or favored existing creditors at the direct expense of minority or non-participating creditors. The borrower extracts value from the existing collateral package and pledges it to new money, thereby diluting or subordinating legacy lenders who had no say in the transaction.

This chapter is the culmination of everything that came before. The covenant mechanics of Chapters 7-11 are the weapons. The amendment thresholds of Chapter 12 are the rules of engagement. This chapter shows how the game is actually played.

---

## 13.1 The Core Objective: Priming and Value Extraction

At the heart of every LMT is a single goal: accessing liquidity or extending maturities by offering "better" debt to new or participating investors. This is achieved by creating a class of debt that is structurally or effectively senior to existing claims—a process called **priming**.

The borrower extracts value from the existing collateral package or restricted group and pledges it to new money, thereby diluting or subordinating legacy lenders.

The transaction is not a consensual restructuring where all stakeholders share pain equitably. It is a zero-sum reallocation where one creditor group gains at another's expense—enabled by the very flexibility that sponsors negotiated into the documents during the good times.

---

## 13.2 The Three Primary Archetypes

While LMT execution can be highly bespoke, three distinct transaction types have emerged as the primary playbook. Each exploits specific weaknesses in modern documentation to achieve the same objective: manufacturing priority for new money at the expense of existing creditors.

### The Drop-Down (J. Crew Style)

**The Objective:** Strip valuable assets from the existing creditors' collateral package and use them to secure new, structurally senior financing.

**The Mechanics:**

1. Borrower identifies valuable, separable assets—typically intellectual property, a profitable division, or key operating subsidiaries
2. Borrower uses investment capacity under the Restricted Payments covenant to transfer these assets to a newly designated **Unrestricted Subsidiary**
3. Once designated as Unrestricted, the subsidiary and its assets are no longer bound by negative covenants or collateral obligations
4. The Unrestricted Subsidiary incurs new secured debt, pledging the transferred assets
5. This new debt is structurally senior to original creditors—the new lenders have a direct claim on the assets, while original lenders have only a claim on the residual equity of the borrower

**The Seminal Case:** J. Crew transferred valuable trademark IP to an Unrestricted Subsidiary, stripping it from the collateral package. The technique has since migrated to Europe (Intralot, Olympic Entertainment, Neiman Marcus/MyTheresa).

**Why It Works:** The transfer is classified as a "Permitted Investment" under the RP covenant, which means it is explicitly *excluded* from the Asset Sale definition. No fair market value requirement. No 75% cash consideration. No proceeds waterfall. The assets simply disappear from the credit group.

### The Uptier (Serta Style)

**The Objective:** Subordinate existing lenders by manufacturing a new super-priority tranche, using majority lender consent to impose subordination on a non-consenting minority.

**The Mechanics:**

1. Borrower identifies a group of existing lenders willing to participate (typically slightly over 50%)
2. Borrower proposes an amendment to permit incurrence of new "super-priority" debt ranking senior to existing facilities
3. Participating lenders consent to the amendment *and* exchange their existing debt for the new super-priority paper
4. The amendment passes with majority consent
5. Non-participating lenders remain in the original tranche—now contractually and effectively junior to the new super-priority debt

**The Key Enabler:** In many credit agreements, subordination is *not* a sacred right. It can be imposed by simple majority (50.1%) consent. The participating majority receives enhanced priority; the non-participating minority is pushed down the waterfall.

**The U.S. Mechanic:** In U.S. credit agreements, "open market purchase" provisions allow the borrower to repurchase debt from select lenders without pro rata sharing—enabling the non-pro rata exchange that characterizes Serta-style transactions.

**The European Dynamic:** In European leveraged loans, Intercreditor Agreements typically require all-lender consent for ranking changes, making Serta-style uptiers significantly harder. However, European high-yield bonds often lack equivalent protection—subordination is rarely a sacred right—making bonds *more* vulnerable to uptiering than loans.

### The Double-Dip

**The Objective:** Provide new creditors with two distinct claims on the borrower's assets from a single financing, mathematically diluting existing creditor recovery.

**The Mechanics:**

1. A non-guarantor subsidiary (or Unrestricted Subsidiary) incurs new debt
2. The restricted group guarantees this new debt
3. The proceeds are on-lent back to the restricted group via an intercompany loan
4. The intercompany loan is *secured* by the same collateral that secures existing creditors

**The Result:** New creditors get two paths to recovery:
- **Claim 1:** Direct claim on the restricted group via the guarantee
- **Claim 2:** Indirect claim via the secured intercompany loan (ranking pari passu with existing secured creditors)

For every €100 of new money advanced, new creditors establish €200 of claims against the asset base. This dilutes legacy creditor recovery by up to 50%.

**Notable Examples:** At Home, Sabre, Ardagh.

## 13.3 The Documentary Enablers

These transactions don't exploit legal loopholes or novel theories. They exploit specific, intentionally flexible provisions that sponsors negotiated into documents during the good times. The "trap doors" are not bugs—they are features.

### Unrestricted Subsidiaries

The designation of a subsidiary as "Unrestricted" is governed by the Restricted Payments covenant. Capacity comes from:

- The Builder Basket
- General Permitted Investments baskets
- Leverage-based investment baskets
- Broadly defined "Similar Business" baskets

Once designated, the Unrestricted Subsidiary is legally outside the credit group. It can incur unlimited debt, grant unlimited liens, and sell assets freely—all without triggering any covenant in the original credit agreement.

### Expansive Debt and Liens Capacity

LMTs require debt incurrence capacity. Modern documents provide this through:

- **Ratio Debt:** Unlimited incurrence if leverage tests are met
- **Credit Facilities Basket:** Often broadly defined to include bonds and other debt
- **General Debt Basket:** Large fixed-dollar capacity, often with growers
- **Reclassification:** Ability to move debt from fixed baskets to ratio capacity, "reloading" the fixed basket for new incurrence

The Liens covenant then permits this debt to be secured—often on a pari passu or even super-senior basis.

### The Builder Basket and Value Leakage

The Builder Basket accumulates capacity from:

- Day-one starter amounts (often with growers)
- 50% of cumulative CNI (penalized 100% for deficits)
- Equity contributions
- Returns on prior investments

This capacity can be used for dividends, investments into Unrestricted Subsidiaries, or—through "Pick Your Poison" provisions—converted into debt incurrence capacity.

### EBITDA Manipulation

Aggressive add-backs inflate reported EBITDA, which:

- Creates ratio debt capacity where none would otherwise exist
- Expands grower baskets beyond their original intent
- Triggers leverage step-downs that release asset sale proceeds

"High-watermark" or "super-grower" features make any increase permanent—basket capacity never decreases even when EBITDA collapses.

### The Asset Sale / RP Interaction

The single most dangerous exception: dispositions structured as Restricted Payments or Permitted Investments are excluded from the Asset Sale definition.

This means the borrower can sell crown-jewel assets, structure the transaction as an "investment" into an Unrestricted Subsidiary, and completely bypass the proceeds waterfall. No requirement to repay debt. No reinvestment requirement. No offer to creditors.

## 13.4 Coercive Exchange Mechanics

LMTs targeting existing debt instruments—rather than simply incurring new priming debt—frequently employ coercive tactics to maximize participation and marginalize dissenters.

### The Exit Consent

In an exchange offer, the issuer requires tendering holders to simultaneously consent to amendments stripping covenants from the old notes. Holders who tender receive new, better-positioned paper. Holders who don't tender are left with "shell" securities—covenant-stripped, illiquid, and effectively worthless.

This creates enormous pressure to participate. Even creditors who believe the exchange is unfavorable may conclude that holding out leaves them worse off.

### The English Law Constraint: Assenagon

English law provides a potential check on the most abusive exit consents. In *Assenagon Asset Management SA v. Irish Bank Resolution*, the court characterized exit consents designed to destroy minority value as an "abuse of the majority's power" and potentially unlawful.

This creates a meaningful distinction:

- **New York law bonds:** Exit consents stripping covenants are common and generally enforceable
- **English law instruments:** Courts may scrutinize exit consents that exist solely to coerce participation through value destruction

Consequently, covenant stripping is prevalent in NY-law governed European high-yield bonds but rarer in English-law loans.

### Payments for Consent as Defense

The "Payments for Consent" covenant—when present—requires that any fee, enhanced terms, or inducement offered to consenting creditors must be offered equally to all holders.

This is a critical defense against non-pro rata uptiers. If the sponsor cannot offer preferential treatment to the consenting majority, the incentive to approve a deal that harms the minority is significantly reduced.

## 13.5 The Transatlantic Divide

The practical application of LMT technology differs significantly between the U.S. and European markets, reflecting distinct documentary conventions and legal frameworks.

### Documentation Differences

| Feature | U.S. Market | European Market |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Pro Rata Sharing (Loans)** | Vulnerable to bypass via "open market purchase" provisions—enabling non-pro rata uptiers | Typically requires all-lender consent for ranking changes; reinforced by standalone ICA |
| **Sacred Rights (Bonds)** | Amendments require consent of *each affected holder*—individual veto power | Supermajority (90%) consent binds *all* holders—minority can be dragged |
| **Snooze Provisions** | Less common | "Snooze-you-lose" and "snooze-drag" provisions can manufacture consent from silence |

### Restructuring Regimes

When out-of-court LMTs fail or are challenged, the dispute moves to formal processes:

**UK Schemes and Restructuring Plans:**
- Schemes require 75% in value *and* majority in number of each class
- Restructuring Plans require only 75% in value (no numerosity requirement)
- Restructuring Plans permit cross-class cram-down—binding entire dissenting classes if conditions are met

**U.S. Chapter 11:**
- Provides orderly framework but introduces DIP financing risk
- Courts can grant new DIP loans super-priority status, priming all pre-existing debt
- Courts have shown willingness to unwind prior LMTs (see *Incora/Wesco*)

### The Adler Precedent

The UK Court of Appeal's ruling in the Adler restructuring case reinforced the *pari passu* principle for similarly-ranked creditors. The court rejected a plan that proposed paying six series of pari passu notes sequentially by maturity date, establishing that equal-ranking creditors cannot be treated unequally without proper justification.

This provides an important judicial check on LMTs that attempt to discriminate among pari passu creditors.

## 13.6 The Creditor Counter-Offensive: Anti-LMT Provisions

In response to high-profile LMTs, sophisticated investors have begun demanding specific contractual protections. This represents a direct and necessary reaction to perceived abuses.

### J. Crew Blockers

Bespoke language restricting transfer of certain assets—particularly IP—to Unrestricted Subsidiaries.

**The Limitation:** Narrowly drafted blockers can be circumvented. The Accell Group transaction demonstrated that a blocker prohibiting IP transfers to *Unrestricted* Subsidiaries provides no protection when the priming debt is instead incurred by a *Restricted* non-guarantor subsidiary.

Effective blockers must anticipate circumvention via:
- Non-guarantor Restricted Subsidiaries
- Asset classes beyond IP
- Intercompany loan structures

### Serta Protection

Elevating subordination to "sacred right" status, requiring supermajority or all-lender consent for any amendment that would subordinate existing claims.

### Chewy Blockers

Preventing release of guarantors through transfer of minority equity stakes. Standard documents only require guarantees from *wholly-owned* subsidiaries—transferring even 1% of equity could release the guarantee. Chewy blockers close this loophole.

### Comprehensive Anti-LMT Provisions

A new generation of provisions explicitly subjects certain LMT-enabling actions to higher consent thresholds:

- Altering enforcement proceeds allocation to favor a subset of creditors
- Increasing super-senior capacity for LMT purposes
- Releasing significant guarantors as part of an LMT
- Amending covenants with the explicit purpose of facilitating an LMT

### Negotiated Tightenings

In consensual restructurings, creditors are extracting documentary improvements in exchange for consent. The Pfleiderer restructuring saw bondholders successfully negotiate:

- Bespoke priming debt covenant
- Supermajority consent thresholds for future subordination
- Enhanced covenant package

The message: if you want an extension, you pay for it with protections.

## 13.7 The Judicial Backstop

When LMTs are challenged, courts increasingly serve as the final arbiter.

### U.S. Developments

The *Incora (Wesco)* bankruptcy court issued a landmark oral decision **voiding** a portion of a 2022 uptiering transaction. This signals that out-of-court LMTs are not immune from subsequent judicial reversal.

The implications are significant: sponsors and participating creditors can no longer assume that a successfully executed LMT will survive bankruptcy scrutiny.

### UK Developments

English courts have shown willingness to:

- Reject restructuring plans that violate pari passu principles (Adler)
- Scrutinize exit consents designed to destroy minority value (Assenagon)
- Apply the "no worse off" test rigorously in cross-class cram-downs

The courtroom increasingly functions as a check on the most aggressive out-of-court maneuvers.

## 13.8 The Arms Race Continues

The dynamic between borrowers/sponsors and creditors is best understood as a perpetual arms race.

**Phase 1 (2010-2018):** Sponsors negotiate increasingly flexible covenant packages. "Cov-lite" becomes market standard. Trap doors are embedded but rarely used.

**Phase 2 (2018-2022):** Distress scenarios reveal the trap doors. J. Crew, Serta, and other transactions demonstrate what's possible. Sponsors and advisors develop the LMT playbook.

**Phase 3 (2022-Present):** Creditors push back. Anti-LMT provisions emerge. Courts begin scrutinizing transactions. Documentary standards tighten—at least for new issuance.

**Phase 4 (Future):** Unknown. Sponsors will find new loopholes. Creditors will demand new protections. The cycle continues.

The core challenge is not to prevent LMTs outright—some form of balance sheet restructuring is inevitable in distress. The goal is to develop contractual norms that provide a predictable and rational framework for navigating distressed situations, balancing the legitimate interests of all stakeholders rather than simply reallocating value through ever-more-complex financial engineering.

## Summary: The LMT Vulnerability Checklist

When evaluating a credit for LMT risk, assess:

**Drop-Down Vulnerability:**
- Size of Permitted Investment baskets
- Builder Basket capacity
- Breadth of "Similar Business" definition
- Presence and strength of J. Crew blockers
- Valuable separable assets (IP, divisions, subsidiaries)

**Uptier Vulnerability:**
- Is subordination a sacred right?
- What consent threshold is required?
- Is there a standalone ICA with all-lender ranking provisions?
- Are there affiliate lender restrictions?
- Presence of Serta protection provisions

**Double-Dip Vulnerability:**
- Can non-guarantor subsidiaries incur debt?
- Can intercompany loans be secured on pari passu basis?
- Are there restrictions on guarantees to Unrestricted Subsidiaries?

**Coercion Vulnerability:**
- Governing law (English vs. New York)
- Presence of Payments for Consent covenant
- Exit consent restrictions
- Snooze-you-lose / snooze-drag provisions

**General Indicators:**
- Sponsor reputation for aggressive LMTs
- Approaching maturity wall
- Liquidity stress
- Leverage trajectory

The creditor who identifies these vulnerabilities *before* investing can price the risk appropriately—or avoid the credit entirely. The creditor who discovers them only when the LMT is announced has already lost.
