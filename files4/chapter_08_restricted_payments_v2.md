# Chapter 8: The Restricted Payments Covenant

## Introduction

The Restricted Payments covenant stands as the central pillar of creditor protection in leveraged finance. Its function is deceptively simple: prevent value from leaking out of the borrower group to shareholders and other parties beyond the reach of creditors. What began as a straightforward dividend restriction has evolved into one of the most complex, heavily negotiated, and frequently exploited provisions in modern credit documentation.

Today, the RP covenant is the foundational enabler for aggressive liability management transactions that subordinate existing creditors and strip assets from the collateral pool. A deep understanding of this covenant is no longer optional—it is essential for survival in the leveraged finance market.

---

## 8.1 The Credit Box Concept

Think of the Restricted Group—the borrower and its guarantor subsidiaries—as a box. Cash and assets flow into this box through operations. The Restricted Payments covenant governs what can flow *out*.

The covenant restricts four primary categories of transactions:

**Dividends and Distributions:** Payments on equity to shareholders. This is the most intuitive restriction—ensuring cash is retained for operations and debt service rather than paid out to equity holders.

**Equity Purchases:** Repurchases or redemptions of the company's own stock. Like dividends, this transfers value directly to equity holders.

**Junior Debt Payments:** Voluntary prepayment, redemption, or repurchase of contractually subordinated debt. This preserves cash for senior creditors and upholds payment priority.

**Restricted Investments:** The catch-all category. An "Investment" is broadly defined, and a "Restricted Investment" is any investment not explicitly permitted. This governs transfers to entities outside the Restricted Group—Unrestricted Subsidiaries, joint ventures, and third parties.

The capacity to make these Restricted Payments is not unlimited. It flows primarily from a dynamic mechanism called the "Builder Basket."

---

## 8.2 The Builder Basket: The Engine of Value Extraction

The Builder Basket—also called the "Available Amount" or "Cumulative Credit"—is the primary engine allowing a company to generate capacity for Restricted Payments. It rewards positive financial performance and equity capitalization, granting flexibility to return value to sponsors or make strategic investments.

### The Seven Components

**1. The Starter Amount**

This is day-one capacity negotiated at closing, providing immediate flexibility before the company has generated earnings. It is typically structured as:

- A fixed dollar amount (e.g., €50 million), *or*
- The greater of a fixed amount and a percentage of EBITDA (the "grower")

The **super-grower** or **high-watermark** variation is particularly aggressive: the fixed-amount component permanently ratchets up to match the highest EBITDA ever achieved, but never decreases. This creates a dangerous disconnect between *current* financial health and *permitted* capacity—allowing a company to rely on "phantom" EBITDA from a prior, more prosperous period.

**2. The CNI Component (The Profitability Engine)**

This is the core performance-based element, and it is intentionally asymmetric:

- The basket builds by **50%** of cumulative Consolidated Net Income
- But is penalized by **100%** of any cumulative CNI deficit

In high-yield bonds, the measurement period typically runs from the beginning of the quarter prior to issuance. In loans, the metric is often **Retained Excess Cash Flow**—ECF not required for mandatory prepayments.

The **Zero Floor** is a critical aggressive feature: it prevents the basket from ever going negative, regardless of cumulative losses. Capacity only goes up, never down.

**3. Equity Contributions**

The basket increases dollar-for-dollar from new equity:

- 100% of net cash proceeds from qualified equity issuances
- 100% of fair market value of non-cash contributions

Certain contributions are excluded to prevent double-counting: amounts used for equity cures and proceeds designated as "Excluded Contributions" (which create separate, more flexible baskets).

**4. Debt-to-Equity Conversions**

If convertible or exchangeable debt is converted into equity, the principal amount increases the Builder Basket—reflecting the deleveraging event as a form of capital contribution.

**5. Returns on Investments**

Because the Builder Basket funds investments in Unrestricted Subsidiaries and joint ventures, it includes a "reload" mechanism:

- Cash returns, dividends, and interest from investments originally made using the Builder Basket rebuild capacity
- Net proceeds from selling such investments also reload the basket
- If an Unrestricted Subsidiary is re-designated as Restricted, its fair market value is added back

**6. Declined Proceeds (Loan-Specific)**

In leveraged loans, if lenders decline a mandatory prepayment (asset sale sweep or ECF sweep), the borrower can often add those "Declined Proceeds" to the Builder Basket—converting what was supposed to be debt repayment into dividend capacity.

**7. Aggressive Non-Standard Components**

In sponsor-friendly documentation:

- **Retained Asset Sale Proceeds:** Proceeds not required to be swept (due to leverage step-downs) may be added, effectively allowing a borrower to sell collateral and dividend the proceeds
- **Pre-Closing Contributions:** Equity contributions made *prior* to closing create artificial day-one capacity
- **Backdating:** As seen in Altice France, issuers may backdate the builder start date years prior to current financing, instantly crystallizing massive dividend capacity based on historical earnings

### Conditions for Use

The basket *builds* automatically, but *using* it requires satisfying "stoppers":

- **No Default:** No Event of Default can exist at the time of usage
- **Ratio Test:** In bonds, typically requires the ability to incur at least €1 of Ratio Debt (often a 2.0x Fixed Charge Coverage Ratio). In loans, typically requires pro forma compliance with a Total Net Leverage Ratio

## 8.3 Permitted Baskets: The Pre-Approved Escape Routes

Beyond the Builder Basket, credit agreements provide "Permitted Investments" and other specific carve-outs granting substantial capacity to move value outside the Restricted Group.

### The General Basket

A straightforward fixed-dollar basket available for any investment purpose, almost always including a grower component linking size to EBITDA.

### The Unlimited Ratio Basket

Modern documentation typically includes a basket permitting **unlimited** dividends or investments provided a leverage ratio is met pro forma.

**The Round-Trip Trap:** This creates a loophole where a sponsor can inject equity (cash) to temporarily lower net leverage, trigger the ratio test, and then dividend that cash back out. The "wash" transaction relies on a fleeting moment of compliance to extract permanent value.

In loans, the ratio for dividends is often set tighter than for investments—on the theory that investments remain within the corporate ecosystem while dividends leave entirely.

### The Similar Business Basket

Permits investments in businesses deemed "similar, reasonably related, synergistic, incidental, or ancillary" to core operations. The definition is often expansive and highly subjective, granting management significant discretion.

### The Excluded Contribution Basket

Equity contributions designated as "Excluded Contributions" create a separate basket, typically with fewer restrictions than the Builder Basket. This allows sponsors to inject capital for the specific purpose of making a Restricted Payment or Investment without building through the CNI mechanism.

## 8.4 Basket Dynamics: Revolving vs. Annual Capacity

Understanding how baskets *behave* over time is essential for capacity analysis.

### "At Any Time" Baskets (Revolving)

These apply to items that naturally fluctuate—like intercompany loans. The test is a snapshot: at this specific moment, does the outstanding amount exceed the cap?

If a borrower uses €10 million of a €20 million basket and subsequently repays €5 million, they regain that €5 million of capacity. Capacity revolves.

### "Financial Year" Baskets (Period-Based)

These apply to "leakage" items—disposals, holding company expenses, monitoring fees. Once capacity is used in a given year, it's gone for that period.

**Carry Forward/Carry Back:** To mitigate rigidity, modern agreements allow:
- **Carry Forward:** Unused Year 1 capacity rolls to Year 2 (typically limited to one year)
- **Carry Back:** Borrowing against future capacity

Lenders usually insist carried-forward amounts are deemed used *last* in the subsequent year—preventing indefinite rolling.

## 8.5 The Unrestricted Subsidiary: The Ultimate Value-Leakage Vehicle

The ability to designate a subsidiary as "Unrestricted" is one of the most powerful and contentious tools in the sponsor playbook. Because Unrestricted Subsidiaries exist entirely outside the covenant package and collateral structure, they can be used to move valuable assets beyond creditors' reach.

### The Mechanics

Designation is treated as an "Investment." Therefore, the action requires sufficient capacity under the RP covenant—from the Builder Basket or, more commonly, from a large Permitted Investments basket.

### The Implications

| Implication | Creditor Risk |
|-------------|---------------|
| **Covenant Exemption** | The Unrestricted Subsidiary is not bound by any negative covenants. It can incur unlimited debt, grant unlimited liens, sell assets freely. |
| **Priming Debt** | It can borrow new money secured by transferred assets, placing that debt structurally ahead of original lenders. |
| **Value Stripping** | Assets transferred are removed from the Restricted Group's asset base and collateral package, directly diluting recovery prospects. |

### The Critical Link to Asset Sales

This is where covenants interact dangerously. Transactions permitted by the RP covenant (as Restricted Payments or Permitted Investments) are typically **excluded from the definition of "Asset Sale."**

The implication is profound: a company can transfer a valuable asset to an Unrestricted Subsidiary using investment capacity. Because this is governed by the RP covenant, it is *not* considered an Asset Sale—and therefore not subject to the requirement that proceeds be used to repay debt.

This is the **J. Crew Trap Door**: the primary mechanism enabling tax-free, debt-repayment-free transfer of crown-jewel assets outside the credit group.

## 8.6 The J. Crew Playbook and Its Progeny

### The Original Trap Door

In 2016, J. Crew exploited its investment capacity to transfer intellectual property worth an estimated $250 million to a newly created Unrestricted Subsidiary. The IP was moved beyond existing creditors' reach, enabling the company to use those assets for future financing.

### The Drop-Down and Spin

This two-step maneuver has been replicated repeatedly:

1. **Investment:** Transfer valuable assets (IP, a business division) to an Unrestricted Subsidiary
2. **Distribution:** Distribute the equity of that Unrestricted Subsidiary to shareholders

**Case Studies:**
- **Ardagh:** Designated its metal packaging business (AMPSA) as unrestricted and spun it off
- **Olympic Entertainment:** Transferred its online gaming business to an unrestricted subsidiary and distributed it to the sponsor
- **Neiman Marcus (MyTheresa):** Moved the valuable MyTheresa asset out and distributed it to sponsors, leaving creditors with the distressed retail operation

### The Blockers

Market response to J. Crew included protective provisions:

- **J. Crew Blockers:** Explicitly limit ability to transfer material assets, particularly IP, to Unrestricted Subsidiaries
- **Chewy Blockers:** Prevent release of a guarantor simply because a small equity stake was transferred to a third party

However, blockers can be circumvented. In the **Accell Group** transaction, the sponsor primed existing lenders by having a *Restricted Subsidiary* borrow new money secured by IP outside the existing collateral package—achieving priming without triggering the Unrestricted Subsidiary blocker.

## 8.7 Asset Sale Proceeds as Dividends

Traditionally, Asset Sale proceeds must repay debt or be reinvested. Modern covenants include leakage provisions here too.

### Leverage Step-Downs

If leverage falls below certain thresholds, the requirement to use asset sale proceeds for debt repayment steps down to 50% or 0%. Retained proceeds are often added to the Builder Basket—converting asset sales into dividend capacity.

### Direct Carve-Outs

Aggressive deals (e.g., AkzoNobel) explicitly designate certain disposal proceeds as "Distributable Asset Sale Proceeds," allowing immediate dividending without debt paydown.

### The "Solely for the Purpose" Loophole

A specific drafting quirk allows asset sales to bypass the repayment waterfall entirely if proceeds are used for a permitted Restricted Payment. A borrower can sell collateral and dividend the cash directly to the sponsor.

## 8.8 Capacity Fungibility: Pick Your Poison and Reallocation

Capacity is increasingly fungible across covenants.

**Pick Your Poison:** Allows reallocating unused RP capacity into Debt capacity—effectively converting dividend capacity into leverage capacity.

**Universal Reallocation:** More aggressive clauses allow capacity to flow the other way—converting Debt or Investment capacity into RP capacity, manufacturing dividend capacity from unrelated baskets.

**Reclassification:** If a company's performance improves, it can reclassify debt originally incurred under a fixed basket as having been incurred under Ratio Debt capacity. This "reloads" the fixed basket, which can be used again even if performance later deteriorates.

---

## 8.9 The Junior Debt Trap

A key function of the RP covenant in high-yield bonds is preserving cash for senior obligations by regulating prepayment of junior debt.

### What "Subordinated Indebtedness" Actually Means

In a typical indenture, "Subordinated Indebtedness" refers **exclusively** to debt contractually subordinated in right of payment. It generally does *not* include:

- Junior lien debt (second-lien)
- Structurally subordinated debt (non-guarantor subsidiary debt)
- Senior unsecured debt

This narrow definition creates a significant loophole: the covenant often places no restrictions on using cash to prepay senior unsecured notes or junior lien debt ahead of schedule.

### The Near-Maturity Exception

The primary restriction prohibits voluntary prepayment more than one year before scheduled maturity. This exception provides flexibility to refinance upcoming obligations.

## 8.10 The English Law Backstop: Sequana and Directors' Duties

While documentation provides *contractual* permission, English law provides a statutory backstop.

The **Sequana** judgment established that directors have a fiduciary duty to consider creditor interests (rather than just shareholders) when a company is "bordering on insolvency." This common law duty can prevent a dividend payment even if covenant baskets technically permit it—serving as a final check on value leakage in distressed scenarios.

## Summary: The Practitioner's Diligence Checklist

When evaluating a Restricted Payments covenant package, seek answers to these critical questions:

**Structural Form:**
- Single omnibus bond-style RP covenant, or separate LMA-style covenants for dividends, investments, and acquisitions?

**Builder Basket:**
- Size of day-one starter amount?
- Does the CNI component have a zero floor?
- Is there a super-grower/high-watermark mechanism?
- What equity contributions are excluded from building?

**Permitted Investment Capacity:**
- Is there an uncapped leverage-based PI basket permitting unlimited value transfer?
- How broadly is "Similar Business" defined?

**Unrestricted Subsidiary Conditions:**
- Are there J. Crew blockers on IP and material asset transfers?
- Can an Unrestricted Subsidiary hold debt of, or receive credit support from, the Restricted Group after designation?

**Guarantor Release:**
- Are there Chewy blockers preventing release via minority stake transfers?

**Junior Debt Scope:**
- What universe of debt is captured by prepayment restrictions?
- Is it limited to payment-subordinated debt, or does it include junior lien and unsecured?

**Asset Sale Linkage:**
- Does "Asset Sale" definition exclude RP/PI transactions?
- Can asset sale proceeds be dividended via leverage step-downs?

**Amendment Thresholds:**
- Can core protections be stripped with 50.1% consent, or is 90% supermajority required?
