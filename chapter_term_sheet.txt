THE ART OF THE TERM SHEET

A Leveraged Finance Practitioner's Guide to Commitment Letters, Mandates, and the Dance of Structured Debt

CHAPTER FIVE

I have spent more than twenty years of my life staring at term sheets. Not the glamorous parts of deals—the ones that make headlines when mega-transactions close. I'm talking about the unglamorous, often tedious, frequently frustrating documents that sit between the moment a sponsor falls in love with a target company and the moment capital actually hits the account. These are the documents where I've watched deals live and die. Where I've seen sponsors gain leverage they didn't expect to have, and where I've watched arrangers concede points they swore they would never surrender.

The term sheet is where leverage is established—and I don't just mean financial leverage. I mean the subtle, psychological leverage that shapes every subsequent negotiation. Every word matters. Every condition, every flex provision, every covenant basket has been fought over in countless rooms across London, Frankfurt, Madrid, and Milan. And yet, I find that most practitioners—even experienced ones—treat term sheets as a necessary evil rather than a strategic instrument. They see them as something to be "got through" on the way to credit agreements, when in fact the term sheet is where deals are truly won and lost.

In this chapter, I'm going to walk you through the architecture of term sheets, not as a legal exercise but as a strategic one. I'll explain what each component actually does in the real world, not what the lawyers tell you it does. I'll share the negotiations I've led, the mistakes I've made, and the principles I've learned from two decades in this business.


UNDERSTANDING THE COMMITMENT LETTER: STRUCTURE AND SUBSTANCE

Before we even get to what a term sheet contains, we need to understand the larger document in which it typically sits: the commitment letter. Americans call these "credit agreements light." In Europe, particularly among practitioners, we have a more sophisticated relationship with them. They're not just preliminary documents. They're binding commitments with serious legal and reputational consequences.

A commitment letter, in its essence, is a promise from an arranger (or a syndicate of arrangers) to lend money on certain terms, provided that certain conditions are met. The simplicity of that sentence masks enormous complexity. I've seen deals fail because sponsors misunderstood what "commitment" actually meant. I've seen arrangers face market pressure that made them regret commitments they'd enthusiastically underwritten just three months earlier.

The typical commitment letter for a leveraged buyout in Europe runs between 150 and 250 pages. The first twenty pages contain the term sheet itself—the headline terms that everyone focuses on. The next fifty pages contain conditions precedent. The remainder contains representations and warranties from the sponsor, boilerplate language, defined terms, fee letters (either embedded or attached), and the mandate letter provisions that establish the relationship between arranger and sponsor.

What strikes me, having reviewed literally thousands of these documents, is how many sponsors don't actually read them. They skim the term sheet, have their lawyers highlight potential problem areas, and then sign. This is a catastrophic error. The term sheet is merely the skeleton. The flesh—the actual commitments, the loopholes, the outs—exists elsewhere in the document.

Let me give you a real example from my early career. In 2006, my firm was arranging financing for a €850 million leveraged buyout in the industrial sector. The term sheet showed a €600 million senior secured facility at 225 basis points over EURIBOR. The sponsor loved these terms. But buried in the documentation, in Condition Precedent 4.2, was language that required "satisfactory" completion of a third-party environmental assessment. The word "satisfactory" was never defined. When the environmental firm came back with minor findings—nothing unusual for an industrial business, but requiring some remediation—the arranger's credit committee tried to invoke this as a reason to back away from the commitment.

This wasn't a design feature intended to give the arranger an out. It was simply sloppy drafting combined with inadequate sponsor due diligence. The sponsor's lawyers had mentioned this condition in a passing comment. The sponsor's investment committee hadn't paid attention. And when the real-world asset examination came back with imperfect results, suddenly that language mattered intensely.

I learned from this episode something that shaped my entire career: the commitment letter is not a negotiation between equals. It's a negotiation between a party that desperately wants money (the sponsor) and a party that has already committed to provide it (the arranger). The arranger, by that point, holds all the leverage. The sponsor has probably already told the target's shareholders that the financing is "committed" or "highly confident." The sponsor may have already signed a purchase agreement with a price tag that assumes certain financing terms. The sponsor has probably already spent six figures on advisors, including myself.

This is precisely why you must negotiate the commitment letter as if you're negotiating the credit agreement. Because in many ways, you are.


THE ANATOMY OF THE TERM SHEET: EACH COMPONENT AND ITS PURPOSE

Let me walk through the actual term sheet section by section. I'll explain not just what each provision means, but what it means in the context of a real transaction, under real market pressure.

Facility Structure and Sizing

The term sheet begins with the most straightforward elements: how much money you're getting, what it's called, and what it's for.

This section typically states something like: "The Arrangers commit to provide Senior Secured Term Loan A facilities in an aggregate principal amount of [X] and Senior Secured Term Loan B facilities in an aggregate principal amount of [Y], and a Revolving Credit Facility of [Z]."

Simple, right? Not quite. The language "commit to provide" is doing significant work here. In English law, this creates a binding commitment. The arranger cannot simply decline to lend. But—and this is crucial—that commitment is conditioned on satisfaction of the conditions precedent. So while the commitment is technically binding, it has enormous escape hatches.

The distinction between Facility A and Facility B matters more than most sponsors realize. Facility A is typically the "institutional" facility, held by large diversified lenders and often syndicated to the widest possible group. Facility B is typically more restrictive, designed for specialist credit investors and hedge funds that will hold higher coupons in exchange for more restrictive terms and tighter covenants.

I have seen sponsors choose between Term Loan A and Term Loan B structure based entirely on the fee spread rather than thinking through the strategic implications. A Term Loan A might be 200 basis points over EURIBOR at 4% all-in, while a Term Loan B might be 350 basis points at 4.5% all-in. The sponsor thinks: "Why would I do Term Loan B when I can get cheaper Term Loan A?"

The answer is: because in a down market, the Term Loan B investors hold the debt longer, have different prepayment expectations, and often have less ability to syndicate. You want to understand your ultimate investor base before you commit to structure. I've been in situations where an "all-in Term Loan A" structure became problematic because, during the arranger's road show to institutional lenders, those lenders insisted on much tighter covenants in exchange for their capital. Suddenly the sponsor had committed to a pricing structure that came with covenants they hadn't anticipated.

The sizing section also typically includes language about fungibility of facilities, whether amounts can be drawn in tranches, and how undrawn amounts work. In €500 million-plus facilities in Europe, you'll often see language permitting draws in tranches tied to milestones. "First Tranche: [€X] available on closing. Second Tranche: [€X] available upon consummation of certain deleveraging conditions within 12 months of closing."

This matters because it affects the arranger's risk profile between signing and closing. If the entire facility is available on day one, the arranger's risk is concentrated in a single moment. If it's phased, the risk is distributed. Sponsors, naturally, prefer undrawn facilities to be available in full at signing. Arrangers prefer them to be gated. I've seen this become a major negotiation point in weaker market conditions. In 2008–2009, some arrangers were literally refusing to commit to facilities unless massive portions were gated behind employment targets, EBITDA thresholds, or other contingencies.

Pricing and Margin

This is where the money gets discussed. The term sheet will specify: margin (the spread over a reference rate), pricing adjusting for leverage, pricing adjusting for time-to-maturity, upfront fees, and potentially pricing for various flex scenarios.

A typical European facility might be priced: "EURIBOR plus 200 basis points, with a EURIBOR floor of 0%." The floor is crucial. It means that even if EURIBOR turns negative—which it did in 2015–2017 in many parts of Europe—the borrower is paying no less than 200 basis points. That spread is the arranger's compensation for risk and effort.

But here's where it gets sophisticated. Many term sheets include "pricing adjusting for leverage," which typically means:

"If at any point the Senior Leverage Ratio exceeds [X], pricing increases by [Y] basis points. If it exceeds [Z], pricing increases by an additional [W] basis points."

This is a device to protect the arranger if the business deteriorates post-closing. If the sponsor told you at signing that EBITDA would be €150 million, and it's actually €120 million by the end of Q1 post-close, leverage automatically rises. And the pricing rises with it.

I've lived through both sides of this provision. In 2007–2008, when sponsor projections proved wildly optimistic, these pricing ratchets became the only additional compensation lenders received. They also created serious tension between sponsor and arranger because the sponsor felt—quite fairly—that they shouldn't be paying higher pricing for consequences outside their control (the financial crisis), while lenders felt that they had underwritten based on sponsor guidance that had evaporated.

Tenor and Amortization

The term sheet specifies how long you get to borrow the money and how quickly you have to pay it back.

A typical leveraged finance transaction in Europe might have a Term Loan A facility with a 7-year tenor and minimal amortization (perhaps 0% in years 1-2, 2.5% annually in years 3-7), while the Term Loan B might have an 8-year tenor with 1% annual amortization. The revolver (the working capital facility) typically has a 5-year tenor, sometimes extended in downsides.

The distinction between "tenor" and "maturity" matters. Tenor is how long the facility runs. Maturity is when the principal must be paid back. In a term loan with 7-year tenor and 0% amortization, the entire principal is due in year 7—the "bullet" maturity. In a facility with annual amortization, principal is paid down gradually.

Amortization is where sponsors try to negotiate aggressively and arrangers try to stay disciplined. A sponsor in a growing business—particularly one in a sector experiencing M&A consolidation—will argue for minimal amortization because they want maximum flexibility to make acquisitions. An arranger, by contrast, wants amortization because it reduces the risk of the bank exposure over time. It's insurance against deterioration in the underlying business.

I've seen this become a critical negotiation point in weaker market conditions. In 2009, when the credit markets were in freefall, sponsors were asking for 0% amortization over 7-year tenors, and arrangers were insisting on 5% annual amortization from year 1. It became a proxy war for who believed what about the recovery. The sponsor was saying: "We'll be strong enough to refinance in 18 months." The arranger was saying: "I don't believe that, and I want guaranteed deleveraging through mandatory amortization."

In the end, most deals split the difference with stepped amortization: 0% for years 1-2, then stepping up. But the negotiation was really about relative confidence in the recovery. The sponsor, with skin in the game, believed more strongly in the recovery than the arranger, who had already structured leverage into dozens of other deals now at risk.

Financial Covenants and Baskets

This is where the term sheet specifies the guardrails—the levels of leverage, interest coverage, or other metrics that the borrower must maintain. If breached, the lender can accelerate the debt (demand immediate repayment), or require a mandatory prepayment.

A typical covenant package in Europe includes:

"Maintain a Senior Leverage Ratio (Senior Secured Debt / LTM EBITDA) of no greater than [3.0x] (stepping down over time). Maintain an Interest Coverage Ratio (EBITDA / Cash Interest Paid) of no less than [2.5x]. Maintain a Total Leverage Ratio (Total Debt / LTM EBITDA) of no greater than [5.0x]."

These are "springing" or "maintenance" covenants—the borrower must maintain these ratios at all times. There are also "incurrence" covenants that govern what the borrower can do (incur additional debt, make acquisitions, etc.). Incurrence covenants say: "You can only do this transaction if, after doing it, you meet these ratios."

The arcane art here is in the definition of "EBITDA" for covenant purposes. The sponsor's lawyers will typically insist on extensive carve-outs and adjustments: add back restructuring costs, add back severance, add back "extraordinary" losses, add back costs related to the acquisition itself. The arranger's lawyers will fight these aggressively because every add-back is a form of covenant relief.

I was once in a deal where the definition of covenant EBITDA included a €15 million "ordinary course" add-back for "costs associated with transition of certain operational functions to lower-cost jurisdictions." This seemed reasonable when drafted in 2007. But in 2008, when the sponsor said they were incurring €45 million in such costs, the arranger suddenly found that language to be far too generous. It became a major point of tension post-closing.

The "baskets" in the covenant package are thresholds below which certain restrictions don't apply. For example, a typical basket might say: "The borrower may make acquisitions provided that (a) no single acquisition exceeds €25 million, (b) annual aggregate acquisitions do not exceed €50 million, and (c) after giving effect, the borrower maintains Senior Leverage of no greater than 3.0x."

These baskets are negotiated to death. The sponsor wants them as large as possible (giving maximum flexibility), the arranger wants them as small as possible (maintaining maximum control). In the 2007-2008 period, baskets were enormous because leverage was cheap and credit conditions were excellent. By 2009, baskets had collapsed. I've seen acquisition baskets go from €100 million ($150 million) to €10 million in successive refinancings as the credit environment tightened.

Financial baskets matter not just for size but for structure. Is it an annual basket that resets? A basket that permits carryover of unused amounts from prior years? A basket where acquisitions "incur" leverage ratios on a pro forma basis? The language shapes the sponsor's actual flexibility post-closing.

In one mid-market transaction I arranged in 2011, we set an annual acquisition basket of €15 million with no carryover. The sponsor felt constrained. By contrast, we included what's called a "pro forma paydown" mechanism where acquisitions made with existing cash (not incremental debt) could exceed the basket up to a certain aggregate amount so long as the borrower's leverage ratios improved post-acquisition. This was a compromise: it gave the sponsor flexibility for bolt-on acquisitions they could fund organically, but maintained our covenant protection.


MANDATE LETTERS: ESTABLISHING THE ARRANGER'S EXCLUSIVE RIGHTS

A mandate letter is separate from the term sheet, though they're often discussed together. The mandate letter is a contractual arrangement between sponsor and arranger governing the arranger's role, compensation, and exclusivity rights.

In European practice, mandate letters typically run 30-50 pages. They specify:

First, exclusivity. Is the mandate exclusive or non-exclusive? An exclusive mandate means the sponsor commits that only this arranger (or maybe a narrow syndicate of arrangers) can arrange the financing. This is valuable to the arranger because it means they don't compete with others for the deal. They can manage the process, syndication, and relationships without worrying that a rival bank has been brought in.

Non-exclusive mandates are far more common in larger transactions. Here, the sponsor brings in multiple arrangers, either as co-arrangers or in a competitive process. The mandate letter still specifies the arranger's role and fees, but they're competing with others.

I've run both types of processes throughout my career. Exclusive mandates are, frankly, easier. You control the process entirely. You set the timing, the documentation, the syndication approach. You're not worrying about a rival arranger proposing a more aggressive term sheet, or a co-arranger cutting fees.

But exclusive mandates come with significant risk. If you're wrong about pricing, if the market moves against you, or if the transaction proves more difficult to syndicate than anticipated, you're holding significant risk yourself. The sponsor can't bring in another arranger as a fallback.

Non-exclusive mandates distribute risk but create complexity. I've been in situations where three co-arrangers all believed different things about pricing, and those differences created tension throughout the syndication.

Let me give you an example from 2009. We (the arranger for which I was working) were brought in as one of three co-arrangers for a €400 million buyout. The lead arranger was convinced the market could handle 250 basis points on the Term Loan A. Our view was that 275 basis points was more realistic. The third arranger thought we could push 300. These weren't small differences—they represented millions in spreads to lenders and millions in "upfront fees" (one-time payments to lenders at closing).

As it turned out, the market came in at 275 basis points, and we were right. But the process was contentious because each arranger was lobbying the sponsor to support their view, and each arranger had reputational capital at stake. If pricing came in much higher than we'd predicted, we looked intelligent. If it came in lower, we looked overly conservative.

The mandate letter also specifies fees. This is critical because fees are where arrangers make money. They typically include:

Arrangement Fees: A one-time payment from sponsor to arranger for arranging the facilities. These typically run 0.75% to 2.0% of facility size, depending on transaction size, complexity, and arranger leverage. In 2007-2008, arrangement fees were closer to 0.75% because arranging was easy and competition was fierce. By 2009-2010, they'd risen to 1.5-2.0% because arrangers were scarce and risk was obvious.

Underwriting Fees: Paid to the arranger for committing to underwrite (hold) unsyndicated portions. These typically run 1.5-3.0% depending on market conditions. In strong markets, underwriting fees might be 1.5%. In weak markets, 3.0% or higher. The economics are that the arranger is holding risk (the unfunded amount) and deserves to be compensated for that risk.

Syndication Fees: Fees paid to arrangers and other lead arrangers for syndication efforts. These might be 0.5-1.5% depending on the syndication complexity.

The fee structure is also where the tension between sponsor and arranger becomes very clear. The sponsor will argue that in a strong market, why would arrangers deserve high fees? They'll point to precedent transactions with lower fees. The arranger will argue that if the market is so strong, why doesn't the sponsor just take on execution risk themselves (which, of course, they can't)?

What's interesting, and what I've come to understand only through extensive market cycles, is that fees are inversely correlated with risk. When risk is high (weak credit markets, uncertain outcomes, difficult syndication), arrangers demand high fees. When risk is low (strong credit markets, confident sponsors, easy syndication), arrangers accept lower fees because volume makes up for it.

But that correlation isn't always obvious to sponsors. In 2007-2008, sponsors were pushing for ultra-low fees on mega-deals because the market was obviously strong. Arrangers accepted those low fees because they expected to syndicate huge amounts and make money on volume. Then, in 2009, sponsors were facing severe refinancing risk, and arrangers were demanding 2.5-3.0% underwriting fees because they might have to hold 30-40% of the facilities. By 2010-2012, the market recovered, and arrangers again accepted lower fees because syndication risk diminished.

The sponsor's leverage in negotiating fees depends entirely on their importance to the arranger. A long-standing, sophisticated sponsor with a track record might negotiate lower fees because the arranger knows they'll generate repeat business. A first-time sponsor with no refinance track record will pay higher fees.

I once worked with a very high-quality sponsor who had a multi-year relationship with another bank and was bringing a complex €600 million refinancing. We were brought in as a junior co-arranger with no lead role. We wanted 1.5% of the arrangement fee, the sponsor pushed back because they were their own customer and had negotiating leverage. We ended up at 1.0%. But we accepted it because we knew that sponsor would refinance successfully and likely would bring us larger opportunities in the future.

Conversely, I've seen first-time sponsors in their first leveraged buyout have to accept 2.5% underwriting fees because they're unknown quantities and arrangers have no confidence they'll successfully manage the business and refinance post-maturity.

The mandate letter also specifies conditions under which the arranger can walk away. In strong markets, these are minimal. In weak markets, these can be extensive. A typical walk-away clause might say:

"Notwithstanding the Arranger's commitment, the Arranger shall have no obligation to provide the Facilities if: (a) there has been a Material Adverse Change in the general credit, debt, financial, or economic conditions, or any law or regulation shall have been enacted that would materially affect the willingness or ability of the Arranger to fund; (b) there is an outbreak or material escalation of hostilities involving major developed nations; or (c) there is a suspension of trading on the principal stock exchanges of the United States or European Union."

During the 2008-2009 financial crisis, the scope of these walk-away provisions became central to numerous disputes. Sponsors argued that a "Material Adverse Change" in general credit conditions wasn't sufficient—you had to show change in the specific borrower, not the market. Arrangers argued the opposite. Several deals nearly failed in this dispute, with sponsors threatening legal action against arrangers who tried to invoke MAC clauses.

What I learned through those episodes is that the language in a mandate letter's walk-away clause can be completely meaningless if the arranger truly wants to fund the deal. An arranger committed to a sponsor will find a way to make the facilities available, even in adverse conditions. Conversely, an arranger looking for a way out will seize on any language in the walk-away clause to justify non-performance.

This is a fundamental truth about commitment letters: they're not self-executing documents. They're expressions of intent between parties. The real question is: how committed is each party? When market conditions are excellent, commitment is easy. When conditions deteriorate and risk becomes real, commitment becomes a matter of will.


MARKET FLEX PROVISIONS: WHERE RISK GETS ALLOCATED

Market flex is one of the most important—and least understood—provisions in leveraged finance term sheets. And yet, I find that many practitioners, even experienced ones, don't think deeply enough about flex mechanics.

In its essence, market flex is a mechanism that allows the arranger to adjust certain deal terms between signing and closing if market conditions change materially. The idea is to protect the arranger from adverse market movement while providing the sponsor some certainty that the deal won't blow up entirely due to market volatility.

Market flex typically applies to three categories: pricing, structure, and amount.

Pricing Flex

Pricing flex typically works like this: "The Arranger shall have the right to increase pricing by up to [100] basis points in the event of adverse market conditions, provided that the increase shall not exceed [X] basis points above the pricing in the original term sheet."

So if your original term sheet calls for 200 basis points, pricing flex of 100 basis points might cap pricing at 300 basis points. This means that if, between signing and closing, EURIBOR rates fall dramatically (reducing the appeal of the security relative to risk), the arranger can increase the margin to maintain their expected return.

The genius of pricing flex, from the arranger's perspective, is that it protects economics without requiring the sponsor to agree to each adjustment. The sponsor doesn't get to negotiate whether pricing should increase—the arranger simply exercises this option if market conditions justify it.

But defining "adverse market conditions" is where the negotiation gets serious. What constitutes adverse conditions? Some term sheets define it very specifically: "adverse market conditions mean a decline of 25 basis points or more in the average spread of comparable facilities for similar borrowers." Others are much vaguer: "material deterioration in market conditions."

I've seen this vagueness become a major source of tension. In 2011, during the European debt crisis, an arranger invoked market flex to increase pricing by 50 basis points. The sponsor argued that market conditions hadn't changed materially enough to justify it. The arranger argued that spreads for comparable companies had widened significantly, and they were entitled to protection. It became a legal dispute about what "material" meant.

What I've learned is that the best term sheets define adverse conditions with specificity: "Pricing flex may be exercised if spreads for comparable facilities have widened by 50 basis points or more as measured by [specific index or precedent transactions]."

Specificity reduces ambiguity, which reduces post-signing disputes.

I was once arranging a deal in 2010 where we insisted on explicit flex language. The sponsor pushed back, saying we shouldn't need flex if we'd underwritten properly. But I argued: "When we signed this term sheet on Monday, EURIBOR was at 1.0%. If I sign a commitment based on 1.0% funding costs and EURIBOR rises to 2.0% before closing, my economics are completely wrong. Flex protects us both—you don't want an arranger who's losing money on your deal, because they'll either walk away or cut corners on syndication."

The sponsor eventually agreed, and we included specific flex language tied to EURIBOR movements and credit spread indices. As it turned out, market conditions didn't change materially, flex wasn't invoked, and the sponsor never had to worry about it. But the language was clear enough that both parties knew where they stood.

Structural Flex

Structural flex allows the arranger to change the structure—typically by shifting amounts between Term Loan A, Term Loan B, and other tranches, or by splitting a single tranche into multiple tranches with different pricing and covenants.

For example, a term sheet might specify:

"€600 million Term Loan A, €300 million Term Loan B"

Structural flex might permit the arranger to change this to:

"€500 million Term Loan A, €350 million Term Loan B, €50 million Institutional Term Loan C"

The idea is that the arranger can respond to what lenders actually want. If, during the syndication roadshow, institutional investors are demanding tighter covenants and higher pricing in exchange for capital, the arranger can create a new institutional-only tranche to capture that demand.

This is where structural flex becomes valuable. In 2009-2010, when credit markets were bifurcating (strong institutional demand for stressed credits, weak bank demand), structural flex allowed arrangers to create specialist tranches for hedge funds and stressed credit investors. Without structural flex, arrangers would have been locked into a single structure even as the market was clearly signaling it wanted something different.

Structural flex is typically limited by caps: "Structural flex may be exercised to create additional tranches, provided that the aggregate principal amount of all facilities shall not exceed [X]% of the original facility amount."

So if you committed to €900 million, structural flex might permit you to create additional tranches up to €900 million × 110% = €990 million.

Amount Flex (Including Reverse Flex)

Amount flex, also called "upward flex," allows the arranger to increase the overall facility size if lenders demand more capacity than anticipated. "Reverse flex," conversely, allows the arranger to decrease facility size if lenders demand less.

Upward flex typically works like this: "The Arranger shall have the right to increase the aggregate principal amount of the facilities by up to [20%] if the Arranger reasonably believes such increase can be syndicated at the original pricing and terms."

This is valuable when market conditions are stronger than anticipated. If, during syndication, you get demand for €1,200 million when you committed to €900 million, upward flex allows you to provide more capital without renegotiating the deal with the sponsor.

Reverse flex is more controversial. A typical reverse flex clause might say: "If, at any time prior to closing, the Arranger reasonably believes that the facilities cannot be syndicated at the committed pricing and terms, the Arranger shall have the right to reduce the facility size by up to [10%]."

From the sponsor's perspective, reverse flex is terrifying. Imagine: you've signed a commitment letter for €900 million, you've signed a purchase agreement for a €1,500 million acquisition, you've told equity sponsors that financing is certain, and then the arranger invokes reverse flex and says you can only have €810 million. You're suddenly short €90 million.

For this reason, sponsors push back hard on reverse flex. They'll argue: "If you committed to €900 million, that's what we're getting. If you can't syndicate at your committed pricing, that's your problem, not ours."

Arrangers, conversely, argue: "If we're forced to hold €90 million in our own portfolio at uneconomic pricing, we're taking on risk you've shifted to us. That's not fair."

In most term sheets, reverse flex is heavily constrained. It might require that the arranger have made bona fide syndication efforts, that the reduction be necessary based on lender feedback, that the sponsor be given notice and opportunity to pay down the commitment, and that there be a minimum floor (reverse flex can reduce by at most 10%, for example).

I arranged a deal in 2008 where reverse flex became relevant. We'd committed to a large facility, but when we started roadshow, lenders were demanding far tighter covenants and pricing significantly higher than committed. It became obvious that we either needed to renegotiate the deal with the sponsor or invoke reverse flex.

Rather than unilaterally reduce the facility, we went back to the sponsor and said: "Here's what lenders are demanding. We can either (a) commit to less capital, (b) accept tighter covenants, or (c) accept higher pricing." The sponsor chose option (c), and we adjusted pricing, which made the syndication easier.

What's interesting about this negotiation is that the sponsor realized that our initial pricing had been too aggressive. Rather than fight us on reverse flex, they recognized the market reality and adjusted. This, to me, is the mark of a sophisticated sponsor who understands markets.


CLEAR MARKET CLAUSES: WHEN ARRANGERS CAN WALK AWAY

"Clear market" is a term that's become increasingly common in European leveraged finance, particularly post-2008. It's essentially an escape hatch for the arranger if certain market conditions aren't met by closing.

A typical clear market clause might read: "Notwithstanding the Arranger's commitment, the Arranger shall have no obligation to fund if, at any time prior to closing, (a) the average bid side pricing for comparable facilities has increased by 100 basis points or more; (b) the Credit Default Swap spread for comparable borrowers has widened by 150 basis points or more; or (c) [other specified metric] has deteriorated materially."

The idea is to protect the arranger from having to fund a deal at committed pricing when market conditions have materially changed. If, when you signed the term sheet, comparable spreads were 250 basis points and six weeks later they're 350 basis points, you shouldn't have to fund at 250.

But clear market clauses are surprisingly controversial. Sponsors hate them because they undermine the "certainty" of a commitment. Arrangers love them because they protect from adverse movement.

The negotiation around clear market clauses typically focuses on: (a) what metrics constitute "clear market," (b) how materially those metrics must move, (c) whether the metric must move for the borrower's credit specifically or for the market generally, and (d) whether the arranger's obligation can be suspended vs. terminated.

In weaker market conditions, arrangers push for broad clear market clauses with generous trigger thresholds. Sponsors push back, arguing that market flex already protects the arranger, and clear market clauses give the arranger two bites at the apple.

I once negotiated a deal where we insisted on a clear market clause with a 150 basis point trigger on comparable spreads. The sponsor's counsel was horrified. "That means you can walk away if spreads widen by 15%," they said. I responded: "Yes. And if spreads widen by 15%, you should want me to walk away, because it means the market doesn't think your deal is fundable at our terms."

The sponsor didn't like this, but they understood the logic. We ended up including the clear market clause, which was never invoked because spreads didn't move that dramatically.

What clear market clauses really do is allocate market risk. Absent a clear market clause, the arranger bears the entire risk of adverse market movement. With a clear market clause, that risk is shared. The sponsor bears some risk (spreads can move somewhat and they're still getting funded), but the arranger is protected from truly catastrophic market moves.

I think of clear market clauses as insurance policies that neither party wants to invoke, but both parties benefit from having. The sponsor benefits because they know the arrangement isn't unlimited—if conditions deteriorate sufficiently, they may need to find alternative solutions. The arranger benefits because they're not exposed to catastrophic losses from market movement.


CERTAIN FUNDS REPRESENTATIONS AND EUROPEAN ACQUISITIONS

The "certain funds" concept is uniquely European, though it's evolved from American practice. It's the sponsor's representation that the equity (and other sources of funds) are actually committed and available.

In a leveraged buyout, you're typically financing with some combination of debt and equity. The term sheet specifies the debt financing. But the sponsor must also contribute equity. This creates a timing question: when does the sponsor have to prove that equity is actually available?

In American practice, this is usually handled with an "equity commitment letter" from the private equity sponsor's fund, showing that the fund has committed the necessary capital. In European practice, particularly for more complex acquisitions with multiple equity sponsors, this often requires representation in the term sheet itself that certain funds are available.

A typical certain funds representation might read:

"The Sponsor represents and warrants that it has received written commitments from all equity sponsors for the full amount of equity to be contributed at closing, and that such commitments are unconditional and irrevocable except for conditions of general application affecting the Sponsor's funds generally."

The operative language there is "except for conditions of general application." This permits the sponsor to walk away from a deal if their equity fund faces general difficulties (e.g., the fund itself defaults on distributions to its investors). But it prevents the sponsor from claiming that their fund's co-sponsors have made capital unavailable.

I've been involved in a few situations where certain funds representations became contentious. In one deal, a co-sponsor of the equity had material performance issues post-signing. The lead sponsor argued that the co-sponsor wasn't making their capital available. The arranger pointed to the certain funds representation and said: "You represented that all equity was committed. We're holding you to that."

The dispute was resolved when the lead sponsor's lawyers found a way to characterize the co-sponsor's refusal as a "condition of general application" (their fund faced broader business challenges). But the situation highlighted how important certain funds representations are. They shift the burden of ensuring equity availability onto the sponsor, not the arranger.

In European acquisition practice, certain funds representations also often relate to public company takeover codes. In the UK, for example, the City Code on Takeovers and Mergers requires that bidders make "certain funds" announcements before they can announce a takeover. A sponsor cannot announce a bid unless financing is certain.

This has created an interesting dynamic in European M&A. Sponsors typically cannot announce a bid with just an arrangement letter from an arranger. The code requires "certainty" of funds. This means the sponsor needs the actual committed facilities, not just a promise from an arranger.

As a result, European arrangers are much more willing than their American counterparts to issue "highly confident letters" or "lender commitment letters" that state, definitively, that facilities are available. An American arranger might be more cautious about issuing such a letter (because it creates potential liability if they later can't fund). But a European arranger knows that without such certainty, the sponsor cannot announce a bid, so the deal doesn't happen.

I've been on both sides of this. As an arranger, I've issued certainty letters knowing they create reputational and legal risk if I later can't perform. But I've also represented sponsors who needed those certainty letters in order to progress bids.

What's important to understand is that certain funds and certainty letters are not the same as legal commitments in a financing commitment letter. They're representations that funds are available. But if, for some reason, the funds aren't available at closing, the arranger may be able to walk away (depending on the language of the commitment letter and the applicable law).

The dynamic became very interesting post-2008, when several sponsors discovered that "certain" funds were not actually certain. Private equity funds had co-investment difficulties, equity commitments fell through, and sponsors found themselves short of equity capital. Some arrangers honored their commitments despite this (taking on the sponsor's equity shortfall themselves, or waiting while the sponsor found alternative equity). Others walked away.


SECURITIES DEMAND AND UNDERWRITING COMMITMENTS

A provision that's become increasingly common in European leveraged finance relates to securities demand. This typically applies when the borrower is undertaking not just bank debt financing, but also capital market financing (bonds, securitizations, etc.).

A typical securities demand provision might read:

"If the Arranger, in its sole discretion, determines that due to adverse market conditions, the Senior Secured Term Loan B facilities cannot be syndicated at the committed terms, the Arrangers may require that the Borrower issue senior secured bonds in lieu of, or in addition to, such facilities, with pricing and terms acceptable to the Arrangers."

This is valuable to arrangers because it allows them to shift from bank financing to capital markets financing if the bank market deteriorates. During the 2008-2009 financial crisis, securities demand provisions were explicitly invoked by numerous arrangers who couldn't syndicate bank facilities at committed pricing but could access the bonds market for investors seeking higher-yielding securities.

The sponsor's concern with securities demand is that bonds are typically more expensive (higher all-in pricing) and more restrictive (tighter covenants) than bank facilities. So if an arranger invokes securities demand, the sponsor's economics deteriorate.

For this reason, sponsors negotiate hard to constrain securities demand. A sponsor might require that:

"Securities demand can only be invoked if (a) the Arranger has made bona fide syndication efforts for at least 20 business days; (b) the bonds to be issued would have pricing no more than 100 basis points higher than the bank facilities they're replacing; and (c) the Arranger has provided written notice to the Borrower of the specific market conditions justifying securities demand."

Securities demand is related to—but distinct from—the "market flex" provisions we discussed earlier. Market flex allows the arranger to adjust pricing on bank facilities. Securities demand allows the arranger to shift from bank to bonds entirely.

In practice, I've found that securities demand is rarely invoked, but its existence in a term sheet is valuable. It gives the arranger an option if the bank market truly is impaired. And the existence of that option often makes sponsors more willing to accept tight pricing initially, because they know the arranger has a fallback if syndication becomes difficult.


FEE LETTERS: WHERE THE MONEY CHANGES HANDS

Fee letters are the documents that specify how arrangers get paid. They're typically separate from the term sheet and main commitment letter, though they're referenced in the main letter.

A typical fee letter will specify:

Arrangement Fee: Usually 0.5-2.0% of facility size, paid by the sponsor to the arranger (and often passed through to co-arrangers) upon execution of the final documentation.

Underwriting Fee: Usually 1.0-3.0% of facility size, paid by lenders to the arranger for committing to underwrite (hold) portions of the facilities that don't syndicate. This is a critical fee because it compensates arrangers for risk.

Syndication Fees: Usually 0.5-1.5%, paid to arrangers and co-arrangers for syndication efforts.

Agency Fees: Usually 0.125%-0.25%, paid to the agent lender (typically the arranger, but could be a different bank) for administering the facility.

What's important to understand is the interaction between these fees and the arranger's incentives.

The arrangement fee is paid once, upfront, and its size doesn't depend on ultimate syndication success. This creates an incentive for the arranger to size the facility based on sponsor needs, not necessarily based on what can easily be syndicated.

The underwriting fee, by contrast, is paid only for the portion that doesn't syndicate. This creates an incentive for the arranger to actually get the financing syndicated, because unsyndicated amounts (which they're holding and compensated for via underwriting fee) are risky for them.

The syndication fees incentivize attracting many lenders. The larger the syndicate, the more syndication fees the arranger earns (because those fees are paid by the sponsor, not the lenders, and paid based on the final facility size, so a 100-strong syndicate generates the same syndication fee as a 10-strong syndicate, but creates more work for the arranger).

I've been on both sides of this fee structure. As an arranger pushing a difficult deal to syndication, I was acutely aware that I was compensated (via underwriting fees) for the portions I held. This incentivized me to be realistic about what I could actually syndicate, rather than claiming I could syndicate everything and then getting stuck holding 40% myself.

Conversely, I've worked with arrangers who clearly hadn't thought through their underwriting exposure. They'd quote a 2% underwriting fee on a €500 million facility, representing €10 million of underwriting risk. If they ended up holding 30% (€150 million), they'd earned €3 million in underwriting fees to hold €150 million of risk. That's effectively 2% of principal held, which might not be adequate compensation for the risk.

Fee letters also specify whether fees are "all-in" or "variable." All-in means the arranger receives a fixed fee regardless of whether the facility is syndicated. Variable means the fee adjusts based on syndication success.

In weaker markets, sponsors push for variable fees, arguing: "If syndication is difficult and you hold more, you should make more money on the underwriting fee. But you shouldn't make the same arrangement fee if you haven't arranged anything."

Arrangers push back, arguing: "Arrangement fees compensate us for arranging, not for syndication success. We're doing the work to arrange the deal whether it syndicates or not."

The compromise in most term sheets is that arrangement fees are all-in (fixed), while underwriting and syndication fees vary based on actual syndication.

I negotiated a deal in 2009 where we pushed hard on underwriting fees. The sponsor wanted to pay 1.5% underwriting. We argued that given market conditions and syndication difficulty, 2.5% was more appropriate. The sponsor said: "If you actually syndicate, we'll pay 2.5%. If you hold material amounts, you'll make money on negative arbitrage (earning more on the underwriting fee than you're paying for funds)."

We eventually settled on a tiered structure: 2.0% on fully syndicated, 2.5% on amounts held up to 20% of the facility, 3.0% on amounts held in excess of 20%. This aligned our incentives—we wanted to syndicate to minimize holding, but we were adequately compensated if holding became necessary.

What many sponsors don't realize is that fee letters, while separate documents, are actually part of the term sheet negotiation. You can't truly negotiate the commitment letter without understanding the fee economics. If you're forcing arrangers to commit to large amounts with inadequate underwriting fees, they'll either walk away, or they'll require much tighter covenants and other concessions elsewhere.


THE NEGOTIATION BETWEEN SPONSOR AND ARRANGER: POWER DYNAMICS ACROSS CYCLES

Having spent two decades arranging leveraged financings, I've observed that sponsor-arranger negotiations follow predictable patterns based on market cycles. Understanding those patterns is key to understanding how term sheets actually work.

In Strong Markets (2003-2007, 2012-2019)

In strong markets, sponsors have leverage. Capital is abundant, credit spreads are tight, competition among arrangers is intense. A sponsor can call five banks and get five different term sheet proposals. The sponsor naturally chooses the bank with the most aggressive terms.

In these conditions:
- Sponsors push for low pricing, low fees, high facility sizes, wide baskets, loose covenants
- Arrangers compete by offering exactly that, trusting that syndication will be easy
- Clear market clauses and pricing flex become minimal—arrangers don't insist on them because they're competing for the deal
- Sponsors dictate documentation timelines aggressively
- Arrangement fees compress to 0.75-1.0%, underwriting fees to 1.5-2.0%

I experienced this in 2005-2006. I was arranging mega-deals (€1 billion+), and sponsors were essentially saying: "Here's what we want. Get multiple arrangers to bid, and we'll choose the best terms." We were bidding competitively, often against 3-4 other banks, and we won deals by being incrementally more aggressive on pricing or baskets.

The irony, which I didn't fully appreciate at the time, is that this aggressive positioning actually put us at risk. We were committing to aggressive terms and then hoping that syndication would be easy. We were betting on the market staying stable.

In Weak Markets (2001-2003, 2008-2010, 2020-2022)

In weak markets, arrangers have leverage. Capital is scarce, credit spreads are wide, arrangers are selective. Sponsors need arrangers far more than arrangers need sponsors. A sponsor calling banks gets one response: "We might be able to help, but here's what we'd need."

In these conditions:
- Sponsors have to accept tight pricing, high fees, lower facility sizes, narrow baskets, tight covenants
- Arrangers insist on clear market clauses, reverse flex, and other outs
- Sponsors have no choice but to accept documents laden with arranger protections
- Arrangement fees jump to 1.5-2.5%, underwriting fees to 3.0-4.0%
- Documentation timelines extend (arrangers have the leverage to move slowly)

I lived through the 2008-2009 period as an arranger. Suddenly, sponsors were grateful—actually grateful—to get financing at any price. We were setting the terms, not negotiating them. A sponsor who'd been pushing us for aggressive terms in 2007 was now accepting conservative ones without negotiation because the alternative was no financing.

What's interesting about these cycles is how they affect sponsor behavior post-closing. Sponsors who have to accept tight covenants and conservative leverage during weak markets often find themselves unable to make strategic investments post-closing. Conversely, sponsors who negotiate aggressively in strong markets often have more flexibility but may have overpaid in terms of fees.

The best sponsors, I've found, are those who approach negotiations strategically regardless of market conditions. They don't try to extract every last basis point in strong markets (recognizing that this generates resentment), and they don't accept obviously unreasonable terms in weak markets (recognizing that they can refinance when conditions improve).

I once worked with a sponsor who in 2007 negotiated hard but fairly for good terms, accepted reasonable fees, and built a relationship with us. When the crisis hit in 2008, they called us back and said: "We know terms have tightened and fees are up. We'll accept current market terms because we know that's fair. But we're hoping that you'll remember that we treated you reasonably when we had leverage."

We did remember. When they had subsequent refinancing opportunities post-2009, we made sure to be competitive and to facilitate their refinance successfully. That relationship-based approach to negotiating was worth far more than the basis points they'd given up in 2007.


HOW TERM SHEETS EVOLVE: FROM IOI TO COMMITMENT

Understanding how term sheets actually evolve in the real world is important because most practitioners don't see the full arc of a transaction.

The process typically starts with an Indication of Interest (IOI). This is a non-binding document that banks produce during the competitive bid process. It shows pricing, structure, and key terms that the bank thinks is fundable.

In my experience, IOIs are deliberately ambitious. Banks know they're competing against other banks, and they know sponsors will choose the bank with the most attractive IOI. So banks produce IOIs that are as attractive as possible—aggressive pricing, loose baskets, minimal fees. The IOI is a marketing document, not a reflection of the bank's true risk appetite.

This creates an inherent tension. The sponsor sees an IOI with attractive terms, selects the bank, and then expects to get those terms in the commitment letter. The bank, having won the mandate, now has to produce a commitment letter. But the bank's credit committee reviews the file and says: "We can't commit to terms this aggressive. It's too much risk."

This is when negotiation becomes real. The bank's head of leveraged finance (me, at various points in my career) has to go back to the sponsor and say: "The IOI showed 275 basis points. My credit committee will only support 300. Here's why, and here's what we need to change in the commitment letter to make 275 work."

The sponsor then has a choice: accept the arranger's revised terms, or go back to other bidders and see if they meant what they said in their IOI.

Sometimes sponsors make that choice. I've lost deals because my credit committee tightened terms relative to our IOI, and the sponsor went with a more aggressive competitor. But I've also been vindicated when that competitor later couldn't syndicate their aggressive terms and had to dramatically revise them, making the sponsor regret their choice.

The evolution from IOI to commitment letter typically also involves information about the target company becoming clearer. At the IOI stage, arrangers are making educated guesses based on public information. As the due diligence process progresses, actual information about the target emerges. EBITDA might be higher or lower than expected. Customer concentration might be tighter. Capex requirements might be higher. Each of these changes affects what the arranger can actually support.

I once was arranging for a sponsor who was bidding on a manufacturing company. The IOI was based on publicly available information showing EBITDA of €100 million. As we progressed with due diligence, actual information suggested EBITDA was more like €85 million. The leverage that our IOI assumed (3.5x) suddenly became 4.1x with actual numbers.

I had to go back to the sponsor and say: "The information we've learned means our initial terms don't work. We either need lower pricing, higher equity, or lower facility size." The sponsor was unhappy, but they understood the logic. We ultimately increased pricing modestly and increased equity, keeping facility size the same.

The commitment letter is also where conditions precedent become formal. IOIs typically include a list of conditions, but they're often vague. ("Credit agreement satisfactory to the Lenders." "Material adverse change has not occurred.") Commitment letters specify those conditions much more explicitly.

This can be another source of tension. A sponsor might have accepted a vague condition in the IOI. But when the commitment letter specifies that condition in detail, suddenly the sponsor realizes how much discretion the arranger actually has. A condition that "significant customers must be retained" becomes, in the commitment letter: "Borrower must retain 90% of historical revenue from customers with revenue > €5 million."

The negotiation around conditions precedent can be nearly as contentious as negotiation around pricing. I've spent entire days negotiating the definition of a single condition—like what constitutes satisfactory completion of environmental due diligence, or what EBITDA adjustments are permitted in calculating pro forma leverage.

The movement from IOI to commitment letter is also where the arranger demonstrates whether they actually have credit committee support for the terms they've proposed. An IOI with 6.0x leverage and minimal covenants might be what the sponsor wants, but if the arranger's credit committee won't support those terms, the IOI was just a sales document, not a real indication of what's possible.

This is why sophisticated sponsors always ask: "Has your credit committee pre-approved these terms?" Arrangers sometimes lie about this. But a sponsor who doesn't ask is missing an important question. If the arranger hasn't gotten credit approval for the IOI, the movement to commitment letter will be ugly.

The evolution from commitment letter to actual credit agreement is also important to understand. The commitment letter contains the key business terms, but the credit agreement contains hundreds of pages of boilerplate language, detailed definitions, and operational provisions. This is where legal counsel becomes critical.

The general rule is that the credit agreement should be consistent with the commitment letter, but in greater detail. But there are always interpretative questions. What does "quarterly financial reporting" mean in the commitment letter? Is it within 45 days of quarter-end? 60 days? Does it include full auditor review or just preliminary numbers? The commitment letter might say "quarterly reporting," but the credit agreement has to specify timing and scope.

I've been in situations where the credit agreement negotiations became contentious because the parties disagreed on what the commitment letter was actually committing to. These disputes can delay closing and sometimes even cause deals to collapse if the parties reach an impasse about what was actually agreed.

This is why, in my practice, I always insisted that the commitment letter be as specific as possible about what the credit agreement would contain. Rather than say "quarterly reporting," specify "audited quarterly financial statements within 45 days of quarter-end." Rather than "normal course management" for certain actions, specify exactly what "normal course" means—dollar thresholds, percentage limits, etc.

The more specificity in the commitment letter, the less room for dispute when drafting the credit agreement.


CERTAIN FUNDS AND THE EUROPEAN ACQUISITION DYNAMICS

I want to spend more time on the "certain funds" concept because it's uniquely important in European acquisitions, and I don't think most practitioners understand its full implications.

In the UK, the Takeover Code requires that before a bidder announces an offer for a public company, the bidder must ensure that "all documents which in the opinion of the Regulated Advisor are necessary to effect the Offer are ready for dispatch."

This creates a timing problem. The sponsor needs to announce a bid to start the clock on hostile takeovers. But the sponsor can't announce a bid without being certain of financing. So the sponsor needs committed financing before announcing.

This is different from the US, where a bidder can often announce a bid with a "highly confident letter" from an arranger that doesn't constitute a binding commitment. But in the UK and Europe, the concept of "certain funds" is much more stringent.

The result is that European sponsors often have to move faster on financing documentation. Whereas an American sponsor might negotiate the term sheet and commitment letter over weeks or months, a European sponsor bidding for a public company needs financing committed almost immediately.

I've been in the middle of this dynamic many times. A sponsor calls and says: "We're about to announce a £500 million offer. We need 'certain funds' committed by tomorrow." The pressure is intense. Our credit committee may not have had time to fully underwrite the transaction. But the sponsor needs certainty to make the bid announcement.

In these situations, arrangers often issue "certain funds" letters that are binding commitments, even when the credit committee hasn't fully approved all terms. The arranger is betting that by the time credit approval becomes formal (at commitment letter signing), the deal will have evolved enough that the terms make sense.

This creates obvious risk for the arranger. If credit committee later rejects the terms, the arranger has a legal problem—they've committed to certain funds that their credit committee won't support.

However, I've found that in practice, this risk is manageable because of the way the process actually works. Once a sponsor has announced a bid with "certain funds" committed, both sponsor and arranger are incentivized to make the deal work. The arranger doesn't want a legal dispute about whether they can pull out. The sponsor doesn't want to deal with a bidding process where their arranger has walked away.

So the "certain funds" commitment, while legally risky, is often backed by a shared interest in deal closure. I've never been involved in a situation where a sponsor announced a bid based on our certain funds letter and we then walked away. Because the reputational damage to us would be catastrophic—we'd never arrange another deal again.

What "certain funds" really represents is the arranger's reputation. You're putting your capital, your name, and your firm's credibility behind the statement that financing is available. You can't casually invoke a MAC clause or reverse flex because you'd never get another mandate.

This is why certain funds letters are, in my experience, far more reliable than the legal language suggests. The law might give an arranger an out, but the market won't allow them to take it.


THE ROLE OF LEGAL COUNSEL IN TERM SHEET NEGOTIATION

Legal counsel plays a critical role in term sheet negotiation that doesn't always get sufficient credit.

For the sponsor, legal counsel focuses on:

1. Understanding the commitment: What is the arranger actually committing to? What conditions can they invoke to walk away?

2. Identifying hidden risks: Conditions precedent that might be hard to satisfy. Baskets that are narrower than they appear. Covenants that might be breached.

3. Protecting optionality: Terms that give the sponsor flexibility post-closing. Conditions that the sponsor can waive if they want to. Rights to replace the arranger if they're not syndicated.

4. Analyzing fees: Are the fees competitive? What are you actually paying in all-in terms when you account for underwriting fees, syndication fees, and expense reimbursements?

For the arranger, legal counsel focuses on:

1. Protecting the commitment: Making sure conditions precedent are enforceable and difficult for the sponsor to satisfy. Making sure that walk-away clauses are airtight.

2. Limiting liability: Making sure that fees and conditions are clearly defined so there's no ambiguity about what the arranger owes.

3. Protecting syndication: Making sure that syndication can happen on comfortable terms. Making sure investor protections are clear.

4. Managing expectations: Making sure that the term sheet is so clear that credit committee will approve it without significant modification.

I've worked with brilliant legal counsel and mediocre counsel. The brilliant ones understand not just legal language, but business dynamics. They understand that a covenant that's technically compliant with the term sheet might be operationally impossible for the borrower to maintain. They understand that a seemingly generous basket might actually be quite restrictive if pro forma calculations are done conservatively.

The mediocre counsel focus on legal language without understanding business implications. They'll negotiate whether a basket is "incurred" or "assumed" without understanding what those words actually mean for the sponsor's flexibility.

I once worked with a sponsor's counsel who spent three days negotiating the definition of "Material Adverse Change." They got the definition down to an extraordinarily detailed single-spaced paragraph that accounted for every possible macro event—financial crises, changes in law, acts of God. It was brilliant legal work.

But when I discussed it with my client (the sponsor), I said: "You've spent enormous energy on a provision that will probably never matter. What I'd rather you focus on is the covenant baskets and conditions precedent, because those are the things that will actually affect your operations post-closing."

The counsel was somewhat offended—they'd done excellent legal work. But they understood my point: the high-leverage negotiation items aren't always the ones that get the most attention.

This is a lesson I've learned repeatedly: the most important term sheet provisions are often not the ones that get fought over the most. Sponsors and counsel get engaged in battles over basis points of pricing, when the real leverage lies in conditions precedent and covenant baskets.

I once told a sponsor's counsel: "I'd rather have 275 basis points pricing with tight conditions precedent, than 250 basis points with loose conditions, because the conditions will determine whether I can actually fund this deal."

The counsel understood. We refocused the negotiation on the conditions, and we actually got better terms overall by being strategic about what we were fighting for.


FLEX MECHANICS IN PRACTICE: WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENS IN REAL TRANSACTIONS

Having explained the theory of flex, let me explain what actually happens in practice, because it's different.

When we sign a term sheet with flex provisions, the arranger doesn't immediately think about how to invoke flex. We think about whether the deal is actually fundable and, if not, what levers we have to make it fundable.

The reality of flex is that it's often used not to protect the arranger from unexpected market movement, but to accommodate changes in the transaction itself.

For example, in 2011, I was arranging financing for a €500 million acquisition. Our term sheet provided for 275 basis points and a €500 million facility. As we got into due diligence, we learned that working capital requirements were higher than expected, which meant the company would need more revolving credit. We had a separate revolver commitment, but increasing it meant increasing total debt.

Rather than go back to the sponsor and renegotiate the term sheet, we used structural flex to create a working capital facility. We reduced the term loan slightly, added a larger working capital facility, and kept the total debt roughly the same but rebalanced the structure.

This is how flex is actually used in practice: it's a management tool that allows the arranger to respond to evolving transaction reality without creating renegotiation friction.

Pricing flex is less commonly invoked than structural or amount flex, but when it is invoked, it's often in response to market movements that make the original pricing uneconomic.

I invoked pricing flex once in 2010. We'd committed to 300 basis points on a facility in early 2009, with pricing flex that allowed up to 350 basis points if market spreads for comparable facilities widened by 100 basis points or more. The deal was signed in September 2009 and closing was scheduled for January 2010.

In December 2009, as we were preparing for closing, the credit market re-widened sharply—the Greek debt crisis was emerging, and credit spreads for European companies widened significantly. Comparable facilities that had been 250 basis points were now 350 basis points. We quantitatively met the pricing flex criteria.

I brought this to the sponsor and said: "The pricing flex is triggered. We can invoke it and go to 325 basis points." The sponsor was unhappy but understood the market reality. They'd seen the same market moves we had. We compromised at 310 basis points—higher than our commitment but less than the maximum flex allowed.

What's important about this example is that flex was used as a negotiation tool, not an escape hatch. We could have invoked the full flex. But instead, we used flex to signal that the market had moved and a compromise was appropriate. The sponsor understood this and accommodated it.

Reverse flex—where the arranger reduces facility size—is much more contentious and rarely invoked. I've only seen it actually invoked once, in 2009, when an arranger committed to a large facility, market conditions deteriorated so dramatically that syndication was nearly impossible, and the sponsor and arranger agreed to reduce the facility by 10% in exchange for tighter pricing on the remaining amount.

What makes reverse flex different from other flex is that it directly harms the sponsor. Pricing flex might increase all-in cost, but you still get the money. Reverse flex means you don't get all the money you committed. Sponsors will fight reverse flex with everything they have.

This is why reverse flex is really a last resort. It's invoked only when the alternative is the arranger walking away entirely. And when it's invoked, it typically becomes the subject of renegotiation that ultimately satisfies both parties somewhere in the middle.


MULTI-TRANCHE STRUCTURES AND COMPLEX CAPITAL ARCHITECTURES

As leveraged finance has become more sophisticated, multi-tranche structures have become more common. Rather than a simple Senior Term Loan and Subordinated Term Loan, deals might include:

- Senior Secured Term Loan A (7-year tenor, institutional investors, tight covenants)
- Senior Secured Term Loan B (8-year tenor, specialist investors, looser covenants)
- Institutional Preferred Equity (quasi-debt, traded by hedge funds)
- Traditional Subordinated Debt (mezzanine layer)
- Junior Capital (like preferred equity but even more junior)
- Conversion Mechanisms (subordinated debt that converts to equity at certain trigger points)

Managing a multi-tranche term sheet is like conducting an orchestra. Each investor group has different expectations. The institutional Term Loan A lenders want tight covenants and clean exit mechanisms. The mezzanine investors want looser covenants and higher returns.

When the borrower is in distress, these different investor groups have conflicting interests. The Term Loan A lenders want to enforce covenants strictly. The mezzanine investors would rather waive covenants and get the borrower through the distressed period because covenant enforcement might trigger a default that wipes them out.

I've seen complex multi-tranche structures create governance challenges post-closing. In one deal, we had seven different investor classes, each with different rights, covenants, and return expectations. When the borrower faced a minor covenant breach in year 3, we had a nightmare situation where we had to manage consent from three different investor classes before deciding how to handle it.

What I learned from these experiences is that multi-tranche structures are only efficient if:

1. The different tranches have truly different investor bases and can't be consolidated
2. The covenant structure is clearly hierarchical (Term Loan A covenants are tightest, then Term Loan B, then subordinated debt)
3. The payment waterfall is clear so investors understand where they rank in the capital structure
4. Governance mechanisms are clear—what requires majority consent, what requires unanimous consent, etc.

The term sheet for a multi-tranche deal is inherently more complex because it has to specify not just the facilities, but how they interact. When Term Loan A and Term Loan B are syndicated to different investors, and those investors have different rights, the term sheet has to specify exactly what those differences are.

I once arranged a deal with three separate term loan facilities plus a subordinated debt component. The Term Loan A had quarterly reporting requirements and maintenance covenants. The Term Loan B had semi-annual reporting and quarterly maintenance covenants (looser). The subordinated debt had annual reporting only. When the borrower was heading for a month-end cash crunch, we had to negotiate with Term Loan A lenders to waive a cash sweep requirement, while Term Loan B and subordinated lenders were fine with the cash sweep.

This complexity is one reason why many sponsors prefer simpler two-tranche structures (Term Loan A and Term Loan B) rather than more complex multi-tranche architectures. Simpler structures are easier to manage, even if they're slightly less efficient from a capital-raising perspective.


THE COMPETITIVE BANK PROCESS: RUNNING A BEAUTY PARADE

Most large leveraged finance transactions involve a competitive process where the sponsor solicits proposals from multiple banks. This "beauty parade" or "bake-off" is where the term sheet competition is most intense.

A typical process involves:

1. Sponsor selects 4-6 banks to pitch
2. Sponsor issues a highly confidential Information Memorandum containing information about the target company
3. Each bank pitches its proposal, typically including an Indication of Interest with proposed terms
4. Sponsor (and their advisors) evaluate the pitches
5. Sponsor selects one or more banks as arrangers
6. Selected bank(s) produce a commitment letter with formal term sheet

The competition in this process is fierce. Banks are bidding against each other on pricing, structure, facilities size, covenant looseness, and fees. The sponsor naturally gravitates toward the bank with the most attractive package.

But running a competitive process has unintended consequences. Banks, knowing they're competing, will propose aggressive terms in their IOI. Once a bank wins the mandate and has to produce a formal commitment letter, reality sets in—the credit committee won't support the aggressive terms that were promised in the IOI.

I've been both the bank that wins on aggressive terms (and then has to renegotiate down) and the bank that proposes conservative terms and loses the mandate to a more aggressive competitor.

What I've learned is that the best way to win a competitive process is not to propose the most aggressive terms, but to propose terms that your credit committee will actually support. This sounds obvious, but many banks don't do it.

I once told a sponsor's advisor: "Our IOI shows 300 basis points. Some competitors are showing 275. But I'm confident that at 300 basis points, we can actually execute this deal. If you choose the competitor at 275, they'll have to renegotiate once their credit committee sees the actual leverage."

The sponsor appreciated my honesty. We didn't win on pricing. But when the other arranger's commitment letter came in at 300 basis points (matching our IOI), the sponsor recalled our conversation and appreciated that we'd been straightforward about what we could support.

The beauty parade process is also where the arranger demonstrates their understanding of the sponsor's business. A sophisticated arranger will do deep due diligence before pitching, understand the business drivers, and propose a financing structure that makes sense given those drivers. A less sophisticated arranger will plug the sponsor's numbers into a standard model and propose a standard structure.

I once pitched for a transaction where the target company had a very capital-intensive business model. Most other banks proposed straight term loans and revolvers. We proposed a structure that included a capex facility, allowing the sponsor flexibility to fund capital investments. Our creative structure won us the mandate even though we weren't the most aggressive on pricing.

Running a competitive process also provides a sponsor the opportunity to learn what the market thinks about their transaction. If all six banks propose 275 basis points, you know that's what the market supports. If five propose 250 and one proposes 350, you know there's uncertainty about where the market actually is.

I've seen sponsors use the competitive process to educate themselves about their own transaction. They'll hear pitch after pitch, and by the time they've heard from six banks, they have a very clear sense of what's realistic.


TERM SHEET VS. CREDIT AGREEMENT: BRIDGING THE GAP

One of the most important transitions in any leveraged finance transaction is the movement from term sheet (commitment letter) to full credit agreement. Both documents contain the business terms, but they play different roles.

The term sheet is the high-level agreement between sponsor and arranger. It specifies facility amounts, pricing, tenor, key covenants, and conditions. It's typically 20-30 pages and is written in relatively simple language.

The credit agreement is the detailed legal document that governs the entire loan. It's typically 150+ pages and includes detailed definitions, operational requirements, reporting obligations, default provisions, and governing law language. It's written in highly technical legal language.

The theory is that the credit agreement implements the term sheet. The credit agreement shouldn't introduce new business terms—it should just provide the detailed implementation of what was already agreed in the term sheet.

But in practice, there's often a gap between what the term sheet says and what the credit agreement actually says. This gap can be unintentional (just lawyers drafting more precisely than the IOI contemplated) or intentional (one side trying to sneak in additional terms they didn't win in the term sheet negotiation).

I once found myself mediating a dispute between a sponsor and arranger about what the credit agreement should include. The term sheet said "quarterly financial reporting." The arranger's counsel drafted language requiring "audited quarterly financial statements within 45 days of quarter-end."

The sponsor's counsel objected: "We said quarterly reporting, not audited reporting. Getting full audits done quarterly is operationally difficult and expensive." The arranger's counsel responded: "The term sheet doesn't say 'unaudited'—it just says reporting. We're assuming audited because that's standard."

This became a significant negotiation. We ultimately compromised on "reviewed quarterly financials"—not fully audited, but subject to more than preliminary review—within 60 days of quarter-end.

The credit agreement negotiation is also where covenant definitions become precise. The term sheet might specify "maintenance of 3.0x leverage." The credit agreement has to specify exactly how leverage is calculated: Is it Senior Leverage (debt less cash) or Total Leverage? Is it LTM EBITDA (last twelve months trailing) or forward EBITDA? What adjustments are made to EBITDA?

Each of these definitional choices affects whether the borrower can actually maintain the covenant. I've seen situations where the term sheet and credit agreement disagreed on covenant definitions, and that disagreement meant the borrower was unknowingly in breach of covenant in months 2 and 3 of the credit facility.

This is why it's critical that legal counsel review the credit agreement against the term sheet carefully, and ensure that every business term is implemented correctly.

I once worked with a sponsor whose counsel didn't carefully compare the term sheet to the credit agreement. The credit agreement included a covenant on capital expenditures that wasn't in the term sheet. When the borrower exceeded that covenant in year 1, we had to negotiate a waiver. The sponsor was unhappy—they felt blindsided by a covenant they didn't know they'd agreed to.

The gap between term sheet and credit agreement is also an opportunity for the arranger to add investor protections. While the business terms should flow directly from the term sheet, there are operational details that investors need protection on. For example, the term sheet might not specify what happens if the borrower fails to deliver quarterly financial statements on time. The credit agreement will specify that failure to deliver within [X] days of quarter-end is a default, unless waived.

These operational defaults are standard and necessary for investor protection. But they should be consistent with the general tenor of the term sheet. If the term sheet emphasizes loose covenant structure and borrower flexibility, operational defaults should be reasonable and not onerous. If the term sheet is more restrictive, operational defaults can be tighter.

The movement from term sheet to credit agreement is also where timing becomes critical. In many transactions, there's a race between signing the commitment letter and closing the debt financing. The sponsor needs to coordinate with the target company's shareholders, obtain antitrust clearance, and get other approvals. If credit agreement negotiation takes too long, the sponsor might miss a closing deadline.

I've been in situations where a sponsor said: "We need to close by March 31st or the deal falls apart. It's now February 15th. You need to have a credit agreement that I can sign without revision by March 1st."

In these compressed timelines, the credit agreement team has minimal time to negotiate details. We typically fall back on "market standard" language and try to get credit agreement closed quickly, acknowledging that there might be operational issues post-closing that we'll have to address through waivers.

This is less than ideal, but sometimes necessary to meet deal timelines. The alternative is missing closing dates, which is catastrophic.

ORIGINAL ISSUE DISCOUNT (OID) MECHANICS

Original issue discount—where debt is issued below par, creating yield above the coupon rate—is one of the most economically important yet frequently overlooked pricing dimensions in leveraged finance. OID functions as a powerful tool for arrangers managing challenging market conditions and for issuers managing cost-of-debt expectations in distressed scenarios.

When a bond is issued at a discount to par (for example, issued at 98 with a 6% coupon), the effective yield to maturity exceeds the coupon. This higher yield (versus a comparable bond issued at par) compensates investors for the fact that they're purchasing below par and will realize the discount as capital appreciation. From the issuer's perspective, the OID represents a form of non-cash interest: it increases the economic cost of the debt without increasing the coupon, which is relevant for credit metrics and debt service coverage calculations.

European data reveals that approximately 16% of bond tranches price with OID (pricing at 99 to below 100) on an annual basis. In the United States, the frequency is lower, at approximately 8.9%, reflecting stronger issuer quality in the U.S. high-yield market and more competitive underwriting practices.

OID has become increasingly correlated with credit quality deterioration. In my experience, when an issuer's fundamentals are weakening or when an issuer is facing covenant compliance concerns, arrangers often suggest tighter pricing with larger OID rather than tighter pricing with higher coupons. The intuition is that higher coupons affect near-term cash flow and operational metrics, while OID is recognized on an accrual basis (relevant for equity holders and accountants but not for month-to-month debt service). An issuer struggling with EBITDA might accept 5% coupon + 3% OID rather than 7% coupon, even though the all-in yield is similar.

This dynamic is visible in stressed situations. I managed a financing for a manufacturer facing margin pressure in 2019. Management was concerned that accepting a higher coupon would trigger debt service coverage covenant issues in years 2-3. Instead, we priced the bonds at 97 with a 5.5% coupon—lower coupon, meaningful OID. This preserved near-term cash flow while giving investors the expected yield.

Hung bridge financings create a particularly complex OID dynamic. When market conditions deteriorate after a commitment letter is signed, banks often flex pricing rather than completely repricing the term sheet. This means they tighten spreads (reducing basis points) and increase OID as a way to maintain all-in yield while moving pricing incrementally. The Twitter/X 2022 financing is a textbook example: Musk's debt financing started at large commitments, market conditions deteriorated, and the debt that had been bid at approximately 60 cents on the dollar reflected not only spread widening but also substantial OID increases. Banks essentially held massive positions in an unremarkable financing, and the OID served as a mechanism to generate additional compensation for that hold.

CLO (collateralized loan obligation) dynamics create unusual incentives around OID. Most syndicated loan assets are valued at par for compliance testing and portfolio management purposes. However, discount obligations—loans trading below par—are valued at market, not par. CLO indentures typically cap the amount of discount obligations at approximately 20% of the portfolio. This creates a pricing dynamic where CLO managers (who are massive buyers of syndicated loans and bonds) have limited appetite for discounted assets because they hit up against compliance limits. The OID dynamic in high-yield bonds is less directly affected by CLO mechanics than syndicated loans, but the broader supply-demand dynamics are similar.

Phantom income is an important U.S. tax consideration for OID. Under U.S. federal tax law, the original issue discount is accrued ratably over the life of the bond, and this accrual is treated as taxable income to the bondholder, even though no cash is received. This means a bondholder holding a bond issued at 97 is recognizing phantom income throughout the bond's life. The de minimis exception applies when OID is less than 25 basis points multiplied by the number of years to maturity (so a 5-year bond with less than 125 bps of OID is de minimis). European bondholders are generally less affected by phantom income because European tax rules are more favorable, but U.S. investors, hedge funds, and taxable mutual funds must account for this.

Tap issuance fungibility rules are relevant when issuers plan to issue additional tranches of bonds ("taps") after an initial offering. The SEC's rules for when a new issuance can be treated as fungible with an existing issuance are important. Within 6 months of the initial issuance, the yield on a tap cannot exceed 110% of the yield on the initial tranche. After 6 months, the 100% yield test applies (yield must not exceed 100% of initial yield). This limits issuers' ability to issue at significantly different pricing on subsequent tranches. I've seen issuers structure tap windows strategically to take advantage of these fungibility windows.

ORIGINAL ISSUE DISCOUNT CHECKLIST:
- Confirm percentage discount from par; calculate implied yield-to-maturity vs. coupon
- Assess whether OID reflects temporary market dislocations or structural credit weakness
- Model phantom income impact for U.S. taxpayer investors; assess whether de minimis exception applies
- Understand whether issuer is planning tap issuances and model fungibility window constraints (110% at 6 months, 100% thereafter)
- For bridge financings, model OID as potential flex lever if spreads need to widen during interim period
- Calculate total debt service including OID accretion impact on EBITDA and cash flow metrics
- Assess CLO investor appetite for discounted assets and whether OID is pricing-limiting factor


MFN PROVISIONS IN EXCHANGE OFFERS AND SYNDICATION

Most-favored-nation (MFN) clauses in the context of bond financings and syndications protect one group of investors from being economically disadvantaged relative to another group. While MFN provisions are standard in certain debt instruments (like senior secured loans), they're less commonly understood in the bond context, where they take different forms and serve different purposes.

Exchange offer MFN provisions protect consenting bondholders from being economically disadvantaged relative to non-consenting bondholders. When a company undergoes a debt restructuring (refinancing, liability management, debt-for-equity exchange, etc.), there are typically two groups: investors who consent to the restructuring terms, and investors who hold out. Exchange offer MFNs protect the consenting group by ensuring they don't receive worse terms than holdouts.

There are two primary structures for exchange offer MFNs, reflecting different negotiating priorities. The first focuses on principal recovery: it establishes a maximum price (or stub note repurchase price) that the company will pay to holdouts. If holdouts negotiate a better recovery, consenting investors are entitled to the same recovery. The Ideal Standard 2009 restructuring exemplified this approach. The second focuses on all-in yield: it ensures that if non-consenting investors receive an economically superior package (higher coupon, better security, longer maturity at same yield), consenting investors are entitled to equivalent economics. Vivion's 2010 restructuring used this yield-focused approach.

The practical difference matters significantly. In principal-recovery-focused MFNs, holdouts are limited in their ability to negotiate higher recovery prices because the company has an incentive to avoid triggering MFN requirements. This tends to limit the holdout premiums available and reduces the downside tail risk to consenting investors. In yield-focused MFNs, holdouts can negotiate higher coupons, but if they do, the consenting group gets a matching coupon increase, which increases the company's debt service. I've observed yield-focused MFNs create problematic dynamics where a few holdout investors negotiate aggressively, triggering company-wide coupon increases that make the restructuring less economic for the overall creditor group.

Syndication MFN provisions are distinct and often documented in side letters between underwriters and institutional investors. These protect against syndication teams offering better pricing to secondary market buyers than to primary investors. The concern is that a bank might sell bonds in a syndication at a discount (for example, offering new investors 99.5 OID when primary investors got only 98 OID), effectively punishing primary investors.

Syndication MFNs typically include floors below which secondary pricing cannot go. For example, a term sheet might provide that if the arranger offers secondary investors more than 50 basis points additional OID, primary investors are entitled to matching OID. I've observed this in several large financing arrangements. The Advent/Evonik methacrylates transaction (over EUR 1 billion in total leverage) included syndication MFN protection with an OID floor of 92, meaning the arranger could offer additional OID to secondary buyers but not below that floor. The KronosNet financing similarly included OID floor protections.

The rationale for syndication MFNs is straightforward: primary investors bear significant risk between commitment and closing (market may deteriorate, company fundamentals may worsen, etc.). If the market deteriorates and the arranger has to deepen OID to place secondary allocations, primary investors are economically disadvantaged—they committed at one price but secondary investors got a better price for the same risk. Syndication MFNs level this playing field.

Syndication MFNs work alongside lock-up periods—contractual restrictions on when underwriters can sell bonds post-allocation. A typical lock-up might prevent the arranger or joint lead arrangers from selling their allocation for 7-30 days post-closing. The lock-up period gives primary investors a window where they have priority before the arranger can flood the market with secondary sales. Lock-up periods have become standard in institutional lending and are increasingly common in bond syndications, particularly in competitive processes where banks are hungry to reduce inventory quickly.

I once coordinated a syndication where the primary allocation was at 6.5%, 450 basis points, with 2% OID. Market conditions deteriorated during the syndication, and secondary placement required 7% coupon, 525 basis points, and 4% OID to attract new buyers. The syndication MFN (which capped additional OID at 2.5%) meant we could offer secondary investors 6.75%, 487 basis points, and 2.5% OID—better pricing than secondary market levels but protected the primary investors from a full repricing.

MFN SYNDICATION CHECKLIST:
- Identify whether exchange offer or syndication MFN provisions apply; confirm whether MFN is principal-focused or yield-focused
- If principal-focused, understand maximum holdout recovery premium and model probability of triggering MFN
- If yield-focused, model coupon increase if holdouts negotiate aggressively
- For syndications, confirm OID floors and pricing floors; understand whether lock-up periods apply
- Assess underwriter incentives to place secondary allocations and potential for aggressive OID increases
- For primary investors, confirm MFN documentation in side letters
- Model impact of MFN triggers on total debt service and covenant compliance
- Review precedent transactions (Ideal Standard, Vivion, Advent, KronosNet) for MFN structures and outcomes


WHAT TWENTY YEARS TEACHES

Let me reflect on what two decades in leveraged finance has taught me about term sheets, commitments, and the art of structuring transactions.

First, term sheets matter far more than most practitioners realize. People often treat term sheets as preliminary documents, with the "real" negotiations happening in credit agreements. But in my experience, the term sheet is where deal economics are truly established. The credit agreement just implements what's already been decided.

Second, the relationship between sponsor and arranger is fundamental. Good sponsors understand that an arranger who's losing money on a transaction will eventually become problematic—either they'll cut corners on syndication, or they'll invoke every technicality to protect themselves, or they'll simply walk away if conditions deteriorate. Smart sponsors negotiate aggressively on pricing and baskets, but they ensure that the arranger's economics are adequate for them to be incentivized to get the deal closed and syndicated.

Third, market cycles are more important than I initially understood. My early career was shaped by abundant credit (2003-2007). That abundance made me confident that most deals could be syndicated, which made me willing to commit to large facilities and aggressive pricing. Then 2008 taught me that credit can disappear overnight, and that arranger risk is not just theoretical—it's real.

After that, I approached every transaction differently. Even in strong markets, I was more conservative about commitment levels and more careful about downside scenarios. I'd seen what happens when an arranger has committed to more than they can actually syndicate, and I didn't want to repeat that experience.

Fourth, the legal language of commitment letters masks the fundamental reality that they're expressions of intent, not absolute guarantees. An arranger who wants to fund a deal will find a way to fund it. An arranger looking for an excuse to pull back will find one. The commitment letter provides the legal framework, but the real commitment is psychological and reputational.

Fifth, sophisticated sponsors and counsel think strategically about which provisions matter most. Everyone argues about basis points of pricing. But the real leverage is in condition precedent baskets, covenant definitions, and the definition of Material Adverse Change. I've seen sponsors win 25 basis points in pricing negotiations while accepting conditions that ultimately constrain their operations far more than the extra basis points cost.

Sixth, fee structures matter more than they appear. Many sponsors focus on the all-in spread (pricing plus fees expressed as one number). But understanding the components of the fee—arrangement fees, underwriting fees, syndication fees—gives you insight into the arranger's actual incentives. An arranger with 1% underwriting fee (compensated mainly when they hold debt) has different incentives than an arranger with 2.5% underwriting fee. The sponsor's counsel should understand those incentives and structure accordingly.

Seventh, relationship history matters enormously. A sponsor with a track record of successfully executing and refinancing deals gets better terms than a first-time sponsor. An arranger with a history of successfully syndicating deals and treating investors fairly gets easier syndication than an arranger with a poor track record. The intangible reputational capital that builds over years of doing deals properly is far more valuable than any individual basis point gained in a single transaction.

Eighth, the most sophisticated negotiations are those where both parties walk away feeling they've won something. I've negotiated hundreds of term sheets, and the ones that have aged best are those where sponsor and arranger both made concessions. The sponsor might have accepted higher pricing in exchange for tighter covenants or clearer conditions. The arranger might have accepted looser syndication terms in exchange for higher fees. But both parties got something they valued.

Conversely, negotiations where one party feels completely steamrolled tend to create problems later. The party that feels disadvantaged will retaliate through credit agreement negotiations, covenant enforcement, or simply by creating operational friction post-closing.

Ninth, clarity about what will actually happen post-closing matters more than theoretical maximum flexibility. A sponsor would love to have unlimited baskets and zero covenants. But that's not realistic. The sponsor should care about what actually constrain their business. If they know their business will generate 3.5x-4.0x leverage over the next three years, a 3.0x maintenance covenant on senior leverage is meaningful—it constrains actions. But a 5.0x covenant is meaningless; they'll never breach it.

Good sponsors negotiate for covenants that are realistic but not meaningless. They give themselves actual operating room while acknowledging that lenders need protection against drift.

Tenth, and finally, market cycles will come. I've lived through multiple cycles—abundant credit markets in 2003-2007, collapse in 2008-2009, recovery in 2010-2011, stability in 2012-2019, crisis in 2020, recovery in 2021-2022. Each cycle felt like it would last forever while it was happening. Each time I was wrong about predictions.

What this has taught me is that term sheets should be negotiated with flexibility in mind. Not through specific flex provisions (though those help), but through covenants and conditions that are calibrated to long-term reality, not to the current market environment.

A sponsor who negotiates term sheets assuming credit will always be abundant is making a mistake. A sponsor who assumes credit will always be scarce is also wrong. The right approach is to negotiate assuming reasonable markets—not great, not terrible, just normal—and structure the deal to work in that scenario.

In closing, term sheets are not just legal documents. They're strategic instruments that shape the entire arc of a transaction, from signing through exit. The practitioners who understand this—who see the term sheet not as a preliminary formality but as a critical negotiation where deal economics and sponsor-arranger alignment are established—are the ones who execute the most successful transactions.

The art of the term sheet is understanding that every word, every number, every condition has consequence. Get it right at this stage, and everything that follows is significantly easier. Get it wrong, and you'll spend years managing the consequences.
