# Module 1: IAS 1 — Presentation of Financial Statements
## IFRS Deep Learning Series | Case Study: Grifols S.A. (FY2025)

---

## 1. Why This Standard Exists

Before IAS 1, companies had enormous discretion in how they presented their financial
statements. One company might show a single line for "total expenses," another might
break expenses into twenty categories. One might bury critical information in footnotes,
another might not disclose it at all. This made it extremely difficult for investors, analysts,
and creditors to compare companies or even to compare the same company across years.

IAS 1 solves this by prescribing the **minimum structure, content, and general requirements**
for the presentation of general purpose financial statements. It is the architectural
blueprint that every IFRS financial statement must follow.

Think of IAS 1 as the building code for financial reporting. It does not tell you how to
measure a lease or recognize revenue (other standards do that). Instead, it tells you how
to **organize and present** whatever you've measured and recognized.

---

## 2. Scope

IAS 1 applies to all **general purpose financial statements** — that is, financial statements
intended for users who cannot demand custom reports (investors, creditors, the public).
It applies equally to:

- Individual company financial statements
- Consolidated group financial statements (like Grifols')
- Both annual and interim reports (though IAS 34 adds specific interim rules)

It does **not** apply to condensed interim financials under IAS 34, although the general
principles still hold.

---

## 3. The Complete Set of Financial Statements

IAS 1 requires a **complete set** of financial statements to include all of the following:

### 3.1 The Five Components

**(a) Statement of Financial Position** (Balance Sheet) as at the end of the period

**(b) Statement of Profit or Loss and Other Comprehensive Income** for the period.
This can be presented as either:
- A single statement combining P&L and OCI, or
- Two separate statements: a P&L statement immediately followed by an OCI statement

**(c) Statement of Changes in Equity** for the period

**(d) Statement of Cash Flows** for the period (detailed rules in IAS 7)

**(e) Notes**, comprising material accounting policy information and other explanatory
information

Plus **(f) comparative information** for the preceding period for all amounts, and
**(g) an additional opening balance sheet** when retrospective restatements occur.

### 3.2 How Grifols Presents This

Grifols' 2025 consolidated accounts follow the standard structure precisely:

| IAS 1 Component | Grifols Title (Spanish) | Page |
|---|---|---|
| Statement of Financial Position | Balances Consolidados | p.3-4 |
| Statement of Profit or Loss | Cuentas de Pérdidas y Ganancias Consolidadas | p.5 |
| Statement of Comprehensive Income | Estados del Resultado Global Consolidados | p.6 |
| Statement of Cash Flows | Estado de Flujos de Efectivo Consolidados | p.7 |
| Statement of Changes in Equity | Estado de Cambios en el Patrimonio Neto Consolidados | p.8-11 |
| Notes | Memoria (Notas 1 a 34) | p.12-145 |

Notice that Grifols uses **two separate statements** for profit/loss and comprehensive
income (option (ii) above). This is a deliberate presentation choice — they show the P&L
on page 5, and then immediately follow it with the OCI statement on page 6. Many
European companies prefer this two-statement approach because it keeps the P&L "clean"
and presents OCI separately.

Also notice: Grifols voluntarily presents **three years** of comparative data for the income
statement, OCI, cash flows, and changes in equity (2025, 2024, and 2023), but only two
years for the balance sheet (2025 and 2024). IAS 1 only requires one year of comparatives,
so the third year is voluntary and shows good practice.

## 4. The Six Foundational Principles

IAS 1 establishes six overarching principles that govern how financial statements are
prepared. Every line item in Grifols' accounts is shaped by these.

### 4.1 Fair Presentation

Financial statements must **present fairly** the financial position, performance, and cash
flows of the entity. The standard presumes that applying IFRS faithfully, with additional
disclosure where necessary, achieves fair presentation.

There is a rarely-used "override" provision: if compliance with a specific IFRS requirement
would be so misleading that it would conflict with the objective of financial statements,
the entity may depart from that requirement. This is extremely rare in practice and
requires extensive disclosure.

**In Grifols**: The declaration of responsibility (page 2) explicitly states that the accounts
give a "fair view" (*imagen fiel*) of the financial position and results. Deloitte's audit
opinion confirms this. The Spanish term "imagen fiel" is the legal equivalent of
"fair presentation."

### 4.2 Going Concern

Financial statements are prepared on the assumption that the entity will continue
operating for the foreseeable future (at least 12 months from the reporting date). If
management intends to liquidate or cease trading, or has no realistic alternative but to do
so, the financial statements must be prepared on a different basis, and that basis must
be disclosed.

Management must assess the entity's ability to continue as a going concern. When
there are material uncertainties about going concern, they must be disclosed.

**In Grifols**: Despite carrying approximately €9.6 billion of gross financial debt, the
auditors issued a clean opinion with no going concern emphasis. This tells you that
Deloitte was satisfied that Grifols can service its debt and continue operating. Key
factors supporting this: €1,047M operating cash flow, an undrawn €798M revolving
credit facility, and improving leverage trajectory. The leverage covenant (7.0x) is
only tested when the RCF is drawn more than 35%.

### 4.3 Accrual Basis

Except for the cash flow statement, financial statements are prepared on an **accrual
basis**. This means transactions and events are recognized when they occur, not when
cash is received or paid.

This is fundamental. When Grifols ships immunoglobulin to a hospital and invoices
€10 million, it recognizes €10M of revenue immediately (subject to IFRS 15 criteria)
even if the hospital pays 60 days later. The €10M shows up as a trade receivable on
the balance sheet until cash arrives.

**In Grifols**: The receivables balance (€651M from clients) represents revenue already
recognized but not yet collected. The payables balance (€841M to suppliers)
represents costs already recognized but not yet paid. The difference between accrual-
basis profit and cash generation is reconciled in the cash flow statement.

### 4.4 Consistency of Presentation

The presentation and classification of items must be retained from one period to the
next unless:
- A significant change in the entity's operations makes a different presentation more
  appropriate, or
- An IFRS standard requires a change

When classification changes, comparative amounts must be reclassified and the nature,
amount, and reason for the reclassification must be disclosed.

**In Grifols**: Nota 2.d reveals that Grifols restated certain 2023 comparative figures
(*Cifras reexpresadas*). The P&L footnote marks 2023 data with an asterisk indicating
restatement. This is IAS 1 in action — when you change classification, you must also
restate the comparatives so users can make meaningful period-over-period comparisons.

### 4.5 Materiality and Aggregation

Each **material** class of similar items must be presented separately. Immaterial items may
be aggregated with other items on the face of the statements or in the notes.

Information is material if omitting, misstating, or obscuring it could reasonably be expected
to influence decisions that users make on the basis of the financial statements.

This is a judgment call. IAS 1 does not set absolute thresholds. The IASB's Practice
Statement 2 provides guidance, emphasizing that materiality is entity-specific and depends
on both quantitative and qualitative factors.

**In Grifols**: Look at the income statement. "Otros ingresos" (Other income) is just €1M
in 2025 — immaterial, so it gets a single line. But "Gastos financieros" (Financial expenses)
is €625M — highly material, so it gets its own line with a detailed breakdown in Nota 25.
The balance sheet similarly aggregates small items (e.g., "Otros activos no corrientes" = €0)
while giving prominent separate lines to material items like Goodwill (€6,833M).

### 4.6 Offsetting

Assets and liabilities, and income and expenses, must **not** be offset against each other
unless required or permitted by an IFRS standard.

Offsetting reduces the information available to users. Showing revenue of €7,524M and
costs of €4,665M separately is far more informative than showing a net margin of €2,859M.

**In Grifols**: The revenue note (Nota 22) is an excellent example. Grifols shows gross
revenue of €10,603M, then deducts chargebacks (€2,524M), discounts, Medicare/Medicaid
adjustments, etc., to arrive at net revenue of €7,524M. This gross-to-net presentation
follows the no-offset principle and is crucial for understanding the business — those
chargebacks represent contractual obligations under US healthcare pricing.

However, note that deferred tax assets and liabilities **are** offset (Nota 28.d) — this is
specifically permitted by IAS 12 when they relate to the same tax authority. Grifols shows
net deferred tax assets of €416M and net deferred tax liabilities of €861M.

## 5. Structure of the Statement of Financial Position (Balance Sheet)

### 5.1 Current vs Non-Current Distinction

IAS 1 requires assets and liabilities to be classified as either **current** or **non-current**.
This distinction is fundamental for assessing liquidity and solvency.

**An asset is current if it satisfies ANY of these criteria:**
- Expected to be realized, sold, or consumed within the normal operating cycle
- Held primarily for trading
- Expected to be realized within 12 months after the reporting period
- Is cash or a cash equivalent (unless restricted for >12 months)

All other assets are non-current.

**A liability is current if it satisfies ANY of these criteria:**
- Expected to be settled within the normal operating cycle
- Held primarily for trading
- Due to be settled within 12 months after the reporting period
- The entity does not have an unconditional right to defer settlement for at least
  12 months after the reporting period

**In Grifols**: This classification has major consequences for the debt structure:

| Item | Non-Current | Current | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Senior Secured Bonds | €5,340M | €127M | Maturities 2027-2030 (NC), with pagarés (C) |
| Term Loan B | €2,186M | €23M | Quasi-bullet due 2027, only small amortization current |
| Lease liabilities | €969M | €113M | Based on payment schedule per IFRS 16 |
| Revolving facility | €0 | €0 | Undrawn at year-end |

The current portion of the Term Loan B (€23M) represents the annual scheduled
amortization payments due within 12 months. The bulk (€2,186M) is classified as
non-current because the quasi-bullet structure means most principal is due at maturity
in November 2027.

**Critical nuance for covenant-based debt**: Under IAS 1 amendments (effective 2024),
if a liability's classification depends on compliance with covenants, and the entity must
comply with those covenants at or before the reporting date, a breach (even if waived
after year-end) would cause reclassification to current. Grifols' leverage covenant is
only tested when the RCF exceeds 35% drawn — since it was undrawn at 31/12/2025,
the covenant was not being tested, and no reclassification issue arose.

### 5.2 Minimum Line Items on the Face

IAS 1 prescribes minimum line items that must appear on the face of the balance sheet.
These include (among others):

- Property, plant and equipment
- Investment property
- Intangible assets
- Financial assets
- Investments accounted for using the equity method
- Biological assets
- Inventories
- Trade and other receivables
- Cash and cash equivalents
- Trade and other payables
- Provisions
- Financial liabilities
- Current tax and deferred tax assets/liabilities
- Non-controlling interests (within equity)

Grifols follows this structure faithfully. Each of these items either appears on the face
of the balance sheet or is clearly disclosed in the notes with cross-references.

## 6. Structure of the Income Statement (Profit or Loss)

### 6.1 Minimum Line Items

IAS 1 requires the following minimum items on the face of the P&L:

- Revenue
- Finance costs
- Share of profit/loss of associates (equity method)
- Tax expense
- A single amount for discontinued operations (if any)
- Profit or loss attributable to non-controlling interests
- Profit or loss attributable to owners of the parent

### 6.2 Expense Classification: By Nature vs By Function

IAS 1 permits two methods for classifying expenses, and this is one of the most
significant presentation choices a company makes:

**By Nature**: Groups expenses by what they are — raw materials, employee costs,
depreciation, etc. This is simpler and more objective.

**By Function** (also called "cost of sales" method): Groups expenses by their purpose —
cost of goods sold, distribution costs, administrative expenses, R&D, etc. This is more
informative for operational analysis but requires more judgment in cost allocation.

**In Grifols**: The P&L uses the **functional classification**:

| Line Item | 2025 | 2024 |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue | 7,524 | 7,212 |
| Cost of sales | (4,665) | (4,418) |
| **Gross Profit** | **2,859** | **2,794** |
| R&D | (426) | (384) |
| Distribution, general & admin | (1,183) | (1,255) |
| **Operating Expenses** | **(1,609)** | **(1,639)** |

But because the functional method obscures the nature of expenses, IAS 1 requires
additional disclosure of expense nature in the notes. Grifols provides this in Nota 23
(personnel costs: €2,170M) and Nota 24 (depreciation & amortization: €450M, broken
down by function).

This dual disclosure is essential for financial modeling — the functional view tells you
the margin structure, while the nature view tells you the cost drivers.

### 6.3 Operating Profit — Not Defined by IAS 1

A critical point: **IAS 1 does not define "operating profit."** This is perhaps surprising
given how central it is to valuation. Companies have significant discretion in what they
include above and below the operating line.

Grifols calls their operating measure "Resultado de Explotación" (Operating Result)
at €1,243M. This includes all revenues and expenses except financial items and
tax. But notice: it includes the €(8)M share of losses from equity-method associates
with similar activities (Nota 10), which some companies would place below the
operating line.

The upcoming **IFRS 18** (replacing IAS 1, effective 2027) will introduce a defined
"operating profit" subtotal, which will reduce this discretion significantly.

## 7. Other Comprehensive Income (OCI)

### 7.1 What Is OCI?

OCI captures gains and losses that IFRS standards require to be recognized outside
of profit or loss. These are items where the IASB judged that including them in the
P&L would distort the picture of operating performance.

IAS 1 requires OCI items to be grouped into two categories:

**(a) Items that WILL be reclassified ("recycled") to profit or loss** in future periods:
- Foreign currency translation differences (IAS 21)
- Cash flow hedge effective portion (IFRS 9)
- Share of OCI from equity-method associates

**(b) Items that will NOT be reclassified** to profit or loss:
- Remeasurements of defined benefit plans (IAS 19)
- Fair value changes on equity instruments designated at FVOCI (IFRS 9)

This distinction matters because items in category (a) will eventually flow through the
P&L (e.g., when a foreign subsidiary is sold, accumulated translation differences are
"recycled" into profit or loss), while items in (b) never will.

### 7.2 Grifols' OCI (2025)

| OCI Component | 2025 (€M) | Category |
|---|---|---|
| Foreign currency translation differences | (1,052) | Will recycle |
| Share of OCI from equity-method associates | (3) | Will recycle |
| Cash flow hedge changes | (1) | Will recycle |
| CF hedge transfers to P&L | — | Will recycle |
| **Subtotal: Items that will recycle** | **(1,056)** | |
| Defined benefit plan remeasurements | 5 | Will NOT recycle |
| Fair value changes on financial assets at FVOCI | (80) | Will NOT recycle |
| Tax effect | (1) | Will NOT recycle |
| **Subtotal: Items that will not recycle** | **(76)** | |
| **Total OCI** | **(1,132)** | |
| **Total Comprehensive Income** | **(632)** | |

The massive €(1,052)M translation loss in 2025 is driven by the strengthening of the
Euro against the US Dollar. Grifols has enormous USD-denominated operations (US is
57% of revenue), and when the Euro strengthens, the translation of those operations
into the reporting currency (Euro) generates a loss in OCI.

This is a pure translation effect — it does not represent a cash loss. But it reduced
total comprehensive income from a net profit of €500M to a comprehensive loss of
€(632)M. Understanding the difference between P&L profit and total comprehensive
income is essential.

## 8. Statement of Changes in Equity

IAS 1 requires this statement to show:

- Total comprehensive income for the period (split between parent and NCI)
- Effects of retrospective application or restatement (IAS 8)
- For each component of equity: opening balance, changes during the period, closing balance

The reconciliation must separately show changes from profit/loss, each OCI item,
and transactions with owners (dividends, share issuances, treasury shares).

**In Grifols**: The statement (pages 8-11) is dense because Grifols has many equity
components: share capital, share premium, reserves, dividends on account, treasury
shares, retained earnings, translation reserves, hedging reserves, FVOCI reserves,
and non-controlling interests.

Key movements in 2025:
- Net profit attributable to parent: €402M
- Dividend on account: €(102)M (first dividend in years — a significant governance signal)
- Translation differences: €(1,052)M (the massive FX impact)
- Non-controlling interests decreased to €2,332M from €2,723M

## 9. The Notes — Where the Real Information Lives

IAS 1 requires notes to:
- Present information about the basis of preparation and specific accounting policies
- Disclose information required by IFRS that is not presented elsewhere
- Provide information not presented on the face of the statements but necessary
  for understanding them

The notes must be presented in a **systematic manner**, with cross-references between
each line item in the statements and the related note.

Grifols has 34 notes spanning 133 pages. For an LBO analyst, the notes are where
you find virtually everything you need:

| What You Need | Where to Find It |
|---|---|
| Accounting policy choices | Nota 4 |
| Segment profitability | Nota 5 |
| Goodwill and impairment testing | Nota 6 |
| Intangible asset lives and amortization | Nota 7 |
| Lease details (IFRS 16) | Nota 8 |
| PP&E and capex | Nota 9 |
| Complete debt structure | Nota 19 |
| Revenue decomposition | Nota 22 |
| Tax rates and deferred tax | Nota 28 |
| Financial risk management | Nota 30 |
| Related party transactions | Nota 31 |

## 10. Key Judgment: What IAS 1 Leaves to Management

Despite being prescriptive about structure, IAS 1 still gives management significant
judgment in several areas:

1. **What is material?** Management decides which items deserve separate disclosure.
2. **Functional vs nature classification** of expenses (Grifols chose functional).
3. **Single vs two-statement** approach for comprehensive income (Grifols chose two).
4. **How much voluntary information** to provide beyond the minimum.
5. **What constitutes "operating"** — until IFRS 18 takes effect, the definition of operating
   profit remains management's choice.

These judgments directly affect how you read and model the financial statements. Two
companies in the same industry may present the same underlying economics quite
differently, and understanding IAS 1 is what lets you see through the presentation to
the substance.

## 11. Looking Ahead: IFRS 18

IFRS 18 (Presentation and Disclosure in Financial Statements) was issued in April 2024
and will replace IAS 1 for annual periods beginning on or after 1 January 2027. Key
changes include:

- A **defined operating profit** subtotal
- New categories in the income statement: operating, investing, financing
- Required disclosure of **management-defined performance measures** (like adjusted EBITDA)
- Enhanced aggregation and disaggregation guidance

Grifols' 2025 accounts are still under IAS 1. The 2027 accounts will be the first under
IFRS 18, which will significantly change how the income statement looks.

---

## 12. Summary: What to Take Away

IAS 1 is not glamorous, but it is foundational. From this module you should understand:

- A complete IFRS financial statement has five mandatory components plus comparatives
- Six principles govern preparation: fair presentation, going concern, accrual basis,
  consistency, materiality, and no-offsetting
- The balance sheet must distinguish current from non-current (critical for debt analysis)
- Companies choose between nature and function expense classification
- "Operating profit" is not defined by the current standard — beware of inconsistencies
- OCI captures gains/losses excluded from the P&L, split into recyclable and non-recyclable
- The notes are where the depth is — 133 pages vs 11 pages of face statements
