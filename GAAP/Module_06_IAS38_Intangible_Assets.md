# Module 6: IAS 38 — Intangible Assets
## IFRS Deep Learning Series | Case Study: Grifols S.A. (FY2025)

---

## 1. Why This Standard Exists

Intangible assets are among the most economically valuable things a company can
possess — patents, brands, customer relationships, proprietary technology, licenses,
software. Yet for most of the twentieth century, accounting standards either ignored
them entirely or handled them inconsistently. Companies could capitalize some intangibles
and expense others based on whatever suited their reporting objectives.

IAS 38 imposes discipline on this. It defines precisely what qualifies as an intangible
asset, when it can be recognized, how it must be measured, and how its cost must be
allocated over its useful life. It also draws a sharp and deliberate line: many of the
most valuable intangibles a company possesses — its internally developed brand, its
organically built customer base, its home-grown scientific expertise — will **never
appear on the balance sheet** under IAS 38.

This is not an oversight. It is a principled decision: the IASB concluded that
internally generated intangibles cannot be measured reliably enough to justify
recognition. The result is a financial statement that systematically understates the
asset base of knowledge-intensive companies — and understanding this gap is essential
for valuing and modeling businesses like Grifols.

For Grifols, the numbers are staggering: **€2,728M of other intangible assets** plus
**€6,833M of goodwill** sit on the balance sheet — together representing 38% of total
assets and more than double the net PP&E base. Almost all of it came from acquisitions.

---

## 2. Definition — What Is an Intangible Asset?

### 2.1 The Three Criteria

IAS 38.8 defines an intangible asset as an **identifiable non-monetary asset without
physical substance**. All three elements must be present:

**Non-monetary**: Not cash, not a financial instrument. Intangibles are not claims
on cash — they are assets whose value derives from the rights or economic benefits
they confer.

**Without physical substance**: If an asset has physical substance, it is PP&E
(IAS 16) or inventory (IAS 2). The intangible itself may be embodied in a physical
medium (a CD containing software, a physical patent document), but the asset is the
rights or knowledge, not the medium.

**Identifiable**: This criterion distinguishes intangible assets from goodwill.
An asset is identifiable if it either:
- Is **separable** (capable of being separated from the entity and sold, transferred,
  licensed, rented, or exchanged — individually or together with a related contract,
  asset, or liability), OR
- Arises from **contractual or other legal rights**, regardless of whether those
  rights are transferable or separable from the entity or other rights and obligations

The identifiability test is the gatekeeper. Customer relationships acquired in a
business combination are identifiable — they could theoretically be transferred or
licensed, and they arise from contractual relationships. An assembled workforce is
not identifiable under IAS 38 — it cannot be separated and sold; it is inherently
part of the business as a whole (this is why workforce value ends up in goodwill,
not as a separable intangible).

### 2.2 Control

Beyond identifiability, IAS 38.13 requires that the entity **controls** the asset —
meaning it has the power to obtain the future economic benefits flowing from the
underlying resource and to restrict others' access to those benefits. Legal rights
(patents, trademarks, licenses) are the clearest form of control. Tacit knowledge
and skilled employees are often not controlled — a scientist can leave and take their
expertise with them, so IAS 38 does not permit recognition of internally generated
human capital.

## 3. The Recognition Question — The Great Divide

The single most important concept in IAS 38 is the fundamental asymmetry between
how **acquired** intangibles and **internally generated** intangibles are treated.

### 3.1 Separately Acquired Intangibles

When an intangible is purchased independently (a patent purchased from another company,
a software license acquired from a vendor, a customer list purchased from a competitor),
it is recognized at **cost** — the purchase price plus any directly attributable costs
of preparing the asset for its intended use.

The probability of future economic benefits criterion is always considered satisfied
for a separately acquired intangible — the fact that a company paid cash for it is
itself evidence that it expects economic benefits. The measurement reliability criterion
is met because the transaction is at arm's length.

### 3.2 Intangibles Acquired in Business Combinations

This is the most significant source of intangibles for Grifols. When a company is
acquired under IFRS 3 (Business Combinations), the acquirer must identify and
separately recognize **every identifiable intangible asset** of the acquired company
at its **fair value** at the acquisition date — even if the acquiree never recognized
that asset on its own balance sheet.

This is a critical point. Grifols acquired Talecris in 2011. Talecris had built up
enormous value in:
- Its immunoglobulin product portfolio and brand
- Its plasma collection center network and donor relationships  
- Its FDA manufacturing approvals and biologics license applications (BLAs)
- Its customer relationships with hospital systems and specialty pharmacies
- Its proprietary fractionation technology and know-how

Talecris itself may have expensed most of the costs that created these assets over
the years (R&D expensed, brand built organically, customer relationships developed
through sales force). None of these would have appeared on Talecris' balance sheet
under IFRS 38 because they were internally generated.

But the moment Grifols acquires Talecris, IFRS 3 requires Grifols to recognize all
of those identifiable intangibles at fair value. The excess of the total acquisition
price over the fair value of all identified net assets becomes goodwill. This is
why goodwill (Module 7) can be thought of as the residual — the value that acquirers
pay for assembled workforce, synergies, going concern value, and anything else that
cannot be reliably attributed to a specific identifiable asset.

### 3.3 Internally Generated Intangibles — The Prohibition

IAS 38.48 contains the most impactful prohibition in the standard:

> "Internally generated goodwill shall not be recognized as an asset."

IAS 38.63 extends this:

> The following shall not be recognized as intangible assets: internally generated
> brands, mastheads, publishing titles, customer lists and items similar in substance.

And more broadly, IAS 38 establishes that internally generated intangibles can only
be recognized if they pass through a two-phase test — and only the development phase
costs (not research phase costs) are ever eligible.

**The consequence for Grifols**: Grifols has spent decades building its plasma
collection network, its donor recruitment systems, its regulatory relationships, and
its immunoglobulin brand. It has invested billions in building market presence in
the US. None of this organically developed value appears on Grifols' balance sheet as
an intangible asset — because IAS 38 prohibits it.

The value is real. It is reflected in the enterprise value that the market ascribes
to the company. But the balance sheet is blind to it. This is why goodwill from past
acquisitions (€6,833M) is such an imperfect proxy for the true intangible value
of the business — it only captures acquired intangibles, not organic ones.

## 4. Research and Development — The Most Important Specific Application

### 4.1 Why Distinguish Research from Development?

Grifols spends **€426M on R&D in 2025** (Nota 23) — one of the largest R&D programs
of any European specialty pharma company. How much of this is on the balance sheet?
Very little. IAS 38 governs R&D expenditure with a strict two-phase framework:

### 4.2 Research Phase — Always Expensed

IAS 38.54 is unambiguous: **expenditure on research shall be recognized as an expense
when it incurred.** No exceptions.

Research is defined as original and planned investigation undertaken with the prospect
of gaining new scientific or technical knowledge and understanding. Examples from
IAS 38.56:
- Activities aimed at obtaining new knowledge
- Searching for, evaluating, and selecting applications of research findings
- Searching for alternatives for materials, devices, products, processes, systems,
  or services
- Formulating, designing, evaluating, and selecting possible alternatives

The rationale for mandatory expensing: at the research stage, it is not sufficiently
certain that a future economic benefit will materialize. The cash spent on exploring
whether a molecule could become a drug has no reliably measurable future benefit
because the outcome is fundamentally uncertain.

### 4.3 Development Phase — Capitalize Only If Six Criteria Are All Met

IAS 38.57 permits (and requires, if met) capitalization of development expenditure.
Development is the application of research findings to a plan or design for producing
new or substantially improved products or processes. It begins when technical and
commercial feasibility has been established.

All **six criteria** must be met simultaneously:

**(a)** The technical feasibility of completing the intangible asset so that it will
be available for use or sale

**(b)** The entity's **intention to complete** the asset and use or sell it

**(c)** The entity's **ability** to use or sell the asset

**(d)** The asset will **generate probable future economic benefits** — either by
demonstrating the existence of a market for the output, or that the asset will be
used internally

**(e)** The **availability of adequate technical, financial, and other resources** to
complete development and use/sell the asset

**(f)** The ability to **reliably measure** the expenditure attributable to the
intangible asset during development

For pharmaceutical companies, this framework creates a near-categorical outcome: most
drug R&D costs are expensed because regulatory uncertainty (FDA approval is not
technically feasible until Phase III completion) prevents reliable demonstration that
criteria (a) and (d) are met. Only very late-stage development costs — after regulatory
approval is effectively certain — might qualify for capitalization.

### 4.4 Grifols' R&D Accounting

Grifols' accounting policy (Nota 4) states that R&D costs are expensed as incurred
unless the development criteria under IAS 38.57 are met, in which case they are
capitalized.

In practice, Grifols expenses the vast majority of its **€426M annual R&D spend**.
The Biotest pipeline assets — Trimodulin (IgM-enriched immunoglobulin for sepsis),
IgM for neurology applications, Fibrinogen concentrate — were in clinical development
when Biotest was acquired. Because they were acquired in a business combination, they
were recognized at fair value at the acquisition date under IFRS 3 (not subject to
the research/development phase test). Post-acquisition development costs for those
programs, however, are subject to IAS 38's criteria.

**The modeling implication**: Grifols' R&D spend of €426M is almost entirely in
the P&L. It is a real cash cost. But because it creates no balance sheet asset, it
also generates no future amortization — which means the P&L charge for R&D is both
the current cost and the full future cost. When you strip out R&D in computing
adjusted EBITDA, you need to understand that those costs genuinely drive future value
even if IAS 38 forbids capitalizing them today.

## 5. Grifols' Intangible Asset Portfolio — Nota 7

### 5.1 Categories and Carrying Amounts

The €2,728M of other intangible assets (excluding goodwill) in Nota 7 breaks down
roughly as follows:

| Category | Net Carrying Value (€M) | Origin | Life |
|---|---|---|---|
| Customer relationships | ~900 | Talecris, Biotest, GDS acquisitions | Finite: 10–25 years |
| Product licenses and trademarks | ~650 | Acquired product portfolios | Finite: various |
| Technology and know-how | ~480 | Fractionation patents, diagnostic technology | Finite: 10–20 years |
| In-process R&D (IPR&D) | ~320 | Biotest pipeline (Trimodulin, IgM, Fibrinogen) | Indefinite until project complete |
| Software and IT systems | ~180 | SAP, manufacturing systems, lab systems | Finite: 3–10 years |
| Other | ~198 | Regulatory approvals, non-compete agreements | Finite: various |
| **Total** | **~2,728** | | |

This portfolio is almost entirely the legacy of three major acquisitions:

**Talecris (2011)**: The €3.4bn acquisition created a large intangible asset pool —
primarily customer relationships with US hospital systems and specialty pharmacies,
the Gamunex-C immunoglobulin brand, and plasma center operational know-how.
After 14 years of amortization, a significant portion of these assets is fully
amortized.

**Biotest (2022)**: The ~€2bn acquisition added European fractionation technology,
the Biotest therapeutic brand in Germany and Central Europe, Haema and BPC plasma
center relationships, and — most valuable prospectively — the IPR&D pipeline
(Trimodulin, IgM concentrate, Fibrinogen).

**Grifols Diagnostic Solutions (2014, formerly Novartis Diagnostics)**: The acquisition
of the diagnostics division established customer relationships with blood banks and
hospital transfusion services globally, plus the technology underlying the DG Gel
blood typing platform and NAT testing systems.

### 5.2 Finite Life vs Indefinite Life — The Most Important Classification

IAS 38.88 requires intangible assets to be classified as either:

**Finite useful life**: Amortized systematically over the useful life. Amortization
begins when the asset is available for use and ceases at derecognition (or
classification as held for sale). The amortization method must reflect the pattern
in which the asset's economic benefits are consumed; if that pattern cannot be
reliably determined, use straight-line.

**Indefinite useful life**: **Not amortized**. Instead, tested for impairment at
least annually (and whenever there is an indication of impairment). An indefinite
life does not mean infinite life — it means there is no foreseeable limit on the
period over which the asset is expected to generate net cash inflows.

The classification has enormous P&L consequences. A €500M asset with a 20-year
finite life generates €25M per year of amortization. The same asset classified as
indefinite life generates zero amortization — but requires annual impairment testing.

**In Grifols**: The in-process R&D assets from the Biotest acquisition (Trimodulin,
IgM, Fibrinogen) are classified as **indefinite life** while they remain in
development — they have no defined use period because clinical development is ongoing.
Once a pipeline asset receives regulatory approval and is launched commercially,
its remaining carrying amount is reclassified to a finite-life asset and amortized
over the expected product life.

This classification has a direct P&L effect: during development, the IPR&D assets
contribute zero amortization. At the moment of regulatory approval, amortization
begins — which can create a sudden step-up in the amortization charge in the year
of product launch.

The Trimodulin program (for sepsis treatment) is a potential multi-hundred-million
Euro asset that currently generates no P&L amortization. If approved, its carrying
amount — established at fair value at the 2022 Biotest acquisition — will be
amortized over the product's commercial life (typically 10-20 years for a specialty
biologic). This is material future amortization that is invisible in today's P&L.

### 5.3 Useful Lives and Amortization

For its finite-life intangibles, Grifols uses straight-line amortization across
all categories. The useful lives reflect both the contractual terms (patent life,
license duration) and management's assessment of competitive dynamics:

| Category | Useful Life | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Customer relationships | 10–25 years | Long-term contracts in blood banking; hospital tenders |
| Product licenses | Life of license/patent | Contractually defined |
| Technology and know-how | 10–20 years | Technology evolution in fractionation and diagnostics |
| Software | 3–10 years | Technology refresh cycles |
| Non-compete agreements | Duration of contract | Contractually defined |

**Total intangible amortization for Grifols in 2025**: approximately **€170-210M**,
embedded within the total D&A charge of €450M reported on the face of the P&L.
The segment note (Nota 5) breaks down D&A by segment; the intangibles note (Nota 7)
provides the reconciliation of opening to closing carrying amounts showing the
amortization charge.

### 5.4 The Opening-to-Closing Reconciliation

As with PP&E (IAS 16), IAS 38 requires a reconciliation of the gross carrying amount
and accumulated amortization for each class:

| Movement | €M |
|---|---|
| Opening net carrying amount | ~2,950 |
| Additions (software, development costs capitalized) | ~20 |
| Acquisitions through business combinations | — |
| Amortization charge | ~(175) |
| Impairment losses (if any) | — |
| Exchange differences (USD/EUR translation) | ~(67) |
| **Closing net carrying amount** | **~2,728** |

The FX translation line is again material: because a significant portion of Grifols'
intangibles (the Talecris-era customer relationships, US product licenses) are
denominated in USD, the Euro strengthening in 2025 reduced their Euro carrying amount
by translation alone — without any change in the underlying asset value.

## 6. Key Judgment Areas

### 6.1 Fair Value of Acquired Intangibles — The Allocation Problem

When Grifols acquires a company, it must allocate the total purchase price across all
identified assets and liabilities at fair value. The intangible asset identification
and valuation exercise is the most subjective part of purchase price allocation (PPA).

**Common valuation methods for intangibles in PPA:**

- **Relief-from-royalty method**: For brands and technology — the value is the present
  value of royalties the company would have paid to license the asset if it didn't own
  it. Used for Grifols' product licenses and fractionation technology.

- **Multi-period excess earnings method (MEEM)**: For customer relationships — the
  present value of the after-tax earnings attributable to the customer relationship,
  after deducting a "contributory asset charge" for other assets used in generating
  those earnings. Used for hospital and blood bank customer relationships.

- **With-and-without method**: For non-compete agreements — the difference in
  enterprise value with and without the agreement in place.

- **Cost approach**: For IPR&D — cost to recreate the research from scratch, adjusted
  for technical risk (probability of success). This is crude but sometimes used
  for very early-stage programs.

**Why this matters for analysts**: The allocation between intangibles and goodwill is
a management choice (within IFRS 3 constraints). A higher allocation to finite-life
intangibles creates higher future amortization, depressing IFRS earnings — but leaving
goodwill lower and reducing the risk of a large, single impairment charge. A lower
intangible allocation leaves more in goodwill (not amortized), flattering current
earnings but creating a larger impairment cliff risk.

For Grifols' Biotest acquisition (€2bn purchase price, 2022), the PPA allocated
significant value to customer relationships, technology, and IPR&D. The assumptions
underlying those valuations — discount rates, revenue forecasts, probability of
clinical success — are disclosed in Nota 7 and are the primary source of estimation
uncertainty for the intangible asset base.

### 6.2 Indefinite Life Reassessment

IAS 38.109 requires that an intangible classified as indefinite life be reviewed at
each reporting period to determine whether the indefinite life assessment continues
to be supportable. If not, the classification must be changed from indefinite to
finite — at which point the remaining carrying amount is amortized prospectively
over the newly determined useful life. This is a change in accounting estimate (IAS 8),
treated prospectively.

For Grifols, the Biotest pipeline IPR&D assets require annual reassessment:
- If Trimodulin Phase III fails, the asset would be impaired (potentially to zero)
- If clinical development is deprioritized or abandoned, the asset is derecognized
- If approved, reclassified to finite life and amortized

This creates a binary risk in Grifols' intangible asset base: the IPR&D assets
(~€320M) could evaporate in a single period if pipeline programs fail. This is not
a gradual, predictable amortization risk — it's a cliff event, similar in nature to
the goodwill impairment risk but driven by clinical rather than business performance.

### 6.3 The Website and Software Capitalization Boundary

Software costs present a classic application of the research-vs-development framework.
IAS 38 and IFRIC 2 guidance establish that:
- **Planning and feasibility phase**: Expense (research analogue)
- **Application development phase**: Capitalize if IAS 38.57 criteria are met
- **Post-implementation/operation phase**: Expense (maintenance analogue)

For Grifols' significant ERP systems (SAP) and laboratory information management
systems (LIMS), the capitalized software development costs represent multi-year
implementation projects where the feasibility of completion was established before
major spend began. The disclosed useful lives of 3-10 years for software reflect
the commercial technology cycle, not the physical life of hardware.

## 7. What Is NOT on the Balance Sheet — The Hidden Value Problem

### 7.1 The Systematic Understatement

IAS 38's prohibition on internally generated intangibles creates a systematic gap
between the economic value of knowledge-intensive companies and their book value.
For Grifols, the following have **zero carrying value** on the balance sheet despite
representing genuine economic assets:

| Invisible Asset | Estimated Economic Significance |
|---|---|
| Organic immunoglobulin brand (Flebogamma, Gamunex, Yimmugo) | Brand premium supports pricing above generic alternatives |
| Organically developed US plasma donor relationships | Cost to develop: estimated $200-500 per donor; Grifols has millions of registered donors |
| Proprietary yield optimization know-how | Operational efficiency advantage; not patentable but extremely valuable |
| Regulatory relationship and regulatory history | FDA track record; reduces approval timeline for new products |
| Assembled and trained scientific workforce | Plasma fractionation expertise; takes years to develop |
| Going concern premium of integrated business | The value of having everything connected and operational |

None of these appear in the €2,728M of other intangibles on the balance sheet.
They are not zero-value assets — they are the most competitively defensible parts
of the business. But IAS 38 says they cannot be measured reliably enough to recognize.

### 7.2 The Price-to-Book Multiple Mystery

This hidden value explains why pharmaceutical and specialty biological companies
trade at high price-to-book multiples. When the market values Grifols at (say)
€8-10bn enterprise equity value, it is not because investors think the book value
of net assets (equity ~€6.8bn including goodwill) is fairly stated. It is because
investors are pricing in the invisible intangible value that IFRS 38 refuses to show.

The gap between enterprise value and tangible book value (i.e., equity minus all
intangibles) is enormous for Grifols — a credit analyst must understand that the
debt coverage ratios based on tangible book value are deeply misleading for a
biological medicines company.

## 8. The Amortization Charge and Adjusted EBITDA

### 8.1 Why Analysts Add Back Amortization of Acquired Intangibles

The amortization of acquired intangibles is one of the most commonly adjusted items
in leveraged finance credit analysis. The logic:

- Amortization of acquired customer relationships, brands, and technology represents
  the P&L impact of a **past capital allocation decision** (the acquisition), not the
  ongoing economics of the business
- It is a **non-cash charge** — no cash leaves the business
- The **underlying asset does not necessarily decline** in value at the amortization
  rate — a customer relationship amortized at €50M/year may be worth exactly the
  same at the end of the year if the customers have been retained
- Replacing the value may not require capital expenditure at all — organic customer
  retention costs are in operating expenses

For Grifols, the amortization of acquired intangibles (~€170-210M per year) is a
significant difference between:

| Metric | 2025 (€M) | Comment |
|---|---|---|
| Net income | 500 | After ~€170-210M intangible amortization |
| Adjusted EBITDA | ~1,693 | Adds back all D&A including intangible amortization |
| Cash EBITDA (=EBITDA − maintenance capex) | ~1,430 | Reflects true cash generation capacity |

When lenders set leverage covenants, they typically define EBITDA to include (i.e.,
add back) amortization of acquired intangibles. This is economically justified —
but it can obscure situations where the intangible base is genuinely deteriorating
(customers being lost, technology becoming obsolete) even as the accounting charge
continues on autopilot.

### 8.2 The Amortization Cliff

A structural feature of acquisitive pharmaceutical companies: acquired intangibles
have finite lives and are amortized to zero over those lives. If no new acquisitions
are made, the amortization charge **declines over time** as historical acquisitions
are fully amortized.

For Grifols, the Talecris-era intangibles (2011 acquisition, ~14 years old) are
largely amortized or nearly so. As they roll off the balance sheet, the amortization
charge from those assets decreases. The Biotest intangibles (2022 acquisition) will
begin declining significantly in the mid-2030s. This creates a natural "amortization
tailwind" in IFRS earnings over the next decade — reported net income will improve
simply from the decline in the amortization charge, even with flat operating performance.

For a 5-year LBO model (2025-2030), this is directly relevant: the amortization charge
in 2030 will be lower than in 2025, creating an automatic boost to reported earnings
and book equity — without any operational improvement.

## 9. Disclosures Required by IAS 38

IAS 38.118-128 requires the following for each class of intangible asset:

**(a)** Whether the useful lives are finite or indefinite; if finite, the useful lives
or amortization rates; the amortization methods used

**(b)** Gross carrying amount and accumulated amortization at the beginning and end
of the period

**(c)** The reconciliation of opening to closing carrying amounts (additions, retirements,
amortization, impairment losses, exchange differences, other changes)

**(d)** For intangibles assessed as having indefinite useful lives: the carrying amount
and the reasons supporting the indefinite life assessment

**(e)** For intangibles acquired through business combinations: the fair value
recognized at the acquisition date, the methods used to determine that fair value,
and key assumptions

**(f)** The amount of R&D expenditure recognized as an expense in the period

The disclosure in (**f**) — total R&D expense in the period — is where Grifols'
€426M R&D spend is reported. It is separately disclosable because the prohibition
on capitalizing research costs means the entire amount flows through the P&L and
investors need to see it explicitly to understand the level of reinvestment in
future product capability.

## 10. Summary: What to Take Away

IAS 38 is the standard that reveals one of the biggest structural tensions in IFRS
reporting: **the assets that matter most for competitive advantage often don't appear
on the balance sheet**.

For Grifols:

- **€2,728M of other intangibles** are almost entirely acquired — customer
  relationships, product licenses, technology, and IPR&D from Talecris, Biotest,
  and GDS acquisitions
- **Research phase costs are always expensed**: Grifols' €426M annual R&D spend
  flows entirely through the P&L; it builds no balance sheet asset under IAS 38
- **Finite-life intangibles amortize** (~€170-210M/year), creating a non-cash P&L
  charge that analysts add back to compute adjusted EBITDA
- **Indefinite-life IPR&D** (~€320M from Biotest pipeline) is not amortized but is
  tested annually for impairment — binary risk, not gradual decay
- **Internally generated intangibles are invisible**: the organic brand, donor
  relationships, and scientific know-how that define Grifols' moat are carried at
  zero on the balance sheet — the enterprise value premium reflects what accounting
  cannot show
- **Amortization tailwind**: As Talecris-era intangibles roll off, the amortization
  charge declines naturally — an automatic earnings boost in the 2025-2030 model period

The €6,833M of goodwill — the residual after identifying all intangibles in past
acquisitions — is the next subject, and with it, the annual impairment test that
Deloitte flagged as a key audit matter.
