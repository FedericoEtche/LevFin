# Module 4: IFRS 8 — Operating Segments
## IFRS Deep Learning Series | Case Study: Grifols S.A. (FY2025)

---

## 1. Why This Standard Exists

Before segment reporting was standardized, investors and analysts faced a
fundamental problem: diversified companies could present consolidated financial
statements that completely obscured which business units were profitable, which
were growing, and which were destroying value. A conglomerate could average
together a brilliant division and a failing one and present a mediocre-looking whole
— hiding the internal cross-subsidy and making it impossible for markets to price
the component parts accurately.

IFRS 8 solves this by requiring companies to disclose **disaggregated financial
information about the parts of their business** that management actually uses to
make decisions. It applies to entities whose debt or equity instruments are publicly
traded — including all companies that have issued bonds or shares on public markets.
Grifols qualifies on both counts: it has listed equity on the Barcelona and Madrid
stock exchanges, and its bonds trade on international markets.

### The Management Approach — The Key Idea

IFRS 8 is built on a single foundational principle: **report through the eyes of
management**. Specifically, it requires companies to disclose information about
operating segments in a way that is **consistent with how the chief operating
decision maker (CODM) monitors performance and allocates resources**.

This "management approach" was controversial when adopted because it means the
segment information in the notes may use non-IFRS measures — whatever the CODM
actually uses internally. Two companies in the same industry may present segment
results using completely different profit measures: one might use EBIT, another
EBITDA, another contribution margin. IFRS 8 does not mandate a uniform metric
for segment profit. It requires consistency between what is disclosed and what is
actually used internally.

**Contrast with the old IAS 14**: The predecessor standard required segments to be
identified by industry and geography using a "risk and rewards" approach — top-down,
based on external factors. IFRS 8 flipped this to bottom-up: look at how the CODM
actually runs the business, and report accordingly.

This matters for a credit analyst because the segment disclosures under IFRS 8 reflect
the actual economic units management is monitoring — not an artificial regulatory
construct. If Grifols' management tells bondholders it monitors four segments, you
can be confident that's genuinely how internal P&L accountability is structured.

## 2. Core Concepts

### 2.1 What Is an Operating Segment?

IFRS 8.5 defines an operating segment as a component of an entity that meets all
three of the following criteria:

**(a) Engages in business activities** from which it may earn revenues and incur expenses
(including revenues and expenses from transactions with other components)

**(b) Its operating results are regularly reviewed** by the entity's chief operating
decision maker (CODM) to make decisions about resource allocation and to assess
performance

**(c) Discrete financial information is available** for the component

The CODM is a function, not necessarily a title. In practice it is usually the CEO,
the executive committee, or the board — whoever looks at segment results and decides
where to allocate capital. IFRS 8 requires the entity to disclose how it identifies
the CODM and how it defines the segments.

### 2.2 Aggregation of Operating Segments

Two or more operating segments may be aggregated into a single reportable segment
if all of the following conditions are met:

- The aggregation is consistent with the core principle of IFRS 8
- The segments have similar economic characteristics (similar long-term average gross
  margins are a key indicator)
- They are similar in each of the following:
  - The nature of the products and services
  - The nature of the production processes
  - The type or class of customer
  - The distribution channels used
  - The regulatory environment

Aggregation is optional — management can always report segments separately.
The risk is that over-aggregation obscures information material to users.

### 2.3 Reportable Segments — The Three Quantitative Tests

Not every operating segment needs to be separately reported. IFRS 8.13 requires
separate disclosure only for **reportable segments** — those that exceed any of
three 10% thresholds:

**10% Revenue Test**: The segment's reported revenue (including inter-segment revenue)
is 10% or more of the combined revenue of all operating segments.

**10% Profit/Loss Test**: The absolute amount of the segment's reported profit or loss
is 10% or more of the greater of:
- The combined reported profit of all profitable segments, OR
- The combined reported loss of all loss-making segments

**10% Assets Test**: The segment's assets are 10% or more of the combined assets of
all operating segments.

If a segment fails all three tests, it can still be reported separately if management
believes the information would be useful. And segments that were reportable last year
but fall below thresholds this year can still be included for consistency.

### 2.4 The 75% Threshold

There is one overriding constraint: the total external revenue of all reportable
segments combined must represent at least **75% of total consolidated external revenue**.

If after applying the 10% tests the reportable segments don't reach 75%, additional
segments must be identified as reportable — even if they fail all three 10% tests —
until the 75% threshold is reached. The rest is reported as "all other segments" or
"corporate/unallocated."

## 3. Required Disclosures

### 3.1 General Information

IFRS 8 requires disclosure of:

- Factors used to identify reportable segments (including the basis of organization —
  by product, geography, or regulatory environment)
- The types of products and services from which each segment derives its revenues
- How the CODM is identified (role, not necessarily name)

### 3.2 Segment Profit or Loss

For each reportable segment, entities must disclose the measure of profit or loss
reviewed by the CODM. Additionally, if these items are included in the CODM's review,
or are regularly provided to the CODM, the following must also be disclosed:

- Revenues from external customers
- Revenues from other segments (inter-segment revenues)
- Interest revenue and interest expense
- Depreciation and amortization
- Material items of income and expense
- Share of profit or loss of equity-method investments
- Income tax expense or income
- Other material non-cash items

This menu is disclosure-optional — if the CODM doesn't use them, they need not be
shown. But if the CODM does use them, they must be disclosed.

### 3.3 Segment Assets

Assets must be disclosed for each reportable segment if they are regularly provided
to the CODM. Segment liabilities require the same disclosure only if they are included
in the measure reviewed by the CODM — many companies do not allocate liabilities to
segments and therefore do not disclose segment liabilities.

### 3.4 Reconciliations — The Bridge to the Audited Numbers

This is one of the most important aspects of IFRS 8 and is frequently under-appreciated.

IFRS 8.28 requires reconciliations of the following segment totals to the corresponding
amounts in the financial statements:

- Total segment revenues to total entity revenues
- Total segment profit or loss to the entity's profit before tax (or after tax if
  presented that way)
- Total segment assets to total entity assets
- Total segment liabilities to total entity liabilities (if disclosed)
- Every other material segment item disclosed

These reconciliations are where you find the "unallocated" corporate costs, eliminations
of inter-segment transactions, and adjustments between segment-level non-IFRS measures
and IFRS profit.

### 3.5 Entity-Wide Disclosures

Even if a company has only one reportable segment, IFRS 8 requires certain
entity-wide disclosures:

**Products and services**: Revenue from external customers for each product/service
(or group of similar ones), unless impracticable.

**Geographic information**: Revenue from external customers attributed to the entity's
home country and to all foreign countries in total. If material, individual foreign
countries must be disclosed separately. Same applies to non-current assets.

**Major customers**: If any single external customer accounts for 10% or more of
total revenues, that fact must be disclosed along with the revenue, and the segment
that earns it (but not the customer's identity).

## 4. Grifols' Four Segments — The Structure

### 4.1 How Grifols Identifies Its Segments

Nota 5 discloses that Grifols has four reportable segments. The CODM — identified
as Grifols' Board of Directors and Executive Committee — reviews performance at the
segment level and allocates resources accordingly. The segmentation reflects distinct
business models, production processes, and customer bases:

| Segment | Business Description | Revenue Mix |
|---|---|---|
| **Biopharma** | Plasma-derived therapeutic proteins (IG, albumin, clotting factors, specialty proteins) | ~79% of external revenue |
| **Diagnostic** | In vitro diagnostics (blood typing, NAT screening, clinical diagnostics) | ~11% of external revenue |
| **Bio Supplies** | Biological materials for life science research and manufacturing | ~4% of external revenue |
| **Others** | Corporate, hospital pharmacy, third-party manufacturing | ~6% of external revenue |

Each segment passes at least one of the 10% tests independently. Biopharma obviously
dominates. Diagnostic and Others pass the revenue and/or profit thresholds. Bio
Supplies is relatively small but is separately reportable because it passes the 10%
asset threshold (plasma inventory and biological materials are capital-intensive).

### 4.2 The Segment Measure of Performance

Grifols discloses segment results using **adjusted EBITDA** as the primary performance
measure reviewed by the CODM. This is a non-IFRS measure — it excludes restructuring
costs, non-recurring items, and other adjustments from the standard IFRS EBITDA figure.

This is precisely the type of situation IFRS 8 was designed to accommodate: if the
CODM looks at adjusted EBITDA, that is what must be disclosed in the segment note,
even though it is not a line item in the audited financial statements. The reconciliation
then bridges adjusted EBITDA back to IFRS profit before tax.

### 4.3 Segment Revenue and EBITDA (FY2025 — Nota 5)

| Segment | Revenue (€M) | EBITDA (€M) | EBITDA Margin |
|---|---|---|---|
| Biopharma | ~5,940 | ~1,390 | ~23.4% |
| Diagnostic | ~825 | ~210 | ~25.5% |
| Bio Supplies | ~300 | ~80 | ~26.7% |
| Others | ~460 | ~13 | ~2.8% |
| **Total segments** | **~7,524** | **~1,693** | **~22.5%** |

Several features of this table are worth examining carefully:

**Biopharma: High volume, strong but not exceptional margin.** The plasma business
has enormous scale (€5.9bn revenue), but plasma collection costs, fractionation
complexity, and the US gross-to-net adjustments (chargebacks, Medicaid rebates)
compress margins. The ~23% EBITDA margin reflects a capital-intensive, regulated
biological manufacturing business — not a software or pharma royalty business.

**Diagnostic: Best margin in the portfolio.** Diagnostic has the smallest capital base
among the revenue-generating segments and benefits from high reagent margins on
an installed instrument base. Once a hospital installs Grifols' blood typing analyzers,
they are essentially locked in — the reagent pull-through is highly recurring.

**Bio Supplies: Smallest but structurally attractive.** This segment monetizes
byproducts from the fractionation process that would otherwise generate minimal value.
The margin is high precisely because the feedstock is essentially a byproduct.

**Others: Near-zero margin.** This catch-all category includes corporate costs that
are not allocated to segments. The near-zero margin reflects overhead absorption.
For modeling purposes, you would typically exclude "Others" from the operating
business and treat it as a corporate cost center.

### 4.4 Segment Assets

| Segment | Assets (€M) | Key Components |
|---|---|---|
| Biopharma | ~18,500 | Goodwill (€6.8bn), PP&E (€2.8bn), inventory (€3.1bn) |
| Diagnostic | ~4,200 | Goodwill (€2.5bn from Grifols Diagnostic Solutions / Biotest DX), instruments |
| Bio Supplies | ~600 | Biological materials, ROU assets |
| Others/Unallocated | ~2,400 | Cash, tax assets, financial instruments |
| **Total** | **~25,700** | |

The goodwill concentration in Biopharma and Diagnostic is striking. Biopharma
contains the legacy goodwill from the Talecris acquisition (2011) — a transformational
deal that made Grifols a global leader but saddled the balance sheet with significant
intangibles. Diagnostic contains goodwill from the Grifols Diagnostic Solutions
formation and the Biotest acquisition's diagnostic assets.

This concentration of goodwill makes the impairment test (Module 7 — IAS 36) a
critical topic. If the Biopharma business underperforms, the goodwill at the CGU level
could be impaired — a non-cash charge, but one that signals a deterioration in the
economic value of assets for which Grifols once paid a premium.

## 5. The Reconciliation — From Segment EBITDA to IFRS Profit

This is where IFRS 8's reconciliation requirement does real work. The bridge
from segment-level adjusted EBITDA to consolidated IFRS profit before tax:

| Reconciliation Item | €M | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| Total segment adjusted EBITDA | 1,693 | Sum of four segments |
| Less: D&A (allocated to segments) | (450) | Depreciation + amortization per Nota 24 |
| Less: Restructuring / non-recurring items | ~(30) | Costs excluded from adjusted EBITDA |
| Less: Net financial expense | (623) | Interest expense net of income (Nota 25) |
| Add: Share of equity-method results | (8) | Nota 10 |
| Other adjustments | — | Various reconciling items |
| **Profit before tax (IFRS)** | **615** | As reported on the P&L (page 5) |

The gap between EBITDA (€1,693M) and profit before tax (€615M) is almost entirely
explained by D&A (€450M) and net financial expense (€623M). This ratio is typical
for a highly leveraged pharmaceutical group — interest cost is the largest single
item between operating profit and reported earnings.

For an LBO analyst, the key number is EBITDA at the segment level before corporate
costs, restructuring, and financial items. The ~€1,693M figure, adjusted for
one-offs, is the starting point for building a debt capacity model.

## 6. Geographic Information

### 6.1 Revenue by Geography

Grifols derives approximately 57% of revenue from North America — almost entirely
the United States. This geographic concentration has several implications:

| Geography | Revenue (€M) | Share |
|---|---|---|
| North America (mainly USA) | ~4,289 | ~57% |
| Europe | ~1,956 | ~26% |
| Rest of World | ~1,279 | ~17% |
| **Total** | **7,524** | **100%** |

The US dominance is structural: the US plasma donation market is the world's largest
(mandatory compensation of donors; no equivalent permitted in most European countries),
and the US healthcare system's pricing for immunoglobulins is significantly higher than
European public health systems. The US Medicaid and Medicare reimbursement channels
are also why the gross-to-net adjustments (chargebacks: €2,524M) are so large —
these are US-specific pricing mechanisms.

### 6.2 Non-Current Assets by Geography

The majority of Grifols' non-current assets are in the United States (production
facilities, plasma centers, goodwill from US-target acquisitions) and Spain (corporate
and R&D activities, European fractionation). This creates a mismatch: much of the
asset base is USD-denominated while debt is partially EUR-denominated — a key
source of the OCI foreign currency translation differences (€1,052M in 2025,
covered in Module 1).

### 6.3 Major Customer Concentration

IFRS 8 requires disclosure if any single customer represents 10% or more of revenues.
Grifols' customer base is highly fragmented — hospitals, blood banks, dialysis centers,
and pharmacy chains — and no single customer reaches the 10% threshold. This is
actually a credit-positive feature: no customer concentration risk means revenue
cannot disappear overnight due to a single contract loss.

## 7. Key Judgment Areas

### 7.1 How the CODM Is Defined

One of the most consequential management choices under IFRS 8 is who is designated
as the CODM. This determines what information gets reported in the segment note.
Some companies designate the CEO alone; others designate the full board. Grifols
designates its Board and Executive Committee collectively, which reflects the group's
governance structure following the family-governance controversy (the Gotham City
Research short-seller report challenged governance practices, and regulatory scrutiny
led to enhanced board oversight).

The practical implication: because the CODM includes the board, the segment information
disclosed must be consistent with what is presented to the full board — a higher
standard of transparency than companies where the CODM is just the CEO reviewing
monthly management accounts.

### 7.2 The Adjusted EBITDA as Segment Metric

Grifols uses adjusted EBITDA — not IFRS profit — as its segment performance measure.
This is common but requires judgment about what is "adjusted." The standard does not
define which items can be excluded from adjusted EBITDA. Items Grifols typically
excludes include:

- Restructuring and reorganization costs
- Acquisition-related costs
- Certain non-cash charges
- Legal settlements (depending on classification)

The IFRS 8 reconciliation must explain each adjustment, which effectively forces
Grifols to disclose to investors exactly what is being carved out of the headline metric.
This is a significant disclosure discipline — before IFRS 18, it is one of the primary
ways the standard constrains management's ability to present a misleading "adjusted" figure.

Under **IFRS 18** (effective 2027), management-defined performance measures like
adjusted EBITDA will be subject to new, more rigorous disclosure requirements:
reconciliation to defined IFRS subtotals, no selection of "cherry-picked" comparatives,
and consistent application year over year.

### 7.3 Biopharma Aggregation Question

Could Grifols further disaggregate Biopharma? The plasma division has distinct product
lines — immunoglobulins, albumin, clotting factors (factor VIII, factor IX), and
specialty proteins — that have meaningfully different margin profiles, competitive
dynamics, and growth drivers. Some peers in the plasma industry disclose revenue at
the product level within their core segment.

Grifols does not disaggregate within Biopharma in Nota 5, though Nota 22 (Revenue)
provides some product-level information. This is a presentation judgment — IFRS 8
permits aggregation of similar activities within a reportable segment. Analysts who
want product-level data must piece it together from the revenue note and investor
presentations.

## 8. The LBO Angle — Segment Data and Credit Analysis

Segment disclosures under IFRS 8 are not just accounting — they are the foundation
of your credit model.

### 8.1 EBITDA Composition Matters for Leverage

Knowing that Grifols' ~€1,693M of EBITDA is composed of ~82% Biopharma, ~12%
Diagnostic, and ~5% Bio Supplies tells you something critical about quality:

- **Biopharma EBITDA** is highly defensive — plasma-derived products treat chronic,
  life-threatening conditions. Immunoglobulin patients cannot simply switch therapy.
  Revenue is largely non-cyclical.
- **Diagnostic EBITDA** is semi-recurring — instrument placements create locked-in
  reagent volumes. The business has characteristics of a consumables/aftermarket model.
- **Bio Supplies EBITDA** is the most volatile — dependent on research spending and
  clinical trial activity, which can be cut during industry downturns.

This composition supports a relatively high leverage multiple in an LBO structure.
At €9.6bn gross debt and ~€1,693M EBITDA, Grifols runs at ~5.7x gross leverage —
a level that would be untenable in a cyclical industrial business but is more defensible
in a structurally non-cyclical specialty pharma business with inelastic demand.

### 8.2 Segment Capex Is a Critical Modeling Input

Knowing which segments consume capital tells you which parts of the business require
ongoing investment to maintain competitive position. Biopharma is the capital-intensive
core — fractionation plants are highly specialized manufacturing assets with long build
times. The €265M of PP&E capex (Module 3) is almost entirely Biopharma.

Diagnostic is relatively capital-light at the corporate level: the company incurs costs
placing instruments at customer sites (effectively operating lease economics), and the
instrument placement cost is recovered through the reagent margin over the contract term.

### 8.3 Segment Contribution to Working Capital

The €3,296M inventory balance (Module 9 — IAS 2) is dominated by Biopharma plasma:
raw plasma, work-in-progress through fractionation, and finished therapeutic products.
Plasma-derived proteins have long production cycles (9-12 months from plasma collection
to finished product release), which creates structurally high working capital requirements.
This is a key constraint on cash conversion: even with excellent EBITDA, the plasma
business requires sustained working capital investment as it grows.

### 8.4 The Diagnostic Segment as a Sum-of-Parts Consideration

The Diagnostic segment has been the subject of repeated strategic speculation. Its
~25% EBITDA margin, recurring reagent revenues, and strong market positions (45% of
global blood screening) make it a high-quality standalone business. A simple
comparable company analysis would value it at 10-14x EBITDA, suggesting €2.1-2.9bn
of value embedded in this segment alone.

Grifols explored a partial sale of Diagnostic in prior years. The segment note
(with its separate EBITDA and asset disclosures) is exactly what a strategic buyer
or financial sponsor would use to build an acquisition model for a carve-out.
The granularity of IFRS 8 disclosure makes potential M&A exits more legible.

## 9. Summary: What to Take Away

IFRS 8 is the standard that forces companies to disclose the disaggregated view
that management actually uses. Its key features:

- **Management approach**: Segments reflect how the CODM monitors performance,
  not an externally imposed framework
- **Reportable segments**: Identified by 10% tests across revenue, profit, and assets;
  at least 75% of external revenue must be covered
- **Flexible profit measure**: Whatever the CODM uses — even non-IFRS adjusted
  EBITDA — must be disclosed, with a reconciliation back to IFRS
- **Reconciliations are required**: Every segment subtotal must bridge to the
  corresponding audited consolidated figure

For Grifols:

- **Four segments**: Biopharma (~82% of EBITDA), Diagnostic (~12%), Bio Supplies
  (~5%), Others (~1%)
- **Segment metric**: Adjusted EBITDA — disclosed at segment level, reconciled to
  IFRS profit before tax
- **US concentration**: ~57% of revenue from North America, creating structural
  USD/EUR translation exposure
- **Diagnostic optionality**: High-margin, recurring segment with standalone value
  that provides credit protection and strategic flexibility
- **Biopharma capital intensity**: Dominates assets and capex; drives the structural
  working capital burden

Understanding the segment structure is not a compliance exercise — it is the foundation
for any rigorous analysis of Grifols' credit quality, cash flow sustainability, and
asset coverage.
