# Module 5: IAS 16 — Property, Plant and Equipment
## IFRS Deep Learning Series | Case Study: Grifols S.A. (FY2025)

---

## 1. Why This Standard Exists

Every manufacturer, processor, and infrastructure-heavy business ties up enormous
capital in physical assets — land, buildings, machinery, vehicles, tools. IAS 16
exists to answer a deceptively simple set of questions about those assets:

- **When is a physical asset recognized on the balance sheet?**
- **At what amount is it initially measured?**
- **How is its cost spread over its useful life?**
- **What happens when it becomes impaired or is disposed of?**
- **What must be disclosed so users can assess the asset base?**

Without IAS 16, two companies with identical physical assets could present dramatically
different balance sheets — one capitalizing everything, another expensing most costs
immediately; one depreciating over 5 years, another over 40. The comparability
of financial statements across entities and periods would collapse.

For Grifols, this standard governs a €3,120M net asset base that includes some of the
most technically complex and capital-intensive biological manufacturing equipment in
the world. Understanding how that asset base is measured, depreciated, and disclosed
is fundamental to understanding the company's true economics.

## 2. Recognition

### 2.1 The Two-Part Recognition Test

IAS 16.7 permits recognition of a tangible asset only when both criteria are met:

**(a)** It is **probable** that future economic benefits associated with the item will
flow to the entity, AND

**(b)** The **cost** of the item can be **measured reliably**

These criteria apply to all items of PP&E — initial acquisitions, subsequent costs
(replacements, improvements), and major inspections and overhauls. The standard
explicitly requires that the same recognition criteria be applied to both initial
and subsequent expenditures.

### 2.2 Subsequent Expenditures — The Capitalize-vs-Expense Decision

This is one of the most practically important judgment calls in PP&E accounting. IAS 16
requires subsequent costs to be capitalized (added to the asset's carrying amount) if
— and only if — they meet the two-part test above. Costs that simply maintain the
asset in working order (ordinary maintenance and repairs) are expensed as incurred.

**The test in practice:**

- **Routine maintenance** (lubricating a conveyor, replacing worn brushes on a motor):
  expense immediately — these costs restore the asset to its expected working condition
  and generate no additional future economic benefits beyond what was originally anticipated.
  
- **Component replacement** (replacing the filtration membranes in a fractionation
  column, installing a new heat exchanger): capitalize — the replacement extends or
  enhances the asset's capacity to generate future economic benefits.

- **Major inspection/overhaul** (biennial shutdown of a fractionation plant for
  regulatory compliance inspection and requalification): capitalize as a separate
  component — the cost is essentially "buying" the right to operate the plant for
  another inspection period.

**In Grifols**: Fractionation plants undergo periodic regulatory shutdowns — the FDA
and EMA require manufacturers of biological medicines to periodically revalidate their
facilities and equipment. The cost of these shutdowns (labor, testing, replacement
parts, revalidation studies) can run into tens of millions of euros per facility.
Under IAS 16.14, Grifols capitalizes these costs as a distinct component of the
plant, then depreciates them over the period until the next scheduled inspection.
This is the **components approach** — discussed in detail below.

### 2.3 Spare Parts and Servicing Equipment

IAS 16.8 clarifies that major spare parts and standby equipment qualify as PP&E
when an entity expects to use them during more than one period. Minor spare parts
are typically treated as inventory under IAS 2. For Grifols, which operates
specialized fractionation columns, centrifuges, and viral inactivation systems that
may take months to source and replace, maintaining major spare components that are
held for installation is common. These are on the balance sheet as PP&E, not
inventory.

## 3. Initial Measurement — Cost

### 3.1 What "Cost" Includes

IAS 16.16 defines cost as comprising:

**(a) Purchase price**, including import duties and non-refundable purchase taxes,
after deducting trade discounts and rebates

**(b) Directly attributable costs** of bringing the asset to the location and condition
necessary for it to be capable of operating in the manner intended by management.
These include:
  - Site preparation costs
  - Initial delivery and handling costs
  - Installation and assembly costs
  - The costs of testing whether the asset functions correctly (net of proceeds from
    selling any items produced during testing)
  - Professional fees (architects, engineers)

**(c) The initial estimate of decommissioning and restoration costs** (if the entity
has an obligation to decommission or restore the site). This links to IAS 37
(Provisions) — the decommissioning obligation is recognized as a provision, and the
corresponding amount is added to the asset's cost.

### 3.2 What "Cost" Excludes

IAS 16.19 explicitly excludes from cost:

- Costs of opening a new facility
- Costs of introducing a new product or service (including advertising costs)
- Costs of conducting business in a new location or with a new class of customer
- Administration and other general overhead costs
- Costs incurred while an asset capable of operating is yet to be brought into use
  (a plant that is complete but idle — the delay does not justify further capitalization)

**The practical implication for Grifols**: When Grifols opens a new plasma collection
center — fitting it out with donor beds, centrifuges, refrigeration units, and
plasmapheresis machines — the construction and installation costs are capitalizable.
But the ramp-up period costs (recruiting donors, training staff, marketing the new
center to potential donors) are expensed as incurred. IAS 16 draws a hard line at
"capable of operating in the manner intended" — once that threshold is reached,
ongoing operating losses are P&L items, not capital costs.

### 3.3 Borrowing Costs — IAS 23 Interaction

IAS 23 (Borrowing Costs) requires that borrowing costs directly attributable to the
acquisition, construction, or production of a **qualifying asset** (one that necessarily
takes a substantial period of time to get ready for its intended use) be capitalized
as part of the asset's cost.

A new fractionation plant — which may take 3-5 years to design, build, commission,
and receive regulatory approval — is a textbook qualifying asset. During the
construction phase, Grifols capitalizes a portion of its borrowing costs. Once the
asset is ready for use, capitalization stops and interest cost flows entirely through
the P&L.

**In Grifols**: The construction-in-progress line in Nota 9 typically includes ongoing
plant expansion and center build-out projects. Interest capitalized during 2025 was
modest relative to the total interest charge (Nota 25) because most of Grifols' major
capital projects are operational. The capitalized interest is disclosed in Nota 9
and in the note on borrowing costs.

## 4. The Components Approach

### 4.1 What It Is and Why It Matters

This is one of the most technically important concepts in IAS 16 and one that most
students underappreciate.

IAS 16.43 requires that **each significant part of an item of PP&E with a different
useful life must be depreciated separately**. This is known as the components approach
(or componentization).

**The intuition**: A commercial aircraft doesn't have a single useful life. The airframe
might last 30 years. The engines might be overhauled every 7 years. The interior
(seats, entertainment systems) might be replaced every 10 years. Treating the whole
aircraft as one depreciable unit (over, say, 20 years) would distort the economics
by spreading costs in a pattern that doesn't match how the components actually wear out.

IAS 16 requires identifying each component whose cost is significant relative to the
total cost of the item, and depreciating it over its own useful life. When a component
is replaced, the old component is derecognized (its remaining carrying amount is
removed from the balance sheet) and the new component is recognized at cost.

### 4.2 In Grifols: Fractionation Plant Components

A plasma fractionation plant is an almost perfect illustration of the components
approach. Consider a facility like Grifols' Clayton, North Carolina plant:

| Component | Useful Life | Reason for Separation |
|---|---|---|
| Land | Indefinite — not depreciated | Land does not deplete |
| Building structure | 30-50 years | Long-lived shell; replaced rarely |
| Fractionation columns | 15-20 years | Core process equipment; periodic replacement |
| Centrifuges and separators | 10-15 years | Higher wear; shorter cycle |
| Viral inactivation systems | 10-15 years | Safety-critical; technology evolves |
| Cold storage and HVAC | 15-20 years | Infrastructure; tied to building |
| Control systems/SCADA | 5-10 years | Technology cycle; replaced more frequently |
| Regulatory inspection component | 2-4 years | Capitalized inspection cost; amortized to next shutdown |

By applying componentization, Grifols aligns depreciation with the actual pattern of
economic consumption. A single 25-year straight-line rate for the entire plant would
understate early-year depreciation (when older technology components wear faster)
and overstate late-year depreciation (when the durable shell remains but equipment
has already been replaced).

The practical consequence: when Grifols replaces fractionation columns at its
Parets del Vallès plant, it writes off the remaining carrying amount of the old
columns (a derecognition gain or loss through the P&L) and capitalizes the cost of
new columns at their acquisition cost. This accurately reflects the economic reality
of partial asset replacement.

## 5. Subsequent Measurement — Cost Model vs Revaluation Model

### 5.1 The Choice

After initial recognition, IAS 16.29-31 requires an entity to choose one of two
models as its accounting policy, applied consistently to an entire class of PP&E:

**Cost Model**: The asset is carried at its original cost, less accumulated depreciation
and any accumulated impairment losses. This is the simpler, more objective approach.

**Revaluation Model**: The asset is carried at a revalued amount — its **fair value**
at the date of revaluation — less any subsequent accumulated depreciation and
impairment losses. Revaluations must be performed with sufficient regularity that
the carrying amount does not differ materially from fair value at the reporting date.
When an asset is revalued upward, the increase goes to Other Comprehensive Income
(a "revaluation surplus" in equity). When revalued downward below cost, the decrease
goes through P&L.

### 5.2 Grifols' Choice: Cost Model

Grifols applies the **cost model** to all classes of PP&E (Nota 4). This is the
majority practice among IFRS preparers for industrial manufacturing assets for
several reasons:

- Fractionation equipment is highly specialized; there is no active market from
  which to derive a reliable fair value
- Revaluations require periodic independent appraisals — costly and time-consuming
  for hundreds of plasma collection centers across the US
- The cost model eliminates the volatility in equity that revaluation surpluses/
  deficits would create

**The consequence for credit analysis**: Grifols' PP&E is carried at historical cost
minus depreciation. If the assets have appreciated in value (e.g., real estate at US
plasma collection centers), that appreciation is invisible on the balance sheet. The
net PP&E figure of €3,120M is a floor, not necessarily an indication of realization
value. In a distressed scenario, the actual value of plasma collection center
infrastructure in prime US locations might differ significantly from book value
in either direction (specialist equipment could be worth less; real property might
be worth more).

## 6. Depreciation

### 6.1 The Three Inputs

Depreciation under IAS 16 requires three inputs:

**Depreciable amount** = Cost (or revalued amount) **minus** residual value.
The residual value is the estimated amount the entity would currently receive if
it disposed of the asset at the end of its useful life (net of disposal costs).
If residual value is immaterial (common for specialized industrial equipment), the
full cost is depreciated.

**Useful life** = The period over which the asset is expected to be available for
use by the entity. Critically, this is the entity's *own* expected use period —
not the asset's physical life or a tax-defined period. If Grifols plans to replace
fractionation columns every 15 years even though the equipment could physically last
25 years, the useful life for depreciation is 15 years.

**Depreciation method** = The pattern in which the asset's future economic benefits
are consumed. IAS 16 permits:
- **Straight-line**: Equal annual charge. Used for most PP&E where consumption is
  uniform (buildings, structures, most machinery).
- **Diminishing balance** (declining balance): Front-loads the charge. Appropriate
  when an asset is more productive in its early years or where maintenance costs
  increase over time.
- **Units of production**: Links depreciation to actual usage (liters processed,
  units produced). Appropriate where the useful life is defined by output rather
  than time.

### 6.2 Grifols' Useful Lives and Depreciation Rates

Nota 9 discloses Grifols' useful life ranges by asset class:

| Asset Class | Useful Life (Years) | Method | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| Buildings and structures | 20–50 | Straight-line | Long-lived; consumption broadly uniform |
| Technical installations | 10–25 | Straight-line | Manufacturing infrastructure |
| Machinery and equipment | 5–20 | Straight-line | Equipment varies by complexity |
| Furniture and fittings | 3–15 | Straight-line | Shorter commercial cycle |
| IT equipment | 3–5 | Straight-line | Technology obsolescence dominates |
| Vehicles | 5–8 | Straight-line | Standard vehicle useful life |
| Plasma collection center fit-out | 10–20 | Straight-line | Lease term constrains useful life |

**Land is not depreciated** — it has an indefinite useful life (with the exception
of quarries and landfill sites where consumption is finite).

### 6.3 The Annual Review of Useful Lives

IAS 16.51 is one of the most underappreciated requirements in the standard: useful
lives, residual values, and depreciation methods must be reviewed **at least at each
annual reporting date**. Changes are treated as changes in accounting estimates
under IAS 8 (Module 2) — prospective treatment, no restatement.

**Why this matters in practice**: If Grifols decides that advances in viral
inactivation technology mean its existing solvent-detergent units will become
obsolete in 8 years rather than the previously assumed 15 years, the remaining
carrying amount of those units is depreciated over the new remaining useful life.
This can materially increase the depreciation charge in subsequent periods.

Conversely, if operational improvements extend the effective life of fractionation
columns (through better maintenance protocols or process improvements), Grifols can
reduce the depreciation rate prospectively. This is not aggressive — it is IAS 16
working correctly. But it is exactly the type of estimate change that a sharp analyst
should track across reporting periods.

## 7. Grifols' PP&E — Reading Nota 9

### 7.1 The Asset Roll-Forward

Nota 9 presents a standard IAS 16 disclosure: a reconciliation of opening to closing
gross carrying amounts and accumulated depreciation for each class of asset.

| Class | Net Book Value (€M) | Character |
|---|---|---|
| Land and land improvements | ~200 | Not depreciated; US plasma center sites, Spanish campuses |
| Buildings and structures | ~900 | Fractionation plants, plasma centers, labs, offices |
| Technical installations/machinery | ~1,300 | Fractionation columns, centrifuges, purification systems |
| Other PP&E | ~220 | Furniture, IT, vehicles, lab equipment |
| Assets under construction (CIP) | ~500 | Ongoing capacity expansions, center builds |
| **Total net PP&E** | **~3,120** | |

**Construction in progress (CIP)** deserves special attention. The ~€500M balance
represents capital already spent on assets not yet in service — plants under
construction, plasma centers being fitted out, equipment being installed and
commissioned. No depreciation is charged on CIP. Once assets are completed and
ready for use, they are transferred to the appropriate category and depreciation
begins.

For a credit analyst, CIP is a leading indicator of future capex commitments: it
tells you that Grifols has already committed capital to projects that haven't yet
generated any revenue. It also represents assets that would not generate immediate
value in a forced liquidation scenario (incomplete plants are worth less than the
capital invested in them).

### 7.2 The Gross-to-Net Reconciliation

The gross carrying amount (cost) of PP&E is significantly higher than the net amount:

| | €M |
|---|---|
| Gross cost | ~6,800 |
| Accumulated depreciation | ~(3,680) |
| **Net carrying amount** | **~3,120** |

The depreciation-to-gross ratio (~54%) tells you that Grifols' PP&E base is
approximately mid-life on average. This is consistent with the profile of a business
that has been building out its infrastructure for decades (Talecris assets acquired
in 2011 are now 14+ years old) while simultaneously investing in new capacity
(Biotest German facilities acquired 2022, new US plasma centers).

For asset coverage analysis:
- Gross PP&E (~€6.8bn) is a rough proxy for replacement cost (though this overstates
  it because accumulated depreciation partly reflects assets that have been replaced)
- Net PP&E (~€3.1bn) represents book value — relevant for balance sheet covenant
  analysis
- In an actual distressed scenario, liquidation value for specialized manufacturing
  equipment would be a fraction of book value (distressed industrial assets typically
  realize 10-40 cents on the dollar of book value)

### 7.3 Capex vs Depreciation — The Investment Intensity Signal

| | 2025 | 2024 | 2023 |
|---|---|---|---|
| PP&E capex (cash invested) | 265 | 233 | 224 |
| PP&E depreciation (approximate) | ~240 | ~235 | ~240 |
| Net investment in PP&E | +25 | (2) | (16) |

The near-parity between capex and depreciation is the classic signal of a business
running at roughly **maintenance capex** levels — it is just about replacing assets
as they wear out, with minimal net capacity addition through PP&E alone. New
fractionation capacity in 2025 comes primarily through the integration of existing
Biotest assets and efficiency improvements, not major greenfield construction.

This is important for LBO modeling: if Grifols is in a period where PP&E capex
approximately equals depreciation, then "normalized" maintenance capex ≈ €240-265M
per year. Growth capex — for new plasma centers or fractionation capacity expansion —
comes on top of this as a discretionary item.

## 8. Derecognition

### 8.1 When an Asset Is Removed from the Balance Sheet

IAS 16.67 requires an item of PP&E to be derecognized when either:

**(a)** It is disposed of (sold, scrapped, or donated), OR

**(b)** No future economic benefits are expected from its use or disposal

The gain or loss on derecognition = **Net proceeds received minus Carrying amount**
at the date of disposal. This goes through P&L — it is not classified as revenue
(because asset disposals are not part of ordinary activities for an industrial company).

### 8.2 In Grifols

The cash flow statement shows €9M of PP&E disposal proceeds in 2025. Grifols
periodically closes and sells underperforming plasma centers — a center that fails
to attract sufficient donor volume is closed, its fit-out written off, and the
leasehold interest (if any) surrendered or sublet.

Closures result in disposal losses (remaining net book value written off) that flow
through the P&L under "Resultado de enajenaciones" or similar line items. These are
relatively small in any given year but represent the ongoing portfolio optimization
of the plasma center network.

## 9. The Impairment Interface — Where IAS 16 Meets IAS 36

IAS 16 requires assets to be tested for impairment whenever there is an indication
that an asset may be impaired. This is governed by IAS 36 (Impairment of Assets),
which is covered in Module 7. The key interface points are:

- If an asset's recoverable amount falls below its carrying amount, the carrying
  amount must be written down to recoverable amount. The write-down is an impairment
  loss and goes through P&L (or reduces the revaluation surplus for revalued assets).
- For PP&E that is part of a cash-generating unit (CGU), impairment is tested at
  the CGU level, not asset-by-asset. Grifols' fractionation plants are likely grouped
  into CGUs by segment or geographic cluster.

## 10. Key Disclosures Required by IAS 16

IAS 16.73-79 requires the following disclosures for each class of PP&E:

**(a)** Measurement bases used (cost model or revaluation model)

**(b)** Depreciation methods used

**(c)** Useful lives or depreciation rates

**(d)** Gross carrying amount and accumulated depreciation at the beginning and end
of the period

**(e)** A reconciliation of opening to closing carrying amounts, showing:
  - Additions
  - Assets classified as held for sale (IFRS 5)
  - Acquisitions through business combinations
  - Disposals
  - Depreciation charge
  - Impairment losses recognized/reversed
  - Exchange differences (translation)
  - Other movements

**(f)** Contractual commitments for acquisition of PP&E

**(g)** For assets carried under the revaluation model: significant information about
appraisals, book vs fair value, revaluation surplus balance and movements

**In Grifols, the disclosures that matter most for analysts:**

The **translation line** in the PP&E reconciliation is significant: because ~57% of
Grifols' revenue (and a large portion of its PP&E) is in the United States, the
Euro/Dollar exchange rate movement directly affects the Euro carrying amount of
US assets. In a year when the Euro strengthens significantly (as in 2025), the PP&E
balance reported in Euros falls simply due to translation, even if the underlying US
asset base is unchanged. This is why the OCI translation reserve (€1,052M negative
in 2025) is so large.

The **capex commitment disclosure** is another key item. If Grifols has contractually
committed to build new plasma centers or purchase equipment, those commitments will
appear in the notes — typically in the €100-300M range in any given year, representing
the pipeline of centers under development.

## 11. The LBO Angle — PP&E in Credit Analysis

### 11.1 Asset Coverage Ratio

In a stressed scenario where debt must be repaid through asset liquidation, PP&E
is the key pool of hard collateral. Grifols' ~€3,120M net PP&E provides partial
coverage of the ~€9.6bn gross debt — a ratio of ~0.32x. This means PP&E alone
provides limited debt coverage, which is typical for a branded pharmaceutical/
biologics business (intangible value — products, market positions, customer
relationships — dominates the asset base, as we will see in Modules 6 and 7).

The broader asset coverage calculation:

| Asset | Carrying Value (€M) | LBO Coverage Comment |
|---|---|---|
| Net PP&E | 3,120 | Specialized; 20-50c on the dollar in forced sale |
| Inventory | 3,296 | Plasma and finished products; partially realizable |
| Receivables | 651 | High quality; 80-90c recovery in distress |
| Cash | 825 | 100c |
| Intangibles + goodwill | 9,561 | Near-zero in liquidation unless business sold as going concern |

The key insight: **Grifols' credit story depends entirely on enterprise value (going
concern), not liquidation value of assets.** The PP&E is the backbone of the operating
business, but it has minimal standalone value. The only way the debt gets repaid is
if the business continues to operate and generate cash — which is precisely why the
going concern analysis (Module 1: IAS 1) and cash flow generation (Module 3: IAS 7)
are so central to the credit case.

### 11.2 Capex Flexibility — A Lever in Distress

One feature of plasma fractionation that matters for distressed analysis: capital
expenditure has significant flexibility in the short term. Maintenance capex is
non-deferrable (you must maintain FDA/EMA compliance), but growth capex —
new plasma centers, fractionation capacity expansion — can be paused.

In 2023, when Grifols was under severe financial pressure (leverage above 9x, operating
cash flow of only €217M before working capital normalization), the company constrained
capex to the minimum required. The €265M total PP&E capex in 2025 (approximately
maintenance levels) versus a prior peak of ~€400M+ shows the operating leverage
available in the capex line.

### 11.3 Sale-Leaseback Potential

Plasma collection centers and fractionation facilities represent potential
sale-leaseback liquidity in an extreme stress scenario. Grifols has explored this:
it has executed sale-leaseback transactions of certain plasma collection centers in
prior years, converting owned real estate into IFRS 16 right-of-use assets and
generating one-time cash proceeds. The economics are usually unattractive
(pharmaceutical real estate buyers demand high yields, and the long-term lease
obligation increases fixed costs), but the optionality exists.

The trade-off under IFRS: a sale-leaseback converts a PP&E asset (IAS 16) into a
ROU asset (IFRS 16). The net debt impact depends on the structure — the new lease
liability replaces the existing debt (if the proceeds are used to repay), but the
income statement effect changes (depreciation replaced by interest + depreciation
on the ROU asset, broadly similar in total).

## 12. Summary: What to Take Away

IAS 16 governs how Grifols reports the €3,120M of physical assets at the heart
of its manufacturing operations. The key concepts:

- **Recognition**: Cost if and only if probable future benefits + reliable measurement
- **Initial measurement at cost**: Includes purchase price + directly attributable costs
  + decommissioning obligations (IAS 37)
- **Components approach**: Significant parts with different useful lives depreciated
  separately — essential for complex manufacturing assets like fractionation plants
- **Cost model** (Grifols' choice): Carried at cost minus accumulated depreciation
  and impairment; no revaluation upside on the balance sheet
- **Depreciation**: Three inputs — depreciable amount, useful life, method. Useful lives
  reviewed annually; changes treated as estimate revisions (Module 2: IAS 8)
- **Net PP&E of €3,120M**: Approximately mid-life on average; capex ≈ depreciation
  implies a maintenance phase, not an aggressive expansion phase
- **LBO insight**: PP&E provides limited liquidation coverage; credit depends on
  enterprise value and ongoing cash generation, not asset realization value

The physical asset base is the foundation of Grifols' manufacturing moat — it
represents decades of investment in plasma collection infrastructure and fractionation
capacity that cannot be replicated quickly or cheaply. But its value is entirely
contingent on the going concern of the business.
