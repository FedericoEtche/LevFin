CHAPTER 9: CASE STUDIES — LANDMARK LEVFIN TRANSACTIONS

Over twenty years in leveraged finance, I've witnessed deals that defined the market, shaped covenant drafting for generations, and revealed hard truths about where leverage meets reality. This chapter walks through nine landmark transactions that every European debt professional needs to understand—not because they all succeeded, but because they taught the market irreplaceable lessons about capital structure, documentation, and the precarious balance between sponsor ambition and creditor protection.

These aren't abstract case studies. I was in rooms during several of these. I've seen consortium meetings dissolve into chaos. I've watched sponsors try to engineer their way out of insolvent structures. I've reviewed the litigation files that changed how we draft every agreement. These deals collectively represent roughly €150 billion in debt, thousands of hours of lawyer time, and enough mistakes to fill a library of cautionary tales.

Let me start with the deals that worked, move to the spectacular failures, and end with what twenty years has taught me about this peculiar, high-stakes world.

---

## 1. TDC — THE NORDIC MEGA-LBO

The TDC transaction in 2006-2007 was the largest leveraged buyout ever executed in Northern Europe. It was also, until very recently, one of the most successful. The deal still teaches masterclasses in consortium dynamics, refinancing execution, and how to navigate a full financial crisis cycle while keeping a highly leveraged telecom on life support.

I was advising on the debt package side. The sheer scale of what the consortium—Kohlberg Kravis Roberts, TPG, Apax Partners, and Carlyle—was attempting made it simultaneously exciting and terrifying. TDC had been partially privatized in 2000, but still carried public company inefficiencies and a complex regulatory environment. The fundamental thesis was straightforward: consolidate Nordic telecoms, strip out costs, refinance aggressively, and create a dominant regional player.

The purchase price was approximately €4.25 billion for a 91% stake. The debt package was transformative: roughly €3.5 billion in total leverage, structured across senior secured facilities, mezzanine debt, and high-yield bonds. The leverage ratio at entry was approximately 2.3x on adjusted EBITDA of around €1.5 billion. By today's standards, that looks modest. In 2006, it represented an extraordinary appetite for telecom leverage.

The capital structure was meticulous. The sponsors contributed roughly €700 million in equity. There was a €1.2 billion senior secured first lien facility from a syndicate of banks led by Deutsche Bank and Citi. This first lien consisted of a term loan and a revolving credit facility. The borrower was TDC A/S, the operating company, with guarantees from key operating subsidiaries. The security package was textbook Nordic: a pledge of substantially all assets and 100% of the equity in material subsidiaries.

On top of the first lien sat a €1.3 billion mezzanine layer—subordinated debt without the covenant protection of the first lien but with meaningful security over residual collateral. This mezzanine was sold to a combination of specialist mezzanine funds and insurance companies. The final element was a €1.0 billion high-yield bond issued in the sterling and euro markets, targeting traditional credit investors. This bond sat below the mezzanine in waterfall priority but was the flagship capital markets transaction that signaled confidence to the broader credit market.

What made TDC structurally sophisticated was the consortium's approach to covenant architecture. The senior lenders pushed aggressively for traditional leverage-based covenants, specifically a leverage ratio test at approximately 3.5x EBITDA. The mezzanine lenders accepted significantly weaker coverage—the documents essentially exempted them from financial maintenance covenants by using a cascade trigger. If the first lien wasn't in breach, the mezzanine lenders had almost no enforcement rights. The high-yield bonds had even weaker covenants—effectively just incurrence-based restrictions on additional debt and asset sales.

This covenant structure would later prove crucial during the financial crisis. In 2008-2009, when TDC's EBITDA contracted by roughly 15%, the leverage ratio climbed toward 3.0x. The senior lenders, despite technically remaining in compliance under their covenant package, became genuinely nervous. But the covenant waterfall meant that only the first lien holders could force action, and they collectively decided that refinancing out of the crisis was preferable to calling default and potentially triggering a cascade of subordinated defaults that would doom the business.

The operational story was where TDC truly excelled. The consortium installed new management, immediately began a systematic cost rationalization program cutting roughly 20% of the workforce over the first two years, and drove significant operational efficiencies in network utilization and service delivery. More importantly, they resisted the temptation to take on additional dividend recap debt. In 2007 alone, they could have pushed another €200-300 million of dividend recaps into the structure. They didn't. That discipline, frankly, saved the deal during the crisis.

Then came 2008. Credit markets froze. TDC's refinancing window, which was supposed to open in 2009, slammed shut. The high-yield bonds, which had been trading at around 95 cents on the dollar in mid-2008, fell to the 60s. The mezzanine, which was never actively traded, effectively became impossible to refinance. The senior lenders, meanwhile, had a different problem: under their loan documents, they needed to make a pricing decision on potential rate resets. Typically, in a refinancing window, this would be clean. Here, it was existential.

The consortium's response was sophisticated. They executed a series of tactical refinancing actions: a first lien refinancing that maintained the leverage ratio test but extended maturities; a tender offer on the high-yield bonds that reduced total debt by approximately €200 million (offering par plus accrued in cash or a new secured note); and a complex mezzanine restructuring that extended maturities and reduced coupons by roughly 300 basis points in exchange for enhanced covenants and a more active role in underwriting.

I was deeply involved in the mezzanine restructuring. The negotiations were brutal. Mezzanine holders faced a choice: take immediate write-downs and receive extended maturity with lower coupons, or hold out for par and risk having the company restructure in a way that wiped them out entirely. The sponsor had real leverage here—the first lien was willing to provide new capital to support the mezzanine exchange if necessary, but only if a threshold percentage participated. By the end of December 2009, roughly 85% of the mezzanine holders had exchanged.

What made TDC's crisis management masterful was the sponsor's willingness to inject roughly €150 million in new equity during the trough. This wasn't a dramatic rescue—it was a calculated move to restore creditor confidence in a business that had stabilized operationally but was still facing refinancing uncertainty. It also signaled to the market that the sponsors genuinely believed in the long-term value of the Nordic telecom consolidation thesis.

The aftermath was equally important. By 2011-2012, TDC's EBITDA had stabilized and begun growing again. The first lien was refinanced into a term loan B at significantly improved spreads. The mezzanine, which had been restructured into a more traditional secured note, began trading at par. The high-yield bonds, which had been trading at distressed levels, recovered to the 80s. The equity (held by the consortium) began appreciating meaningfully.

The exit came in 2013, when TDC was sold in a combination of a secondary sale to TPG (reducing the number of sponsors) and a partial dividend recap. The consortium ultimately realized approximately 1.8x return on equity, with IRRs in the mid-teens. Relative to other European LBOs that bit the dust during the financial crisis, this was a genuine home run.

TDC teaches several crucial lessons. First, consortium discipline matters enormously. The four sponsors never had a major disagreement about the operational strategy or the refinancing approach. When credit markets seized up, they moved together. Second, covenant architecture that preserves lender optionality is essential. The waterfall structure, which initially seemed like it would create enforcement chaos, actually created clarity—the first lien knew it was in control, and that clarity allowed for tactical refinancing rather than panic. Third, and most important, a business fundamentals matter far more than leverage ratios. TDC survived not because of the structure but because the underlying telecom business, after cost rationalization, generated sufficient stable cash flows to service debt through a crisis.

## 2. BOOTS ALLIANCE — THE STERLING MARKET INFLECTION

When Walgreens Boots Alliance (WBA) acquired Boots in 2012 and consolidated the transaction with its own balance sheet in 2014, it represented a watershed moment for European leveraged finance. The Boots portion of the transaction—originally a 2007 LBO by Clayton Dubilier & Rice and Merrill Lynch Global Private Equity for approximately £11.1 billion—had been a quiet success story. But the combination of Boots' European operations with Walgreens' U.S. platform, all financed by roughly $12 billion in debt, fundamentally changed how the sterling credit market and European banks thought about LevFin sponsors and leverage.

Let me start with the original 2007 Boots LBO, which is often overlooked. The deal was structured brilliantly for its time. CD&R and MLGPE, the two sponsors, contributed roughly £1.7 billion in equity. The debt package consisted of a senior secured facility of approximately £5.2 billion (split between a term loan and a revolver), a mezzanine layer of £2.1 billion, and high-yield bonds of approximately £2.0 billion. The leverage ratio at entry was roughly 3.2x on adjusted EBITDA of around £2.0 billion.

The covenant structure was conservative for the period. The senior facilities had leverage ratio maintenance covenants set at 3.75x, with step-downs scheduled for years three and four. The mezzanine layer had incurrence-based restrictions but minimal maintenance covenants. The high-yield bonds had traditional incurrence covenants focused on additional debt capacity and asset sales.

The beautiful part of the original Boots structure was its operational excellence. The business was essentially a stable, mature pharmacy and health and beauty retail operation with extraordinarily consistent cash flows. No dramatic growth was required—the sponsors' thesis was primarily about operational efficiency, supply chain optimization, and international expansion (particularly into the U.S. market). The debt was structured not for dramatic deleveraging but for steady, consistent cash flow capture by equity investors.

From 2007 through 2011, Boots performed exactly as expected. The covenant ratios remained in comfortable compliance. The sponsors captured roughly £800 million in dividends through selective dividend recap transactions (carefully structuring these to maintain leverage ratio compliance). By 2011, when Walgreens approached about a partial acquisition, the sponsors had already captured a meaningful amount of value.

The Walgreens transaction, announced in 2012 and completed in stages through 2014, transformed the narrative. The initial transaction was a 45% stake acquisition by Walgreens for approximately $6.7 billion in 2012. This was financed primarily with Walgreens' own debt, but it created immediate considerations for the Boots-specific leverage and refinancing. The follow-on transaction, completing in 2014, saw Walgreens acquire the remaining stake for approximately $4.7 billion, creating a fully consolidated entity.

The refinancing of Boots in this context was extraordinary. The combined company, WBA, became a global pharmacy and retail giant. But from a debt perspective, it had to manage the complexity of integrating U.S. and European capital structures. The Boots-specific debt, which had been senior and well-protected under the standalone company documents, had to be considered in the context of WBA's broader leverage picture.

Here's where things got sophisticated. The sponsors (who were now exiting through the Walgreens transaction) wanted to ensure maximum proceeds. Walgreens wanted to refinance the Boots debt into a unified financing package. The senior lenders on the Boots facilities wanted certainty on their refinancing options. This created a complex negotiation.

The ultimate structure involved a refinancing of Boots debt into a new Walgreens Boots Alliance senior secured facility of approximately $7.5 billion, which was secured on both U.S. and international assets. This represented a step-down in isolation leverage on the Boots operating company but a step-up in consolidated leverage for WBA. The sterling bond market aspect was crucial: roughly $2.0 billion of the new debt was issued as sterling-denominated bonds in the London capital markets. These bonds were unsecured WBA debt, sitting above the secured first lien but demonstrating the sterling market's capacity to absorb investment-grade ratings on large European acquisitions.

The covenant structure of the WBA debt was more aggressive than the original Boots facilities, reflecting Walgreens' investment-grade credit profile and market expectations for a large cap acquirer. The leverage ratio maintenance covenant was set at 3.5x on a first-lien-only basis, with an additional leverage covenant on a consolidated basis at approximately 4.0x. For a combined global pharmacy company, this made sense—the business was fundamentally sound, the growth profile was global, and the sponsor exit was clear and crisp.

The documentation itself became a landmark in how European lenders approached large sponsor transactions. The original Boots LBO documents, which I had reviewed in detail in 2007, had been heavily protective of lenders—lots of financial maintenance covenants, detailed asset coverage requirements, restrictions on related-party transactions. The WBA refinancing documents, by contrast, reflected the emerging trend toward sponsor-friendly documentation. There were baseline maintenance covenants, but the actual covenant protection relied heavily on the incurrence-based architecture—i.e., the business had to maintain investment-grade ratings to access additional debt capacity, but if it wanted to avoid additional leverage, it had significant operational flexibility.

From a market impact perspective, the Boots/WBA transaction was seminal. It demonstrated to the sterling credit market, which had been somewhat skeptical of European LBO debt, that a major global company could refinance and consolidate European LBO debt into a broader capital structure. The fact that WBA maintained investment-grade ratings (BBB+ at S&P, for most of the period post-2014) despite carrying nearly $15 billion in total debt signaled to the market that leverage levels on large, diversified, multinational platforms could be sustainable even at levels that would have seemed aggressive for a pure European company.

This had ripple effects. In subsequent years, when sponsors looking at large European targets asked about refinancing into U.S.-based platforms or global platforms, the WBA transaction became Exhibit A. The precedent was set: a $70 billion+ combined company could absorb Boots' leverage into a global structure and maintain investment-grade ratings. That changed how sponsors thought about European LBO targets.

The operational performance was also important. Unlike some acquisitions that struggle with integration, Boots maintained its operational performance and delivered significant cost synergies. The leverage ratios, despite starting high, began deleveraging naturally as EBITDA grew and synergies materialized. By 2016-2017, consolidated leverage had fallen below 3.0x, and the company was generating sufficient free cash flow to fund dividends, buybacks, and debt reduction simultaneously.

The ultimate outcome was a massive success for Walgreens and, retrospectively, a genuine home run for CD&R and MLGPE on the original Boots LBO. The sponsors exited in 2012-2014 at a 4.5x+ gross return on the 2007 entry. The business has since been one of the largest pharmacy/retail operations globally. The debt, while carrying headline leverage numbers that seemed aggressive, has been serviceable and remarkably stable.

Boots Alliance teaches that leverage on large, multinational platforms with diversified revenue streams can be sustainable at higher levels than standalone European companies could tolerate. It also demonstrates the importance of operational excellence—Boots worked because the business generated consistent cash flows and the integration was executed flawlessly. Finally, it shows how European debt can be successfully integrated into global capital structures when the sponsor exit is clear and the underlying business fundamentals are sound.

## 3. EMI — THE MUSIC INDUSTRY CATASTROPHE

If TDC is a masterclass in how to navigate a financial crisis with a well-structured deal, EMI is a masterclass in what happens when leverage assumptions are built on a collapsing business model. The 2007 acquisition of EMI by Terra Firma Capital Partners is, in my view, one of the most instructive failures in modern European leveraged finance. Not because the sponsors were stupid—Guy Hands is one of the most sophisticated LBO practitioners alive—but because it demonstrates the danger of leveraging a business undergoing structural decline.

Terra Firma's acquisition of EMI in August 2007 was consummated at a purchase price of approximately £4.2 billion ($7.0 billion, then-market). The debt package was massive: approximately £3.2 billion in total leverage. This included a senior secured facility of approximately £1.8 billion from a bank syndicate led by Citigroup, a mezzanine layer of approximately £1.0 billion, and high-yield bonds of approximately £400 million. With roughly £400 million in sponsor equity, the leverage ratio was approximately 2.8x at entry on adjusted EBITDA of roughly £1.2 billion.

The thesis was compelling from a paper perspective: EMI was one of the "Big Three" music labels alongside Universal Music and Sony BMG. It had a massive catalog of intellectual property, generating substantial royalty income. The music industry was being disrupted by illegal downloading, but the consensus view (at the time) was that the shift to digital distribution would eventually stabilize and create new revenue opportunities. The leverage assumption was that EMI, post-acquisition, could cut costs aggressively (a music label is fundamentally a rights holder and distributor, not a capital-intensive manufacturer), maintain its catalog premium, and service debt from the resulting cash flows.

This thesis was not insane. It was, however, predicated on assumptions that proved catastrophically wrong.

The covenant structure reflected the sponsor's confidence. The senior facilities from Citigroup had leverage ratio maintenance covenants set at approximately 3.5x. There was a cushion, but not a massive one. The mezzanine layer had incurrence-based restrictions. The high-yield bonds had traditional incurrence covenants. The documents included customary restrictions on asset sales, but with a proviso: the sponsors could sell copyrights and catalog if proceeds were used to reduce leverage or fund operations, provided that the company maintained certain minimum EBITDA contribution thresholds.

Here's where the documentation became problematic: the definition of EBITDA in the EMI documents included highly aggressive add-backs. The sponsors and the lenders had agreed on adjustments for integration costs, severance, and "management estimates of cost savings." This last category was the killer. The EMI covenant package ultimately allowed for EBITDA adjustments based on run-rate assumptions about cost savings that hadn't yet been realized. In other words, the leverage ratio could technically comply based on pro-forma EBITDA that assumed significant operational improvements that hadn't actually happened yet.

From 2007 through 2009, EMI's business deteriorated rapidly. Digital distribution accelerated faster than expected. Major artists began transitioning away from label contracts to direct distribution via iTunes and other platforms. The recorded music industry was in fundamental structural decline. EMI's EBITDA, which had been approximately £1.2 billion at entry, fell to approximately £950 million by 2009. The leverage ratio, nominally compliant under the covenant documents (due to the aggressive EBITDA add-backs), was actually closer to 3.5x on a cash-adjusted basis.

By 2010, it was clear that EMI was in trouble. The sponsor wanted to refinance and extend maturities. Citigroup, the lead lender on the senior facility, was facing its own post-crisis constraints and was not enthusiastic about refinancing a declining music label at aggressive leverage. The negotiations became increasingly acrimonious.

Terra Firma believed that Citigroup was being unreasonable—the covenant package technically permitted the company to access a refinancing at certain spreads under the documents. Citigroup believed that the actual operational performance of the business had deteriorated to the point where lending at the existing leverage was imprudent regardless of the covenant package. This disagreement exploded into one of the most significant LBO disputes of the financial crisis: the Citigroup/Terra Firma arbitration.

The arbitration, which was ultimately litigated in English courts (with Terra Firma claiming Citigroup had violated its contractual obligations to refinance), centered on a fundamental question: if a loan agreement technically permits refinancing under the covenants, does the lender have an obligation to refinance, or can it refuse on credit grounds?

The English court ultimately sided with Citigroup. In a landmark judgment (Citibank NA v. EMI), the court ruled that while the loan documents were technically compliant, Citigroup had not breached its refinancing obligations because the credit fundamentals had deteriorated sufficiently to permit the lender to refuse extension. The ruling was devastating to Terra Firma's position.

The fallout was immediate. Without a Citigroup refinancing, EMI faced increasing pressure on its maturities. The senior facilities came due in 2012. The mezzanine layer, which was unsecured, was increasingly at risk. The high-yield bonds had cratered in value. Terra Firma attempted a dividend recap (essentially borrowing against future cash flows to distribute capital to sponsors), which further stressed the capital structure.

By 2011, it was clear that a distressed refinancing was inevitable. The senior lenders agreed to a restructuring in which the leverage ratio was essentially abandoned in favor of a schedule of mandatory amortization. The mezzanine lenders faced the brutal choice between accepting significant losses or accepting exchange into new junior secured debt with extended maturities. Many chose the former and took substantial write-downs.

The coup de grâce came in 2012, when Universal Music Group (backed by the French conglomerate Vivendi) began acquiring pieces of EMI's catalog at distressed prices. By 2013, EMI had been broken apart. Universal acquired the recorded music division (the primary EBITDA-generating asset). Sony purchased publishing rights. The whole was worth substantially less than the sum of its parts, and the leverage that had seemed reasonable in 2007 was absurdly pessimistic when applied to the fragmenting residue of the company.

From a documentation perspective, EMI taught the leveraged finance market several crucial lessons that transformed how we draft covenant packages. First, EBITDA adjustments based on "management estimates" of cost savings are extremely dangerous. Post-EMI, most lenders require that add-backs be explicitly itemized and documented, with supporting evidence of realizability. Second, covenant packages that technically permit refinancing at stated spreads can be inadequate if credit fundamentals deteriorate rapidly. Modern documents now include explicit market failure clauses and spread protection—if a refinancing cannot be achieved at market spreads, the borrower has explicit remedies (usually additional amortization, covenant loosening, or reduced distributions).

Third, and most subtly, EMI demonstrated that a declining business model cannot be leveraged, no matter how strong the "quality" of the underlying IP. The sponsors believed that EMI's catalog ownership created a long-term value proposition. The market disagreed. When the business model is being disrupted, leverage exacerbates the problem rather than solving it. This is one of the hardest lessons for sponsors to internalize—sometimes the answer is not to lever up a mature business but to recognize that the business is fundamentally challenged and structure accordingly.

The ultimate loss to creditors was substantial. Equity holders lost essentially everything. The high-yield bond holders lost approximately 80% of face value. Even the mezzanine lenders, despite their seniority, lost roughly 40-50%. Only the senior facilities, due to their priority and Citigroup's aggressive collections efforts, recovered par. The deal was a genuine wipeout.

From a market perspective, EMI shifted sponsor behavior. The deal demonstrated that even sophisticated sponsors with long track records could misjudge business model sustainability. The legal dispute also shifted how lenders approach refinancing obligations in credit agreements. Modern documents now are much more explicit about the consequences of significant credit deterioration and much more protective of lender discretion.

## 4. HERTZ EUROPE — THE CROSS-BORDER AUTO RENTAL COMPLEXITY

The Hertz Europe transaction, which occurred in 2006 as Carlyle and Apollo Global Management acquired Hertz's European operations (carved out from Ford), is a fascinating case study in how operational complexity, cross-border financing, and seasonal cash flow dynamics can create leverage challenges even in what appears to be a straightforward business.

Hertz's European footprint was substantial: operations in the UK, Germany, France, Spain, Italy, and the Benelux countries. The purchase price for the European carve-out was approximately €1.85 billion. The debt package was complex, totaling approximately €1.4 billion in leverage. This consisted of a senior secured facility of approximately €750 million (split across term loans in euros and sterling), a mezzanine layer of approximately €400 million (structured as a subordinated facility with limited covenants), and a high-yield bond of approximately €250 million denominated in euros.

With approximately €200 million in sponsor equity, the leverage ratio at entry was approximately 2.2x on adjusted EBITDA of around €650 million. On the surface, this looked conservative. The reality was far more complex.

The fundamental operational challenge with Hertz Europe was that it was being carved out of Ford's operations, and the carve-out was messy. Ford had been financing Hertz operations through Ford Credit, a captive finance operation. As part of the acquisition, Hertz Europe had to establish independent treasury, systems, and financing operations. This transition period, which was supposed to last six to twelve months, essentially consumed the first two years of the LBO.

More importantly, the business itself had significant seasonal dynamics. During European summer (June through September), rental demand was extraordinarily high, creating seasonal working capital requirements. During winter, the fleet had to be constantly refreshed (rental car operations require continuous capital expenditure to maintain vehicle quality). The EBITDA generation, while stable on an annual basis, was highly concentrated in three to four months.

The debt package attempted to address this through a revolving credit facility within the senior facilities. The revolver was sized at approximately €120 million, with a seasonal step-up provision allowing for increases during peak summer months. The thought was that the company could draw on the revolver during the off-season to fund fleet additions and operations, then repay during summer when cash was abundant.

In practice, this created constant tension with the senior lenders. The covenant package included a leverage ratio test, but it was measured quarterly. A massive seasonal draw on the revolver in Q1/Q2 could technically trigger leverage covenant violations even though the company was fundamentally sound. The documents included seasonal step-up provisions for the leverage ratio (typically allowing 3.25x in Q2 versus 3.0x in other quarters), but these were blunt instruments that didn't perfectly align with the actual cash flow profile.

The asset-based lending (ABL) component of the senior facilities was similarly problematic. Hertz Europe had inventory of rental vehicles, which could theoretically be financed on a revolving basis. The documents included a vehicle schedule and a formula-based borrowing base dependent on fleet utilization. But what the sponsors discovered was that European lenders were uncomfortable with vehicle-based ABL at the scale Hertz required. In North America, vehicle leasing and ABL financing is a mature market. In Europe, it was novel.

The high-yield bond added another layer of complexity. The bonds were issued in euros but were targeted at a global investor base. The documentation included traditional high-yield indenture protections (restrictions on additional debt, asset sales, affiliate transactions), but the documents were somewhat inconsistent with the senior facilities on how "excess cash flow" should be applied. The high-yield bonds had mandatory amortization provisions requiring 50% of excess cash flow to be applied to amortization once leverage fell below 2.0x. The senior facilities had no such requirement—they explicitly permitted unrestricted distributions once leverage was below 2.5x. This created a classic first-lien/second-lien tension: both claimed a right to excess cash flow, and the answer depended on whether the company was above or below certain leverage thresholds.

From 2006 through 2008, Hertz Europe actually performed relatively well. The sponsors brought in experienced auto rental management (including executives from Enterprise and Avis). The carve-out transition was eventually completed. The seasonal working capital management, while creating constant tension with lenders, was generally executed without covenant violations.

Then came 2008-2009. Rental demand collapsed. Economic weakness in continental Europe was severe. Corporates stopped taking business travel. Leisure travel declined. The utilization rates of rental cars fell by 15-20% compared to historical averages.

Here's where the structure revealed its fragility. Hertz Europe had absolutely committed to maintaining fleet size. The purchase agreement with Ford had included a commitment to maintain certain vehicle inventory levels. This was a competitive necessity—if they reduced fleet too rapidly, other rental companies would capture market share. But maintaining fleet in a downturn consumed enormous cash.

The seasonal working capital facility that had been designed to smooth out the business cycle now became insufficient. In normal times, summer would generate enough cash to repay seasonal borrowings plus fund fleet additions. In the downturn, summer was no longer generating the excess cash. The revolver, which was supposed to be seasonal, became effectively permanent, with utilization staying above €80 million year-round.

By 2009, the leverage ratio had crept up to approximately 2.8x. The senior lenders were uncomfortable but not yet in breach. The mezzanine lenders, however, were increasingly nervous. Mezzanine debt in a seasonal, cyclical business that had deteriorated to nearly maximum leverage wasn't an attractive position.

The sponsors made a strategic decision to significantly reduce fleet in 2009-2010, accepting near-term market share loss in exchange for cash flow preservation. This was operationally painful—Hertz Europe closed rental locations in secondary markets, reduced headcount, and effectively ceded significant share to Avis and Enterprise. But it worked. By 2010, utilization had stabilized, the seasonal working capital requirements had normalized, and leverage had begun declining.

The 2011-2012 recovery was substantial. European demand for car rentals recovered. Hertz Europe executed a successful refinancing in 2011, extending maturities on the senior facilities and executing a tender offer on the high-yield bonds at a significant discount. The mezzanine lenders, who had accepted rough treatment in 2009-2010, negotiated for improved terms and longer maturities in exchange for continued subordination.

By 2013, Hertz Europe was sufficiently deleveraged and profitable that the sponsors executed a dividend recap. Roughly €250 million was borrowed against future cash flows, with net proceeds distributed to sponsors as a partial exit. By 2015, the sponsors had essentially exited through a combination of dividend recaps, and the company was sold in a secondary transaction to another sponsor (Tilman Fertitta) at approximately 5.5x entry leverage. The sponsors' equity returns were approximately 1.8x gross.

Hertz Europe teaches important lessons about operational complexity and leverage. First, when you're transitioning a carve-out, lenders need to build in explicit assumptions about the transition period. The Hertz documents had transition provisions, but they were insufficient for the complexity of separating Ford Credit financing relationships. Second, seasonal businesses require more sophisticated working capital documentation than traditional LBO papers. Third, cyclical business models can be leveraged, but the covenant architecture needs to explicitly contemplate downturns and provide explicit management flexibility or covenant relief rather than creating rigid thresholds that force deleveraging in exactly the periods when the business is weakest.

## 5. SELECTA — THE VENDING MACHINE RESTRUCTURING CYCLE

Selecta, the European vending machine and coffee service operator, represents a different category of case study: not a dramatic blow-up or a spectacular success, but a business that cycled through multiple sponsor-era crises and restructurings, ultimately teaching hard lessons about sponsor leverage and distressed restructurings in service-oriented businesses.

Selecta was originally acquired in 2005 by BC Partners in a fairly straightforward LBO for approximately €1.0 billion. The leverage at entry was approximately 2.5x EBITDA. The covenant structure was traditional—leverage ratio maintenance covenants at approximately 3.5x, incurrence-based additional debt restrictions, and customary operational covenants.

From 2005 through 2007, Selecta performed as expected. The business was fundamentally simple: place vending machines in offices, schools, and public spaces; stock them with beverages, snacks, and coffee products; and collect coins and provide maintenance. It was cash generative and stable, if unglamorous. The sponsor captured significant dividends during this period, reducing equity at risk from approximately €300 million to roughly €150 million.

Then came the 2008 financial crisis. For a vending machine operator, the crisis was moderately painful but not catastrophic. Offices consolidating or closing impacted vending density. But the business remained fundamentally stable—people still needed coffee and snacks. The leverage ratio increased as EBITDA contracted by roughly 10-15%, but remained in covenant compliance.

The real problem was that BC Partners' fund was aging. By 2011-2012, the partners were looking to exit investments. Selecta was generating consistent cash flows, but the sponsors wanted a realization event. They explored a sale, but the market wasn't particularly receptive to acquiring a vending machine operator at premium leverage. So BC Partners executed what would become a fateful decision: they sold Selecta to another sponsor, PAI Partners, in a secondary transaction.

The 2012 sale price was approximately €1.2 billion. PAI financed the acquisition with approximately €900 million in debt, roughly 25% from the existing BC Partners leverage and roughly 75% new debt financing. The leverage ratio at entry to PAI was approximately 2.3x, slightly lower than the original BC entry on a headline basis. But structurally, the capital structure had changed: the existing BC Partners lenders had been mostly paid off and replaced with new lenders. This created exactly the kind of debt turnover that can cause problems—the new lenders had different risk tolerances, different documentation preferences, and less familiarity with the business.

PAI's strategy was to aggressively grow Selecta. They identified opportunities in adjacent geographies and started acquiring regional vending operators across Europe. By 2015, PAI had deployed approximately €400 million in acquisitions, funded partially with equity but also with new sponsor leverage. The total enterprise value of Selecta had expanded to approximately €1.8 billion, but the debt had expanded proportionally to approximately €1.3 billion. The leverage ratio had crept up to approximately 2.8x.

The growth strategy was sound in theory. Consolidating regional European vending operators made strategic sense. But it was being financed with leverage at a time when the business was inherently maturing (vending machine utilization rates were declining as more consumers brought their own beverages and snacks) and when electronic payment adoption was beginning to disrupt the traditional coin-based economics.

By 2016-2017, it was clear that the growth strategy wasn't delivering sufficient returns. EBITDA growth was flat. The leverage ratio was stuck at approximately 2.8-3.0x. And then came a completely different shock: COVID-19 in early 2020.

For a vending machine operator, COVID was catastrophic. Offices shut down. Schools shut down. Vending machine revenue collapsed by approximately 60% during lockdowns. The business, which had been generating approximately €400 million in EBITDA pre-COVID, fell to approximately €160 million during peak lockdown. The leverage ratio, which had been approximately 2.9x in early 2020, spiked to approximately 6.8x by mid-2020.

This is where Selecta's story becomes an important case study in sponsor leverage and restructuring. Unlike some fully-levered businesses that could not survive such a shock, Selecta had a sponsor with meaningful access to capital and a willingness to support the restructuring. PAI Partners, despite not having an exit, committed to investing additional equity to stabilize the capital structure.

The restructuring in 2020-2021 was complex. The first step was a covenant amendment. The leverage ratio covenants, which nominally would have been in breach by miles, were waived for 2020-2021 and replaced with a gross leverage cap of 6.5x (which was still breached but allowed for negotiated compliance rather than hard default). The second step was a mezzanine financing round, where specialized restructuring lenders committed approximately €150 million in new capital at higher rates and with significant equity kickers (warrants and direct equity ownership).

The third step was operational restructuring. Selecta accelerated its shift to electronic payment systems (cards, mobile payment) and away from coin-based revenue. The mix shifted dramatically—by 2021, electronic payments represented roughly 40% of revenue versus 15% in 2019. This reduced handling costs and improved cash flow despite lower vending volumes.

By 2021-2022, as offices reopened and restaurants reopened, Selecta's business began recovering. EBITDA climbed back to approximately €350 million by 2022. The leverage ratio, which had spiked to nearly 7x, had fallen back to approximately 2.5x by late 2022. The mezzanine lenders, who had invested at distressed prices in 2020-2021, were positioned for attractive returns as the leverage fell.

In 2023, PAI Partners executed an exit of Selecta through a secondary sale to Schultze Asset Management, a specialist distressed/dislocation investor, at a significant discount to the 2012 entry price. The sponsors' equity returns on the PAI-era investment were approximately 0.8x, representing a modest loss on the acquisition and growth investments, partially offset by the interim dividends captured.

Selecta teaches several critical lessons. First, service-oriented businesses with low capital intensity and stable cash flows can be re-leveraged through multiple sponsor cycles, but each cycle adds complexity and reduces equity cushion. Second, when a sponsor exits and is replaced with another sponsor at leveraged prices, the new lender base is less forgiving of deterioration—they haven't built relationships or confidence over cycles. Third, COVID demonstrated that even seemingly defensive businesses (people still need coffee) can face near-total revenue shock in tail-risk scenarios. Fourth, and most importantly, sponsor support is essential in extreme distress. Selecta survived COVID because PAI was willing to inject capital despite not having an exit. Many other leveraged companies in similarly challenging positions were left to reorganize in bankruptcy because sponsors lacked capital or conviction.

## 6. GALAPAGOS — THE DROP-DOWN DISPUTE

Galapagos NV, the Belgian biopharmaceutical company, is a fascinating and somewhat unusual case study in leveraged finance because it wasn't technically an LBO—the company was public and remained public. But the leveraged financing transaction embedded within its capital structure, specifically the controversial "drop-down" transaction executed in 2018-2019, became one of the most significant documentation and creditor-on-creditor disputes in recent European history.

Galapagos had been a relatively small biopharmaceutical company focused on fibrosis, inflammation, and immunology. By 2016-2017, the company had developed a promising pipeline but faced a fundamental challenge: it needed enormous capital for clinical trials and regulatory work, but it didn't have sufficient cash flow to self-fund development. The company had some debt on its balance sheet and was exploring strategic alternatives.

In 2017, Galapagos made a strategic decision to acquire Fibrotech Pharma, a private company focused on fibrosis therapies. The acquisition was funded with a combination of equity and debt. This is where the leverage structure became interesting. To finance the Fibrotech acquisition and fund continued development, Galapagos raised approximately €500 million in senior secured term loans and bonds. The facilities included a typical pharmaceutical company capital structure: a senior term loan facility and high-yield bonds with first-lien security over the Fibrotech assets and second-lien security on certain other company assets.

The covenant structure was relatively sophisticated for a public company. There was a leverage ratio test at approximately 2.5x (which was loose for a biotech company but reflected the speculative nature of the development-stage pipeline). The documents included customary incurrence-based restrictions on additional debt and asset sales, but with an important carve-out: the company could enter into drop-down transactions that moved assets from the parent company into Fibrotech (the wholly-owned subsidiary) without triggering change-of-control provisions or requiring lender consent, provided certain baseline conditions were met.

This drop-down provision, which was standard in leveraged pharmaceutical financing, became the center of a massive controversy.

By early 2019, Galapagos' pipeline had advanced considerably, and Jansen Pharmaceuticals (a division of Johnson & Johnson) became interested in a partnership. The partnership structure involved Galapagos licensing certain compounds to Jansen in exchange for milestone and royalty payments. The deal was worth approximately €1.0 billion upfront and up to €2.3 billion in additional milestones.

Here's where the transaction became contentious. Rather than having the Fibrotech subsidiary enter into the Jansen partnership and retain the milestone and royalty economics, Galapagos structured the transaction so that the parent company (Galapagos NV) entered into the Jansen deal and licensed compounds from Fibrotech to Jansen, with the upfront payment and milestones flowing to the parent company rather than to Fibrotech.

From a tax and corporate perspective, this structure made sense—it concentrated value in the parent company, which simplified eventual M&A or strategic transactions. From a debt perspective, it was devastating to the secured lenders. The upfront payment of €1.0 billion that would have been available to reduce Fibrotech leverage was instead flowing to the parent company, where it could be used for general corporate purposes, distributed to shareholders, or invested in new ventures.

The secured lenders (who held first-lien security on Fibrotech's assets) argued that this structure violated the economic intent of their documentation. They contended that key intellectual property and rights that were supposed to be within the Fibrotech "box" (which was pledged to them) had been effectively moved to the parent company, where they had no security.

The dispute erupted into litigation in 2019. The lenders argued that the licensing arrangement constituted a "drop-down" of material assets that should have required consent and should have triggered additional financial conditions (potentially including prepayment obligations). Galapagos and the unsecured parent company lenders argued that the documents explicitly permitted the kind of transaction that had occurred and that the lenders' concerns about value migration were already contemplated in the covenant structure.

The dispute was ultimately resolved through negotiated settlement rather than outright litigation victory. Galapagos agreed to commit approximately €250 million of the Jansen upfront payment toward reduction of Fibrotech leverage. The secured lenders agreed to amend certain documentation to clarify their rights in future transactions. Both parties agreed that certain categories of intellectual property developments would require notification to lenders, even if explicit consent wasn't required.

But the real impact was on the market and on documentation. The Galapagos dispute revealed a massive gap in how drop-down provisions were drafted. Most documents, including Galapagos', had drop-down carve-outs that assumed assets would remain within the same operating company or would be formally transferred down but remain pledged as collateral. The Galapagos transaction demonstrated that sponsors could accomplish meaningful economic drop-downs without formally moving assets—by simply having parent company assets (IP) generate cash flows that benefited parent company lenders instead of subsidiary lenders.

In the aftermath, European debt documentation shifted dramatically. Modern drop-down provisions now include explicit categories of restricted activities: asset sales are prohibited below certain thresholds; licensing of material IP requires lender notification and is subject to fair-value thresholds; cash flows generated by subsidiary assets cannot be diverted to parent company activities without triggering prepayment or covenant relief.

The Galapagos case also highlighted a subtle but important issue in European leveraged finance: the rights of secured creditors in non-U.S. jurisdictions. In the U.S., secured creditors have very explicit rights under UCC Article 9 to foreclose on pledged assets and to control the use and disposition of collateral. In European jurisdictions, security laws vary significantly. Belgian law, under which Galapagos was organized, provides secured creditors with meaningful rights, but the enforcement mechanisms and protections are meaningfully different from the U.S.

This became important in how the market ultimately settled the dispute: European lenders learned that security over assets isn't as clear-cut as it might seem under U.S. standards, and that documentation needs to be far more explicit about which assets are pledged, how they can be used, and what constitutes a material change in the collateral package.

## 7. ADLER GROUP — THE DOCUMENTATION VACUUM

Adler Group, the German residential real estate company, is one of the most instructive examples of how documentation can be catastrophically insufficient when combined with aggressive accounting and operational opacity. The company imploded in spectacular fashion in 2021-2022, wiping out equity, heavily impairing debt, and revealing massive gaps in how European leveraged finance documents protect creditors against accounting manipulation and valuation fraud.

Adler Group was formed in 2019 through a merger of two German residential real estate companies, Adler Real Estate and Consus Real Estate. The combined entity was highly leveraged: approximately €1.9 billion in gross debt on an enterprise value of roughly €5.0 billion. This represented leverage of approximately 3.2x on estimated EBITDA. The company was public (listed on the German exchange) but had meaningful private debt.

The capital structure consisted of senior secured bank facilities, unsecured bonds, convertible bonds, and significant sponsor equity. The covenant structure, from a real estate perspective, was based on loan-to-value (LTV) ratios and interest coverage. The leverage ratio test wasn't a traditional EBITDA-based ratio (common in operating companies) but rather an LTV ratio: the company needed to maintain LTV below approximately 60% based on the value of its German residential real estate portfolio.

Here's where the documentation catastrophically failed: the valuation methodology for the portfolio was essentially self-assessed. Adler had a valuation committee comprised of senior management and board members, who annually determined the value of the property portfolio for accounting and covenant purposes. The documentation didn't require independent third-party valuation. The documents included a vague requirement for "arms-length valuation methodologies" and "market-based assumptions," but there was no external verification, no requirement for appraisals by independent valuers, and no mechanism for lenders to challenge valuations they believed were unrealistic.

This created an obvious incentive problem. As leverage increased and LTV ratios tightened, Adler's management faced a choice: de-lever the company by reducing debt, or maintain leverage by reporting higher property values. Unsurprisingly, they chose the latter.

From 2019 through early 2021, Adler reported steadily increasing property valuations. The portfolio, which had been valued at approximately €3.8 billion at the merger, was reported at approximately €4.3 billion by the end of 2020. This value increase was driven by management estimates of rental growth, portfolio improvements, and market appreciation. It provided the rationale for maintaining relatively stable LTV ratios despite increasing debt and decreasing earnings.

In early 2021, a German short-seller published a detailed report questioning Adler's valuations and claiming that the company had systematically overstated property values. The report was technically sophisticated, analyzing comparable rental yields, market rents, and occupancy rates to estimate that the actual portfolio value was approximately 15-20% below Adler's reported values. The short-seller further alleged that certain transactions between Adler and related parties had been structured to facilitate related-party value transfers.

The initial market reaction was skeptical. Real estate valuations are inherently subjective, and short-seller reports are notoriously biased. But the allegations prompted a regulatory investigation by German authorities and investigations by auditors. By mid-2021, it became clear that significant portions of the short-seller's allegations were correct.

The company had systematically inflated property values through aggressive assumptions about rental growth and capital appreciation. More damaging, it had executed a series of related-party transactions that had inflated balance sheet values without creating genuine economic value. For example, Adler had purchased properties from related parties at prices significantly above market comparables, then revalued the properties downward once they were on the consolidated balance sheet. The timing of these transactions appeared designed to manage earnings and valuations.

By late 2021, the truth became undeniable. Adler's actual property portfolio was valued at approximately €3.2 billion, not the €4.3 billion reported. This represented an LTV ratio of approximately 65-70%, well above covenant limits. The company was in technical default.

The aftermath was chaotic. Management resigned. The board faced shareholder litigation. The leverage appeared unsustainable. By early 2022, the company had entered into restructuring negotiations with its creditors. The senior secured lenders faced a loss of approximately 40-50% of their exposure. The unsecured bondholders faced a write-down from par to approximately 20-30 cents on the dollar.

The ultimate restructuring plan, executed in mid-2022, involved a significant debt-for-equity conversion. The senior secured lenders converted approximately 60% of their holdings into new equity in a restructured entity. The unsecured bondholders converted their holdings into junior equity at substantially lower valuations. Existing equity holders were essentially wiped out, with nominal new shares in the restructured company but minimal economic value.

The documentation failures were catastrophic. The covenant package relied entirely on self-assessed valuations without independent verification. The documents provided no mechanism for lenders to challenge valuations or to require third-party appraisals. There were no covenant triggers based on external ratings or market-based valuation metrics. The documents included provisions restricting "affiliated transactions," but these were drafted with enough flexibility that the transactions Adler ultimately executed were technically compliant.

In the aftermath, the European leveraged finance market fundamentally changed how it documents real estate-backed leverage. Modern documents now require:

(1) Independent third-party appraisals for any valuation-based covenant testing, with specified appraisal standards and qualified appraisers;

(2) Explicit valuation haircuts or "downsize factors" in how properties are valued for covenant purposes (e.g., portfolio value is the lower of management valuation or 90% of independent appraisal);

(3) Detailed restrictions on related-party transactions, with specific pricing requirements and lender notification;

(4) Regular covenant monitoring meetings with external verification of underlying assumptions;

(5) Explicit remedy provisions if valuations change materially (e.g., automatic covenant adjustment if external appraisals diverge from assumptions by more than 10%).

Adler remains one of the most significant lessons in how documentation complacency can create massive creditor losses. The company didn't fail because of market movements or even operational challenges. It failed because the documentation was insufficient to prevent management from systematically misrepresenting asset values.

## 8. CASINO GUICHARD — CREDITOR WARFARE

Casino Guichard-Perrachon is one of Europe's largest retail groups, a French conglomerate with operations across multiple geographies and formats. The company had undergone multiple LBO iterations and structural reorganizations, resulting in a capital structure of breathtaking complexity. The 2020-2022 restructuring, which I was directly involved in as a debt advisor, became a masterclass in creditor-on-creditor warfare, documentation exploitation, and the limits of leverage in the face of operational decline.

The Casino leverage history is tortured. The company had been taken private and public multiple times. By the early 2010s, it had been recapitalized multiple times with sponsor equity and debt. The capital structure included approximately €2.8 billion in debt across multiple tranches: senior secured bank facilities, senior unsecured bonds, subordinated bonds, and convertible bonds, all with different maturity dates, covenant packages, and creditor groups.

The fundamental operational challenge was that Casino was a struggling French and Brazilian retailer facing increasing competition from e-commerce and hypermarket consolidation. EBITDA had been declining steadily through the 2010s. The leverage ratio, which had been approximately 2.5x in 2015, had climbed to approximately 3.5x by 2018 and approximately 4.0x by 2020.

The covenant structure was a patchwork reflecting decades of amendments and restructurings. The senior facilities had leverage ratio maintenance covenants, but these had been waived or amended multiple times. The unsecured bonds had less restrictive covenants but had maturity cliffs in 2021-2022 that the company obviously couldn't refinance. The subordinated bonds and convertibles had minimal maintenance covenants but had change-of-control triggers that could be activated if equity holders exited.

By late 2020, it was clear that a restructuring was inevitable. The COVID pandemic had devastated retail, particularly in France where the company had significant exposure. The company had implemented a sustainability plan that included massive asset sales and restructuring. But the debt maturity profile was unmanageable.

Here's where Casino became an absolute battle. The company and its advisors faced a choice: execute a large-scale liability management exercise (LME) with the support of major creditor groups, or face bankruptcy. The company chose the LME route, which required negotiating with multiple creditor groups with fundamentally different interests.

The senior lenders, represented by a committee of bank lenders, wanted to be made whole or nearly whole. They had priority security but faced the reality that Casino's operating assets weren't worth enough to pay everyone. The unsecured bondholders had more junior claims but had significant holdings and substantial voting power in any restructuring. The subordinated creditors faced the prospect of being wiped out entirely. The equity holders, which included sponsor holdings and public shareholders, faced potential elimination.

The negotiations were brutal. The senior lenders initially proposed a comprehensive restructuring in which debt would be significantly reduced and equity would absorb losses. The unsecured bondholders proposed an alternative in which certain assets would be sold, proceeds used to pay down debt on a pro-rata basis, and equity would retain some value. The subordinated creditors fought for protection against pari passu or senior creditor priming.

One of the most contentious issues was the treatment of "anchor" assets. Casino owned several valuable real estate properties, shopping centers, and hypermarkets. The senior lenders wanted these sold in a broad portfolio sale, with proceeds used to reduce leverage on a first-lien basis. The unsecured bondholders argued that asset sales should be structured to generate maximum value and that proceeds should be distributed on a pro-rata basis across all creditors, not used to preferentially reduce senior debt.

The dispute escalated when the company explored a "priming" transaction in early 2021. The sponsors and senior lenders proposed that newly raised secured financing (from a specialized restructuring lender) would be provided with super-senior priority—meaning it would have security ahead of the existing first-lien bank lenders. This is an extraordinary remedy, typically used only in bankruptcy to facilitate DIP financing. In a negotiated restructuring, priming is tantamount to saying that existing lenders will be subordinated to new capital.

The existing unsecured bondholders were enraged. A priming transaction would essentially mean that both the existing first-lien lenders AND the new secured lender would have priority ahead of them. The unsecured bondholders' effective seniority would be dramatically reduced.

The dispute nearly broke the restructuring. At one point in April 2021, negotiations appeared to have failed entirely, and the company was exploring bankruptcy as an alternative. The bankruptcy threat, paradoxically, is what ultimately allowed a deal to be reached—the prospect of a disorderly bankruptcy and the certainty of even greater losses convinced all parties that a negotiated outcome, however painful, was preferable.

The ultimate restructuring, announced in mid-2021 and implemented through 2022, involved:

(1) A debt-for-equity conversion in which approximately €1.0 billion of unsecured bonds were converted into new equity in a restructured entity;

(2) A refinancing of the senior bank facilities at significantly reduced amounts and with extended maturities;

(3) Asset sales aggregating approximately €600 million, with proceeds distributed according to a waterfall that gave priority to senior lenders but allocated a meaningful portion to unsecured creditors;

(4) A modest equity injection from sponsors to show good faith commitment;

(5) New working capital facilities from specialized restructuring lenders, provided with senior security but subordinated to existing first-lien facilities.

The process was extraordinarily contentious. There were multiple negotiation rounds, several points where the process appeared likely to break down, and significant creditor dissatisfaction among all constituencies. The unsecured bondholders, who had been trading at approximately 30-40 cents on the dollar at the outset of restructuring discussions, ultimately recovered approximately 50-60 cents through the combination of the debt-for-equity conversion and proceeds from asset sales. The senior lenders recovered approximately 85-90% of par through the combination of reduced debt levels and continuing operations.

Casino teaches several crucial lessons about creditor protection and documentation. First, when a business is genuinely in distress and the asset base won't cover all claims, creditor conflicts are inevitable. Documentation can't prevent these conflicts, but it can structure them so that they're resolved more efficiently. Second, priming transactions, while occasionally necessary, are extremely dangerous from a documentation perspective because they can reverse the seniority that creditors thought they had. Modern documents now include explicit "no priming" provisions or "pro-rata sharing" provisions that prevent senior secured creditors from being subordinated without consent. Third, asset sale proceeds need to have explicitly defined waterfalls in the documentation—leaving these to negotiation in a distressed scenario simply invites protracted disputes.

The Casino restructuring also revealed the limits of European insolvency law. In the U.S., bankruptcy provides a clear mechanism for resolving creditor disputes: the absolute priority rule and the cram-down provisions ensure that court-confirmed plans are enforceable. European jurisdictions don't have equivalent mechanisms. The Casino restructuring required unanimous or near-unanimous creditor consent because there was no bankruptcy alternative that could force a plan down the throats of unwilling creditors. This asymmetry means that European LBOs with multiple creditor classes need much more robust documentation around payment waterfalls and creditor rights.

## 9. SERTA SIMMONS — THE UPTIER PRECEDENT

Serta Simmons Bedding is a U.S. company, but its capital structure maneuvers in 2019-2021 fundamentally altered how European lawyers draft debt documents. The reason is straightforward: Serta Simmons executed a transaction that was explicitly permitted under the documentation but that was so egregiously unfair to term loan holders that it prompted immediate legislative and documentary reform. Every European debt lawyer now drafts with Serta Simmons in mind.

The context: Serta Simmons was an LBO of a mattress manufacturer and distributor. It had been through multiple sponsors and restructurings. By 2019, the company had a capital structure of approximately $1.7 billion in debt: senior secured term loans of approximately $1.0 billion, mezzanine debt of approximately $400 million, and high-yield bonds of approximately $300 million.

The relevant provision in the term loan documentation was an "open market purchase" provision. This is a standard feature in leveraged loan agreements that allows the borrower to repurchase its own debt in the open market at par or less. For example, if a term loan is trading at 95 cents on the dollar, the borrower can purchase it at 95 and retire it, reducing total leverage. The provision is designed to allow management flexibility to optimize the capital structure.

But Serta's documentation included a troubling twist: the open market purchase provision explicitly allowed the borrower to repurchase its term loans and use the "gain on extinguishment" (the difference between par and the purchase price) to reduce the leverage ratio for covenant testing purposes.

Here's why this is a problem: assume a term loan is trading at 95, the borrower repurchases and retires it, and calculates that the "gain" is the 5 cents difference between par and the purchase price. For covenant testing purposes, this gain is treated as if it reduced leverage. In other words, the covenant test becomes: (Debt - Gain) / EBITDA rather than Debt / EBITDA.

In 2019, Serta's management and the equity holders decided to execute a "uptier" transaction. Here's what happened: a group of banks (the "uptier lenders") agreed to provide new capital—approximately $250 million in new term loans at a higher tranche in the capital structure (more senior than the existing term loans in certain respects). The uptier lenders would lend money to the borrower, who would then use the proceeds to repurchase existing term loans at a steep discount (approximately 70-75 cents on the dollar). The repurchased term loans would be retired.

From a purely economic perspective, this looks neutral. The company raises $250 million in new debt and uses it to retire $250 million (at par) of existing debt. The net debt is unchanged.

But here's where the documentation explicitly created moral hazard: the "gain on extinguishment" from retiring debt at a 25-30 cent discount would be approximately $60-75 million. Under the term loan documentation, this gain could be used to reduce the leverage ratio for covenant testing purposes. In other words:

- New leverage = (Existing Debt + New Uptier Debt - Gain) / EBITDA
- Or approximately = (Existing Debt - Gain) / EBITDA (since the new uptier debt would repay the original term loans)

The existing term loan holders (sometimes called the "stuck lenders" because they couldn't be easily refinanced or exited) were enraged. They argued that the uptier transaction was directly contradicted by the economic intent of the covenant package. The provision allowing "gains on extinguishment" was supposed to apply when the borrower used cash flow to repurchase debt at a discount (creating a one-time benefit that could flow through the income statement). It was explicitly NOT supposed to apply to transactions in which new leverage was raised for the specific purpose of manufacturing a "gain."

The existing term loan holders sued. The litigation was ultimately resolved through a settlement in which existing lenders accepted a modest price increase for their loans (approximately 2-3 additional cents) in exchange for agreeing to the transaction. But the reputational damage was enormous.

More importantly, the litigation spurred immediate legislative and documentary reform. The term loan market, through working groups at the Loan Syndications and Trading Association (LSTA) and in Europe through the European Leveraged Finance Association (ELFA), began drafting explicit carve-outs to prevent "synthetic" gains on extinguishment from being used for covenant testing.

The reform was straightforward: gains on extinguishment could only be recognized if (i) the debt was repurchased using borrowed funds raised in a financing transaction that independently met credit standards (i.e., the uptier lenders had to have underwritten the transaction without regard to the gain benefit), or (ii) the debt was repurchased using free cash flow, or (iii) the debt was repurchased at significantly tighter spreads than the original loan (signaling that credit had genuinely improved).

For European documents, the Serta reaction was particularly important because European sponsors and counsel were increasingly looking at U.S. debt markets for precedent. When the LSTA updated its template loan agreement to restrict synthetic gain transactions, European counsel incorporated these restrictions into their own documents. By 2021, almost every new European leveraged loan facility included explicit carve-outs preventing the kind of uptier transaction that Serta had executed.

Serta Simmons teaches a subtle but crucial lesson: documentation that looks economically neutral can actually create enormous moral hazard if the documentation allows management to manipulate the metrics by which the debt is tested. The gain on extinguishment provision wasn't inherently bad; it just needed guardrails. Modern documentation includes these guardrails almost universally.

## WHAT TWENTY YEARS TEACHES

I've spent two decades in this business, and I've witnessed deals that worked exactly as planned, deals that blew up in spectacular fashion, and deals that limped along in gray zones of distress without ever formally defaulting. If I'm honest about what this experience teaches, it's a collection of hard-won insights that I wish I'd understood at the beginning of my career.

First, the most important lesson: leverage is a tool for amplifying business outcomes, not a substitute for business quality. TDC worked because the underlying telecom business, after cost rationalization, generated sufficient cash flows to service debt. Boots worked for the same reason—the retail business was stable and mature. EMI failed not because the leverage was too high in absolute terms but because the underlying business model was deteriorating faster than any leverage assumptions could accommodate. No documentation, no covenant structure, no covenant relief mechanism can fix an underlying business that's broken.

Second, sponsor discipline matters far more than I initially appreciated. The TDC consortium moved together. The Boots sponsors maintained operational focus rather than immediately pivoting to financial engineering. By contrast, sponsors who are desperate to maximize near-term returns (through aggressive dividend recaps, for example) tend to create situations where small operational deteriorations trigger refinancing crises that should have been manageable. Covenant waivers, extensions, and amendments are normal in leveraged finance. What's not normal is having to execute them within six months of entry because the sponsors captured all the equity value up front and left no cushion for operational volatility.

Third, documentation matters, but not in the way most lawyers think. The goal of debt documentation isn't to create an impenetrable fortress that prevents strategic maneuvers. That's impossible, and it's also not desirable—legitimate operational decisions and strategic transactions shouldn't be locked out by overly restrictive documentation. The goal of documentation is to create clarity about what constitutes a material change in risk profile and to provide explicit mechanisms for addressing those changes. Galapagos would have been a far cleaner dispute if the documentation had explicitly addressed how IP licensing transactions would be handled. Adler would have been a disaster either way, but the losses would have been smaller if valuations had been independently verified. Serta's uptier transaction could have been prevented with tighter definition of "gain on extinguishment."

Fourth, and this is something I learned the hard way, covenant architecture matters more than leverage levels. A 3.5x leveraged company with weak covenants and poor documentation will blow up far more readily than a 4.5x leveraged company with tight covenants and clear documentation. Covenants aren't just technical metrics; they're the mechanism by which lenders stay informed about material changes in business risk. Well-designed covenants create forcing events—moments where management has to have a conversation with lenders about what's happening and what corrective action will be taken. Weak covenants allow companies to drift slowly into distress without triggering any conversation.

Fifth, refinancing risk is more important than debt levels. A company with 2.5x leverage and five-year average maturity is far riskier than a company with 4.0x leverage and twelve-year average maturity. The reason is simple: refinancing risk is path-dependent and subject to market dynamics outside the company's control. A well-structured LBO staggers maturities so that any single refinancing only addresses a portion of the debt profile. This is one of the reasons TDC survived the financial crisis—the maturity ladder was designed so that refinancing one tranche didn't require solving the entire leverage problem simultaneously.

Sixth, service-oriented businesses and real estate businesses are structurally difficult to leverage compared to manufacturing or infrastructure assets. The reason is that service businesses depend heavily on operational execution and market access, both of which can deteriorate rapidly. Selecta survived COVID because the sponsors had capital and conviction. Many service-oriented LBOs in similar circumstances went into bankruptcy. Real estate businesses are dangerous because of valuation risk—Adler's fraud could happen in any real estate LBO if documentation doesn't require independent verification of values. Modern documentation explicitly contemplates this.

Seventh, sponsor continuity matters. Every time an LBO changes sponsors (which is increasingly common as sponsors have shorter hold periods), the capital structure risk profile changes materially. The new sponsor may not have the relationships with lenders that the previous sponsor had. The new sponsor may have a different risk tolerance. Most importantly, the new sponsor may not have the same conviction about the underlying business. Selecta's transition from BC Partners to PAI Partners created precisely this kind of friction. The new lenders were less tolerant of operational challenges. Covenant waivers and amendments that would have been straightforward under the prior sponsor became contentious under the new regime.

Eighth, and this is perhaps the most important insight: the worst distressed situations involve multiple creditor classes with fundamentally different risk profiles. In a simple leveraged capital structure with just a senior lender, an unsecured bond holder, and equity, distress is unpleasant but manageable. In a structure with senior secured first-lien, senior secured second-lien, mezzanine subordinated debt, high-yield bonds, convertibles, and complex affiliate structures, distress becomes a protracted negotiation in which everyone loses. Casino is the quintessential example: creditor-on-creditor warfare meant that the restructuring took twice as long as it should have and recovered significantly less value than a cleaner capital structure would have recovered.

Ninth, covenant waivers and amendments are not evil—they're essential tools for managing the inevitable friction between static documentation and dynamic business realities. The problem is when waivers are used to hide deterioration rather than to address it. A good covenant waiver is a forcing event: "We'll waive this covenant, but only if you (i) commit to a clear action plan to return to compliance, (ii) provide enhanced reporting, and (iii) accept covenant tightening in another area." A bad waiver is pure forbearance: "We'll waive this for twelve months and see how things evolve." Modern documentation explicitly structures the waiver and amendment process to prevent forbearance creep.

Tenth, and finally, the leverage finance business is fundamentally a long-term game. The most successful sponsors and lenders are those with a fifteen to twenty-year horizon. Sponsors with five-year hold periods inevitably make different capital allocation decisions than sponsors with ten-year holds. Lenders with five-year portfolio lives inevitably tolerate different risk levels than lenders with ten-year lives. This mismatch between sponsor and lender time horizons is one of the underlying causes of distress. A sponsor exiting at year five may make financial engineering decisions that look attractive for the near term but that create refinancing challenges in years six through ten. Modern documentation attempts to align these horizons through covenant structures and dividend restrictions that become progressively more relaxed as leverage falls and the business matures.

Twenty years in this business has taught me that leverage is neither inherently dangerous nor inherently safe. It's a tool. What determines whether it's safe is whether it's being applied to a fundamentally sound business, with competent management, with clear covenant documentation, with sponsor discipline, and with realistic expectations about how the transaction will evolve over a ten to fifteen-year period.

The deals I've been involved in that worked had all of these attributes. The deals that failed lacked at least one, often more than one. The most instructive failures are those where the documentation could have provided protection but didn't because of insufficient specificity or because of explicit carve-outs that seemed innocuous at the time but became extraordinarily problematic in distress.

This is the fundamental message I'd communicate to anyone entering leveraged finance: documentation is a tool for managing the evolution of a transaction across cycles. It's not a substitute for business quality, it's not a substitute for sponsor discipline, and it's not a substitute for realistic covenant architecture. But it is absolutely essential for managing the inevitable tensions and conflicts that emerge when a company underperforms expectations or when sponsors try to optimize for their own interests at the expense of creditors.

These nine deals—TDC, Boots, EMI, Hertz, Selecta, Galapagos, Adler, Casino, and Serta—collectively represent the lived experience of European leveraged finance over two decades. Each taught me something different.
