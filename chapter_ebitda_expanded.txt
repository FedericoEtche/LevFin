CHAPTER 18: EBITDA — THE NUMBER THAT RULES EVERYTHING
Why Understanding Adjustments Might Save Your Portfolio

18.1 INTRODUCTION — WHY EBITDA MATTERS MORE THAN YOU THINK

I remember sitting across from a CFO of a mid-market manufacturing business in 2015 when I asked him a simple question: "What's your EBITDA?"

He paused. Then he said: "Well, that depends."

I thought he was joking. EBITDA is a single number, right? Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, amortisation. There's only one answer.

But I was naïve. He spent the next forty-five minutes walking me through five different EBITDA calculations. There was "reported EBITDA," which was what the auditors signed off on. There was "covenant EBITDA," which was what the credit agreement allowed. There was "sponsor EBITDA," which was what the PE firm reported to their investors. There was "operational EBITDA," which excluded one-time charges and treated run-rate savings as if they'd already been achieved. And there was "aspirational EBITDA," which was what management believed they could achieve if everything went right.

These five numbers ranged from €18 million to €26 million. A 44% spread.

When you're running leverage calculations, covenant tests, and debt capacity analyses on a number that has this much variance, you're not really doing mathematics. You're doing storytelling. And that's when I understood that EBITDA — far more than interest rates, leverage ratios, or cash flow coverage — is the single most important and most misunderstood metric in leveraged finance.

EBITDA doesn't measure what the finance textbooks say it measures. It measures what the deal parties agreed to measure. And that agreement is the product of negotiation, convention, industry practice, and — often — optimism bordering on fiction.

Over the past two decades, I've seen EBITDA used brilliantly to structure deals that actually work, and I've seen it abused shamelessly to underwrite loans that should never have been made. I've seen adjustments that were fully justified by operational reality, and I've seen adjustments that were pure fantasy. I've sat in credit committees where the entire decision to approve a €100 million loan hinged on whether to allow a €2 million add-back for "cost synergies."

This chapter is about understanding EBITDA as it actually operates in leveraged finance — not as a financial metric, but as a negotiated construct. By the end, you'll understand not just how EBITDA is calculated, but why the numbers don't reconcile, how to detect when you're being sold a story vs. receiving actual information, and how to protect yourself when your entire thesis depends on a number that might not be what you think it is.

18.2 EBITDA DEFINED: FROM SIMPLE CONCEPT TO COMPLEX REALITY

Let me start with the basic framework and then show you why that framework collapses almost immediately in practice.

**The Textbook Definition**

EBITDA = Net Income + Interest Expense + Taxes + Depreciation + Amortisation

In other words, starting from net profit (which is the bottom-line earnings number after everything), you add back the expenses that are either non-cash (depreciation, amortisation) or financing-related (interest), or tax-related (tax expense). The idea is that you want to see the earnings generated by the business, independent of how it's financed or taxed.

This makes sense for a basic comparison. It removes the effect of financing decisions (a heavily levered company shows lower net income than an unlevered company, even if both have identical operations). It removes the effect of capital structures (an old asset base with high depreciation shows lower earnings than a new asset base, even with identical revenues). It removes tax rate differences, which vary by jurisdiction and company situation.

So in theory, EBITDA is a "pure" measure of operational performance.

**The Bridge from Net Income to EBITDA**

In practice, you construct EBITDA by working up from net income. Let me walk through a simplified example.

Company XYZ has net income of €10 million.

You add back:
- Interest expense: €4 million (financing cost)
- Tax expense: €3 million (tax cost)
- Depreciation: €2 million (non-cash operating cost)
- Amortisation: €1 million (non-cash operating cost)

EBITDA = €10m + €4m + €3m + €2m + €1m = €20m

Now, the question I'm going to ask is: Is €20 million the "true" EBITDA of Company XYZ?

The answer: Maybe. Or maybe not. It depends on what adjustments have been excluded from reported net income.

**Why the Definition Breaks Down Immediately**

Here's where it gets interesting. The definition assumes that net income is "clean" — that is, it includes only the actual operational performance of the business. But in reality, net income often includes charges that are non-recurring, unusual, or contested.

Suppose Company XYZ's net income of €10 million includes a one-time charge of €2 million for restructuring, a goodwill impairment of €1 million, and a gain on the sale of a subsidiary of €1.5 million.

Now, what's "true" EBITDA? Is it the €20 million calculated above? Or should we adjust for these items?

Most practitioners would say: Yes, you should adjust. The €2 million restructuring charge is one-time, not recurring. The €1 million impairment is non-cash and probably not repeatable. The €1.5 million gain is from an asset sale, not operations.

So you'd calculate "Adjusted EBITDA":
€20 million (calculated EBITDA) + €2 million (add back restructuring) + €1 million (add back impairment) - €1.5 million (subtract gain) = €21.5 million

But wait. Why are we subtracting the gain? We didn't add back a loss. The logic is: The gain is a one-time event that inflates earnings, so we remove it to see "recurring" earnings. But the logic is inconsistent. If you're normalising to recurring earnings, you should also add back recurring expenses that didn't occur in the period.

You see the problem immediately: EBITDA adjustment becomes a philosophy. Are we trying to measure what the company earned in the period? Or are we trying to normalise earnings to show what they might earn recurrently? Are we including one-time items that reduced earnings but excluding one-time items that increased them? Are we adding back expenses that we think are excessive, or are we sticking to a pure cash basis?

Different people answer these questions differently. That's why that CFO had five different EBITDA numbers.

**Why Cash Flow Matters More Than Profit in Leveraged Finance**

Here's the fundamental insight of leveraged finance: A company that generates €20 million of EBITDA might be able to service €15 million of debt, or it might only be able to service €5 million. The difference depends on cash conversion.

EBITDA assumes all earnings are cash earnings. But they're not. A company with significant non-cash charges (like amortisation of a large intangible), or a company with working capital requirements (more inventory, more receivables), might convert EBITDA to cash at a much lower rate than another company.

This is why, in a leveraged finance context, EBITDA serves a special purpose: It's a standardised baseline for calculating leverage ratios. Leverage = Total Debt / EBITDA. This allows you to compare companies with different tax rates, financing structures, and capital requirements on a similar basis.

But the covenant doesn't say "The company must not have excessive debt." It says "The company's leverage ratio must not exceed 4.0x." That's a mechanical statement that relies entirely on how EBITDA is defined. And since EBITDA is adjustable, it becomes a number that can be engineered.

**The Philosophy of Leverage: Covenant Tightness**

A company with 4.0x leverage (€100 million of debt, €25 million of EBITDA) is more stressed than a company with 4.0x leverage on "true" recurring cash-based EBITDA. But the covenant doesn't distinguish. It just says 4.0x.

This is where, in my experience, the most aggressive misuse of EBITDA occurs. Not necessarily fraud (though that happens too), but creative interpretation. The sponsor, the management team, and sometimes even the lender's own credit committee get aligned around a particular EBITDA definition, and suddenly things that should be questioned are accepted.

I've seen several instances of this over the years. In one case, I was reviewing a sponsor's investment thesis for a specialty retail company. The sponsor had modelled out growth assuming store productivity improvements of 15% over five years. When I asked how this assumption compared to historical performance, they said: "We're assuming the new management team will execute better."

That's fine. But then they'd incorporated the 15% productivity improvement into the "run-rate" EBITDA for covenant calculations. So in year one, even though stores hadn't actually achieved the productivity improvement yet, they were calculating leverage as if they had. This meant leverage appeared lower, covenants were easier, and the debt looked safer. But it was based on something that hadn't happened yet.

This is one example of where EBITDA adjustments can mislead.

18.3 THE ADD-BACK UNIVERSE

Let me walk through the universe of add-backs that actually appear in credit agreements, ordered from most defensible to most questionable.

**Category One: Non-Cash Charges (High Defensibility)**

These are straightforward. They're charges that reduce net income but don't represent actual cash outflows in the period.

*Depreciation and Amortisation*

These are the core add-backs. Depreciation reduces earnings because assets lose value over time, but depreciation doesn't involve a cash outflow (the cash outflow happened when the asset was purchased, in a prior period). Similarly, amortisation of intangibles reduces earnings on a non-cash basis.

In leverage calculations, you're asking: Can the company service debt from its cash-generating operations? If the cash isn't actually leaving, it's available to service debt. So you add it back.

Non-cash charges also include:
- Stock-based compensation (employees receive equity instead of cash)
- Gains or losses on derivatives, marked-to-market (non-cash until settlement)
- Deferred tax charges

These are relatively uncontroversial. Most people agree they should be added back.

*Fair Value Adjustments*

When a company revalues an asset (like property, or derivative positions) under accounting rules, this might create a gain or loss that doesn't involve cash. Many credit agreements allow adding these back.

This gets more subjective. Is the fair value adjustment a real economic loss that you're ignoring? Or is it a purely accounting construct? Reasonable people disagree.

**Category Two: One-Time/Non-Recurring Charges (Medium Defensibility)**

These are charges that occur in the measurement period but are stated to be non-recurring. The rationale is: They don't reflect the recurring earning power of the business.

*Restructuring Charges*

The most common item in this category. A company closes a plant, lays off employees, consolidates facilities. There are severance costs, lease termination costs, asset write-downs, disposal costs.

These are real cash outflows. But they're one-time. In a normal year (once the restructuring is done), they won't occur. So you add them back to get to "normalised" or "run-rate" EBITDA.

This makes sense conceptually. The question is: How do you define "one-time"? If a company does a major restructuring every other year, is each one "one-time," or should you reserve a recurring amount for ongoing restructurings?

*Acquisition and Integration Costs*

Similar logic. A company acquires another company and incurs fees (investment banking, legal, accounting), and then incurs integration costs (systems harmonisation, severance for duplicate positions, facility consolidation). These are one-time costs associated with the acquisition.

The credit agreement typically allows these to be added back. The rationale is similar: Once the acquisition is closed and integrated, these costs won't recur.

But here's where it gets subtle. Some sponsors use "acquisition costs" as a catch-all for any expense they can characterise as acquisition-related. I've seen sponsors try to add back executive recruiting costs, systems upgrades, even marketing costs as "acquisition integration." The line between "acquisition cost" and "normal capital expenditure" blurs.

*Non-Recurring Litigation and Settlement Costs*

A company settles a lawsuit, incurs environmental remediation, or faces a one-time product liability claim. These are real cash outflows but one-time in nature.

Again, the conceptual justification is sound — these shouldn't recur. But in practice, many companies face some level of litigation and settlement costs regularly. The line between "one-time" and "recurring" is blurred.

*Facility Opening/Closing Costs*

For retailers and hospitality companies especially, opening a new location or closing an existing one involves costs. These are sometimes added back as one-time.

**Category Three: Run-Rate and Synergy Adjustments (Lower Defensibility)**

Now we get into territory where EBITDA becomes increasingly fictional.

*Run-Rate Adjustments*

A company implements a cost-saving initiative and realises €1 million in savings in the measurement period. But management believes that if the initiative had been in place for the entire year, the savings would have been €2 million (because it was only in place for six months, or it ramped up over time).

They propose a "run-rate" adjustment to add back the €1 million for the full-year impact.

The question: Is this the measured EBITDA of the business, or is this a forecast of what EBITDA could be?

In traditional accounting, you'd measure what actually happened. But in leveraged finance, especially with sponsor-backed deals, there's a lot of appetite for "run-rate" adjustments because they show a "truer" picture of run-rate earnings power.

The danger is obvious: You're moving from measuring actual performance to forecasting expected performance. The company hasn't achieved the run-rate savings yet. They might. They might not.

I've seen sponsors propose run-rate adjustments for:
- Cost reductions that were approved but not yet implemented
- Productivity improvements that were planned but not yet achieved
- Headcount reductions that were announced but not yet executed
- Contract renegotiations that were proposed but not yet finalised

The sponsor's argument: "We've committed to these actions. The EBITDA will be €X." The credit committee's question should be: "But has it been achieved yet? And if it hasn't, why should we count it in covenant calculations?"

*Synergy Adjustments*

In an acquisition context, the buyer identifies synergies: Cost overlaps that will be eliminated, revenue cross-sell opportunities, procurement savings by combining purchasing power.

In pro forma EBITDA calculations, it's standard to add back expected synergies. This allows you to see the "full-year, fully-synergised EBITDA" of the combined company.

But the emphasis should be on "expected." These are synergies that management believes will be achieved. They might be. They might partially be. They might not be at all.

In my experience, acquirers systematically overestimate synergies. A McKinsey study found that acquirers typically realise about 70% of identified synergies, and often take longer to realise them than expected. But in credit agreements signed at the time of the acquisition, management is projecting 100% of synergies into the run-rate EBITDA.

This creates a situation where the pro forma leverage at closing looks very attractive (say, 3.5x), because it assumes all synergies are achieved. But in reality, if synergies are only 70% realised, actual leverage is 4.0x+. The credit looked better on paper than it was in reality.

*Management Actions / Continuous Improvement*

Some credit agreements allow EBITDA add-backs for "management actions" or "continuous improvement initiatives" that management believes can be achieved within a specified timeframe (typically 12 or 24 months).

The logic is similar to run-rate adjustments: We're not measuring historical EBITDA, we're measuring expected EBITDA conditional on management executing their plan.

This is where I've seen the most aggressive and creative add-backs. I've seen sponsors propose adding back:
- Productivity improvements in supply chains that are speculative
- Staffing optimisations that haven't been planned in detail
- Procurement savings from supplier consolidation that haven't been negotiated
- Revenue growth assumptions from "customer wins" that are still being proposed

In each case, the sponsor's argument is: "This is achievable within 24 months." The lender's question should be: "But is it actually likely, and are you committed to achieving it?"

**Category Four: Owner/Sponsor-Related Add-Backs (Lowest Defensibility)**

These are add-backs that relate to the cost of ownership or the sponsor's structure, rather than the operating business.

*Management Fees to Sponsor*

In sponsor-backed deals, the sponsor often receives a management fee — typically 1-2% of enterprise value annually, to pay for the sponsor's management and monitoring of the investment.

This is a real cash outflow, but it's a financing decision, not an operational reality. A different sponsor (or an owner) might not charge this fee. Different capital structures have different sponsor fees.

Some credit agreements allow management fees to be added back to EBITDA on the theory that they're a financing cost, not an operational cost. Others don't.

This is a contentious area. From a pure cash flow perspective, the management fee is real cash leaving the business, and that cash isn't available to service debt. So excluding it from EBITDA (i.e., not adding it back) is the more conservative approach.

*Professional Fees and Advisory Costs*

Related to the above: Investment banking fees, legal fees, advisory fees for the transaction (at close), and ongoing transaction-related costs.

Some sponsors argue these are one-time and should be added back. Others argue that post-acquisition, the company incurs some ongoing advisory costs as part of the sponsor's relationship, and these should be added back.

From the lender's perspective, cash is cash. If the money's leaving the borrower's bank account, it's not available for debt service.

*Excess Founder Compensation or Related-Party Charges*

In some family-owned or founder-led businesses that are acquired by sponsors, the previous owner might have been taking excessive compensation or benefits. Once the sponsor takes over, this is eliminated.

If the founder was taking €2 million per year in salary and benefits that market rates would suggest should be €1 million, the sponsor might propose adding back €1 million.

This is sometimes defensible (you're normalising to market compensation levels), but sometimes it's used to hide the fact that the business can't actually afford its full cost structure.

**Category Five: The "Kitchen Sink" — Everything Else**

Finally, there are add-backs that are so creative I can only describe them as negotiated fantasies.

I've seen proposed add-backs for:
- Marketing expenses deemed "above normal"
- Training and development costs that "exceed industry norms"
- Insurance costs that are "higher than necessary" (even though they're actual costs incurred)
- Travel and entertainment deemed "discretionary"
- Maintenance capital expenditures deemed "below normal levels" (added as a negative adjustment, to show what EBITDA would be if capex were higher)

The philosophy behind these is: "The business has inefficiencies that a better-run business wouldn't have, so we're adjusting for them."

The problem is obvious: You're not measuring the actual EBITDA of the business you're lending to. You're imagining a hypothetical version of the business, better-run and more efficient, and calculating leverage on that.

If the actual business has higher marketing costs, higher insurance costs, or higher travel costs than average, those are real costs. The cash is leaving the bank account. It's not available for debt service.

18.4 THE EVOLUTION OF EBITDA DEFINITIONS

Understanding where we are in the cycle requires understanding how EBITDA definitions have evolved. The standards that apply in leveraged finance have shifted several times, and the current environment (2024-2026) is in a new aggressive phase.

**The Pre-Crisis Era (1990s-2007): The Beginning of Complexity**

In the 1990s, EBITDA was simpler. It was basically calculated by credit agreements as:
Net Income + Interest + Taxes + Depreciation + Amortisation

You'd add back reported restructuring charges and maybe one other category (extraordinary losses, gains on asset sales). That was it.

Leverage ratios were tighter, too. A 3.5x leverage ratio was considered quite levered for a mid-market company. Higher leverage (4.5x, 5.0x) was rare, generally reserved for "sure things" or highly cash-generative businesses.

As the credit markets became more competitive and capital more available in the early 2000s, sponsors pushed for looser covenants and more permissive EBITDA add-backs. Lenders competed to win business, and one of the easiest ways to compete was to allow more EBITDA add-backs (which made the leverage look lower).

**The Bubble Era (2005-2007): When EBITDA Became Fiction**

By 2005-2007, EBITDA definitions in new leveraged finance deals had become baroque. You'd see credit agreements that allowed:

- All restructuring charges
- All acquisition and integration costs (generously defined)
- Run-rate adjustments for "identified cost savings" (even if unachieved)
- Synergy adjustments for acquisitions
- Add-backs for "management fees" and "sponsor management costs"
- Add-backs for unusual or infrequent losses
- Management action add-backs for "continuous improvement" (basically unlimited, as long as management believed it was achievable)

The result: Leverage ratios that looked great on the offering memorandum (say, 3.5x), but actual leverage (measured on a stricter EBITDA basis) might have been 4.5x or 5.0x.

By 2006-2007, it was widely understood (among experienced practitioners, at least) that EBITDA numbers in offering memoranda were optimistic. But the deals were still being done, because capital was abundant, spreads were tight, and everyone understood that if the business performed to even 80% of the sponsor's plan, the deal would work.

Then the financial crisis hit, and suddenly 80% of the plan was optimistic.

**The Post-Crisis Tightening (2008-2012): A Return to Conservatism**

After Lehman, credit spreads blew out, and suddenly the attractiveness of a deal depended critically on whether the EBITDA projections were achieved. Lenders began to demand tighter EBITDA definitions.

By 2010-2012, many new credit agreements had much more restrictive EBITDA language. There were:
- Caps on run-rate adjustments (e.g., "run-rate adjustments capped at €5 million in aggregate," or "capped at 5% of EBITDA")
- Caps on synergy adjustments
- Tighter definitions of what qualified as "restructuring" or "one-time"
- Requirements that adjustments be "reasonably identifiable" or "good faith estimated"

The idea was to get back to more conservative EBITDA definitions that actually reflected reality.

For investors, this was healthier. Leverage ratios actually meant something. You could look at a 4.0x leverage ratio and have reasonable confidence that the business could service debt.

**The Erosion (2013-2019): Loosening Again, But More Subtly**

As credit conditions improved and capital became abundant again, lenders gradually loosened EBITDA definitions. It happened quietly, through small changes to language and interpretation, rather than through explicit loosening.

By 2014-2016, you were again seeing:
- Run-rate adjustments without clear caps
- Synergy adjustments for acquisitions at 100% (not discounted for achievement risk)
- Discretion-based EBITDA add-backs for "management actions"
- Working capital adjustments that inflated EBITDA

But the loosening wasn't as blatant as the pre-crisis era. There were still caps, still guardrails. But they were increasingly ignored or work-around.

By 2017-2019, a strong competitive dynamic had reasserted itself. If you required strict EBITDA definitions and your competitor didn't, you lost deals. So many lenders gradually accepted looser definitions.

Leverage ratios started creeping up. By 2019, the median leverage ratio for sponsor-backed deals had reached 4.5x or higher (depending on the sub-sector). This was at cycle peak, with capital abundant and economic growth steady.

**The 2020-2021 Spike and Reversion: COVID and Recovery**

COVID in 2020 hit suddenly, and many sponsor-backed credits saw EBITDA fall 10-40% depending on the sector. A company that had been modeled at 4.0x leverage suddenly found itself at 5.5x+ leverage.

For a brief moment (March-June 2020), credit spreads blew out and lenders demanded much tighter covenant packages and EBITDA definitions.

But by late 2020, it became clear that the downturn was temporary and cyclical (for most industries), not permanent. Capital markets reopened. Spreads tightened. EBITDA definitions loosened again.

By 2021, we were back to aggressive EBITDA add-backs:
- Run-rate adjustments at high levels
- Synergy adjustments for acquisitions at 100% (or even at optimistic levels above 100% for the most aggressive sponsors)
- "Pro forma EBITDA" adjustments at acquisition closing that bore little relationship to actual run-rate earnings

**The Current Cycle (2022-2026): Back to Aggressive**

Fast forward to 2024-2025. Interest rate increases in 2022-2023 caused spreads to blow out again. Leverage ratios, measured on traditional bases, have moved up.

But what's interesting is that despite tighter spreads and higher rates, sponsors have achieved tighter leverage ratios at closing by using more aggressive EBITDA add-backs than ever before.

According to conversations with syndication advisers and credit analysts at major institutions, the add-backs permitted in sponsor-backed deals in 2024 are at levels not seen since the pre-2008 bubble. Here are the dimensions:

**Run-Rate Adjustments**: In 2024, run-rate adjustments routinely exceed 10% of reported EBITDA. I've seen deals where sponsor-identified cost savings (not yet achieved) represent 15-20% of the run-rate EBITDA used in leverage calculations. Time caps on these are often 24 months (the savings must be realised within two years of closing), but there's little enforcement mechanism if they're not achieved.

**Synergy Adjustments**: For acquisitions, sponsors are again assuming 100% realisation of synergies, with some sponsors even assuming synergies above their base case (assuming that management will outperform initial plans). The discounting for execution risk that was common in 2010-2015 has largely disappeared.

**Sponsor Cost Add-Backs**: Management fees to sponsors are routinely added back. Some credit agreements even allow add-backs for the sponsor's administrative and monitoring costs.

**Professional Fees**: Transaction fees (investment banking, legal, advisory) are routinely added back if incurred at closing. Some credit agreements even allow ongoing advisory costs to be added back.

**Continuous Improvement Add-Backs**: The language around "management actions" and "continuous improvement" has become very permissive. As long as management believes something is achievable within 24 months, it gets added.

The result: A sponsor-backed leveraged buyout that appears to be at 3.75x leverage on a "fully adjusted" EBITDA basis might actually be at 4.5x+ leverage on a more conservative basis.

**The Data: What Are the Add-Backs Actually Reducing Leverage By?**

I don't have access to proprietary databases, but based on conversations with credit analysts at major institutions and my own deal experience, the picture is clear:

In 2024-2025, sponsor-backed credits are using adjustments that reduce measured leverage by an average of 0.75 to 1.0 turns. That is, if you measured leverage using only actual reported EBITDA (with no add-backs), the leverage would be about a full turn higher than what's shown in the offering memorandum.

On a €500 million EBITDA business with €2 billion of debt, this difference is €500 million. That's material.

The question for investors is: Are these add-backs actually going to be realised? If the answer is yes, then the leverage is accurately stated. If the answer is no, then the business is more levered than it appears, and it's more vulnerable to disruption.

18.5 THE CAP GAME: LIMITING ADD-BACKS (OR NOT)

One of the key levers in a credit agreement's EBITDA definition is how add-backs are capped or limited. This is where the real negotiation often happens.

**Percentage-of-EBITDA Caps: The Circularity Problem**

A common approach is to cap add-backs at a percentage of EBITDA. For instance: "Adjustments shall not exceed 10% of EBITDA."

This sounds reasonable on the surface. But there's a mathematical problem: You're capping the adjustments as a percentage of a number that includes the adjustments.

Let me illustrate:

Reported EBITDA (with no adjustments): €10 million
Proposed adjustments: €2 million
Cap: 10% of EBITDA

What's the cap? Is it 10% of €10 million (€1 million), or 10% of €12 million (€1.2 million, the fully adjusted number)?

If it's 10% of €10m, then the adjustment is too large (€2m exceeds €1m), and it's capped at €1m.

If it's 10% of €12m, then the adjustment is within the cap (€2m < €1.2m).

This matters. If the cap is circular (based on the adjusted EBITDA), then the cap becomes a simple mathematical statement rather than an actual limit. As long as adjustments are less than 11% of reported EBITDA, they'll always be within the cap (because the denominator will grow enough to accommodate them).

Most credit agreements try to avoid this by specifying that the cap is a percentage of "reported EBITDA" or "calculated EBITDA" (the pre-adjustment number). But even then, there's ambiguity in how you calculate the reported number and how you sequence the adjustments.

I've seen credit committees spend hours debating the precise language around capping adjustments. The difference between "capped at 10% of EBITDA" and "capped at 10% of the sum of reported EBITDA and adjustments" can be millions of euros.

**Hard Caps: The Blunt Approach**

Some credit agreements take a simpler approach: "Total add-backs shall not exceed €X million."

For example: "Adjustments may not exceed €15 million in aggregate."

This is simpler. But it's also crude. A €15 million cap might be reasonable for a €100 million EBITDA business, but excessive for a €30 million EBITDA business.

To address this, agreements sometimes specify multiple hard caps. For example:
- Run-rate adjustments capped at €8 million
- One-time charges capped at €10 million
- Restructuring charges capped at €6 million

The sponsor negotiates for the highest hard caps possible. The lender negotiates for the lowest. The final number is usually a compromise that both sides can live with.

**The Time Cap: "Achieved Within 12/24 Months"**

A critical constraint on run-rate and synergy adjustments is a time cap. The language typically reads: "The company must achieve the adjustment within 12 months (or 24 months) of the closing date."

The idea is that if the adjustment hasn't been realised by the time cap, it gets reversed. The covenant calculation goes back to a lower EBITDA, which tightens leverage.

But there's a problem: Enforcement. How do you police this? The lender has to remember the adjustment, track whether it was achieved, and then proactively require the reversal if it wasn't. This requires:

1. Good documentation (the specific adjustment is clearly defined)
2. Good governance (the lender actually tracks it)
3. Willingness to enforce (the lender is willing to tighten covenants if the adjustment lapses)

In my experience, the third point is often missing. By the time a time-capped adjustment is supposed to lapse, relationships have been established, the sponsor has other deals with the lender, or the credit is already stressed and you don't want to tighten covenants further.

The result: Time-capped adjustments often quietly stay in place past their expiration. The sponsor and borrower just keep calculating EBITDA using the adjustment, and nobody pushes back.

**The "Good Faith" vs. "Reasonably Identifiable" Standard**

The language used in credit agreements to govern adjustments is important.

Some agreements say adjustments must be "good faith estimated." This is a relatively high bar — it requires that management's belief in the adjustment be genuine and based on a reasonable analysis.

Others say adjustments must be "reasonably identifiable." This is a lower bar — it just requires that the adjustment be identifiable as a real cost or benefit that the business expects to achieve.

The difference in practice:

"Good faith" requires that you have a plan, analysis, and actual management commitment. If you're adding back €3 million in "restructuring costs," you need evidence that management has committed to restructuring, that the costs are real, and that the amounts are reasonable.

"Reasonably identifiable" is softer. It just means you can identify what the adjustment is for. You might have an idea that there's a way to save €3 million through efficiency improvements, and that might be enough.

Lenders prefer "good faith." Sponsors prefer "reasonably identifiable."

**The Most Aggressive Language: Unlimited Discretion**

The most permissive language you see allows adjustments for "management actions," "operational improvements," or "synergies" with essentially unlimited discretion.

For example: "Adjustments shall be permitted for any restructuring, synergies, cost savings, or operational improvements that management believes can be achieved within 24 months, provided that such adjustments are not greater than 20% of EBITDA."

With language like this, the door is wide open. As long as management can argue that something is an "operational improvement" (which could be almost anything), and it's under the 20% cap, it gets added.

I've seen this language used to add back:
- Productivity improvements that haven't been implemented
- Staffing optimisations that are speculative
- Revenue synergies that are entirely theoretical
- Supplier renegotiations that haven't actually occurred

The credit agreement is supposed to constrain the lender's risk, but language like this actually shifts the risk definition to one party's (management's) unilateral assertion of what's achievable.

18.6 PRO FORMA EBITDA AND THE ACQUISITION CONTEXT

When a company acquires another company, the EBITDA picture becomes more complex. This is where "pro forma EBITDA" comes in.

**The Basic Concept of Pro Forma EBITDA**

Pro forma means "as if." Pro forma EBITDA is the EBITDA of the combined company, calculated as if the acquisition had been in place for the full period.

For example, suppose:
- Buyer has EBITDA of €50 million
- Target has EBITDA of €20 million
- Acquisition closes on July 1

For the fiscal year in which the acquisition closes (which ends, say, December 31), the buyer will report only six months of target EBITDA (July-December).

But the pro forma EBITDA shows the EBITDA of the combined company for the full year (€50m + €20m = €70m), as if the acquisition had closed at the beginning of the year.

This is useful for giving a sense of the combined entity's earning power. But it's hypothetical. The business didn't actually earn €70 million in that year; it earned €60 million (€50m from buyer + €10m from six months of target).

**Run-Rate Synergies vs. Achieved Synergies**

The next layer is synergies. When you acquire a company, you identify cost overlaps, procurement savings, and revenue opportunities.

*Achieved Synergies*: These are synergies that have actually been implemented by the time you're calculating EBITDA. For example, you've eliminated duplicate corporate functions, so you're saving €5 million per year. That €5 million should be added to the pro forma EBITDA.

*Run-Rate Synergies*: These are synergies that you've identified and begun to implement, but haven't fully ramped. For example, you've laid off half of the duplicate corporate staff, saving €2.5 million, and plan to lay off the other half by the end of Q2 next year. You might project the "run-rate" of the synergy as €5 million (the full impact once complete) even though only €2.5 million has been achieved to date.

*Projected Synergies*: These are synergies you believe you'll achieve in the future, but haven't started to implement yet. For example, you plan to consolidate suppliers and save €3 million, but you haven't begun the process.

When sponsors construct pro forma EBITDA for a financing, they often use all three:
- Add back achieved synergies (fully justified)
- Add back run-rate synergies at some level (partially justified)
- Add back projected synergies at a discounted rate, or within specific caps (increasingly speculative)

The result is an offering memorandum that shows pro forma leverage at 3.5x, but if you removed the projected and run-rate synergies and only included achieved synergies, leverage might be 4.5x+.

**The Waterfall in an Offering Memorandum**

A well-done offering memorandum (the document marketing the debt to investors) will show a clear waterfall of EBITDA adjustments. For example:

Buyer reported EBITDA (LTM): €50m
Target reported EBITDA (LTM): €20m
Unaudited pro forma EBITDA (combining buyer and target): €70m

Adjustments:
- Achieved run-rate synergies: +€3m
- Projected run-rate synergies (within 12 months): +€2m
- One-time acquisition costs (to be added back): +€2m
- Restructuring charges (one-time): +€1m

Fully adjusted pro forma EBITDA: €78m

Pro forma leverage (total debt / fully adjusted EBITDA): €280m / €78m = 3.6x

Now, a disciplined investor can look at this and ask: Which of these adjustments am I confident will be achieved?
- Achieved synergies: 100% (they've already happened)
- Projected run-rate synergies (12 months): 70% (probably happens, but there's execution risk)
- One-time acquisition costs: Should be reversed, not added (these are real costs)
- Restructuring charges: Partially add back (if truly one-time, 75%)

On that basis, a more conservative EBITDA might be:
€70m + €3m + (€2m × 70%) + €0 + (€1m × 75%) = €75.4m

More conservative pro forma leverage: €280m / €75.4m = 3.7x

The difference is small in this case. But in deals with very large synergies, it can be material.

**When Pro Forma Numbers Diverge from Reality**

Here's where it gets interesting: After an acquisition closes, you're supposed to track whether the pro forma projections (especially the synergy assumptions) are actually being achieved.

In many cases, they're not. The McKinsey study I mentioned earlier found that companies typically achieve 70% of projected synergies, and often later than expected.

But in the leverage calculations going forward (in quarterly reporting to lenders), the EBITDA used is "actual" EBITDA, not pro forma. So when synergies fall short, the actual measured leverage ends up higher than the pro forma leverage at closing suggested.

For example:

Leverage at closing (pro forma): 3.5x (assuming €80m EBITDA, including synergies)
Leverage 12 months later (actual): 4.2x (because only 70% of synergies were achieved, so actual EBITDA is €74m)

The credit looked safer at closing than it actually was.

This is where careful investors who buy into leveraged deals shortly after closing sometimes get surprised. The leverage that was modeled looks better than what actually materialises.

18.7 EBITDA IN THE COVENANT ARCHITECTURE

EBITDA doesn't exist in isolation. It's embedded in a system of covenants, tests, and calculations. Understanding how EBITDA feeds into that architecture is critical to understanding whether a credit is actually stressed or not.

**Leverage Ratios: The Primary EBITDA-Based Test**

The leverage ratio (Total Debt / EBITDA) is the most common covenant test in leveraged finance. For instance:

"Total Debt shall not exceed 4.0x EBITDA at all times."

When you calculate leverage for a compliance test, you use:
- Total Debt: This is defined in the credit agreement, usually including the term loan, any drawn revolvers, and any other financial indebtedness (bonds, other loans, etc.). It might include estimated interest payable (LIBOR accrual for the next quarter).
- EBITDA: This uses the EBITDA definition and add-backs allowed in the credit agreement. It's typically measured for a trailing twelve-month (LTM) period, but can be measured on a pro forma basis in acquisition contexts or a forward-looking basis in incurrence-based tests.

The covenant is typically a "maintenance" covenant, meaning it's tested quarterly (after each quarter's results are reported).

If a company's measured leverage exceeds the covenant maximum, it's technically in default. This gives lenders the right to accelerate the loan, demand repayment, or negotiate amendments (which usually involve loosening the covenant).

**Interest Coverage Ratios**

A secondary measure often used is interest coverage: EBITDA / Interest Expense.

This measures the company's ability to service interest. A company with €100 million EBITDA and €10 million of annual interest has 10.0x coverage. A company with €50 million of annual interest has 2.0x coverage.

Coverage tests might require a minimum of 2.5x or 3.0x. The tighter the coverage, the less cushion the company has.

Here again, EBITDA definition matters. If you're adding back €10 million of "projected synergies" that haven't been achieved, and actual interest expense is €10 million, then actual coverage is lower than the EBITDA-based calculation suggests.

**Maintenance Tests vs. Incurrence Tests**

Some covenants are "maintenance" tests, meaning they're tested every quarter. Others are "incurrence" tests, meaning they're tested only if the company does something specific (like incurring new debt or making distributions).

An incurrence test might say: "The company may not incur additional debt unless pro forma leverage would not exceed 4.5x."

When calculating pro forma leverage for an incurrence test, you use pro forma EBITDA (including synergies, run-rate improvements, etc.), which tends to be looser than the maintenance covenant EBITDA.

This is intentional. The idea is that incurrence tests allow flexibility for transactions that management believes are positive (acquisitions, refinancings) while maintenance tests prevent drift into excessive leverage.

**EBITDA in the Restricted Payments Capacity**

In many credit agreements, the company's ability to pay dividends, make loans to affiliates, or buyback shares is constrained by a "restricted payments basket."

A common restriction might be: "The company may not make restricted payments unless:
(a) No event of default has occurred and is continuing, and
(b) Pro forma leverage would not exceed 2.0x after giving effect to the payment, and
(c) Availability under the revolver exceeds €10 million."

Here, EBITDA is used to calculate the pro forma leverage threshold for distributions. A higher EBITDA means a higher capacity to distribute cash.

This is why sponsors care deeply about EBITDA definitions. A €5 million EBITDA add-back translates directly into a higher ability to take cash distributions from the borrower.

**EBITDA in Debt Incurrence Tests**

An acquisition by the borrower might be permitted, and leverage is recalculated on a pro forma basis assuming the acquired company's EBITDA. For example:

"The company may acquire target company X if pro forma leverage would not exceed 4.5x EBITDA."

Here, the target's EBITDA is added to the buyer's EBITDA, and pro forma adjustments (synergies, etc.) are included. This determines how much the buyer can afford to pay.

If EBITDA is calculated liberally (including all projected synergies), the buyer can afford a higher price. If EBITDA is calculated conservatively, the affordable price is lower.

This is another point of tension in credit negotiations. Sponsors want EBITDA definitions that are generous (allow higher incurrence capacity), while lenders want definitions that are conservative.

18.8 THE EUROPEAN DIMENSION

EBITDA calculation in European credit agreements differs from US practice in several ways.

**LMA Definitions vs. LSTA Definitions**

LMA (Loan Market Association) credit agreements, which are the standard for European leveraged finance, tend to have slightly different EBITDA language than LSTA (Loan Syndications and Trading Association) agreements from the US.

LSTA language is often very detailed and prescriptive. For example, the definition of EBITDA might be four pages of detailed calculations, specifying exactly how to treat different items.

LMA language tends to be more flexible and principle-based. The definition might be a paragraph that establishes the concept, and then allows adjustments for items that are "not in the ordinary course" or are "extraordinary."

In practice, LMA agreements often allow more sponsor discretion in EBITDA calculations because the language is less prescriptive.

**Working Capital Adjustments**

European EBITDA definitions sometimes include working capital adjustments that are less common in the US.

For example, if the business grows and requires more working capital (more inventory, more receivables), this can be a drain on cash. Some European credit agreements allow an adjustment to EBITDA for expected increases in working capital above a baseline.

The idea is that if the baseline level of working capital is €20 million, and growth causes it to increase to €25 million, the €5 million is a cash drag that's not within management's control. So you might adjust EBITDA upward for this.

This is speculative because it assumes you can predict future working capital needs. But it reflects European pragmatism about the fact that EBITDA (a profit measure) and cash flow are different things.

**Cross-Currency EBITDA**

As I mentioned earlier, European businesses often have EBITDA in multiple currencies. The credit agreement must specify how to translate foreign currency EBITDA into the reporting currency (usually Euros).

The translation methodology matters. If you use monthly average rates (which many agreements specify), EBITDA can fluctuate based on FX movements, even if actual operational performance doesn't change.

Some agreements try to "hedge" this by using a fixed rate (the rate at the time of closing) for FX translation. Others use period-end rates. Others allow for a collar (FX movements within a certain band are ignored).

**The Cashflow Waterfall and Its Relationship to EBITDA**

European credit agreements often include detailed cashflow waterfall provisions that specify how cash is deployed (which debt gets paid first, which gets paid second, what's available for distributions, etc.).

In some structures, EBITDA feeds directly into the waterfall logic. For instance, the waterfall might specify that if leverage exceeds 4.0x (measured on EBITDA), cash must be used to delever before distributions are allowed.

This ties EBITDA definitions directly to cash deployment. If EBITDA is calculated liberally, it might push the company below the 4.0x threshold, loosening the waterfall constraints. If it's calculated conservatively, the constraints stay tight.

**Dividend Blocking and EBITDA**

Some European agreements have "dividend blocking" provisions that prevent sponsors from taking distributions if leverage exceeds a certain threshold (often 4.5x) or if EBITDA has declined by more than a certain amount.

These provisions are often less strict than US equivalents but reflect the same concern: You don't want sponsors extracting cash from a credit that's deteriorating.

18.9 AUDITOR COMFORT AND THE REPORTING REALITY

Here's an underappreciated dimension: What do auditors actually sign off on?

Many people assume that EBITDA numbers used in credit covenants are audited and therefore reliable. This isn't quite right.

**What Auditors Verify**

External auditors (the firm that audits the company's financial statements) verify that the financial statements are fairly presented in conformity with accounting standards (GAAP or IFRS). They verify that net income, revenues, and major expense categories are properly presented.

But EBITDA is not a standard accounting measure. It's a metric derived from the financial statements. Auditors don't separately audit EBITDA; they audit the underlying numbers that feed into it.

Moreover, the adjustments to EBITDA (for restructuring charges, one-time items, synergies, etc.) are often not separately audited. The auditor might see a charge for restructuring and confirm that it's real, but they don't independently verify that it's truly "non-recurring" or that it meets the credit agreement's definition.

**The Compliance Certificate Process**

When a company reports quarterly results, it typically provides a "compliance certificate" that shows the calculation of EBITDA and leverage ratios, and confirms compliance with covenants.

This certificate is typically signed by the company's CFO. But it's not audited. The lender is relying on the company's management to calculate EBITDA correctly.

In practice, this works fine most of the time. But I've seen cases where companies' calculations of EBITDA (especially for add-backs) differ from lenders' calculations.

**Disputes Over EBITDA Calculations**

When a company is stressed, and leverage is tight, EBITDA disputes sometimes emerge.

For instance, the company adds back €2 million for "projected cost savings" that management believes are achievable within 12 months. The lender looks at the plan for the savings and thinks it's speculative — unlikely to be achieved. Dispute.

Or the company adds back €1 million for "one-time restructuring charges," but the lender looks at the company's history and sees that it has restructuring charges almost every year. The lender argues these are recurring, not one-time. Dispute.

Most of these disputes get worked out. But they reflect the reality that EBITDA definitions are contested territory. When a company is healthy and covenants are comfortable, these disputes don't matter much. When a company is stressed and covenants are tight, they can determine whether there's technically a default or not.

**Case Studies of EBITDA Disputes**

I recall a mid-market distribution company that had EBITDA of €25 million by audited financials. But when you included all of the adjustments the company (and the sponsor) wanted to include, "adjusted EBITDA" was €32 million. A 28% difference.

The company wasn't in distress, but leverage was tight (3.9x on the strict basis, 3.0x on the "adjusted" basis). The company was doing well, and there was no intent on anyone's part to mislead. But if the company had hit a downturn and EBITDA had contracted, that 28% gap would have mattered enormously.

The lenders (a mix of debt funds and banks) never formally objected to the adjusted EBITDA calculation. But several of them were uncomfortable with it and looked more carefully at the underlying metrics (cash flow, working capital, capex) when evaluating covenant stress scenarios.

This is the pragmatic reality: When EBITDA numbers don't fully reconcile with underlying cash flow or operational metrics, sophisticated lenders get skeptical, even if the specific calculations aren't technically incorrect per the credit agreement.

18.10 WHAT TWENTY YEARS TEACHES

Let me end this chapter with what I've actually learned about EBITDA in two decades of dealing with it daily.

First: EBITDA is the most important number in leveraged finance, and also the least trusted. Everyone knows that EBITDA can be adjusted and manipulated. Everyone assumes that sponsor-provided EBITDA is optimistic. But everyone also uses it, because there's no better single metric for comparing leverage across different companies.

This creates a strange dynamic where everyone understands the limitations but proceeds as if the number is reliable anyway. It's a kind of collective suspension of disbelief.

Second: The sophistication of EBITDA adjustment has increased over time. In the 1990s, adjustments were crude and obvious. Now they're subtle and complex. A sponsor can construct a plausible EBITDA narrative that's very different from the "pure" operating earnings, and many lenders will accept it because the individual adjustments seem reasonable.

Third: EBITDA aggressiveness correlates with credit quality. In strong credits (growing, de-levering, profitable), EBITDA adjustments matter less because the company has cushion. In weak credits (slow-growing, flat-leveraging, marginal profitability), EBITDA adjustments are critical to whether covenants are breached.

And of course, it's in weak credits where you most need to know whether the EBITDA is real or fictional.

Fourth: The time cap on run-rate and synergy adjustments is critical but often not enforced. If a 24-month run-rate adjustment lapses because management didn't achieve the synergy, you need to enforce the reversal. But enforcement is hard, and relationships get in the way. Be thoughtful about what run-rate adjustments you're comfortable with, because you might be stuck with them.

Fifth: Pro forma EBITDA in acquisition contexts is almost always optimistic on synergies. This is just a fact of life. When you're evaluating an acquisition, assume that synergies will be 70% of what was projected. This gives you a margin of safety.

Sixth: The best lenders don't just look at EBITDA. They look at cash conversion. Does EBITDA convert to cash? Is the company burning working capital? Are there large capex requirements that eat into cash? A company with high EBITDA but poor cash conversion is riskier than the EBITDA number alone suggests.

Seventh: When you're evaluating a credit, push on the add-backs. Ask the sponsor specifically what each adjustment is for, how it was calculated, and whether management is committed to achieving it. You'll learn a lot about how realistic the sponsor is being. If the sponsor can't articulate a specific plan for a €5 million add-back, that should trigger skepticism.

Finally: Remember that EBITDA is a construct. It doesn't measure cash flow, or true earning power, or the business's capacity to service debt. It measures a number that's been negotiated and adjusted. The credit agreement says "leverage shall not exceed 4.0x EBITDA," which is a mechanical statement. But whether the credit is actually safe or not depends on cash flow, operational trends, competitive position, and management capability. EBITDA is an input into the decision, but it's not the decision itself.

SUMMARY: THE EBITDA PRACTITIONER'S CHECKLIST

When evaluating a credit where EBITDA matters (i.e., whenever leverage is tight or the business is marginal):

**EBITDA Definition Review:**
☐ What's the starting point? Audited net income? Unaudited management accounts? Pro forma combined?
☐ What add-backs are included? Restructuring? One-time items? Run-rate synergies?
☐ Are there caps on total add-backs? Are they percentage-based or hard caps?
☐ For run-rate/synergy adjustments, what's the time cap? Is there an enforcement mechanism?
☐ What's the standard for add-backs? "Good faith"? "Reasonably identifiable"? Broad discretion?

**Comparison and Reality-Check:**
☐ What's reported EBITDA vs. adjusted EBITDA? What's the variance?
☐ If the variance is more than 10%, understand where it's coming from.
☐ Compare the adjusted EBITDA to actual cash flow. Is EBITDA converting to cash?
☐ If there's a gap between EBITDA and cash flow, understand why (working capital, capex, one-time items).

**Add-Back Specificity:**
☐ For each significant add-back (>€500k), ask: What specifically is this for, and is it actually going to be achieved?
☐ For run-rate adjustments: Is there a detailed plan? Has the initiative been approved? What's the timeline?
☐ For synergy adjustments: Is this achieved, in-process, or projected? What discount should you apply for execution risk?
☐ For one-time items: Is this truly non-recurring, or does this company incur this charge regularly?

**Acquisition/Pro Forma Context:**
☐ If this is an acquisition deal, how much EBITDA is from achieved synergies vs. projected?
☐ What's a realistic assumption for synergy achievement? (Assume 70%, not 100%)
☐ Has the target's EBITDA been independently verified, or are you relying on seller-provided numbers?
☐ Pro forma leverage looks better than actual leverage by how much? 0.3x? 0.5x? 1.0x?

**Covenant and Portfolio Context:**
☐ How much cushion is there between current leverage and the covenant maximum?
☐ How much of that cushion is from add-backs vs. actual operational performance?
☐ If the business performs at 90% of plan, does it still comply with covenants?
☐ For each material add-back, ask: If this add-back lapses, do we breach covenant?

**Cash Flow and Underwriting:**
☐ What's the company's cash conversion rate (Operating Cash Flow / EBITDA)?
☐ Is working capital a source or use of cash?
☐ What capex is required to maintain/grow the business? Is there cushion for this from cash generation?
☐ Can the company service debt and fund growth without dilution?

**Red Flags:**
☐ EBITDA adjusted more than 15% from reported
☐ Major run-rate adjustments that haven't been implemented yet
☐ Pro forma leverage that improves dramatically from closing due to synergies
☐ Management compensation or related-party costs that seem "adjusted" rather than real
☐ Multiple extension periods or amendment/restatement of the EBITDA definition
☐ EBITDA from multiple sources (reported, management, sponsor-provided, audit-adjusted) with inconsistent numbers
☐ Covenant cushion that exists only because of one large add-back

**Final Decision:**
☐ If I remove all add-backs and use only reported EBITDA, what does leverage look like?
☐ Am I comfortable lending at that leverage?
☐ If not, then I'm relying on the add-backs being achieved. What's the probability of that?
☐ Is the economics of the credit sufficiently attractive to justify that execution risk?
