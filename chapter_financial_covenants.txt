CHAPTER 19: FINANCIAL COVENANTS — THE QUANTITATIVE GUARDRAILS

19.1 Introduction — The Covenant-Lite Revolution and Its Consequences

I spent the better part of fifteen years believing that financial covenants were the spine of any properly structured leveraged loan. Maintenance covenants — the monthly or quarterly tests that bound a company to meeting leverage and interest coverage targets — were, in my mind, the lender's early warning system. They were how you knew, before it was too late, whether a company was drifting toward distress. You tested the numbers quarterly, and if the borrower was approaching the red line, you had time to react. You could tighten covenants, demand an equity cure, amend the credit agreement, or position yourself for a workout.

Then, somewhere around 2003-2004, the world changed. The covenant-lite movement began in the high-yield bond market — a deliberate push by sponsors to strip out maintenance testing and replace it with weaker incurrence tests. The logic was simple from an equity sponsor's perspective: why give lenders a quarterly opportunity to constrain your operational flexibility? Why accept covenants that might trigger at the worst possible moment? Better to have wide covenants that you cross only when you want to take a specific action — a dividend, an acquisition, a refinancing.

By the mid-2000s, this thinking had infected the leveraged loan market. Covenant-lite loans became increasingly common in the United States, and by 2007, in the era of maximum leverage and minimum protection, cov-lite had become standard in large sponsor-backed transactions. Then the financial crisis hit, and everyone suddenly remembered why maintenance covenants mattered. But the lesson didn't stick. By 2010-2012, cov-lite had returned. By 2020, in many segments of the market, maintenance covenants had almost disappeared entirely.

I remember a conversation with a debt fund manager in 2019 who told me, somewhat triumphantly, that his fund had just completed a large leveraged loan investment where there were precisely zero maintenance covenants. None. The only financial covenant was an incurrence test on additional debt and a leverage step-down that applied when leverage fell below a certain level. He was proud of this. It meant, he said, that the company had full operational flexibility. What it also meant, though he didn't quite articulate this, was that his fund had zero early warning of distress until the moment it became acute.

For a European banker like me, the slow adoption of cov-lite in Europe has been both maddening and instructive. European lenders have always been more conservative than their US counterparts. The cultural preference for relationship banking, the influence of the German banking model, and the simple reality that European insolvency proceedings are more intricate and expensive than Chapter 11 — all of this meant that European credit agreements continued to feature robust maintenance covenant packages well into the 2010s. Even now, a traditional European leveraged loan from a bank syndicate will typically include leverage and interest coverage maintenance tests.

But the pressure toward cov-lite has been relentless. Sponsors pushing for leverage headroom. Arranging banks competing to win mandates by offering lighter documentation. Institutional investors — CLO managers, debt funds, insurance companies — making similar calculations that tighter financial structures would be acceptable if the pricing compensated them adequately. And so, gradually, European maintenance covenants have become less onerous. We've seen thresholds pushed higher. We've seen testing frequencies shift from quarterly to annual. We've seen carveouts and relief mechanisms multiply. The architecture has remained, but the teeth have been filed down.

In this chapter, I want to take you through the mechanics of financial covenants — both the maintenance tests that still exist and the incurrence tests that have largely replaced them. I want to show you how these numbers are calculated, how they're manipulated, and what they actually reveal (and fail to reveal) about a company's financial condition. I want to walk you through real cases where covenant breaches became crises, and where sponsors used cures and amendments to stay one step ahead of the lender. And I want to be honest about what the covenant-lite world means: it means that equity holders have won a remarkable victory in shifting risk back to debt holders, and that victory has been expressed in the language of "flexibility" and "commercial reasonableness" — which are precisely the arguments that make you most vulnerable.

19.2 Maintenance vs. Incurrence: The Fundamental Divide

The distinction between a maintenance covenant and an incurrence covenant is, in theory, simple. In practice, it determines everything about how a loan operates.

A maintenance covenant is tested at specified intervals — quarterly, or more rarely annually — regardless of what the company is doing. You test on your earnings for the quarter. You measure leverage as of quarter-end. You calculate interest coverage based on the twelve months of EBITDA you've earned. If you fail the test, you're in breach, and the lender has remedies. These remedies might include the right to accelerate, the right to increase the interest rate, the right to block certain actions, or the right to call for an equity cure. The point is that the lender has leverage — because the test is happening whether the company wants it to or not.

An incurrence covenant, by contrast, is triggered only when the company takes a specific action. Typically, the actions that trigger incurrence testing are: additional debt issuance, acquisitions above a certain size, restricted payments (dividends, special distributions), or investments outside the ordinary course. The company might fail an incurrence covenant test — say, they want to issue additional debt and the pro forma leverage calculation exceeds the threshold — but they don't have to do the action. They simply don't issue the debt. The breach only occurs if they ignore the covenant and proceed anyway.

From a lender's perspective, the difference is profound. A maintenance covenant is an early warning system. If leverage starts trending upward, or EBITDA starts declining, you see it quarterly. You have time to react — to demand an equity cure, to initiate an amendment discussion, to tighten the terms of the credit if possible. You're like a doctor running regular blood tests. You spot the disease before it becomes terminal.

An incurrence covenant, by contrast, is a permission slip. It says: the company can operate with complete freedom, as long as they don't take certain actions. But the company controls when those actions are taken. They can manage their own covenant tests by simply not doing the thing that triggers them. And if they do trigger a test and fail it, they can usually negotiate around it. The lender's leverage is weakest at the moment of breach, because the company is only breaching because they're doing something they want to do, and they're willing to negotiate.

Over the course of the financial crisis and the recovery that followed, I watched this distinction play out in real time. In 2007-2008, when the economy seized up, companies that had maintenance covenants were caught cold. Their leverage was rising, their EBITDA was falling, and they had no discretion — the tests happened whether the company wanted them to or not. J. Crew, Serta Simmons, Boardriders — all of these companies bumped into maintenance covenants that forced renegotiations and restructurings. The lenders had leverage because they had data, and the data was unforgiving.

By contrast, companies with covenant-lite structures had more breathing room. No quarterly tests meant no forced confrontations. The sponsors could still operate, still make decisions, and the lenders were largely blind to deterioration until it became severe.

The industry — lenders and sponsors alike — drew different lessons from this. Lenders learned that they needed to understand covenants better, to test them more carefully, to negotiate them more aggressively. Sponsors learned that they had successfully moved the risk to lenders, and that the market would reward them for it. Arranging banks learned that sponsors would pay for leverage, and leverage was easier to sell if it came without the anchor of tight maintenance covenants.

The "early warning" vs. "permission slip" debate has never been resolved. Intellectually, most lenders understand that maintenance covenants are superior protection. Practically, most lenders have accepted cov-lite because they've accepted that the alternative — losing the mandate to a more aggressive competitor — is worse. The result is that most leveraged loans today are cov-lite or near-cov-lite. Maintenance testing, when it exists, is often so wide as to be almost meaningless. A leverage covenant tested at 5.5x or higher with multiple carveouts and add-backs is not really an early warning system — it's just barely a permission slip.

In Europe, we've resisted this somewhat longer, but the resistance is crumbling. The new generation of European sponsors — more influenced by US practice, more aggressive in their capital structures — have pushed their banks to accept cov-lite. And the arranging banks, competing for mandates in a tight market, have obliged. The result is that even traditional European bank-led syndications are increasingly cov-lite, or feature maintenance covenants so attenuated that they've become almost ceremonial.

The consequence of all this is that lenders are now, in my view, significantly under-protected. We're running a credit market where institutions that have provided hundreds of millions of euros in leverage to companies have almost no quarterly visibility into whether those companies are deteriorating. We're relying on the integrity of financial reporting — which is better than it was, but still imperfect — and on the market's ability to reprice risk in secondary market trading. We're betting that sponsors will operate in good faith and that bad situations will develop slowly enough that we'll have time to react. Those are all losing bets, over time.

But I don't control the market. I can only navigate it. And so I've learned to live with cov-lite, to scrutinise the incurrence covenants more carefully, and to pay more attention to other protections — security, guarantees, priority, the business itself. The covenant-lite world demands more skill from lenders, more analysis of underlying cash generation, more judgment. It also makes us more passive, because we have fewer tools to force action. And it makes us more exposed when things go wrong.

19.3 The Leverage Ratio Test

Of all the financial covenants, the leverage ratio is the most important and the most misunderstood. Everyone thinks they know what it means to be "leveraged at 5x." But the devil is in the definitions, and I've spent enough time fighting over leverage calculations to know that the phrase "5x net leverage" is essentially meaningless without another fifty pages of definitions.

The leverage ratio, at its simplest, is debt divided by EBITDA. The higher the leverage, the greater the financial risk. A company with 5x leverage has five years of earnings to pay off its debt (in theory). A company with 3x leverage has only three years. Lenders prefer lower leverage because it means greater protection: the company has less debt relative to its earning power.

But here's where it gets complicated. There are several different leverage ratios, and they matter.

Total net leverage divides total debt by EBITDA. This is the broadest measure. It includes all forms of debt — bank debt, bonds, finance leases, guarantees, off-balance-sheet obligations. And the "net" part means you subtract cash. So if a company has €500 million in total debt and €100 million in cash, the net debt is €400 million. If EBITDA is €100 million, the net leverage is 4.0x. This is the headline number that sponsors and lenders usually throw around.

Senior secured net leverage is more granular. It measures only the debt that is secured by the company's collateral — typically the bank debt and any secured bonds. It doesn't include unsecured debt. So if the same company has €300 million in senior secured debt, €200 million in unsecured bonds, €100 million in cash, and €100 million of EBITDA, the senior secured net leverage is (300 - 100) / 100 = 2.0x. This number matters because it tells you how much leverage is actually supported by hard assets. An unsecured bondholder cares about total leverage. A secured lender cares about senior secured leverage — because that's what their security covers.

First lien net leverage is even narrower. It measures only debt that has first-lien security (the most senior collateral). In many structures, the bank debt is first-lien, the bonds are second-lien. So first lien net leverage might be (250 - 100) / 100 = 1.5x. This number matters enormously for the bank lenders, because it tells them how much cushion they have between their debt and their collateral.

The "net" part — subtracting cash — is where the fun starts. What counts as cash? Unrestricted cash in bank accounts, obviously. But what about restricted cash? Cash held in a Dutch auction treasury? Cash in escrow pending some condition? What about short-term securities, money market funds, standby letters of credit? Each credit agreement defines "cash equivalents" differently, and these definitions matter because they affect the leverage calculation.

I worked on a transaction once where the company had €80 million in what it wanted to count as "cash" — but it was actually restricted cash held in a customer deposit account pending final contract performance. When the customer received the final deliverable, the cash would go to the customer. It wasn't really the company's money. We fought hard not to count it, and eventually we won, which meant that the leverage calculation was €80 million higher, and the company was in breach. The amendment that followed that discovery was expensive and time-consuming. We should have settled the definition of cash at the outset.

The "netting" question also extends to revolving credit facilities. A company might have a €150 million revolving credit facility (RCF), with only €50 million drawn. Should you count the undrawn portion as cash? The standard answer in recent years has been: no, only the actual cash counts. The undrawn RCF is a liquidity safety valve, but it's not the same as cash. However, in some older credit agreements, there are carveouts that allow netting of the undrawn RCF under certain circumstances — typically if the RCF is otherwise unencumbered and available.

This matters enormously in stressed situations. A company might have €200 million in net debt and €100 million of EBITDA, so 2.0x leverage. That looks fine. But if the €200 million includes €100 million in unrestricted cash that's actually meant for working capital, the real leverage is actually higher. Or if the €100 million undrawn RCF isn't actually available because of financial covenants or cross-default issues, then the liquidity position is worse than it looks.

Pro forma leverage calculations are where the real manipulation happens. In the context of an incurrence covenant test — say, the company wants to issue new debt — they calculate what leverage would be "pro forma" for the new transaction. This means they adjust for the effect of the new debt (increasing leverage) and for the use of proceeds (which might reduce leverage if the proceeds are used to pay down debt, or increase it if used for an acquisition).

Here's where I've seen the most egregious abuses. A company wants to issue €200 million in debt to acquire another company. They run a pro forma leverage calculation. They include the new €200 million in debt (which increases leverage). But they also include "run-rate" adjustments for the acquired company's EBITDA, even though the acquisition closes mid-quarter and the company won't actually earn that EBITDA for months. They include synergies that they say will be "realized" in the pro forma period, even though they haven't happened yet. They adjust for "known and quantifiable" cost reductions that the investment banker assures them will happen.

The credit agreement will typically say that pro forma adjustments must be "reasonable" and "made in good faith," but these terms are almost never tested. A sponsor's investment banker will produce a 40-page model showing leverage "adjustments" that would make a CFO blush. And because the company controls the numbers, and the lenders don't have the resources or expertise to thoroughly forensically audit every pro forma model, these adjustments often slide through.

I remember a case where a company wanted to issue debt to acquire a target. The combined company, they claimed, would have EBITDA of €150 million — enough to meet their leverage covenant. The problem was that €40 million of that EBITDA was supposed to come from "synergies" — cost reductions and revenue enhancements that hadn't been executed. We pushed back hard. We said: you can show us the cost reductions if they're already in process. We can include half of the anticipated run-rate. But we're not going to include synergies that exist only in a pitch book. Eventually the deal got done at a lower leverage, which meant the sponsor had to put in more equity. But I've seen plenty of cases where the lenders just capitulate and accept the aggressive pro forma.

Step-downs are another critical element of leverage testing. A credit agreement might provide that leverage can be 5.0x in years one through three, but then steps down to 4.5x in year four and 4.0x in year five. The idea is that over time, the company should be deleveraging — using EBITDA to pay down debt rather than to fund dividends or acquisitions. The step-down creates an incentive for the company to deleverage, and it provides the lender with tightening protection over time.

But step-downs are also areas where sponsors negotiate relief. A step-down to 4.5x in year four might seem to tighten protection, but if the leverage covenant is only tested annually, and then only if an incurrence event occurs, the step-down might never actually bite. Or the credit agreement might provide for a "leverage holiday" — a suspension of the step-down in years when the company's underlying business is struggling. Or it might provide for a "covenant reset" every two years, where the step-down schedule resets if the lender consents.

The practical reality is that leverage ratio testing has become so attenuated in most new transactions that it's almost theatrical. The thresholds are set so high that under normal circumstances, a company will rarely breach them. It takes a genuinely catastrophic deterioration in the business — a recession, a loss of major customer, a technology disruption — to actually trigger a leverage breach. And when that happens, the company is usually in such bad shape that a covenant waiver is the least of its problems. The real negotiation is about what happens next: restructuring, refinancing, or liquidation.

19.4 The Interest Coverage Test

If leverage is about debt relative to earnings, interest coverage is about earnings relative to debt servicing costs. The interest coverage ratio divides EBITDA (or sometimes Adjusted EBITDA, or sometimes just operating cash flow) by interest expense. A company with €100 million in EBITDA and €20 million in annual interest expense has 5.0x interest coverage. They earn five times what they need to pay in interest.

The fixed charge coverage ratio is a related concept, and it's typically more restrictive than simple interest coverage. It measures not just interest, but interest plus principal payments plus other "fixed charges" like rent, lease payments, and contingent obligations. So a company might have 5.0x interest coverage but only 3.0x fixed charge coverage, because once you include all the fixed obligations, the cushion shrinks.

Interest coverage covenants are, in my view, much more useful than leverage covenants. Here's why: leverage is a backward-looking stock measure. It tells you what your balance sheet looked like at a point in time. Interest coverage is a forward-looking flow measure. It tells you whether the company is currently generating enough cash to service its debt. A company can have high leverage but strong interest coverage (earnings are growing, leverage is coming down). Or it can have moderate leverage but weak interest coverage (earnings are declining, the company is in cash burn mode).

When interest coverage starts to deteriorate, that's an early warning sign. The company is not generating enough cash to comfortably pay its debt service. It's either going to have to cut costs, increase cash collection, or refinance. All of those are things that a lender wants to know about, and wants to be able to influence.

The calculation of interest coverage sounds straightforward but has enormous complications. Start with EBITDA — earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization. That should be earnings before you account for financing costs. But what's the right numerator? Is it last quarter's EBITDA? The last twelve months of EBITDA (LTM)? A run-rate or annualised number? For a company with highly seasonal earnings, this choice matters enormously. Retail companies have higher EBITDA in the fourth quarter. Ski resorts have higher EBITDA in winter. If you test interest coverage on Q4 numbers, you might get a false sense of comfort. If you test on LTM, you smooth out the seasonality.

The denominator — interest expense — also has complexities. Does it include interest on finance leases? What about interest accrued but not yet paid? What about the accretion of original issue discount on bonds issued at a discount? What about the interest rate that would be payable on floating rate debt, assuming a certain LIBOR or EURIBOR rate? If debt is swapped to floating, do you include the economic interest cost or just the contractual cost?

And here's the kicker: the definition of "interest expense" for covenant testing is almost never the same as GAAP interest expense. The credit agreement will define a specific "interest expense" number that's used for covenant testing, and it's usually more expansive than GAAP. It includes things like the interest element of finance leases, the implied interest cost on contingent obligations, and often includes a "assumed rate" on unhedged floating rate debt — typically LIBOR or EURIBOR plus a spread.

I once worked on a covenant test where a company had issued floating-rate debt and entered into an interest rate swap, so their economic interest cost was fixed. But the credit agreement required that they test interest coverage including an "assumed" LIBOR of 5%, even though actual LIBOR was 1%. This meant that their interest coverage appeared much weaker than it actually was. It's a sledgehammer of a protection tool, but it creates a perverse incentive: the company would be better off hedging less, because that would make their covenant math look better, even though it would actually increase their economic risk.

Interest coverage covenants, particularly in European credit agreements, often have minimums of 2.5x to 3.0x. This sounds like it should provide decent protection, but in practice it's a relatively loose covenant. A company that's earning 3.0x interest coverage is still generating significant EBITDA cushion. The real trouble usually starts when interest coverage falls below 2.0x, or when it's declining rapidly toward that level. A company at 3.0x but trending down is more concerning than a company that's stable at 3.0x.

The interaction between interest coverage and floating rate debt is crucial. In a rising rate environment, a company's interest coverage can deteriorate rapidly as LIBOR or EURIBOR moves up. I remember the period of 2022-2023, when central banks were aggressively raising rates. Companies that had massive amounts of floating-rate debt saw their interest expense increase by 30%, 40%, 50% or more. Their leverage stayed the same, but their interest coverage fell off a cliff. A company that had been comfortably above its interest coverage covenant found itself suddenly in jeopardy — not because the business deteriorated, but because rates went up.

This is why debt structures usually include some form of rate hedging. A company might hedge 75% of its floating-rate debt exposure to fixed rates, or might use an interest rate cap to limit the impact of rising rates. But hedging is expensive, and sponsors don't like to pay for it, so many structures run with minimal hedging. The result is that in rising rate environments, lenders get nervous, and rightly so.

19.5 The Equity Cure Mechanism

The equity cure is one of the most important — and misunderstood — tools in the leveraged finance toolkit. It's a mechanism that allows a company to temporarily cure a financial covenant breach by injecting equity, rather than by actually improving operating performance.

Here's how it works at its simplest: a company is in breach of its leverage covenant. Leverage is 5.1x, but the covenant requires 5.0x or lower. Normally, this would be a default. The lenders would have the right to accelerate the debt, increase the interest rate, or demand an amendment. But the credit agreement includes an "equity cure" provision. This provision allows the company to inject equity capital into the borrower, and for purposes of calculating whether they're in breach, the borrowed money is deemed to be applied to pay down debt.

So if the company injects €10 million of equity, and EBITDA is €100 million, then for covenant testing purposes, the debt is reduced by €10 million, and leverage becomes (total debt - 10) / 100, instead of total debt / 100. If that brings leverage down to below 5.0x, the company is no longer in breach.

The "deemed EBITDA" cure is a variation on this. Instead of injecting equity to pay down debt, the company can inject equity, and the credit agreement deems an additional amount of EBITDA to have been earned. The idea is that the equity injection is in the form of cash, which the company can then use operationally, which will theoretically generate additional EBITDA. So if the company injects €10 million in equity, and the credit agreement assumes a 10x multiple on that EBITDA (which is the reciprocal of the current leverage), the deemed EBITDA might be €1 million. Leverage then becomes (total debt) / (EBITDA + 1), which might be enough to bring the company into compliance.

The deemed EBITDA cure is economically bizarre, because it assumes that the equity injection will immediately generate EBITDA at exactly the leverage multiple that's relevant to the covenant. It's a mathematical convenience, not economic reality. But it exists because it's useful: it allows a company to cure a breach without having to actually repay debt.

Both types of cures are usually limited. The credit agreement might say: you can cure three times in any twelve-month period. Or: you can only cure twice. Or: you can only cure on consecutive quarters if the first cure was triggered by a different covenant. These limitations exist because lenders are nervous about companies that repeatedly cure breaches. If a company needs to cure quarterly, that's a sign that it's chronically under-performing, and a cure is just delaying the day of reckoning.

There's also usually a cure window — a time period within which a cure can be applied. It might be: the company can cure within thirty days of the covenant test date. Or: within fifteen business days of the lender providing notice of breach. The window is important because it affects the timing of negotiations. If the window is short, the sponsor needs to move quickly. If it's long, the company has time to get board approvals, document the equity injection, and negotiate terms.

The European approach to equity cures has historically been more restrictive than the US approach. US lenders have become comfortable with generous equity cure mechanisms — sometimes up to four cures per year, with minimal restrictions on the number of times a particular covenant can be cured. European lenders, influenced by more conservative credit philosophies, traditionally limited equity cures to once per covenant per year. But as European arrangers compete with US banks, this distinction has blurred.

There's also a distinction between a "debt repayment" cure and a "deemed EBITDA" cure. The debt repayment cure is cleaner conceptually: actual cash is injected, actual debt is paid down. No deeming or assumptions. The deemed EBITDA cure is more sponsor-friendly: it requires less cash injection (because you're creating EBITDA out of thin air). The more cures depend on EBITDA deemed in connection with an equity injection, the less I trust the financial health of the company.

I've seen equity cures used effectively and responsibly. A company hits a temporary covenant breach because of a one-time charge, or because of a timing issue in working capital. The sponsor injects equity to cure the breach, and the company continues. The cure is used, documented, and the company moves on. I've also seen equity cures abused. A company is in chronic breach. It cures quarterly. The lender watches, knowing that the company is not actually improving, but unable to force action because the contractual right to cure exists. Eventually the company runs out of equity to inject, or the sponsor decides it's not worth injecting any more, and suddenly the situation becomes acute.

The cases of J. Crew, Serta Simmons, and Boardriders are instructive here. All three companies had equity cure provisions, and all three companies used them at various points. But cures only buy time. They don't fix the underlying problem. Eventually, if the business doesn't improve, cures become impossible. The sponsor's capital is exhausted, or the sponsor simply decides it's not worth putting good money after bad.

19.6 Financial Covenants in European Leveraged Loans

European leveraged loan structures have historically featured more robust financial covenant packages than their US counterparts. But this distinction is increasingly blurred as European practice converges toward US norms.

The traditional European approach to covenant packages involved a "springing" leverage covenant on the revolving credit facility. The RCF is typically an undrawn facility that sits on the balance sheet as a liquidity backstop. Unlike term debt, which is drawn at closing and has fixed amortisation schedules, the RCF is available to be drawn as the company needs liquidity. This creates a problem for covenant testing: if the RCF is undrawn, when is it tested for financial covenants? The answer is that it "springs" — it becomes subject to financial covenants only when it's drawn above a certain threshold (typically 35% or 50% utilisation). Until that threshold is crossed, the RCF is covenant-free.

The thinking behind this structure is that the RCF represents short-term liquidity management. As long as the company isn't heavily using its RCF, lenders don't need to test financial covenants. Once the RCF is heavily drawn, the lender wants to know that the company is on track financially. So the covenant springs, and the company has to satisfy leverage or interest coverage tests.

But in practice, this creates a perverse incentive. A company that's deteriorating can manage its covenant exposure by avoiding drawing the RCF beyond the threshold. It might stagger draws, or use other financing sources, or manage working capital more aggressively, all to keep the RCF below the trigger. Lenders lose visibility. They don't see a covenant test that would alert them to deterioration.

The testing dates and compliance certificate mechanics in European structures are also different from US practice. In the US, financial covenants are typically tested and certified on specific dates — quarter-end, usually. The company prepares a certificate of compliance showing that the covenants have been satisfied. If the certificate is late, or the company doesn't certify, there's often a default. The discipline is enforced through the calendar.

In Europe, the certificate process is more flexible. A company might have thirty days after quarter-end to deliver the certificate. Or the lender might waive the requirement for a certificate if the company provides audited financial statements (which take longer). Or there might be a grace period if the certificate is "reasonably expected" to be delivered. This flexibility is meant to be accommodating. It's also meant to reflect the reality that European financial reporting is often slower and more involved than US reporting (because of different accounting standards, audit requirements, etc.).

This flexibility creates what I call the "snooze you lose" problem. If a company is going to be in breach, it has an incentive to delay delivering the compliance certificate as long as possible. It can use the delay to negotiate with lenders, to arrange an amendment, or to effect a cure. If the certificate is late, technically the company is in breach of the obligation to deliver the certificate. But the actual financial covenant breach hasn't been formally documented. This gives the company negotiating leverage: they can say to the lender, "If you acknowledge that the certificate is overdue, we'll both acknowledge that a covenant breach has occurred. But maybe we can negotiate around that." The lender is in a bind. Demand the certificate, and the company is in formal breach. Don't demand it, and the situation festers.

I once worked on a transaction where a company was clearly going to be in covenant breach as of the quarter-end. The lender demanded the compliance certificate on day 30 after quarter-end (the due date). The company asked for a five-day extension, which was reasonable and was granted. On day 35, the company delivered a certificate showing breach and immediately requested a waiver. By delaying delivery, the company had bought itself five days of additional negotiating time, and had shifted the conversation from "you're in breach" to "we agree you're in breach; now what terms do you want for waiving it?" It's a subtle difference, but it gave the company leverage.

The other distinctly European issue is cross-border covenant testing. A typical European leveraged loan might be structured with a borrower in the Netherlands (or Luxembourg, or some other jurisdiction), with subsidiaries in multiple countries. Guarantees might be limited by local law. Collateral can be difficult to perfect across borders. And financial covenants are typically tested on the basis of the borrower's standalone financials, plus the financials of its material subsidiaries on a consolidated basis.

This creates complexity when you're calculating EBITDA. The borrower might have minimal operational EBITDA itself; it might be an operating company's parent. The EBITDA that matters is the EBITDA of the operating subsidiaries. But the subsidiaries might have different fiscal year-ends, different accounting standards, different reporting timelines. Consolidating those numbers into a single covenant test is messy.

19.7 EBITDA and the Financial Covenant

EBITDA — earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization — is the numerator in almost every financial covenant. It's the measure of operating cash generation, theoretically purified of financing, tax, and accounting effects. And it's one of the most manipulated numbers in leveraged finance.

The problem is that "EBITDA" is not a GAAP measure. It's a definition that exists in the credit agreement. And that definition can include a vast array of add-backs: stock-based compensation, severance costs, restructuring charges, transaction costs, non-recurring items, management fees, costs associated with acquisition synergy realization, costs related to cybersecurity attacks, costs of litigation, and on and on.

Each add-back makes some sense individually. You're trying to calculate what the company's "normalized" operating earnings would be, absent one-time or non-recurring items. But collectively, these add-backs can inflate EBITDA substantially. A company might have GAAP operating earnings of €80 million, but credit agreement EBITDA (after add-backs) of €110 million. That 38% uplift is material.

Here's what I've seen: a company undertakes a restructuring. The restructuring costs are €15 million. The credit agreement allows add-back of "restructuring costs, including severance and related costs." So the company adds back the €15 million, and EBITDA goes up by €15 million. The covenant is satisfied. But what's happened economically? The company has spent cash on the restructuring. That cash is gone. It's not going to generate future earnings. The add-back is economically nonsensical — it's as if the cost never happened.

The best credit agreements have disciplined EBITDA definitions with narrow add-backs and specific dollar caps on how much can be added back in any given period. The worst credit agreements have open-ended add-backs where "reasonableness" is the only limit. And because the company's management, auditors, and lenders all want the deal to work, there's pressure to be generous in add-back interpretations.

Run-rate adjustments are another major source of EBITDA manipulation. Suppose a company makes an acquisition mid-quarter, or hires a major customer mid-month. The pro forma EBITDA calculation will include a "run-rate" adjustment for the new EBITDA expected from the acquisition or customer, even though it hasn't been earned yet. The idea is that you're calculating what EBITDA would have been if the acquisition or customer had been in place for the full period.

This is reasonable in concept. It's important to understand the pro forma leverage impact of an acquisition. But in practice, run-rate adjustments are massively optimistic. The acquired company's historical EBITDA might be €20 million, but you're adding back €30 million in "run-rate" because you "know" that the customer will have a higher margin, or the acquired company's costs will be lower once integrated. The confidence in these assumptions is often unwarranted.

I worked on a transaction where the sponsor wanted to acquire a bolt-on company for €80 million. The acquisition was supposed to generate minimal EBITDA in year one (just the acquired company's baseline €10 million) but €25 million in year two due to synergies. For the pro forma leverage test on the debt issuance, the sponsor wanted to include the year-two run-rate EBITDA of €25 million, even though it was acquisition day. The leverage calculation was impossible without including these run-rate adjustments. We pushed back, but ultimately accepted a compromise: we included 50% of the expected synergies (€7.5 million), contingent on the sponsor providing a detailed integration plan and monthly reporting on synergy realization. The deal got done, but it was leverage-positive only if the synergies materialized. They did, mostly. But that's not always the case.

The "circularity" problem in EBITDA testing is subtle but important. Suppose a company buys back €50 million of debt with equity proceeds. That reduces interest expense. Interest is subtracted in calculating net leverage, so reducing interest expense means reducing the denominator, which means improving leverage. But the interest savings don't come from the business. They come from a financial transaction. The business's operating performance is unchanged, but the covenant math looks better.

This is why some lenders are moving toward "interest-based" leverage calculations, where you measure leverage as total debt divided by EBITDA, but measure interest coverage as EBITDA divided by interest expense. Separating the two metrics prevents this circularity. But most credit agreements still use a single leverage metric, and the metric is almost always affected by financing changes.

19.8 Case Studies in Financial Covenant Stress

J. Crew is the poster child for covenant-lite gone wrong. Buyout by TPG in 2011 at the height of the market. The capital structure was aggressive — leverage approaching 5x. But because it was cov-lite, there were no quarterly tests. The covenant package was strictly incurrence-based. The company couldn't issue additional debt if leverage exceeded a certain level, and it couldn't make restricted payments (dividends) if leverage was too high. But as long as the company didn't take these actions, it had complete operational freedom.

For a few years, this worked fine. J. Crew was generating cash, paying down debt, managing its capital structure. But starting around 2013-2014, the business deteriorated. Fast fashion, e-commerce competition, changing consumer preferences — all of these pressures started hitting J. Crew. The company's EBITDA began to decline. Leverage, which had been declining, started to rise again.

The problem was that TPG and the lenders had limited visibility into this deterioration. There were no quarterly covenant tests. The company reported publicly (as a private company with registered debt, J. Crew had to file annual reports), but public reports came late, and by the time they did, the damage was visible in the capital structure rather than in the covenants.

By 2017-2018, J. Crew's leverage was rising, margins were compressing, and the cov-lite structure meant that the lenders had limited recourse. They couldn't demand an equity cure (because there were no maintenance covenants to cure). They could demand higher interest, but only at refinancing. They could push for amendments, but the company knew that the lenders had limited leverage. The situation deteriorated. J. Crew eventually filed for bankruptcy in 2020, and the lenders took significant losses.

The lesson from J. Crew wasn't that cov-lite was inherently bad — though I think it is. The lesson was that lenders need to understand the underlying business. J. Crew's competitive position was precarious. The company's business model was vulnerable to exactly the pressures that eventually hit it. Lenders who understood fast fashion retail should have been nervous about this structure from the start, regardless of covenants.

Serta Simmons is a different story. Here, the company hit a maintenance covenant breach in the early days of the financial crisis. Leverage spiked. The company couldn't meet its tests. At this point, the lenders had leverage: the company was in breach, and the lenders could force a restructuring or demand an amendment on tough terms.

What happened next is instructive. The sponsors and lenders negotiated an amendment. The covenant thresholds were relaxed (leverage was allowed to spike higher temporarily). But in exchange, lenders got additional rights. The interest rate was ratcheted up. The covenant package was tightened in other areas. Restricted payments were suspended. The company had to implement a mandatory prepayment requirement if leverage didn't improve by a certain date.

This is how covenant breaches should play out: the breach forces a renegotiation, and the cost of that renegotiation is the price of flexibility that the company got. In the Serta case, the company survived the crisis, deleveraged over the following years, and the situation normalized. The breach was painful, but it was also informative — it signaled to management and sponsors that the business model needed to change.

Boardriders (the company that owned Quiksilver) is another case study in covenant stress and management. The company faced multiple covenant breaches during the financial crisis. What's interesting about Boardriders is that the sponsors — Altamont Capital and TPG — were willing to put in significant additional equity to cure breaches and support the company. Rather than walking away, they doubled down. The company eventually emerged from distress and was successfully refinanced. That's not a common outcome, and it's a testament to the sponsors' belief in the business.

Casino Guichard is instructive as a European case. The company, a major French retailer, undertook an aggressive leverage-financed expansion in the early 2010s. The capital structure was complex — multiple senior and subordinated bond tranches, alongside traditional bank debt. As the retail environment deteriorated in the mid-2010s, the company found itself in covenant stress. The lenders were fragmented: some wanted to push for restructuring, others wanted to work with the company.

The company's response was to undertake asset sales — selling high-quality retail properties and leasing them back. The proceeds were used to pay down senior debt. This reduced leverage in a mechanical sense, but it didn't address the underlying business deterioration. It was, in essence, a form of value leakage (which we'll discuss in the next chapter). By selling real estate and leasing it back, the company got cash today but higher expenses tomorrow.

The Casino case is a reminder that covenant math is only one piece of the credit analysis. A company can manipulate its way through covenant tests through asset sales, add-backs, pro forma assumptions, and cures. But if the underlying business is broken, that manipulation only delays the inevitable.

19.9 The Market Trend: Less Protection, More Risk

The long-term trend in leveraged finance is unmistakably toward lighter covenant packages. Data from various loan rating agencies and market participants shows that cov-lite deals have gone from roughly 10-15% of the market in the early 2000s, to 50%+ by 2020, to over 70% in certain segments and regions by 2023.

The argument for cov-lite is straightforward: operational flexibility. A CEO managing a company doesn't want to be constrained by quarterly covenant tests. If the company is trying to manage through a difficult period, the last thing the CEO wants is lenders calling to discuss a covenant waiver. If the company is trying to execute an acquisition, the CEO doesn't want to be blocked by pro forma leverage calculations. The covenant-lite structure gives the management team freedom to operate.

From a sponsor's perspective, the argument is about value creation. If a company has covenant-light debt, it can be more aggressive with its capital structure. It can lever up more because it doesn't have covenant constraints. That leverage can be used to return capital to equity holders or to fund growth. The sponsor gets more upside. And indeed, cov-lite has become the standard in sponsor-backed transactions because sponsors view it as a capital structure optimisation.

The counter-argument is that lenders are taking on much more risk. Without maintenance covenants, lenders have no early warning of distress. They're relying on public information and market pricing to signal deterioration. And even when they see warning signs, they have limited tools to force action. They can threaten default and acceleration, but if the company is in covenant breach only on an incurrence basis, acceleration might not be available. They have to negotiate from weakness.

The private credit market — including direct lending funds and asset-based lenders — has responded by moving in the opposite direction. These lenders are re-introducing maintenance covenants, springing leverage tests, and more restrictive covenant packages. The thinking is that as the syndicated loan market became lighter, these lenders could differentiate themselves by providing tighter monitoring and control. They can do this because they're often lending to mid-market companies where they have more negotiating leverage, and where the capital structures are less aggressive.

I suspect that over the next cycle — and we're building toward some kind of correction in leverage and credit metrics — the market will re-discover the value of covenants. Companies that maintain cov-lite structures will be punished in refinancings. Lenders will demand covenant-heavy packages in new transactions. The pendulum will swing back.

But probably not all the way. The industry has learned that covenants impose costs. There's real friction to quarterly testing. There's real value in operational flexibility. So the eventual outcome is probably a middle ground: covenants come back, but they're lighter and more flexible than they were in the pre-cov-lite era. Maybe leverage thresholds are 4.5x instead of 3.5x. Maybe testing is annual instead of quarterly. Maybe there are generous carveouts for one-time items. But the idea that lenders have zero visibility and zero ability to force action — that extreme probably doesn't survive the next downturn.

19.10 Covenant Suspension and Fall-Away Mechanics

The theoretical answer to the covenant-lite problem — at least from a lender's perspective — is the suspension covenant. The idea is elegant: you accept lighter covenants during the growth phase of the company, but those covenants spring back into effect (are "reinstated") if the company's credit quality deteriorates. Or alternatively, the covenants "fall away" permanently once the company reaches a certain level of credit quality — typically an investment grade rating or a successful IPO combined with a leverage test.

In theory, suspension mechanics offer a middle path. Sponsors get operational flexibility early, when they need it most. Lenders get protection later, when the company has stabilized or improved. It's the kind of elegant compromise that makes sense in committee meetings and pitch books. In practice, suspension mechanics have become one of the most dangerous and manipulated elements of modern leveraged credit agreements.

19.10.1 The Mechanics of Suspension and Fall-Away

Let me start with the baseline structures, because there are important distinctions that get glossed over.

A fall-away covenant is a covenant that, once triggered, is permanently removed from the credit agreement. This is rare. I've seen it in a handful of transactions — most notably Contour Global, which had covenants that fell away upon receipt of an investment grade rating. Once triggered, those covenants were gone. The thinking was that an IG-rated company no longer needed the same level of restrictions. But this is an extreme position, and I'd never recommend it as a lender. A company can be IG one quarter and deteriorating the next. An IG rating is a backward-looking assessment, not a guarantee of future performance.

Much more common is the suspension covenant. When triggered, the covenant is "suspended" — meaning that the company is not tested against that covenant threshold while the suspension condition remains in place. But the covenant is still on the books. If the suspension condition reverses — say, the company drops below investment grade — the covenant reinstates.

The investment grade trigger is the classic suspension mechanism. The definition usually requires either a single Moody's rating of Baa3 or above, or a single S&P rating of BBB- or above. Some deals — particularly in the crossover credit world where companies move in and out of IG status frequently — require that the company achieve two IG ratings, one from Moody's and one from S&P. This matters more than you'd think. A company might be Baa3 from Moody's (IG) and Ba1 from S&P (high-yield). Is it IG or not?

Here's the practical point: check whether the company is already IG on day one. I worked on a transaction for a large airline that thought its IPO would trigger investment grade status. The company was relying on covenant suspension to drive its leverage and capital structure. But it turned out that the rating agencies were hesitant — the company was Moody's Baa3 but S&P Ba1. Under a "one rating" trigger, it would have been in the clear. Under a "two ratings" trigger, it was stuck. The company's entire refinancing strategy had to be recalculated, which cost months and millions in fees. Read the rating agency commentary carefully. Don't assume anything.

The IPO-plus-leverage trigger is increasingly common in sponsor-backed transactions. The mechanics are: covenant suspension occurs if (1) the company completes a non-change-of-control IPO, and (2) leverage is below a specified level (typically 4.0x to 5.0x on a pro forma basis). The idea is that sponsors are willing to accept high leverage so long as there's a public market checking function, and once the company is public, covenant restrictions become less important.

But here's where it gets tricky. Some sponsors negotiate triggers that are even looser — suspension occurs with an IPO alone, or with any non-CoC transaction plus a leverage test, not necessarily an IPO. I've also seen deals where the leverage trigger is only tested on the day of the IPO, not on an ongoing basis. So if the company goes public at 4.5x leverage, the covenants suspend. Then the company's leverage rises to 6.0x. The covenants remain suspended because the trigger was met on day one of the IPO.

The reinstatement triggers — the conditions that bring covenants back into effect — are critical. Typically, covenants reinstates if: (1) the company loses its IG rating (falls back to high-yield), or (2) the company's leverage rises above a specified level (typically the IPO trigger level, or slightly higher), or (3) there's a default or event of default.

What's dangerous is that reinstatement is sometimes only prospective. The covenants reinstated going forward, but any actions or transactions taken during the suspension period are grandfathered — they remain effective even though they wouldn't have been permitted under the covenant that just reinstated.

19.10.2 The Real Problem: Suspension as a License to Lever and Manipulate

Here's where the warning lights should flash: suspension mechanics have become a playground for leverage optimization and covenant arbitrage.

The most dangerous modern trend is the standalone leverage trigger — a suspension covenant that triggers based solely on achieving a leverage level, with no requirement for investment grade status or IPO. I've seen deals where the leverage trigger is set at less than 0.5x below the opening leverage. So a company opening at 4.5x leverage gets covenant suspension at 4.0x. It means that leverage suspension is almost guaranteed to happen, essentially converting the deal to de facto covenant-lite.

But the real problem emerges when you combine suspend covenants with other loose covenant language. Consider a credit agreement with (1) a leverage-based covenant suspension, (2) a broad debt exclusion from the leverage calculation (common in deals with revolving credit facilities or acquisition facilities), and (3) minimal restrictions on asset sales and related-party transactions.

What you've created is an LME — a lightly monitored equity — abuse window. The company reaches its suspension threshold, covenants suspend. While covenants are suspended, the company (1) designates an unrestricted subsidiary, which isn't guaranteed by the parent company, (2) has the unrestricted subsidiary incur new debt, which doesn't count in leverage calculations, (3) upstreams the proceeds to the parent as a "loan," or (4) releverages its collateral by priming existing liens.

I've watched this play out in real deals. A sponsor-backed company opens at 4.5x leverage with tight leverage maintenance covenants and a covenant suspension trigger at 4.0x. The company's business deteriorates slightly, but it still hits 4.0x leverage. Covenants suspend. The company's CFO (under sponsor pressure) immediately initiates a plan: incur debt in an unrestricted subsidiary, relink collateral, and increase shareholder distributions. None of this would be possible if covenants were in effect.

The second dangerous trend is the suspension of security and guarantee requirements. Some deals — not many, but an increasing number — provide that guarantees and security requirements are suspended along with financial covenants. This means that once covenants suspend, a portfolio company might be able to escape the guarantee. Or collateral might be released.

I remember a case where a sponsor-backed software company had covenant suspension triggered by an IG rating. As part of the suspension, the requirement that subsidiary guarantees be maintained fell away. Once the main company had an IG rating, it no longer had to guarantee the debt of its subsidiaries. This meant the sponsor could move value out of guaranteed subsidiaries into non-guaranteed entities. The senior lenders had thought they had 100% of subsidiary guarantees. Suddenly they didn't.

The third issue is the treatment of covenant suspension in the context of cross-default and acceleration rights. Some credit agreements have been drafted with suspension language that's ambiguous: does the suspension suspend the entire covenant, or only the numerical test? If the covenant is suspended, can the lender still claim a default based on materiality qualifiers or other conditions in the covenant itself?

I worked on a covenant administration issue where a company's financial covenants were suspended due to an IG rating. But the credit agreement had language saying that even if the covenant is suspended, a covenant event of default still exists if the company's leverage reaches a specified "blow-out" level. The question was: can you have a suspended covenant that still triggers a default at an extreme level?

The answer turned out to be yes, according to the credit agreement's text. But it created a strange situation: the normal covenant test was suspended, but an extreme-case covenant event of default existed. It meant the company thought it had covenant relief, but actually it only had relief from normal covenant testing. The first time this issue came up in a stressed situation, it was a major source of conflict.

Post-Reinstatement Grandfathering

Here's the critical risk that most sponsors and lenders don't fully grapple with: the treatment of actions taken during suspension.

If covenants are suspended for 18 months, and during those 18 months the company incurs new debt, completes acquisitions, enters into related-party transactions, or makes restricted payments — what happens when covenants reinstate?

In a properly drafted credit agreement, reinstatement works like this: the covenant reinstates, and from that point forward, the company is tested against the covenant threshold. But all debt incurred during suspension is deemed to have been incurred under the original covenant. It counts toward baskets and thresholds. Restricted payments made during suspension don't count toward builder baskets. The company has to reset to zero on certain measurements (e.g., capex baskets, related-party transaction limits).

But in practice, many credit agreements have grandfathering language that protects actions taken during suspension from being undone or restricted. The language says something like: "Any actions taken during the suspension period shall remain in effect and shall not be reversed upon reinstatement."

This creates a one-way door. During suspension, the company can take aggressive actions. When covenants come back, those actions are locked in. The company can't be forced to unwind acquisitions or divest assets. Related-party transactions that occurred during suspension are grandfathered. Debt incurred during suspension remains on the balance sheet.

I've seen this structure weaponized. A sponsor deliberately engineers a situation where covenants will suspend, takes aggressive actions during the suspension period, and then when covenants reinstate (due to market deterioration), the company is locked into a capital structure with more leverage and less flexibility than the lender ever intended.

19.11 What Twenty Years Teaches

I've been through multiple cycles of covenant expansion and contraction, and here's what I've learned: covenants matter most when you don't think you need them.

When credit is loose and leverage is low, covenants feel like needless burden. The company is printing money, EBITDA is growing, leverage is falling. Covenant tests are passed with room to spare. Lenders feel protected, so they agree to lighter packages. Sponsors feel constrained, so they demand relief.

When credit gets tight and leverage is high, covenants are the difference between orderly restructuring and chaotic bankruptcy. A company with quarterly covenant tests has to face its problems early. A company with cov-lite can ignore problems until they're acute.

The art of being a lender in a cov-lite world is to build other forms of protection. More collateral. Earlier amortisation. Stricter accounting. More regular financial reporting. Better visibility into working capital and capital expenditure. Tighter restrictions on assets sales, acquisitions, and other transactions. You lose the early warning system of covenants, so you have to build visibility through other means.

But no amount of other protections fully compensates for the loss of maintenance covenants. Because covenants do something unique: they create a regular, predictable, contractually-mandated moment when the company has to demonstrate financial health. That moment is valuable. It's when lenders have leverage, when management has to focus on compliance, when sponsors have to make decisions about capital.

If I could change one thing about the current leveraged finance market, it would be to restore maintenance covenants to their position of primacy. Not heavy-handed covenants that constrain operational flexibility. But quarterly leverage and interest coverage tests that force transparency. That's not a controversial view among experienced lenders. It's just increasingly a view that's outvoted by sponsors and arranging banks.

SUMMARY: THE FINANCIAL COVENANT PRACTITIONER'S CHECKLIST

When you're negotiating or administering a financial covenant package, keep these principles in mind:

□ Maintenance covenants (quarterly tests) provide early warning; incurrence covenants (event-based) provide operational flexibility. Understand which you have and what that means for visibility and control.

□ Leverage ratios come in multiple versions: total net, senior secured net, first-lien net. Understand which metric matters for your position in the capital structure, and don't assume consistency across transactions.

□ Watch the cash netting definition closely. What counts as cash? Does it include restricted cash? Undrawn RCF? These details move leverage numbers materially.

□ Pro forma leverage calculations are highly subject to manipulation through run-rate adjustments and synergy assumptions. Build in a discipline: baseline data is historical, add-backs require documentary support, synergies are included at a discount to expectations.

□ Interest coverage testing is forward-looking and more informative than leverage. A company with declining interest coverage is in distress before leverage breaches. Pay more attention to coverage trends.

□ Equity cure provisions are useful but limited. If you're seeing regular cures, the company is not improving. Be prepared to push for amendment rather than accept repeated covenant cures.

□ EBITDA definitions are massively important and heavily negotiated. The difference between a tight definition and a loose definition can be 20-30% of EBITDA. Nail down add-back categories, put dollar caps on individual items, and require documentary support for non-recurring items.

□ Covenant-lite has won the market. Build in other protections: springing leverage covenants on RCFs, restrictions on asset sales and related-party transactions, tighter leverage step-downs, higher mandatory prepayment triggers.

□ Covenant suspension is not the same as no covenant. Understand the suspension trigger (investment grade, IPO-plus-leverage, or standalone leverage threshold). Check whether it's already satisfied on day one, and if not, assess the probability it will be triggered.

□ Be extremely cautious about covenant suspension triggers combined with permissive debt carveouts. A leverage suspension trigger paired with broad exclusions of acquisition facility debt or unrestricted subsidiary debt creates an LME abuse window. Resist such structures.

□ Reinstatement triggers matter as much as suspension triggers. Understand what brings covenants back into effect (rating downgrade, leverage deterioration, default). Make sure reinstatement is actually tested, not just theoretical.

□ Resist grandfathering language on reinstatement. Actions taken during suspension should be subject to the reinstated covenant. Insist that debt incurred, assets acquired, or payments made during suspension count against covenant baskets when covenants reinstall.

□ If suspension language waives or suspends guarantees and security requirements, that's a serious structural risk. Such provisions are not standard and should trigger heightened monitoring. Understand what value leaves the guarantor in a suspension period.

□ Test the company's capability to deliver compliance certificates. Late certificates are a warning sign. Build in cure windows and remedies for non-delivery.

□ Leverage step-downs matter only if they're actually tested. Make sure your incurrence events are broad enough that leverage tests bite when they matter.

□ European covenant practices are converging to US practice. Don't assume that an old-style European credit agreement has less robust covenants than a US equivalent. Read the details.

□ The covenant package is only as good as the lender's willingness to enforce it. Build in governance around covenant administration, escalation procedures for breaches, and a clear policy about waivers.
