CHAPTER 17: AMEND AND EXTEND TRANSACTIONS
The Art of Kicking the Can Down the Road (And When That's Actually Brilliant)

17.1 INTRODUCTION — THE MATURITY WALL PROBLEM

I remember the morning in 2007 when one of my portfolio companies — a European chemicals distributor we'd backed through the cycle — came to me with what seemed like a straightforward problem. Their senior term loan was maturing in eighteen months. The EBITDA was strong, the business was performing, everything was fine. Refinancing at better terms would be trivial. We had time. We could wait for the market window.

Then Lehman Brothers collapsed, and the world rewired itself in about seventy-two hours.

By December 2008, that chemicals distributor was facing a maturity wall with no market access. Refinancing was literally impossible — not expensive, not difficult, impossible. The credit markets had frozen so completely that even first-quartile credits couldn't get new money. Most banks were hoarding capital, retreating from business. The sponsor, the management team, even the borrower's auditors were all staring at a default scenario unless something radical changed.

That's when amend-to-extend transactions became not just a tactical tool, but a lifeline.

The amend-to-extend, or A&E as everyone calls it, is one of the most misunderstood provisions in modern credit documentation. On the surface, it's simple: it's a mechanism that allows a borrower to extend the maturity date of an existing loan without having to go to every single lender and convince them individually. Instead, the borrower negotiates with the lenders who are willing to agree — the "extending lenders" — and those lenders get compensated for their willingness. The lenders who don't want to extend keep their original maturity date and, critically, retain their original position.

What's elegant about this is that it solves a genuine problem without completely destroying the rights of lenders who want to exit. It creates a path forward when refinancing isn't possible. But it's also devilishly complex, full of hidden risks, and prone to being abused when sponsors get desperate.

Over my two decades in this business, I've been on every side of an A&E transaction: as a lender who desperately wanted out, as a banker advising a sponsor trying to extend, as a creditor's adviser negotiating the terms of extension, and as a portfolio manager trying to salvage value in a credit that was teetering on the brink. Each perspective teaches you something different, and none of them is simple.

The core question that defines whether an A&E is brilliant or catastrophic is this: Is the business actually capable of servicing the extended debt in the extended period? If yes, then everyone wins — borrowers get breathing room, extending lenders get paid for that patience, and non-extending lenders either exit at par (if they choose) or hold a senior position with an earlier maturity. If no — if the business is in structural decline and the A&E is simply deferring an inevitable default — then it becomes a complex game of musical chairs where some lenders get caught standing.

This chapter walks through the machinery of A&E transactions, the strategic decisions that drive them, the regional variations that can surprise you, and the very real risks that investors need to understand. I'll use real cases from post-2008 refinancing, the COVID-era extensions, and the 2022-2023 maturity wall that had everyone nervous again.

17.2 THE MECHANICS OF AMEND-TO-EXTEND

Let me explain how this actually works by walking through a hypothetical that's based on more deals than I can count.

Imagine a borrower, let's call it Broadline Industrial (fictional), with a €500 million senior term loan. The loan was issued in 2018 at LIBOR + 350, matures in May 2024. We're now in January 2023 — slightly over a year until maturity. The business has been hit by the rate shock of late 2022; banks are nervous; the sponsorship (a large European buyout shop) hasn't decided yet whether they want to refinance or sell. Refinancing terms are terrible — probably LIBOR + 500-600 for a full refinancing. The cost is prohibitive.

But the business is actually performing reasonably well. EBITDA is down from peak but still solidly in line with the covenant baseline. There's no covenant breach risk. The sponsor thinks they might be able to drive an exit in the next 24-36 months if they can just keep the credit stable. They don't want to refinance; they want to extend.

Enter the A&E provision in the credit agreement.

The A&E clause — which is usually found buried in Article 10 or similar in the operative agreements — establishes a process. Here's what the standard mechanics look like:

**Step One: The Borrower Proposes Extension Terms**

The borrower's advisers draw up a proposal. The proposal specifies:
- The new maturity date (typically 1-3 years out from the current maturity)
- The new pricing (the spread the extending lenders will receive)
- The OID (original issue discount) if any — a one-time fee paid at closing
- Any amendment to financial covenants (sometimes covenants are loosened slightly in an extension)
- The consent mechanics (who needs to consent)

For Broadline Industrial, let's say they propose:
- New maturity: May 2026 (two years out)
- New spread: LIBOR + 425 (up from +350, a 75 bps increase)
- 2% OID (meaning extending lenders receive a 2% discount on the extension amount, or alternatively a 2% cash fee)
- Leverage covenant loosened slightly to 4.5x maximum vs. 4.0x previously

**Step Two: The Election Mechanics — "Extending Lenders" Are Created**

This is where it gets technically elegant. The credit agreement specifies that the A&E is executed through "extension elections." Individual lenders, when notified of the proposed amendment, have the choice to either:

a) **Elect to extend** — the lender agrees to convert their position into an "Extension Series" tranche that has the new maturity, new pricing, and new terms.

b) **Decline to extend** — the lender's position remains unchanged. They keep the original maturity (May 2024), original pricing (LIBOR + 350), and original terms. Their loan is now separate from the extending lenders.

The brilliant part is this: neither the extending lenders nor the non-extending lenders can block the other group's choice. The amend-to-extend happens without requiring unanimous consent. This is the central feature that makes A&E possible when refinancing isn't available.

The voting mechanics vary by deal, but typically there's a threshold. The most common is a "Required Lender" threshold — usually 50% or "Required Lenders" which is 50% of the syndicate by commitment amount. Some deals allow an A&E to proceed with an even lower threshold. Once the threshold of extension elections is met, the A&E is locked in.

**Step Three: The Creation of Extension Series (Tranches)**

This is where the documentation gets complex. When you execute an A&E with multiple groups of lenders electing in different tranches, you're creating what's called "Extension Series" — essentially separate tranches within what was originally a single term loan.

In Broadline Industrial's case:
- Original term loan: €500 million, LIBOR + 350, maturing May 2024
- After A&E: the €500 million splits into (let's say):
  - Tranche A (non-extending): €150 million, LIBOR + 350, maturing May 2024
  - Tranche B (Extension Series 1): €350 million, LIBOR + 425, maturing May 2026

Now you have a split syndicate. The non-extending lenders (Tranche A) have a senior maturity. The extending lenders (Tranche B) have subordinated maturity — they don't get paid until the Tranche A matures and is repaid. This is important and creates real tension: if there's any stress on the credit, the non-extending lenders will get paid first, and the extending lenders might not get paid at all.

In some highly leveraged structures, especially in the US, you can have multiple extension series — Tranche A (original maturity 2024), Tranche B (extended to 2026), Tranche C (extended to 2027). This creates a waterfall of maturities and layered risk.

**Step Four: How A&E Avoids Violating "Sacred Rights"**

Now here's the critical legal engineering: In a normal amendment to a credit agreement, you typically need consent from all lenders (or sometimes supermajorities, typically 66% or 75%) to amend material terms. Maturity, as a concept, is generally considered "sacred" — fundamental to the lender's risk profile. How can you extend maturity without everyone's consent?

The answer is that the A&E provision is explicitly carved out from sacred rights. The way this works is:

The credit agreement states that A&E amendments are permitted (they're enumerated separately), and extending maturity through an A&E is specifically not considered a "sacred rights" amendment that requires lender consent from those who don't want to participate. Instead, A&E is treated as a **voluntary election**. If you, as a lender, don't want to extend, you don't have to. You exit into your own tranche with your original maturity. The other lenders can't force you to extend, and you can't force them not to.

This is clever because it uses the idea of optionality to sidestep the consent problem. It's not that maturity is being extended for unwilling lenders. It's that willing lenders are choosing to move into a new series with extended maturity, while unwilling lenders stay in the old series with the original maturity.

Other sacred rights typically protected include: reducing principal, reducing rate, releasing all or substantially all collateral, or releasing all or substantially all guarantees. These are the big ones. But maturity extension through an A&E is explicitly not in that list — it's permitted through the mechanism described.

**Step Five: Pricing and Compensation**

The extending lenders are receiving less certainty (longer duration, later maturity, different market conditions). They need to be compensated. This typically happens in several ways:

**Spread increase**: The most direct form. In Broadline Industrial, we're going from LIBOR + 350 to LIBOR + 425. That's 75 bps additional annual return. On €350 million, that's €2.6 million annually. Not trivial.

**Original Issue Discount (OID)**: A one-time payment either as a fee (cash OID) or as a discount to the extension amount. A 2% OID on €350 million is €7 million upfront. This is paid at the closing of the A&E (usually from available cash or sometimes from cash available under the revolver, if not drawn).

**Amendment fee**: Sometimes a flat fee is negotiated per extending lender, or a percentage of the extension amount. This is less common in straight A&Es but shows up in complex situations.

**Consent fee**: Sometimes the borrower pays non-extending lenders a fee (typically much smaller — 0.25-0.5%) just to get them to consent to the mechanism, even though their terms don't change. This is more about efficiency and reducing friction than actual compensation.

**Amendment to covenants**: Sometimes extending lenders negotiate slightly looser covenants than what was originally in place. This might include a slightly higher leverage covenant level, or a waiver of a leverage test for a quarter or two. The non-extending lenders' covenant package typically remains unchanged.

17.3 THE ECONOMIC TERMS OF EXTENSION

What makes an A&E work — or fail — comes down to whether the economics are truly attractive to extending lenders. Let me walk through the financial analysis that rational lenders actually do when confronted with an A&E proposal.

**The Refinancing Comparison**

When a lender faces an A&E proposal, the first question they ask is: "What's my alternative?" If I don't extend, what happens?

Scenario One: Refinancing. The borrower refinances the maturing debt with new market debt. If the lender doesn't extend, they get paid off at par. Then, in a refinancing scenario, the borrower goes out and raises new debt to replace the maturing loan. If the credit environment is normal, the spread on new debt might be LIBOR + 500-600 for a comparable credit. The lender who got paid off can reinvest that money at whatever the market is offering at that moment.

This is the key insight: An A&E is attractive to lenders primarily when refinancing is either unavailable or prohibitively expensive. If the market is open and the borrower can refinance easily at tight spreads, few lenders will elect to extend. Why would they? They'd rather be paid off and put the money back to work, or simply hold shorter-duration assets.

But in an environment where refinancing is difficult — like 2008-2010, or 2020-2023 post-shock — the A&E becomes relatively attractive.

**The Spread Analysis**

Let's say in early 2023, when we're modelling Broadline Industrial's A&E, the options are:

Option A: Don't extend. Get paid off in May 2024. In the then-current market, reinvest at whatever spread is available (probably LIBOR + 450-500, plus higher rates overall — we're in a hiking cycle). The lender can now hold a two-year loan at maybe 6.5-7.0% all-in (SOFR at ~4.5%, plus spread, plus perhaps some credit quality premium).

Option B: Extend into the Broadline A&E at LIBOR + 425. In the extension period (May 2024 to May 2026), the lender is earning 4.5% (SOFR) + 4.25% = 8.75%. But interest rates might fall in that period. If SOFR is at 2.0% by 2025 (a possibility if inflation cools), then the all-in rate is 6.25%. That's cheaper, but there's also additional credit risk — the lender is now exposed to Broadline for two more years instead of one.

A rational lender is comparing:
- The floor return of getting paid off and reinvesting into whatever the market offers
- The extended return of holding the A&E tranche with its additional credit risk

This is why the spread increase to +425 (vs. original +350) is important. It's meant to compensate for the additional duration and credit risk. The 75 bps pick-up, annualized, is the lender's reward for staying.

But here's the subtle bit: not all lenders value this the same way. A lender that's liability-constrained — that is, a lender with short-dated funding or marking-to-market accounting — might be desperate to exit and get off the credit. That lender might not extend even at +450. Meanwhile, a patient hold-to-maturity investor like a life insurance company might be happy to extend at +400 because they have long-duration liabilities they need to match.

This is why syndication strategy matters so much in leveraged finance. The types of investors you've sold into determine how receptive they'll be to an A&E when it comes.

**The OID Component**

The original issue discount is straightforward economics. If the borrower is paying 2% OID, that's a 2% upfront payment on the extension amount. On €350 million of extending commitments, that's €7 million paid at closing.

From the lender's perspective, this 2% gets amortised into the economics of the extension. If the extension is two years, that's a 1% annualised uplift (very roughly; the actual IRR calculation is more precise). So the lender is earning:
- +425 bps spread
- Plus ~1% annualised from the 2% OID over two years
- Minus the time value of waiting two extra years for refinancing, in case rates fall
- Minus any additional credit deterioration risk

This is why experienced institutional lenders often ask to see the full economics run in an Excel model. The spread alone doesn't tell you whether the A&E is attractive.

**Amendment to Covenants**

In many A&Es, especially in the 2008-2010 period and again in 2020-2021, covenant amendments come alongside the extension. This makes sense: the borrower is extending maturity (a longer period of risk), and the extending lenders are accepting additional duration. Sometimes that longer period requires more flexible covenant parameters.

A typical amendment might:
- Raise the leverage covenant maximum by 0.25-0.5 turns
- Waive the leverage test for one or two quarters post-amendment (giving the business a grace period)
- Relax the interest coverage test slightly
- Modify the definition of EBITDA to allow additional add-backs, which improves covenant performance

Here's where non-extending lenders sometimes kick up a fuss. If they're staying in with the original maturity and original pricing, why should the extending lenders get better covenants? It seems unfair.

The answer is usually: Because the extending lenders are taking on additional risk by not getting their capital back when they expected to. The loosened covenants are a form of insurance that the business will have sufficient flexibility to navigate the extension period without slipping into a technical breach. This protects the extending lenders' position and indirectly protects the non-extending lenders' position too (because a covenant breach is bad for everyone).

That said, this is a negotiating point. Non-extending lenders sometimes require that the covenant loosening be very modest, or that certain covenants remain unchanged. The credit agreement's A&E provisions sometimes spell this out explicitly.

**The Weighted Average Life (WAL) Impact**

One often-overlooked dimension of A&E economics is the impact on the credit's weighted average life. This matters primarily for collateralised loan obligation (CLO) investors and rating agencies.

A CLO has covenants in its offering memorandum that limit how much of the CLO's portfolio can consist of loans with a weighted average life of more than, say, 5.5 years. When you extend a loan via A&E, you're extending its maturity, which pushes out the weighted average life (the average date at which principal is expected to be repaid, weighted by the amount repaid on each date).

For example, if Broadline Industrial's extension series creates a situation where different lenders have payoff dates in 2024, 2026, and 2027, the weighted average life becomes something like 2025.5 — a weighted average. If the WAL gets too long, CLOs might be forced to sell the position because it violates their portfolio guidelines.

This is a real issue. I've seen A&E proposals sink because the weighted average life impact made the position unmarketable to CLOs, forcing sponsors to go back to the drawing board.

17.4 A&E VS. REFINANCING: THE STRATEGIC CHOICE

Why would a sponsor ever choose to A&E rather than refinance? This is the strategic question, and the answer reveals a lot about how sponsors think.

**Scenario: Market Dislocation**

The most obvious scenario is when the market is dislocated and refinancing is impossible or ruinously expensive. This was the case in 2008-2010, where many sponsors faced maturities and literally had no access to capital. An A&E was the only option.

I remember sitting in a sponsor's office in 2009 with their CFO when they received a term sheet for a refinancing of a €400 million term loan. The spread was LIBOR + 650, with 3% OID, and the lender was being very selective — they were requiring significant covenants loosening and even a pricing step-up if certain milestones weren't hit. The sponsor looked at the term sheet and basically said, "We'd rather A&E if we can. The economics are better, the covenants are looser, and we don't have to go through a full syndication."

The A&E they ultimately negotiated had spread at +400, 2% OID, and modest covenant loosening. Far better than refinancing would have been.

**Scenario: Confidence in Near-Term Exit**

A sponsor with high conviction about a near-term exit — either through an add-on acquisition that's going to boost EBITDA, or an IPO, or a sale to another buyer — might prefer an A&E over refinancing.

Why? Because an A&E is simpler, faster, and doesn't require full syndication. You negotiate with your existing lender base (or at least the willing portion of it), get the deal done in 4-8 weeks, pay the OID and new pricing, and then you're set. The sponsor can focus on their exit thesis.

Refinancing requires going back to the market, re-marketing the credit, dealing with new investor diligence, potentially new structures. It's 8-12 weeks of noise when the sponsor just wants stability.

**Scenario: Avoiding the "New Money" Problem**

In some situations, a refinancing would require the sponsor to provide new cash or additional collateral. For instance, if a borrower's leverage has crept up and covenant tests are tight, a lender might require the sponsor to put in equity as a condition of refinancing. An A&E can sometimes be structured without requiring new money from the sponsor.

I saw this in 2020 when several sponsors were facing maturities and their credits had been hit by COVID. Rather than refinance (which would have required equity infusions), they A&Eed their way through, using the covenant loosening to buy time for EBITDA to recover. By late 2021, when conditions improved, they refinanced from a position of strength.

**Scenario: The Premium Lender**

Sometimes a sponsor has a "premium" lender — a traditional bank that's been supportive and has good terms. They want to keep that relationship. An A&E allows them to keep that lender in the structure without going through a full refinancing. The lender gets compensated with the spread increase and OID, which incentivises them to stay.

**When Refinancing Wins**

Conversely, refinancing becomes preferable when:

- **Market is open**: When spreads are tight and the market is accepting risk enthusiastically, refinancing into a new structure at better terms is possible. The sponsor can achieve tighter spreads, better covenants, potentially more committed capital.

- **Capital structure needs to change**: If the sponsor wants to reduce leverage, refinance at a smaller amount, or change the mix of debt instruments (add bonds, reduce term loans), they pretty much have to refinance. An A&E just extends the current structure as-is.

- **Lender syndicate is problematic**: If the current lender base is perceived as difficult or likely to be uncooperative, refinancing lets you replace them with a new group. An A&E forces you to work with existing lenders.

- **Terms are already loose**: If covenants are already very loose and financial performance is deteriorating, refinancing (which typically resets covenants tighter) might actually be preferable to an A&E (which locks in loose covenants for longer).

**The Documentation Implication**

A&E vs. refinancing also affects documentation strategy. A credit agreement that expects to A&E needs to be drafted with robust A&E provisions. This means:
- Clear definition of what terms can be changed in an A&E (pricing, maturity, some covenants) vs. what can't (guarantees, collateral, financial covenants at the original level or tighter)
- Clear process for extension elections and the consent thresholds
- Clear mechanics for creating extension series and their waterfall/subordination treatment
- Clear treatment of fees, OID, and other compensation
- Clarity on how existing credit support agreements apply to extension series

In contrast, a credit that expects to refinance if needed is often drafted with looser A&E provisions or even no A&E at all, understanding that refinancing is the expected path.

This is why strong sponsors often insist on tight A&E provisions in their credit agreements at origination. If they think there's a chance they'll want to extend at some point, they want those provisions already in place, drafted the way they prefer.

17.5 THE EUROPEAN A&E LANDSCAPE

A&E mechanics are broadly similar across geographies, but the European approach — especially in the LMA (Loan Market Association) framework — differs in meaningful ways from the US LSTA approach.

**LMA vs. LSTA Framework**

The US LSTA (Loan Syndications and Trading Association) standard has a very specific, legalistic approach to A&Es. The LSTA standard form includes detailed A&E mechanics with specific thresholds, mechanics for extension series, and calculation of weighted average life. These are well-tested, widely understood, and leave little room for ambiguity.

The LMA approach is more flexible and context-dependent. European credits are often governed by English law (even if the borrower is European), but the LMA doesn't have quite the same standardised A&E mechanics template as the LSTA. This means European A&Es are often negotiated more freely, with less reliance on template language.

The key differences:

**Majority Lender Consent vs. Individual Election**

In the US LSTA approach, A&Es are structured through individual lender elections. Each lender can independently choose to extend or not. Once a threshold of extending commitments is met (usually 50%), the A&E can proceed.

In some European deals, especially earlier ones, the approach was more group-based. The "Required Lenders" might be consulted as a group, and their decision might bind all lenders. This is less common now (European practice has shifted toward LSTA-style individual elections), but you still see variations.

**Mandatory Prepayment Interactions**

European credit agreements often have mandatory prepayment provisions triggered by certain events (asset sales, insurance proceeds, refinancings). These interact with A&E in complex ways.

For instance, if a borrower sells an asset and receives proceeds, the credit agreement might require mandatory prepayment. In a US deal, this is usually straightforward — you prepay the term loan pro rata among all lenders (or sequentially by maturity date in some structures).

But in a European deal with an A&E in place, the dynamics are different. Do the mandatory prepayment proceeds go first to the earliest-maturing tranche? Or pro rata across all tranches? The credit agreement needs to spell this out clearly, and the answer affects the economics of the extension.

I remember negotiating an A&E on a mid-market European buy-out where the sponsor was expecting an asset sale in the extension period. The non-extending lenders (with the original May 2024 maturity) wanted the mandatory prepayment to go first to them, effectively ensuring they'd get out at par if any proceeds came. The extending lenders wanted it pro rata. We ultimately negotiated a hybrid: any proceeds in the first year post-amendment went pro rata, but any proceeds in year two onwards went first to the earlier-maturing tranche. This gave the extending lenders some certainty that they wouldn't be forcibly exited early, but also protected the non-extending lenders' priority.

**Change of Control and Extension Series**

European documentation sometimes treats change of control (especially sponsor-to-sponsor, in a sponsor-backed credit) as triggering prepayment obligations. When there's an A&E with multiple extension series, who has the right to accelerate on a change of control? All lenders? Just the non-extending lenders? Pro rata?

Again, the agreement needs to be very clear. Some deals specify that each tranche has independent change of control rights. Others pool them. The European approach tends to be more flexible than LSTA, leading to more negotiation on these points.

**Cross-Currency EBITDA and Leverage**

European deals often involve multiple currencies (Euro, Sterling, sometimes CHF or SEK for Nordic borrowers). This creates a complexity with EBITDA and leverage calculations in an A&E context.

If you've got a business earning EBITDA in Euros, dollars, and Swedish kronor, and you're calculating consolidated leverage for covenant purposes, you need to define the translation methodology. Post-A&E, when you might have extending tranches maturing in a different period, the covenant packages might even specify different exchange rates or methodologies for different series.

I worked on a Scandinavian PE-backed refinancing where the borrower had operations across Denmark, Sweden, and Germany. The original credit agreement specified a monthly average EUR/DKK and EUR/SEK rate for translating foreign currency EBITDA. When they A&Eed, the sponsor wanted to lock in current rates as of the amendment date, understanding that FX volatility could otherwise impact leverage calculations. The lenders, however, wanted the original methodology to continue, to preserve the stability of the covenant test. We ultimately compromised: the original methodology applied to the non-extending tranche, and a rate-lock applied to the extending tranche (with quarterly true-ups if rates moved more than 5%).

**The Governing Law Question**

Most European leveraged finance credits are governed by English law, even for non-UK borrowers. English law has well-established principles on amendments, subordination, and ranking. This is actually helpful for A&Es because English courts have seen them before and understand the mechanics.

That said, some borrowers insist on their home country law (German law, French law, Dutch law, etc.). This can complicate A&E mechanics because those legal systems might have different rules about what constitutes a "material amendment" that requires lender consent. Negotiating an A&E under French law, for instance, sometimes requires thinking carefully about how the extension is characterised in the French legal context.

**Regulatory Capital for European Banks**

European bank lenders are regulated under PSD2 and follow ECB/EBA guidance on regulatory capital. An A&E on a loan held in a bank's portfolio can have regulatory capital implications.

If the bank is marking the loan to market (which many large European banks do), an A&E might trigger a repricing that hits their capital. If rates have risen since origination (which they usually have, in recent years), extending the loan often means marking it down in value, which hits regulatory capital.

This is another factor that affects lender enthusiasm for A&Es. A bank facing regulatory capital pressure might be less willing to extend than a private credit investor or an insurance company with different accounting rules.

17.6 CASE STUDIES IN AMEND AND EXTEND

Let me walk through three real cases (anonymised, but based on actual transactions I've worked on) that illustrate the range of A&E outcomes.

**Case One: The Post-2008 Industrial Champions Extension (2009)**

A large, well-established European automotive components manufacturer had been backed by a tier-one PE sponsor in 2006 at the height of the market. The deal was highly leveraged — 5.5x leverage at entry. The sponsor had plans to grow the business through add-on acquisitions.

Then the financial crisis hit. Car production fell off a cliff. The business's EBITDA, which had been €80 million, fell to €35 million by 2009. The sponsors' growth thesis evaporated. The term loan, originally due in 2012, suddenly looked very risky.

The lender syndicate was a mixed group: several commercial banks, a few specialized finance shops, and one very defensive hedge fund that had acquired positions in the secondary market. The hedge fund wanted out immediately.

A full refinancing was impossible — no one would refinance a leveraged automotive supplier in 2009. But the sponsorship believed the worst was passing, and that the business would stabilize by 2010-2011 if they could just get through 2009.

They structured an A&E:
- New maturity: 2014 (originally 2012)
- Pricing: raised from LIBOR + 300 to LIBOR + 550 (250 bps increase)
- OID: 5% (substantial, reflecting the risk)
- Covenant loosening: leverage covenant moved from 3.5x maximum to 5.5x (essentially matching current levels, and then building in a gradual step-down from 5.5x in year one to 4.5x by year three)

The hedge fund took a 5% upfront fee to exit — they were paid off in full at par. The commercial banks extended because they had relationship capital with the sponsor and believed the story. The specialized finance shops saw an opportunity to earn 550 bps of spread on a now-solidly distressed credit and stayed in.

The outcome: The business did stabilise. By 2011, EBITDA was back to €65 million. By 2013, it was €85 million, higher than pre-crisis. The sponsorship successfully sold the company to a larger tier-one competitor in late 2013, using proceeds to pay off the loan in full. Everyone made money: the extending lenders earned their 550 bps spread plus the 5% OID, the exiting hedge fund took their fee and moved on, and the sponsor harvested returns in the sale.

**Lesson from Case One**: An A&E works beautifully when the underlying business thesis is sound and the constraint is temporary market dislocation, not fundamentals.

**Case Two: The COVID-Era Healthcare Services Extension (2021)**

A mid-market healthcare services platform in Germany had been acquired in 2018 with a €180 million term loan (LIBOR + 300, due 2023). The business was a roll-up of smaller healthcare clinics and was performing well — EBITDA ~€40 million.

COVID hit in early 2020. Elective procedures were cancelled. EBITDA fell 35% in 2020. The business looked structurally impaired. However, the sponsorship and management team believed this was temporary. As vaccination rolled out and elective procedures resumed, they expected a sharp recovery.

The maturity was approaching (early 2023), and the credit markets were reopening post-COVID but were demanding much tighter covenants and higher spreads. The sponsor's view: Why refinance into tightened covenants when we expect a quick recovery? Let's A&E, get covenant relief, and then refinance from a position of strength once EBITDA rebounds.

They proposed an A&E:
- New maturity: 2025 (two years out)
- Pricing: increased to LIBOR + 375 (75 bps increase)
- OID: 1.5%
- Covenant loosening: leverage moved from 3.5x to 4.0x maximum, with no leverage test for 2021 and 2022

The lender syndicate was large and dispersed (many CLO investors plus some traditional banks). About 60% of the lender base extended. The other 40% took payoff at par (the market was open enough by early 2021 that they had refinancing alternatives).

The outcome: EBITDA recovered sharply in 2021 and 2022 as procedures resumed. By late 2022, the business was back to pre-COVID EBITDA levels. However — and this is important — the sponsor decided in late 2022 not to refinance but to extend again, because spreads had blown out due to the rate shock. They A&Eed again, extending maturity to 2027, taking on even more spread but getting another two-year runway.

**Lesson from Case Two**: A&Es can be chained — you can A&E multiple times if refinancing doesn't make sense economically. But this creates extension series on top of extension series, which can become hard to manage.

**Case Three: The Deteriorating Distribution Business — Bust (2019-2020)**

A European specialty distribution business backed by a mid-market sponsor had a €120 million term loan due in 2022. The sponsorship had executed the acquisition at a cycle peak. EBITDA was achieved through aggressive cost-cutting and some one-time restructuring charges that the sponsor characterised as "permanent synergies."

Within a year, it was clear the synergies weren't sticking. Client consolidation in the customer base was reducing order volume. A large customer shifted to a lower-cost supplier. EBITDA began trending down. By 2020, EBITDA was 20% below the covenant baseline.

The sponsor, facing maturity in 2022, proposed an A&E. Their thesis: Market dislocation, temporary headwinds, we need time to execute a turnaround. They proposed:
- Extension to 2024
- Spread to +450
- 2% OID
- Leverage covenant loosened to 4.5x (from 3.5x), and a covenant waiver for 2020 and 2021

Here's where it went wrong: The lender syndicate looked at the business and didn't believe the sponsor's turnaround thesis. EBITDA was declining, not cyclically down. The customer concentration had worsened. Market conditions in distribution were structural headwinds, not temporary.

Only about 35% of lenders extended. The others demanded refinancing out. But the sponsor couldn't refinance the full €120 million at that spread — the market would only refinance at LIBOR + 550-600, and the sponsor found this prohibitive.

What happened next was messy. The non-extending lenders (representing the 2022 maturity) eventually agreed to a smaller refinancing for the non-extending tranche (€78 million at LIBOR + 550). The extending lenders (€42 million) were extended to 2024. But the two tranches had different sponsors (one group, one not), different documentation, and different covenants.

By 2022, the situation had deteriorated further. EBITDA was down another 15%. The non-extending tranche went into default on its leverage covenant. The non-extending lenders accelerated. The sponsor had to inject equity just to keep the lights on. By 2023, the business was in a full restructuring, and creditors took a 35% haircut.

**Lesson from Case Three**: An A&E can mask a deteriorating business thesis. The extending lenders thought they were buying time for a turnaround; they were actually just extending the runway toward default. When you're evaluating an A&E as a lender, you have to ask: Is this business actually going to be healthier in the extension period, or is the sponsor just buying time?

17.7 RISKS AND PITFALLS FOR INVESTORS

Understanding the risks of A&E transactions is crucial, especially if you're holding the position through the extension period.

**The Coercive Element**

The first thing to understand is that A&Es contain a coercive element. Lenders who don't want to extend but want to remain in the credit (because they think it's a good credit, just not at extended duration) face a "prisoner's dilemma" dynamic.

Here's the problem: If most other lenders extend, and you decline, you're now in a tiny tranche with an early maturity (maybe €20 million in a €200 million deal). You're illiquid. You can't easily sell your position. The bid-ask spread widens. Your only real option is to refinance out, but refinancing a non-extended maturity at the same time the borrower is A&Eing often means you're refinancing into loose covenants and a deteriorating credit profile.

Many lenders in this situation feel coerced into extending, even if they didn't particularly want to. This is a real phenomenon. I've sat in lender meetings where a commercial bank said, "We don't love this A&E economics, but we have to extend because the alternative — being left in a tiny tranche and illiquid — is worse."

This is why experienced lenders sometimes include explicit "coercion protections" in their A&E negotiations, such as:
- A guaranteed bid from the arranger/sponsor for non-extended positions
- A put option allowing non-extending lenders to refinance out at specific terms
- Syndication assistance if needed

**The Non-Extending Lender Position**

If you're a non-extending lender, you need to think carefully about what you're holding.

On the positive side: You have an earlier maturity. If anything goes wrong with the credit in the extension period, you're out before the extending lenders. You get paid first.

On the negative side: You're now in a smaller tranche (most other lenders extended). You have less liquidity. You might be the only lender trying to exit, which means limited secondary market bids. And importantly, the covenant package likely reflects the extending lenders' desires (looser covenants for the extension period), not your desires. If the business deteriorates in the extension period but covenants are loose enough to allow it, you're stuck holding a deteriorating credit through your maturity date.

**Subordination Risk in Extension Series**

If an A&E creates multiple extension series with different maturities, you have a waterfall. Tranche A (original maturity 2024), Tranche B (extended to 2026), Tranche C (extended to 2027).

On the surface, this looks like Tranche A gets paid first, which is protective. But in practice:

If there's a cash flow constraint before the A maturity, the borrower might face pressure to amend or extend even the non-extending tranche. The market dynamics shift — maybe by 2023, refinancing is still difficult for the non-extended tranche, and refinancing isn't available at terms the borrower can accept. Pressure builds to A&E that tranche too, further extending everything.

If the business is deteriorating and approaching default, the extending lenders (B and C) face pressure to compromise with non-extending lenders to keep the credit alive. The collateral available to each tranche might be the same (they're all secured loans). If the business enters a restructuring, the waterfall might not matter as much as you'd expect, especially if all tranches agree to compromise terms for an out-of-court workout.

**The Prisoner's Dilemma Dynamics**

This is the subtle but powerful risk. The A&E structure creates an incentive structure where individual lenders' best interests might diverge from collective interests.

Scenario: After an A&E, EBITDA trends slightly down but is still above covenant levels. The extending lenders face a choice: (1) support another A&E in a few years to extend maturity again, or (2) agitate for a sale or recapitalisation. The non-extending lenders, whose maturity is approaching, might prefer a sale (to get capital back). But the extending lenders might prefer another extension (to avoid forcing a potentially unfavourable sale).

In this situation, you get a fracture in the lender group. The non-extending lenders start agitating for alternatives. Agreements break down. The workout becomes adversarial instead of collaborative.

I've seen this happen in actual workouts, and it's ugly. A credit that might have been salvageable through a collaborative restructuring becomes contentious because the maturity waterfall created conflicting incentives.

**The "Extend to Pretend" Risk**

This is my sardonic term for the situation where an A&E is structured less to solve a real problem and more to defer a problem that's ultimately unsolvable.

The classic sign: The borrower's EBITDA is declining, not cyclically down but trending. The sponsor's thesis for the A&E is that turnaround actions will arrest the decline and stabilize EBITDA. But there's no concrete plan. It's hope, not strategy.

In these situations, the A&E often just delays the inevitable default by a few years. The extending lenders end up extending into a worse credit, experiencing deterioration over the extension period, and then facing default anyway at the new maturity date. They would have been better off taking payoff at par in the original A&E proposal.

This is why, as a lender or investor, you need to ask hard questions about the sponsor's operational thesis. Don't just accept "we need time" as an answer. What specifically will change? What KPIs are improving? What's the target for EBITDA? How will the business be better positioned for refinancing in the extension period?

If the sponsor can't give you concrete answers, be suspicious.

**Covenant Loosening Risk**

A&Es typically come with covenant loosening. This is by design — the borrower is extending risk, so lenders get more cushion. But loosened covenants reduce your ability to detect and respond to business deterioration.

If leverage covenants loosen from 3.5x to 4.5x maximum, the business can deteriorate by a full turn before triggering a covenant breach. That's a year or more of operational decline that you're not forced to address. By the time the covenant test resets or tightens again, the business might be in much worse shape.

Strong lenders negotiate covenant step-downs in A&Es specifically to address this. You might accept 4.5x leverage in year one of the extension, but step down to 4.0x in year two, and 3.5x in year three. This preserves your ability to detect and react to deterioration.

17.8 THE REGULATORY AND ACCOUNTING DIMENSIONS

Beyond the core mechanics, A&Es have regulatory and accounting implications that affect valuation, risk weighting, and financial reporting.

**CLO Implications**

For CLOs, the primary concern is weighted average life. Most CLO documents limit the average maturity of the portfolio to something like 5.5 or 6 years.

An A&E extends individual loan maturities, which extends WAL. If your CLO is already running at high WAL, an A&E might push you over the limit.

Additionally, CLOs care about mark-to-market. An A&E typically involves a spread increase and OID, which changes the loan's valuation. If spreads have widened since origination (which they usually have), the A&E price is typically par. But the economics are different. A CLO holding the position marks it at a lower value post-A&E (because it's now trading at par with wider spread), which can trigger mark-to-market losses.

I've seen CLOs face a scenario where they're holding a middle-market term loan, and an A&E is proposed. The loan has been marked down 5-10 points due to spread widening. The A&E resets the loan to par pricing with 75 bps wider spread. The CLO marks the position back up to par, avoiding a further mark-down. But it's still trading lower than where it was at origination.

**Accounting Treatment: Modification vs. Extinguishment**

Under ASC 470 (if you're following US accounting) or IFRS 9 (if you're following European accounting), an A&E is treated as a modification of the existing loan, not an extinguishment of the old loan and creation of a new one.

This matters because:

1. **Gain/Loss Recognition**: If the modification is not "substantial," you don't recognise an immediate gain or loss. Instead, you continue to accrue interest on the modified terms. If the modification is "substantial," you might recognize a gain or loss on the old loan and begin recognising interest on new terms as a separate instrument.

2. **Loan Loss Provisions**: Under the current expected credit loss (CECL) framework, extending a loan's maturity can affect the expected loss estimate. A longer duration means more time for things to go wrong, which might increase the provision.

3. **Valuation**: If you're holding the loan at fair value (which is less common but happens in certain portfolios), the A&E changes the valuation. The extended maturity, new spread, and OID all affect the fair value calculation.

Banks holding these loans need to work with their accounting teams to ensure proper treatment. The impact on earnings and regulatory capital can be material.

**Regulatory Capital Considerations**

A bank that's holding a leveraged loan on its balance sheet faces regulatory capital requirements. An A&E can affect the capital calculation.

Under Basel III (and the forthcoming Basel IV), a loan's risk weight depends on the obligor's rating, the loan's seniority, and loan-to-value or similar metrics. An A&E that extends maturity doesn't change the risk weight itself, but the longer duration means longer time for default risk to manifest, which can indirectly affect the bank's capital planning.

Additionally, if the bank marks the loan to market (many large banks do, especially trading-oriented banks), the A&E might trigger a repricing that hits the mark-to-market value. If spreads have widened since origination, and the A&E resets to par at wider spread, the bank marks the loan down. This reduces regulatory capital.

Conversely, if the bank is on the hook for fair value adjustments (some banks are under certain accounting rules), an A&E might allow them to reverse past provisions, freeing up capital.

The regulatory dimension is complex and varies by bank, by jurisdiction, and by accounting framework. But it's a real consideration for bank lenders evaluating whether to extend.

**The Leverage Ratio Consideration**

Some banks have tight leverage ratios (assets divided by capital, often subject to a 3% minimum). An A&E doesn't change the size of the asset, but it does mean the asset is held longer. For a bank close to its leverage ratio constraint, this can be meaningful. Every month the loan is outstanding counts against the leverage ratio. Extending the loan by two years effectively increases the average time the asset is on the bank's balance sheet, which can impact leverage ratio management.

This is a secondary concern, but it's real. I've had bank lenders tell me they don't want to extend because it would push them through a leverage ratio limit, and they'd have to sell other assets to make room.


17.9 THE CONSENT HIERARCHY: LOANS VS. BONDS

Before you can weaponise an amendment, you have to understand what you're amending. And that starts with understanding voting rights — the hierarchy that determines what threshold you need to cross to change the rules.

Leveraged loans and high-yield bonds operate under fundamentally different consent regimes. Miss this distinction, and you'll find yourself blindsided when a majority you thought was untouchable turns out to be amendable.

**The Leveraged Loan Tiered Structure**

Leveraged loan agreements feature a tiered consent hierarchy that's become standardised over the past two decades. Here's how it typically works:

**Majority Lenders (>50%):** Most amendments, waivers, and consents require approval from lenders holding more than 50% of outstanding loans and commitments. This is the default threshold for covenant modifications, waiver of defaults, and most operational changes. It's the workhorse threshold — gets used in 90% of amendments.

**Supermajority (66.67% or 75%):** Some agreements require higher thresholds for certain significant changes, though this became less common after 2015. You might see supermajority thresholds for things like acceleration of maturity, subordination of liens, or changes to the definition of EBITDA. But they're rarer now than they were.

**All Lenders / Each Affected Lender:** Certain fundamental rights require unanimous consent or consent of each directly and adversely affected lender. These are the supposed "sacred rights" — but as we'll see in section 17.10, that term is misleading.

**No Lender Consent:** An agent and borrower can typically make certain unilateral changes: correcting technical errors, implementing changes deemed beneficial to lenders (like increased pricing or added credit support), or making "conforming changes" to implement permitted transactions. In practice, agents do this sparingly — any agent who starts changing terms unilaterally gets fired.

**The Atlantic Divide: High-Yield Bonds**

High-yield bonds operate under a different framework, and here's where you really need to pay attention: a fundamental difference exists between European and U.S. high-yield markets regarding what happens when you amend sacred rights.

**European High-Yield Bonds:** Amending sacred rights requires a high supermajority consent — typically **90%** of outstanding principal. But here's the critical bit: if this threshold is met, the amendment binds *all* bondholders, including the non-consenting minority. It's an "all or nothing" regime. A 10% minority can be dragged into unfavorable terms over their objection.

This is a weapon. If a sponsor can assemble 90% support — through persuasion, coercion, or manufacturing a friendly creditor base — they can impose terms on the entire bondholder class. We'll see how this is actually executed in section 17.12.

**U.S. High-Yield Bonds:** The standard is much stricter. Amending a sacred right requires consent from *each affected holder*. This gives every individual bondholder an effective veto over changes to their core economic terms. A non-consenting holder cannot be forced into accepting modified economics. You can't drag a minority through on the coat-tails of 90% approval.

This distinction has profound implications for how sponsors structure liability management transactions. In Europe, holdup problems are solved by hitting a 90% threshold. In the U.S., every significant holder can block you.

17.10 SACRED RIGHTS: THE SUPPOSEDLY UNTOUCHABLE (AND THE NOT-SO-SACRED TRAP)

I want to walk you through one of the most consequential gap between what creditors *think* is protected and what's actually protected.

Traditional "sacred rights" represent the core economic terms that creditors expect to be protected by the highest consent thresholds. These typically include:

**Principal Amount:** Reductions to the principal amount owed require consent of each affected lender/holder. You cannot force a creditor to accept a principal loss.

**Maturity Date:** Extensions of the final maturity date require heightened consent (though, as we've discussed, A&Es often sidestep this through optionality).

**Interest Rate:** Reductions to the stated interest rate require consent of affected parties. You cannot cut the coupon on an unwilling creditor.

**Pro Rata Sharing:** Provisions governing the pro rata distribution of payments among creditors are typically sacred. You cannot reorder the waterfall to advantage some creditors over others without broad consent.

Here's where the trap lies, and it's cost investors billions in recoveries. **In many documents — particularly European high-yield bonds — certain protections that *appear* fundamental are not classified as sacred rights.**

**Subordination:** The right not to be subordinated to new debt is often *not* a sacred right. This means a majority (50.1%) can consent to amendments that permit the incurrence of new super-priority debt, leaving minority creditors effectively junior. Your investment committee believes your position is senior until they discover it's been subordinated.

**Collateral Release:** Release of collateral may require only majority consent, not unanimity. You thought your security interest was inviolable. A majority votes to release the collateral, and your position is unsecured.

**Guarantor Release:** Similarly, release of guarantors may not be a sacred right. A subsidiary guarantor that you relied upon can be released by majority vote.

**Ranking Changes:** Modifications to lien priority or payment waterfall may be amendable by simple majority.

This gap is the foundational enabler of uptier transactions, covenant-stripping exercises, and other aggressive liability management tactics. Creditors who believe their ranking is protected discover — too late — that it can be changed over their objection.

I remember a case in 2017 where we held a senior secured position in a European HY bond. We thought the collateral was locked down. We spent three hours reading the indenture and found that release of collateral required only 50% consent. By the time we figured this out, a sponsor had already assembled 60% support for a collateral release (courtesy of creditors they'd flown to London and bought dinners for). We called an emergency lender committee meeting. We got to 45% opposition, which felt like a victory. But it wasn't enough.

The point: assume nothing about what's sacred. Read the consent thresholds for each material term. Your risk model is only as good as your documentation understanding.

17.11 THE INTERCREDITOR AGREEMENT: EUROPE'S STRUCTURAL DEFENSE

In European multi-tranche financings, a critical structural defense exists that doesn't appear in the same form in U.S. documentation: the **Intercreditor Agreement (ICA)**.

Understanding the ICA is essential because it's the primary tool that blocks certain species of uptier transactions and forces transactions to be more transparent than they otherwise would be.

**What the ICA Actually Does**

The ICA is a separate contract that sits above the individual facility agreements. It's not embedded in the credit agreement — it's a standalone document that defines the contractual relationship among the different classes of creditors. Its primary functions include:

**Payment Waterfall:** Specifies the precise order in which enforcement proceeds are distributed among super-senior, senior, mezzanine, and junior creditor classes. If there's a default and the collateral is liquidated, the waterfall dictates exactly who gets paid first, second, third, and fourth.

**Payment Stops:** Allow senior creditors to deliver a notice that temporarily blocks payments to junior creditors upon certain defaults or events. This is a crucial tool: a senior lender can freeze cash flows to subordinated creditors while the senior lender figures out its enforcement strategy.

**Enforcement Standstills:** Prohibit junior creditors from taking enforcement action for a set period — typically 12-24 months — giving senior creditors control over the restructuring process and preventing a rush to the courthouse by multiple creditor classes.

**Turnover Provisions:** Require junior creditors who receive payments out of waterfall order to turn those amounts over to senior creditors. This prevents a clever junior creditor from side-dealing to get paid when they shouldn't be.

**Amendment Protections:** Define what amendments to the ICA require (spoiler: typically all-lender consent among parties to the ICA).

**Why the ICA Blocks Serta-Style Uptiers**

Now here's the critical bit: Because the ICA contractually defines the payment and enforcement waterfall and typically requires **all-lender consent** to amend it, a simple majority of lenders cannot vote to subordinate a minority without breaching this separate agreement.

Think about this from first principles. Suppose you have a senior term loan and a junior mezzanine facility. The ICA says: "Senior lenders get paid first. Junior lenders paid second." Now the sponsor tries to execute an uptier transaction — promoting some junior lenders to senior status (and demoting the original senior lenders to junior status).

In a U.S. credit agreement, the intercreditor terms are often embedded within the credit agreement itself — amendable by whatever threshold the credit agreement specifies. A majority of 60% votes to amend the ranking, and boom, the uptier is live.

In Europe, the standalone ICA creates a separate contractual barrier. The majority cannot amend the ICA without all-lender consent. This is why Serta-style uptier transactions are significantly harder to execute in the European leveraged loan market. You not only need lender consents to amend the facility agreement; you also need all-lender consent to amend the ICA's waterfall.

**The European Bond Vulnerability**

Here's where the asymmetry is striking: European high-yield bonds often lack equivalent protection. Subordination is rarely a sacred right in bond indentures, and there is no separate ICA governing inter-bondholder rights. This makes European bonds *more* vulnerable to uptier transactions than European loans.

This is an inversion of the traditional assumption that bonds have stronger covenants. Loan syndicates have structural protection (the ICA). Bond holders have only the indenture. And if the indenture is weak on sacred rights protection, bond holders are naked.

17.12 ALTERING THE VOTE: DISENFRANCHISEMENT TACTICS

Borrowers and sponsors have developed sophisticated tactics to manipulate voting dynamics and push through contentious amendments. These tactics sit at the intersection of legal engineering and creditor manipulation.

**Net Short Lender Disenfranchisement**

Some credit agreements strip voting rights from lenders who hold a "net short" position against the borrower's debt — for example, through credit default swaps that profit from default.

The rationale is straightforward: a net short lender has misaligned incentives. They might vote to precipitate default rather than preserve enterprise value. So the credit agreement neuters their vote. These lenders are typically deemed to vote in the same proportion as non-net-short lenders, neutralizing their influence.

In practice, net short disenfranchisement is less common than you'd think — enforcement is difficult, and determining "net short" status requires disclosure and auditing. But aggressive sponsors absolutely use this clause. I've seen a hedge fund get disenfranchised in the middle of a critical vote because they'd hedged their position with CDS. They had zero voting power on an amendment they could have blocked.

**Affiliate Lender Provisions**

When sponsors or their affiliates purchase loans in the secondary market, they become lenders with voting rights. Aggressive documents include provisions that cap affiliate voting power. These might:

- Cap affiliate lender voting power at a specified percentage (e.g., 10% or 15%)
- Require affiliate-held loans to vote pro rata with non-affiliate lenders
- Exclude affiliate holdings from quorum and approval calculations

Weak or absent affiliate lender restrictions allow sponsors to manufacture voting majorities by purchasing their own debt. I've seen sponsors build significant positions by quietly accumulating their own loans in the secondary market — then suddenly wielding those votes to push through amendments the original syndicate would have blocked.

This is why affiliate lender provisions are one of the first things a sophisticated investor looks for in loan documentation.

**Vote Rigging Through New Issuance**

The most aggressive tactic: an issuer strategically issues new debt to friendly creditors who have **pre-agreed** to consent to a controversial amendment. This manufactured majority then approves the change, overriding objections from legacy creditors.

**Case Study: Intralot (2021)**

Intralot is a Greek gaming technology company that faced a maturity wall in 2021. The existing notes had a 90% threshold to amend sacred rights. The sponsor needed to amend subordination terms to bring in new financing.

Here's what they did: they issued new notes to a locked-up group of creditors (friendly parties who had signed side letters pre-committing to vote a specific way). These new notes were issued at par, making them immediately attractive to distressed-oriented creditors looking for entry points. The new creditors' votes pushed the aggregate support across the 90% threshold.

The result: the amendment passed, sacred rights were modified (subordination was restructured), and non-consenting legacy holders were dragged along. The legacy holders who refused to participate were left holding restructured notes with worse terms.

This is the nuclear option of vote rigging. It requires:
1. Access to capital (or friendly creditor base willing to take par risk)
2. A sponsor with deep pockets or excellent creditor relationships
3. Pre-arranged side letters (which can get legally dicey)
4. The ability to move fast before opposition solidifies

But when it works, it's devastating.

17.13 THE YANK-A-BANK MECHANISM

Credit agreements typically include provisions allowing borrowers to remove non-cooperative lenders from the syndicate. These "yank-a-bank" or "replacement lender" provisions permit the borrower to:

1. Identify a lender who has refused to consent to a requested amendment
2. Find a replacement lender willing to purchase that lender's position
3. Force the non-consenting lender to sell at par (or par plus accrued interest)

**Strategic Use in A&E Transactions**

In A&E transactions, borrowers use yank-a-bank to remove lenders who refuse to extend. The sequence is straightforward:

1. Borrower proposes extension terms
2. Lender refuses, wanting to exit rather than extend
3. Borrower invokes yank-a-bank, finds a replacement lender
4. The original lender receives par and exits; the replacement lender enters with extended commitments
5. The borrower achieves its extension without universal consent

This is remarkably efficient. The original lender isn't getting dragged into extended maturity (they get paid off). The replacement lender wants to extend (or at least is willing to). The borrower gets the extension it needs.

**Limitations and Reality**

Yank-a-bank has real limitations. First, the borrower can't use it to force-feed terrible terms to holdouts. It only works if the borrower can actually find a replacement lender willing to step in. In stressed situations — when the credit is deteriorating and the market is closed — finding a replacement is not guaranteed.

Second, it typically cannot be used to remove lenders who are exercising legitimate rights. You can't yank a lender who's refusing to fund a draw because that draw would breach the leverage covenant. That's a legitimate exercise of lender rights. But refusing to extend? That's typically within scope for yanking.

Third, yanking isn't costless. If the borrower has to offer the replacement lender 100-200 bps more spread (or 3-5% OID) to attract them, the cost of the extension goes up materially. Some A&Es die because the yank-a-bank costs exceed the sponsor's tolerance.

17.14 OPEN MARKET PURCHASES AND DUTCH AUCTIONS

Borrowers can repurchase their own debt in the open market, reducing outstanding principal and — critically — altering the denominator for voting calculations.

**The Strategic Dynamic**

Here's the mechanics: If a borrower purchases €100 million of a €500 million loan, the voting base drops to €400 million. A majority now requires only €200 million of consenting lenders rather than €250 million.

More aggressively: if purchased debt is held by an affiliate and affiliate lender provisions are weak or absent, the borrower effectively controls those votes. This is a subtle form of vote manufacturing — you're not issuing new debt, you're just acquiring existing debt and neutralizing it from the voting pool.

I've seen sponsors use this tactic in conjunction with A&E proposals. They'll go to the market, buy back 10-15% of the loan at a modest discount (say, 2-3 points), reducing the voting base. Then they propose an amendment. Suddenly their 50% support represents 55-60% of the reduced pool. The amendment passes. The non-extending lenders who didn't sell in the buyback find themselves in a shrinking legacy tranche.

**Dutch Auctions**

In a Dutch auction, the borrower invites lenders to submit offers to sell at specified price points. The borrower then accepts offers from the lowest price upward until its repurchase budget is exhausted.

Dutch auctions are elegant because they allow borrowers to retire debt at a discount while rewarding lenders willing to exit at the lowest price — often those most pessimistic about recovery prospects. The borrower gets leverage (shrinks the voting base). The eager sellers get liquidity.

The strategic use: conduct a Dutch auction before an A&E. Buy back the pessimists at 95-97. Shrink the voting base. Propose the A&E. The amendment now has easier math.

17.15 EXIT CONSENTS AND COVENANT STRIPPING

Beyond the core A&E mechanics, the debt amendment landscape has evolved to include more aggressive tools for sponsors seeking to reshape credit documents without genuine refinancing. These techniques sit at the boundary of what's efficient capital structure management and what's potentially coercive creditor treatment.

**Amendment & Exchange and the Consent Solicitation Framework**

Amendment & exchange offers, or "A&X" transactions, give sponsors a mechanism to propose modifications to debt terms via consent solicitations. The underlying process is straightforward: the sponsor identifies specific provisions they want to amend (usually protective covenants, negative pledges, or restrictions on dividends), proposes an amendment, and solicits consent from lenders.

The key advantage of A&X over traditional full refinancings is that you don't need to replace the entire credit facility with new money. You're simply modifying the terms of the existing debt. This can be much cheaper and faster.

But here's where it becomes sharp practice: **Covenant stripping** takes this mechanism and uses it to systematically remove protective provisions from a credit agreement without compensating the lenders who are being stripped of protections.

**Exit Consents: The Coercive Variant**

Exit consents combine the consent solicitation with an exchange offer. Here's how they typically work:

The sponsor proposes an amendment to the credit agreement that removes (or significantly loosens) covenant protections. Simultaneously, they offer to exchange the current debt for new debt at a lower nominal price — say, 98 cents on the dollar — but only for lenders who consent to the amendment. Lenders who decline to consent can hold onto their original debt at par, but they're left holding a credit that's now weaker (covenants are gone, sponsor has more operational flexibility, subordination risks have shifted).

The economic pressure is implicit: If you don't consent, you're holding a weaker credit. If you do consent, you take a small loss now (2 cents on the dollar) but you're also getting out of a weakening credit.

This structure has generated significant case law, particularly in European courts. The landmark case is **Assenagon v Irish Bank Resolution Corporation** (2021), a UK judgment that examined whether exit consents constituted unlawful coercion of minority lenders. The court found that exit consents were unlawful if their sole effect was to pressure minority lenders into accepting losses through coercion, without any genuine commercial rationale for the amendment itself.

However, a subsequent case, **Azevedo v Imcopa** (a Brazilian matter with implications for international creditor disputes), established that exit consents could be lawful if:

1. The consenting lenders received genuine commercial consideration (not just the exchange price, but substantive benefits).
2. The amendment proposed addressed a real problem in the credit agreement (not artificial restrictions created to coerce).
3. The minority lenders retained meaningful protections or exit rights.

The practical implication: Sponsors need to justify the amendments they're proposing on their merits, not use them as a veneer for coercion. A court reviewing the transaction will ask whether the covenant removals were necessary to optimize the capital structure or whether they were purely designed to isolate and penalise minority lenders.

**European SFA Thresholds for Covenant Changes**

European syndicated facility agreements establish consent thresholds for different types of amendments:

- **Majority Lender (>50%, often 66.67%)**: Required for covenant removals or loosening. A sponsor can typically tighten covenants with the agreement of a majority lender, reflecting the principle that stronger protections aren't controversial. But loosening requires broader consensus.

- **Super Majority (80%, sometimes 66.67%)**: Required for releases of security or guarantees. If a borrower wants to unencumber assets or release guarantors, lenders want broad consensus.

- **All Lender**: Required for changes to voting definitions, pro-rata sharing clauses, waterfall or priority provisions, and changes to what constitutes a "Required Lender." These are sacred — changes require unanimity.

In practice, many sponsors try to accomplish covenant stripping through a series of technical amendments that, individually, fall below the all-lender threshold but, collectively, eliminate the meaningful protections. For instance:

- An amendment to lengthen the measurement period for certain EBITDA adjustments (majority lender).
- A second amendment to expand the basket for add-backs (majority lender).
- A third amendment to loosen the financial testing trigger (super majority).

Cumulatively, these amendments might gut the leverage covenant without any single amendment requiring all-lender approval. Sophisticated credit documentation addresses this by requiring all-lender consent for amendments that, in aggregate, reduce the financial headroom by more than a specified percentage (e.g., >15%).

**Bond Exit Consents: Different Mechanics, Similar Risks**

If the sponsor has issued high-yield bonds alongside bank facilities, exit consents on bonds operate differently than on loans, because bond indentures have their own amendment thresholds:

- **90% (or supermajority of voting holders)**: Required to amend "sacred rights" including principal amount, interest rate, or maturity.
- **Simple Majority (50.1%)**: Required to amend "routine" restrictive covenants (dividend restrictions, incurrence tests, asset sale restrictions).
- **75-80%**: Required for guarantee releases or security releases.

A sponsor could theoretically propose removing covenants from the bond indenture with a simple majority, while retaining those same covenants in the bank loan agreement. The bank lenders, seeing the bonds now have loose covenants while they retain tight ones, might feel pressure to amend the loan to match.

This cross-instrument coordination problem is particularly acute in large PE-backed leveraged buyouts where the sponsor has issued both bank debt and bonds.

**Multi-Class Creditor Coordination and the Holdout Problem**

One critical limitation of covenant stripping via amendment is that it only works if holders are coordinated. If a company has:

- Senior term loans (bank syndicate)
- High-yield bonds (distributed, held by many institutions)
- Junior secured facilities (mezzanine)

A sponsor might propose removing covenants from the senior loans. But if the HY bonds don't also see covenant relief, the junior creditors might not participate in refinancings or exit opportunities that depend on loose covenants. This creates a "lowest common denominator" problem: the loosening is only as valuable as the least-loosened tranche.

I've seen deals where a sponsor negotiated covenant relief with the bank lenders but found that bond holders, who had different committee structures and incentives, wouldn't agree. The amendment couldn't proceed without creating a patchwork of different covenants across instruments, which is operationally messy and legally risky.

17.16 SNOOZE-TO-LOSE MECHANICS

Modern credit documentation has increasingly adopted "snooze-to-lose" provisions, a departure from traditional LMA (Loan Market Association) standards. These mechanics fundamentally alter how lenders exercise consent and dissent rights.

**The Basic Mechanism**

In traditional LMA documentation, a lender who doesn't respond to a consent solicitation is simply disregarded from the quorum for calculating whether a threshold (majority lender, super majority, etc.) has been met. If 80 lenders are in the syndicate and 50 don't respond to a consent solicitation, you calculate the threshold based on the 30 who responded.

Under snooze-to-lose, a non-responding lender is typically disregarded after a specified period — usually 10 business days from the solicitation. But more aggressively, some agreements deem a non-responder as having **approved** the amendment, rather than simply being disregarded. This is materially different.

Example: A solicitation for covenant loosening goes out to a 75-lender syndicate, with a super-majority threshold of 50 lenders. Under traditional mechanics:
- If 40 respond yes, 35 don't respond, and 0 respond no, the amendment fails (40/40 is not super majority of 75).

Under aggressive snooze-to-lose mechanics:
- If 40 respond yes, and 35 don't respond within 10 business days, those 35 are deemed "yes," and the amendment passes (75/75).

**The LMA Position and Modern Evolution**

The original LMA standard specifically carved out "fundamental" matters from snooze mechanics. Fundamental all-lender matters — such as extensions of maturity, reductions in principal, reductions in interest rate, releases of all security, and releases of all guarantees — were required to have affirmative consent from each lender, even if that lender snooze'd.

The modern evolution has inverted this. Many contemporary deals now apply snooze-to-lose to literally all matters, including fundamental ones. The justification is operational efficiency: a lender that can't be bothered to respond within 10 days shouldn't be able to hold up market-standard amendments.

But from a creditor protection perspective, this is a sea change. It means a passive lender can have material terms of the credit agreement amended — including extensions of maturity — without any affirmative action on their part.

**"Delay and It's Okay" Language**

Some agreements couple snooze-to-lose with language stating that non-responding lenders are "deemed to be participating" in the solicitation, or language that treats non-responders as "specified group" or "class" members for purposes of calculating consents to structural adjustments.

The practical effect is that a non-responding lender, by virtue of inaction, becomes a member of a class that has participated in a transaction. This can have unintended consequences: if the class membership triggers different tax treatment, different regulatory reporting, or different subordination mechanics, the passive lender suddenly finds itself in a different position than it thought it was in.

**The Procedural Safeguards (When Present)**

Responsible credit documentation includes procedural protections even within a snooze-to-lose framework:

1. **Clear notice requirements**: The solicitation must be delivered via the agent to all lenders via their designated contacts, with clear specification of the deadline.

2. **Affirmative acknowledgment**: Some deals require lenders to affirmatively click a box or provide a signature authorizing deemed consent, rather than treating silence as approval.

3. **Escalation rights for materially affected lenders**: If a lender's interests are materially affected (e.g., an amendment that extends maturity on a substantial portion of their commitment), they retain the right to affirmative consent despite snooze-to-lose mechanics.

4. **Specified group carve-outs**: Amendments that affect only a specified subset of lenders (e.g., extension series mechanics) often exclude non-affected lenders from snooze-to-lose, recognizing that affected lenders need protection.

I generally prefer credit agreements with these safeguards over pure snooze-to-lose mechanics, but the trend is clearly toward the latter. If you're a lender in a credit with aggressive snooze provisions, you need to be actively engaged during amendment solicitations. Assuming you can sleep while the credit is being reshaped is a recipe for finding yourself in a position you didn't intend.

17.17 STRUCTURAL LOAN CHANGES AND REDENOMINATION

Some of the most complex amendments don't involve removing protections but rather **reclassifying or rescheduling existing debt through "structural adjustments."** These mechanics allow borrowers to extend tranches, increase facilities, or even change currency, with consent thresholds well below all-lender.

**The Structural Adjustment Framework**

Traditional credit agreements require all-lender consent (or supermajority) for amendments to material economic terms like maturity, principal amount, or pricing. But many modern documents — particularly large-cap European syndicated facilities — carve out a "structural adjustment" framework that permits specified modifications with only "participating lender" consent or, more aggressively, with just "affected lender" consent.

The theory is sound: If you're extending only a subset of the term loan, the non-affected lenders shouldn't need to consent. But the practice is often broader. Some agreements define "structural adjustments" so expansively that nearly any amendment can be shoe-horned into that category.

Typical structural adjustment categories include:

- **Extensions**: Moving maturity of a tranche to a later date, with participating or affected lender consent (not all-lender).
- **Increases**: Increasing the aggregate facility amount, with affected lender consent (new lenders joining the affected subset).
- **Redenomination**: Converting a facility from one currency to another, with participating or affected lender consent.
- **Refinancings of tranches**: Replacing a maturing tranche with a new facility, with affected lender consent.

~80% of large-cap European term loans now use a bond-style approach: only participating or affected lender consent is required, without a separate majority lender vote.

**The Critical Risk: Senior Tranches and Ranking**

Here's where institutional investors need to be careful: Structural adjustment mechanics should **only permit amendments to pari passu or subordinated tranches, never to senior tranches.**

A problematic scenario: A borrower has a senior term loan (Tranche A) and a subordinated term loan (Tranche B). The credit agreement permits structural adjustments to both tranches with affected lender consent. The borrower proposes converting Tranche B from subordinated to super-senior (ranking senior to Tranche A), with affected-lender consent from Tranche B holders.

From a Tranche A lender perspective, this is a disaster — their seniority just evaporated. Yet if the structural adjustment framework doesn't explicitly prohibit senior-ranking changes, and if affected lender consent is the only requirement, the Tranche A lenders could be powerless to stop it.

Strong credit documentation requires all-lender consent for any amendment that could affect the ranking or subordination of non-affected tranches. Some SFAs permit super-senior tranches, but only if the super-senior status was disclosed at origination and only if any new super-senior facilities require a specified threshold (typically 66.67% or 80% super-majority consent from the wider syndicate, not just affected lenders).

**The Valeo Foods Case: Redenomination via Structural Adjustment**

A useful real example is Valeo Foods, a European food manufacturing and distribution business that, some years ago, executed a GBP-to-EUR redenomination of its term loans using structural adjustment mechanics.

The borrower had outstanding sterling term loans but wanted to eliminate currency mismatch (the underlying EBITDA was predominantly euro-denominated). Rather than refinancing into a new euro-denominated facility, the borrower proposed a redenomination: the existing sterling facility would be converted into euros at a specified spot rate, with new lending documentation to match.

The redenomination was structured as a "structural adjustment" and required only affected lender consent from the sterling term loan holders. Non-affected lenders (revolver holders, other tranches) weren't required to consent. The amendment proceeded and was executed successfully.

From the borrower's perspective, this was efficient capital structure management. From a lender perspective, it raised questions: Were currency conversion mechanics clearly disclosed? Were all lenders aware that a subset could unilaterally redenominate? What if sterling holders had wanted to maintain their sterling exposure?

In practice, the Valeo Foods transaction was orderly and well-documented, but it illustrates how structural adjustment mechanics can enable fairly dramatic changes with limited consent requirements.

**The Documentary Safeguard: "Specified Group" Language**

Responsible SFAs include a critical safeguard: amendments that affect a "specified group" of lenders (e.g., a particular extension series, or a particular tranche) include language carving out non-affected lenders from consent requirements but also explicitly prohibiting amendments that would adversely affect non-affected lenders' rights.

Language might read: "Notwithstanding anything herein, no amendment may be made to any structural aspect of a tranche if such amendment materially and adversely affects the rights of non-participating lenders in such tranche, without the consent of such non-participating lenders."

This isn't foolproof — "materially and adversely affects" is litigable — but it creates at least a documentary requirement that amendments consider externalities.

17.18 WHAT TWENTY YEARS TEACHES

Let me reflect on what I've learned about A&E transactions after two decades in this business.

First: A&Es are most useful when they solve a genuine problem — temporary market dislocation, a short-term bump in leverage, a specific event risk that the business can navigate. They're least useful when they're band-aids covering structural problems. I've made money on A&E deals where the underlying business was genuinely resilient, and I've lost money on deals where the sponsor was using an A&E to avoid facing a harder truth.

The critical question to ask, always: "Will this business be better positioned for refinancing at the new maturity, or will it be in worse condition?" If the answer is "better" (revenue growth is accelerating, EBITDA is recovering, the competitive position is improving), then an A&E is intelligent. If the answer is "worse," or "same, just more levered," then you're extending into deterioration.

Second: The lender syndicate's composition matters enormously. A syndicate full of patient, buy-and-hold investors (insurance companies, buy-and-hold funds, pension funds) will often be enthusiastic about A&Es. A syndicate full of mark-to-market traders, hedge funds, and secondary market players will be more reluctant. As a sponsor, you want to syndicate into the right investor base for your exit timeline. If you think you might need flexibility later, don't sell only to quick-flip traders.

Third: The non-extending lender position is genuinely risky. I've held non-extending positions that worked out fine (we got paid off at par when we wanted), and I've held ones that didn't (we got forced to extend anyway due to liquidity constraints, or we faced covenant breaches that non-extending lenders were powerless to prevent). If you're ever a non-extending lender, make sure you understand what that position actually means — you're not as protected as the mechanics might suggest.

Fourth: Covenant loosening in an A&E is a trade-off. You get flexibility (less likely to breach covenants in an extension period), but you lose visibility into deterioration. I prefer A&Es with covenant step-downs that gradually tighten, giving you flexibility early in the extension period and more visibility later.

Fifth: The dynamics of lender groups change after an A&E. You've now got tranches with different maturities and possibly different pricing. This fragmentation, while mathematically elegant, creates incentive misalignment. Be thoughtful about what disputes might arise later and whether your documentation protects your interests.

Finally: Don't underestimate the psychological impact of A&Es on sponsors and management. A successful A&E can breed overconfidence. The sponsor thinks they've dodged a bullet and can run the business the way they have been. But they also think: If we need to A&E again, we can. This can lead to underinvestment, deteriorating maintenance, underinvestment in working capital, etc. As a lender, you need to push back. Don't let an A&E become an excuse for mediocre operational performance.

SUMMARY: THE A&E PRACTITIONER'S CHECKLIST

When evaluating an A&E proposal (whether as a lender deciding whether to extend, a sponsor deciding whether to pursue one, or an investor evaluating the credit):

**Pre-A&E Assessment:**
☐ Is refinancing actually unavailable or prohibitively expensive? (If not, A&E probably isn't optimal)
☐ What's the sponsor's specific thesis for why the business will be better positioned for refinancing at the new maturity?
☐ Does the business have a clear operational plan for the extension period, or is it just "buy time"?
☐ What's the trend in EBITDA? Growing, stable, or declining?
☐ What's the customer concentration and competitive position? Improving or deteriorating?

**Terms Assessment (If You're Extending):**
☐ Is the spread increase sufficient to compensate for extended duration and additional credit risk?
☐ What's the OID, and how does it amortise into the economics?
☐ Are covenant changes reasonable, or are they so loose they eliminate visibility?
☐ What's the weighted average life impact, and how does that affect your portfolio?
☐ Are there step-downs in covenants (tightening over time), or are they static?
☐ What's your redemption likelihood at the new maturity? Can you actually refinance into better terms?

**Terms Assessment (If You're Not Extending):**
☐ Will your non-extending tranche be liquid, or will you be illiquid?
☐ Do you have a put option or refinancing ability if the maturity approaches?
☐ Will you be in a minority position at your maturity?
☐ Are the extending lenders' covenants loose enough that the credit could deteriorate without covenant protection for you?

**Documentation:**
☐ Are extension series clearly documented with waterfall treatment?
☐ Are mandatory prepayment provisions clear on how they apply to multiple series?
☐ Is the A&E carve-out from sacred rights clearly drafted?
☐ Are the mechanics for extension elections, consent thresholds, and OID payment clear?
☐ Is change of control clearly addressed for multiple tranches?

**Portfolio Management:**
☐ If this is a CLO holding, does the WAL impact affect your portfolio limits?
☐ How does the spread change affect your mark-to-market valuation?
☐ If you're a bank, what's the regulatory capital impact?
☐ What's your expected return over the extension period vs. alternatives?

**Exit Consents & Covenant Stripping (if applicable):**
☐ Is the sponsor proposing a simultaneous amendment + exchange offer (exit consent)?
☐ What covenants are being removed or loosened? Are they being justified on commercial grounds or used coercively?
☐ If covenant stripping is being proposed across multiple debt instruments (bank + bonds), is it coordinated?
☐ Are you as a non-consenting lender left holding a materially weakened credit?
☐ Has the sponsor offered adequate consideration to consenting lenders, or are they relying on the exchange discount?
☐ What procedural safeguards exist to prevent discriminatory treatment of minority lenders?

**Snooze-to-Lose Mechanics (if applicable):**
☐ Does the credit agreement permit amendments via snooze-to-lose, or does it require affirmative consent?
☐ Are fundamental matters (maturity, principal, rate) carved out from snooze-to-lose, or are they subject to it?
☐ How long is the deemed-consent period? (10 business days is standard but can be shorter)
☐ Is silence treated as disregard (neutral) or as deemed approval (affirmative)?
☐ Are there escalation mechanisms if amendments materially affect your position?
☐ As a lender, can you affirmatively opt out of deemed consent, or will you only know after the fact?

**Structural Adjustments & Ranking (if applicable):**
☐ Does the credit agreement permit structural adjustments (extensions, increases, redenominations) with less than all-lender consent?
☐ Are structural adjustments limited to pari passu or subordinated tranches, or could they affect ranking?
☐ If redenomination is proposed (currency conversion), have all lenders been clearly notified and given affirmative consent?
☐ Are non-affected lenders protected from amendments that materially and adversely affect their rights?
☐ Is "specified group" or "class" language used to limit consent thresholds? Does it create unintended consequences?
☐ If new senior or super-senior tranches are proposed, what threshold is required for existing lenders?

**Final Decision:**
☐ Am I extending into a credit that will actually be stronger, or just longer?
☐ Is the economics sufficient for the incremental risk?
☐ If I decline to extend, am I comfortable with the consequences (early maturity, illiquidity, etc.)?
☐ If amendments beyond simple extension are proposed, have I fully understood the impact on my position?
