THE ROLE OF RATING AGENCIES IN LEVERAGED FINANCE

## Introduction: The Gatekeepers Nobody Trusts Completely

Walk into any leveraged finance origination meeting in London or Frankfurt, and within the first hour you'll hear the rating agencies blamed for something. Too conservative on working capital assumptions. Too lenient on covenant flex. Changing their methodology just before your roadshow. Moving a credit to negative watch on a Tuesday afternoon, destroying weeks of distribution work. I've been in this business for over two decades, and my relationship with the rating agencies has evolved from skeptical respect to what I'd call pragmatic acceptance—they are essential infrastructure that everyone loves to hate.

The curious paradox of modern finance is this: investors claim they don't blindly follow ratings anymore, especially after the 2008 financial crisis exposed the catastrophic failures of the rating agency model. Yet every CLO manager you speak with will immediately tell you about rating constraints that prevent them from holding a particular credit. Every loan agreement contains language that ties consequences to a rating change. The entire leveraged finance ecosystem is architected around these three oligopolistic firms: Moody's Investors Service, Standard & Poor's, and Fitch Ratings. Love them or despise them, you cannot build a modern leveraged structure without them.

This chapter represents two decades of observation from across the table in these negotiations. I've been a banker pitching ratings assumptions that got cut in half by a skeptical analyst. I've been a borrower watching my company's credit rating deteriorate through circumstances partially beyond my control, and I've represented sponsors who hired outside advisors specifically to fight rating agency determinations. I've watched the big three navigate post-crisis regulation that fundamentally altered their business model and their incentive structures. What I've learned is that rating agencies, for all their flaws, operate within a remarkably consistent framework—if you understand their methodology, their politics, and their constraints, you can navigate a transaction toward a more predictable outcome.

Let me be clear about something from the outset: this is not a defense of rating agencies. The 2008 financial crisis occurred in substantial part because they got leverage ratings fundamentally wrong, and the structural incentive problems in the issuer-pays model remain largely unresolved. But in my experience, the most successful investment bankers and borrowers are not those who rail against the system—they're those who understand it deeply enough to work within its bounds and occasionally push against them strategically.

## Understanding the Big Three: Methodology and Philosophy

The first thing to grasp about leveraged finance rating methodologies is that while Moody's, S&P, and Fitch all arrive at credit ratings, they do so through frameworks that differ in meaningful ways. These are not just academic distinctions—they produce actual rating differences that matter enormously for CLO distribution, pricing, and covenant structure.

Moody's Investors Service has traditionally been viewed as the most aggressive rater in leveraged finance, though I'd characterize this differently. They are the most formula-driven, at least for leverage metrics. Their traditional framework relies heavily on leverage multiples measured in a very specific way: their EBITDA definition is often the most conservative of the three. They have historically been willing to forecast EBITDA recovery relatively quickly after transaction close, but their starting point is usually lower than S&P's, creating a kind of offsetting conservatism. What I've found over the years is that Moody's analysts tend to be the most rigorous about exploring downside scenarios and are genuinely skeptical of aggressive working capital forecasts. In 2019, when I worked on a large European acquisition financing for a plastics company, Moody's aggressively questioned management's assumption that they could reduce days sales outstanding from 65 days to 45 days post-closing. S&P accepted the same assumption more readily. This wasn't malice or incompetence—it reflected a genuine philosophical difference about how much credit to give to integrating management teams.

Standard & Poor's has cultivated a reputation as the "middle" rater—not as aggressive as Moody's, not as lenient as Fitch. In my experience, this characterization misses something important. S&P's framework is notably more scenario-based than Moody's'. They construct multiple stress cases and evaluate how a credit would perform across different economic states. This framework actually makes them harder to predict in some ways, because the same leverage metric might receive a different rating depending on which industry factors and macroeconomic assumptions they're emphasizing that quarter. I've sat through presentations where S&P analysts emphasized commodity price volatility as the critical risk factor for a materials company, while Moody's focused primarily on the company's acquisition integration capacity. Both approaches had merit, but they produced different rating outcomes.

Fitch Ratings has, for most of my career, occupied the position of the most "accommodative" of the three big raters. This is not entirely fair to them, but there is a kernel of truth in it. Fitch has historically been more willing to give credit for sponsor support and owner involvement in operational improvement. In several sponsor-backed transactions I've worked on, Fitch rated the credit one notch higher than S&P in the initial rating, creating an interesting distribution dynamic. There's a well-established pattern in the market where arrangers deliberately craft a transaction to obtain Fitch's cooperation on a particular rating, knowing that this will help with CLO placement even if the other two raters dissent.

What's important to understand is that these philosophical differences don't exist because the agencies want to be different. Rather, they emerge from different institutional histories and different client bases. Moody's has a larger presence in structured finance and CLO management, which means their methodology evolved with particular attention to the needs and perspectives of CLO managers. S&P has traditionally had strong relationships with investment-grade corporate clients and brings some of that discipline into leveraged finance. Fitch, while smaller, has historically been more aggressive about developing relationships with sponsors and has built their methodology partly around sponsor-focused questions that the other two sometimes neglect.

Over the past fifteen years, I've watched these methodological differences narrow. There was a genuine process of convergence after 2008, as all three agencies faced intense regulatory scrutiny and conscious efforts to make their processes more rigorous and more transparent. This convergence is imperfect—splits still happen regularly—but it's real. I've also noticed that all three have become more uncomfortable with consensus assumptions about economic recovery. In 2015, after the oil price collapse, I worked on a transaction in the energy services sector where all three agencies immediately tightened their stress scenarios. None of them wanted to be the rater that had approved an overly aggressive credit that subsequently deteriorated. This post-crisis conservatism is one of the underappreciated impacts of 2008.

## Corporate Family Rating Versus Instrument Ratings: Understanding the Architecture

One of the most important concepts for navigating leveraged finance rating dynamics is the relationship between a corporate family rating (CFR) and the specific ratings assigned to individual instruments. Most practitioners have a surface understanding of this distinction, but the details matter enormously for structuring and negotiations.

The corporate family rating represents Moody's, S&P, or Fitch's assessment of the overall creditworthiness of the issuing company, evaluated as if all debts were equally senior and unencumbered. Think of it as the "naked" credit quality, without regard to the specific structure of a transaction. For most leveraged credit analysis, this is the foundational rating that everything else gets built from. When a banker tells a client "we think you can get a B+ rating," what they typically mean is that the CFR will be in the B+ category, usually translating to specific sub-ratings depending on instrument seniority.

The instrument ratings—the specific ratings assigned to term loans, senior notes, subordinated notes, and whatever other securities make up the capital structure—are then derived from the CFR through a notching framework. Notching is the technical term for the adjustments made to the CFR to reflect the position of a given debt instrument in the capital structure. A senior secured term loan might be rated two or three notches higher than the CFR. An unsecured senior note might be one notch below. Subordinated notes are typically three to five notches below. This framework creates a consistent logic across the transaction: the CFR anchors everything, and the notching adjusts for structural factors.

Here's where things get practically important. In the last six months of 2021, I worked on a large divisional sale where the leveraged buyout sponsor wanted to maximize the amount of senior secured debt in the structure. The CFR was set at BB-, and the sponsor wanted the senior term loan rated BBB-, which would have been seven notches of uplift from the CFR. This was immediately rejected by all three agencies as inconsistent with their notching frameworks. What ultimately happened was that we reduced leverage expectations, which improved the CFR to B+, and then applied the agencies' standard notching to achieve a BBB- rating on the senior term loan. This required the sponsor to accept lower leverage than originally modeled, which in turn affected equity returns. But it was the only path to the rating the sponsor wanted.

The reason this matters is that CLO managers, bank credit committees, and institutional investors often have specific rating buckets they will or will not hold. A CLO might be constrained to hold no more than 20 percent B-rated assets. Another might have a mandate to hold only investment-grade securities and a small basket of BB/B rated assets for return. If your senior term loan can only achieve a B+ rating due to notching constraints, but the CFR is BB-, the CLO manager needs to understand this relationship clearly. Many distribution failures occur because arrangers and issuers haven't communicated the notching story with sufficient clarity to the investor base.

One nuance I've grappled with repeatedly is how the rating agencies treat the intercompany structure within a leveraged platform. Suppose you're financing an acquisition where the parent company will be levered, and the target company will be held mostly unlevered at subsidiary level. How does this affect notching and the relationship between CFR and instrument ratings? The answer is that it depends on covenant linkage and structural subordination, which I'll discuss in detail below. But the practical point is that the "family" in "corporate family rating" can be defined different ways, and this creates strategic opportunities for arrangers to push for favorable notching treatment.

The most interesting case I've encountered involved a sponsor-backed industrials platform that had acquired three operating companies over two years. The question for the rating agencies was whether to rate them as one consolidated family or as separate rating entities. The consolidated approach would have produced lower ratings due to higher total leverage. Separately, the most creditworthy operating company could have been rated significantly higher. Through careful argument about operational independence and separate management teams, we managed to convince two of the three rating agencies to treat the platform more as a federation of companies than as a single consolidated entity. The third agency remained skeptical but didn't actively fight us. This meant the platform could access more favorable financing terms for the most creditworthy operating company while still maintaining debt capacity at the platform level. This kind of architectural sophistication is where deep relationships with rating agencies actually pay dividends.

## The Rating Process: Timeline, Engagement, and Analyst Psychology

Most bankers have a surface understanding of the rating process—you submit financials, the agencies rate your credit, you get a rating notification. In reality, the process is far more nuanced and offers multiple strategic intervention points for those who understand it. Let me walk through the typical timeline and discuss what rating agencies are actually looking for at each stage.

The process begins with the initiating call between an arranger and the lead rating analyst. This call typically happens about six to eight weeks before the expected CLO closing or syndication launch. The banker will provide preliminary information about the transaction structure, the target company, the leverage profile, and the projected financial performance. What's happening in this conversation is subtle but critical: the banker is trying to gauge which assumptions the analyst is likely to accept and which ones will face skepticism. The analyst is trying to understand the business, assess the quality of the management team, and form initial impressions about the credit.

I've learned over decades that these initial conversations establish assumptions that prove remarkably sticky. In 2014, during an initial call about a transportation and logistics acquisition, I made a comment about the company's strong market position and its ability to pass through some cost increases to customers. The Moody's analyst latched onto this and made it a cornerstone of her rating rationale. When market conditions tightened a year later and the company faced genuine pushback from customers on price increases, Moody's held firm on their rating, citing the structural advantage we had identified in that initial call. They had essentially made a commitment, even if informally.

Following the initial call, a typical timeline looks like this: the banker provides a rating package that includes audited financials from the target company, preliminary management presentations, an arranger-prepared business overview, and preliminary financial projections. The rating agencies typically need two to three weeks to conduct initial due diligence on this material. For more complex transactions, they may conduct teleconferences or calls with management to explore specific questions.

What rating analysts look for in these materials often surprises people. Yes, they care about leverage metrics—EBITDA multiples, interest coverage, free cash flow generation. But they care equally about qualitative factors: the credibility of management, the sustainability of the business model, the quality and stickiness of revenue, and the sponsor's track record if it's a sponsor-backed deal. I've seen credits with identical leverage metrics receive different ratings because one had a management team with a strong operational track record in the specific industry, while the other had a newly hired CEO from a different sector.

The analyst also cares deeply about the model build and the rigor of the financial projections. In 2019, I worked on a healthcare services transaction where Moody's spent an entire call with management questioning the company's assumption about retention rates for nursing staff. The financials projected 90 percent retention, but industry surveys suggested the benchmark was closer to 85 percent. The analyst wasn't being difficult—she was stress-testing a critical assumption that, if wrong, would materially impact cash flow coverage. When management revised down to 87 percent, the analyst accepted it. This kind of granular interrogation is standard and is actually valuable if you're on the receiving end of a credible feedback.

After the initial review period, the rating agencies conduct what they call a "rating committee" or "rating action committee" meeting. This is where the analyst presents their findings and proposes a rating to a committee of other analysts, managing directors, and sometimes external perspectives. The outcome is not necessarily the analyst's recommendation—committees can and do push back on analysts, ask for more due diligence, or demand changes to the rating rationale.

Here's something I've learned that's crucial: the outcome of a rating committee meeting often depends on how well the arranger and issuer have "sold" the credit story to the lead analyst before the committee convenes. A well-briefed analyst who believes in the credit and has developed a thoughtful rationale for it will present confidently to the committee. A skeptical analyst will highlight risks and vulnerabilities. The difference can be a full rating notch.

The timeline from the analyst's initial due diligence to a preliminary rating to the arranger typically takes four to six weeks for a straightforward deal. For more complex transactions—particularly sponsor-backed deals or situations involving operational improvement assumptions—it can stretch to eight to ten weeks. During this period, the arranger and issuer typically provide supplemental information, address questions, and sometimes push back on preliminary rating assumptions.

One practice that I've employed with increasing frequency is to engage a rating specialist firm that works with rating agencies between transactions. These firms employ former senior rating analysts from the big three agencies, and they can provide candid feedback about how your credit is likely to be received before you go formal. In 2018, we hired such a firm to evaluate a complex sponsor-backed industrial platform, and they identified that Moody's would likely be skeptical about management's operating margin assumptions. Instead of finding this out in a formal rating committee and having to scramble to provide more conservative numbers, we adjusted our package upfront and presented more achievable margins. The result was that we obtained the rating we targeted without surprise downgrades.

The rating agencies don't typically disclose preliminary ratings—the first rating you officially receive is what they call the "expected rating," which comes in the form of a letter to the issuer and the arranger. At this point, the issuer has the option to challenge the rating, provide additional information, or accept it. If there's a material disagreement, the issuer can request a rating committee review before the rating is finalized.

I want to be candid here about the psychological dimension of rating analyst interactions. These are smart, professional people who are acutely aware that their ratings have economic consequences. Many of them have experienced internal pressure following ratings that proved incorrect—whether too aggressive or too conservative. This creates an interesting dynamic: analysts are often more comfortable defending cautious assumptions than optimistic ones. If a transaction deteriorates and the analyst was conservative, they can point to their skepticism. If they were aggressive and the credit performs well, they get less credit than you might expect.

This asymmetry means that when you encounter an analyst who seems unreasonably negative on assumptions that you believe are sound, it's often not about the specific assumption—it's about their risk tolerance and their past experience. The best strategy I've found is to acknowledge the risk, provide evidence that mitigates it, and propose a compromise position that the analyst can defend credibly to their committee. I once spent an entire call with a Fitch analyst discussing the appropriate level of hedging for commodity price exposure in a chemicals transaction. The analyst wanted full hedging of price exposure; I wanted partial hedging. We eventually landed on a structure where we hedged 75 percent of anticipated exposure, with an annual tranche renewal process. The analyst could defend this as appropriately risk-conscious, and we achieved the rating we needed.

## Recovery Ratings and Loss-Given-Default Analysis: The Structural Reality

One of the most consequential yet often misunderstood aspects of rating methodology is how rating agencies assign recovery ratings and quantify loss-given-default in leveraged finance. This is where the mathematical foundation of ratings intersects with real-world insolvency and restructuring dynamics.

Recovery ratings are assigned separately from credit ratings. A company might have a BB credit rating, which reflects the likelihood of default, but also a "Recovery Rating 2," which reflects the expected recovery if default occurs. The recovery rating (on a scale from 1 to 6, where 1 is highest recovery) essentially factors in the structure of the debt capital stack and the quality of collateral.

The fundamental calculation underlying recovery ratings goes like this: if a company defaults, the rating agencies estimate what percentage of their debt gets recovered through the value of assets available to creditors. This is where collateral quality, lien position, and the leverage of the company all matter critically. A senior secured term loan on a business with unencumbered assets worth 3x the loan amount is likely to recover 85-95 percent of principal. A subordinated unsecured note on the same company would recover much less, perhaps 30-50 percent.

Here's what I've observed over twenty years: rating agencies have genuinely improved their recovery analysis post-crisis, but they still make systematic errors in predicting what assets are actually recoverable in a real default. They tend to assume orderly liquidation scenarios and often don't account for fire-sale dynamics or the impact of extended workouts on asset values.

In 2011, I worked on a restructuring where a sponsor-backed energy services company defaulted on its debt. The rating agencies' recovery assumptions, which had been explicitly stated in the original credit rating, contemplated asset sales at 90 percent of going-concern value. In the actual restructuring, assets sold at 60-70 percent of going-concern value. The difference was attributable to market timing, economic conditions at the time of the default, and the specific assets being sold. Rating agencies had been correct about the seniority and lien position of the debt, but wrong about the recoverable value.

This distinction matters because recovery ratings influence structured finance professionals' behavior. A secured lender who believes recovery is 90 percent may take more risk on default probability, knowing they'll recover most of their principal. A subordinated lender who believes recovery is 40 percent needs much higher spread to compensate for expected loss. The product of default probability and loss-given-default is expected loss, and expected loss should be reflected in pricing.

The recovery rating framework is also where secured versus unsecured debt gets explicitly addressed. Senior secured debt gets the benefit of any identified collateral pool. In a typical leveraged buyout, you might have a term loan secured by first liens on substantially all assets, plus a second lien facility secured by junior liens. The first lien term loan gets rated much higher (typically 2-3 notches above unsecured) than a first lien subordinated note, which in turn gets rated much higher than unsecured notes.

What I've grappled with repeatedly is that recovery assumptions are sometimes inconsistent within a single rating. In one transportation transaction in 2016, the rating agencies assumed relatively modest asset values in estimating recovery from a default scenario, yet they also assumed those same assets would support a higher leverage multiple. The logic seemed to be that the assets were worth a lot in a going-concern sale to a strategic buyer, but much less in a bankruptcy liquidation. Fair enough, but it creates an asymmetry in the modeling that's worth thinking about.

One specific aspect of recovery rating that I've learned to pay close attention to is how rating agencies treat sponsor support in recovery scenarios. Early in my career, I assumed that sponsor equity was essentially worthless in a default—that you needed to underwrite recoveries based purely on collateral. Rating agencies, particularly Fitch, sometimes assigned higher recovery ratings to sponsor-backed credits based partly on an assumption that the sponsor would inject additional capital in a distress scenario. This assumption was tested and validated in the 2008-2012 period in some cases, and invalidated in others. Sponsors will sometimes support credits if it's strategic to do so, but they won't throw good money after bad in a truly hopeless situation.

Loss-given-default (LGD) analysis is closely related to recovery ratings but operates at a different level of specificity. While recovery ratings are ordinal rankings, LGD is typically expressed as a percentage (e.g., 35 percent LGD means a 65 percent recovery). Rating agencies use LGD assumptions to calibrate their default probability assumptions. A credit with low LGD due to strong recovery prospects can be assigned a lower credit rating (meaning higher default probability) than a credit with high LGD, because the overall expected loss is similar.

This interplay between default probability and LGD is critical for understanding why two credits with similar leverage might receive different ratings. If one has much higher collateral coverage and recovery prospects, it can tolerate higher leverage because the loss magnitude in default is lower. This is correct conceptually, but it places enormous importance on getting collateral valuation and recovery assumptions right.

In 2017, I worked on a retail holding company acquisition where the sponsor wanted to maximize leverage. The real estate collateral available was substantial, but valuation assumptions were aggressive. We engaged an independent appraiser to provide third-party collateral valuations, which were 10-15 percent lower than management's estimates. The rating agencies eagerly used the independent appraisals rather than management's estimates, which meant lower collateral coverage and therefore lower recovery ratings. This pushed down the overall credit rating despite the sponsor's argument that the management valuations were more accurate. The lesson: third-party validation of collateral matters enormously in the rating process, and biased valuations get caught.

## Rating Triggers and Credit Documentation: Where Ratings Become Contractual

One of the most direct ways rating agency methodologies affect leveraged finance transactions is through rating triggers embedded in credit documents. These are covenant provisions that get activated or modify terms if a debt instrument's rating changes.

The most common rating trigger is the step-down (or step-up) provision in CLO documents. A CLO is essentially a portfolio of loans and bonds managed by a professional manager, with multiple tranches of notes sold to investors. The CLO manager's ability to buy and sell assets in the portfolio is constrained by the ratings on the CLO tranches. If the AA-rated notes get downgraded to A+, the CLO documents might require the manager to reduce leverage on the CLO, which forces asset sales to pay down notes. This creates a mechanical relationship between the underlying credit ratings and the CLO's financial profile.

In leveraged loans, rating triggers typically appear in the form of pricing adjustments. A loan might have a base coupon of SOFR + 450 basis points, but include a provision that if the company's credit rating drops from BB to B+, the spread increases by 25 basis points. These are called "rating-based pricing grids" and they're standard in loan documents. What's interesting is that they create a perverse incentive: if a credit is already deteriorating, the rating-based pricing increase comes precisely when the company can least afford to pay higher rates.

I've also seen rating triggers that affect covenant flexibility. An indenture might include a provision that if the issuer is rated BB or higher by at least two of the three rating agencies, certain covenants are suspended or loosened. This creates a strong incentive to maintain a minimum rating. In 2020, during the COVID-19 crisis, I worked on a credit where a BB rating was the threshold for covenant relief. When Moody's moved one credit to negative watch, creating genuine uncertainty about whether a downgrade would follow, the issuer and sponsor immediately focused resources on addressing whatever concerns the analyst had, specifically to prevent a BB downgrade. The company ultimately narrowly maintained its BB rating.

From an arranger perspective, crafting rating triggers is an art. You want them tight enough to be meaningful to the borrower (so they create the right incentives) but loose enough not to create cascade effects that force unnecessary deleveraging or covenant tightening. I've seen deals where poorly structured rating triggers created a kind of death spiral: a credit rating downgrade triggered a pricing increase, which made the credit less able to cover debt service, which created more financial stress, which led to another rating downgrade. This is why rating triggers need to be carefully calibrated and stress-tested for their practical impact.

One specific example that illustrates this: in 2015, a large leisure company had a CLO financing with a "step-down" provision that allowed the CLO manager to sell assets if certain ratings thresholds were crossed. When that company's credit ratings came under pressure, the rating trigger forced the CLO manager to sell the position, which created a fire-sale dynamic. The manager's forced selling depressed prices, which affected other CLO managers' valuations on similar credits, creating a negative feedback loop. This is an example of how rating triggers designed with good intentions created real market dysfunction.

The practical lesson I've learned is this: rating triggers should be transparent and should be designed to provide adequate early warning before they materially impact a transaction. A step-down trigger that forces CLO asset sales should be at a rating level and within a rating band that provides meaningful time for managers to adjust holdings gradually. A rating trigger that kicks off a covenant modification should give the borrower time to address whatever credit concerns triggered the rating action.

## The CLO Rating Constraint: How Rating Requirements Shape Entire Transactions

To understand the modern leveraged finance market, you absolutely must understand how CLO rating constraints shape deal structure, leverage, and pricing. This is one of the areas where rating agencies have the most mechanical and most profound impact on the market.

A CLO typically issues multiple tranches of notes with different credit ratings and seniorities. Investment-grade CLO notes (AAA to BBB-) are sold to a certain type of investor—insurance companies, pension funds, asset managers with conservative mandates. Below-investment-grade CLO notes (BB, B) are sold to different investors. The constraints of these different investor bases create a mathematical constraint: a CLO manager cannot hold too much below-investment-grade collateral without the notes themselves becoming below-investment-grade.

This is where the concept of "rating constraints" becomes critical. If a CLO's portfolio contains collateral with an average rating of B+, the CLO notes will likely be constrained to BB at best, maybe B or lower. To achieve an AAA-rated note from a CLO with investment-grade quality paper, the collateral pool needs to be rated in the BB to BBB range on average. This creates an enormous market pressure to keep loan ratings in the BB and BBB ranges.

Here's the practical impact: I've worked on transactions where the natural credit quality of the borrower and the leverage implied a B+ or B rating, but the arranger and sponsor collectively pushed back hard on that rating because it would have limited the universe of potential CLO buyers. The sponsor was willing to reduce leverage and improve credit quality specifically to achieve a BB rating, knowing that this would make the loan more attractive to CLOs and therefore easier to syndicate at tighter spreads.

In 2018, during a period of very strong leverage and tight spreads, I worked on a mid-market acquisition where the natural rating was B+. Getting to BB required reducing leverage by 0.5x, which reduced sponsor returns by roughly 200 basis points. The sponsor made this trade-off explicitly because CLO demand for BB and BBB paper was strong, while B paper faced less certain demand. This is what you call being a rational participant in market structure.

The flip side of this dynamic is that CLO constraints create a kind of leverage ceiling for the market. When CLO managers are forced to reduce portfolio leverage due to rating constraints (because their notes are downgrading), it creates selling pressure in the loan market. This was most evident in 2020 when COVID volatility created rating concerns across the loan universe, forcing CLO deleveraging, which forced loan selling, which depressed prices further. This is why CLO rating constraints matter not just for individual transactions but for the macro leverage structure of the market.

One phenomenon I've observed over time is that rating agencies have become increasingly aware of their role in constraining CLO leverage and have become more cautious about assigning ratings that would force CLOs to deleverage. This is good market design—it prevents cascade effects—but it also means that rating agencies are conscious of second-order effects of their ratings, not just the direct credit analysis.

The mathematical relationship between collateral ratings and CLO note ratings is not purely mechanical; there's judgment involved in how much below-investment-grade collateral a AAA CLO note can support. This is where different rating agencies' methodologies create different outcomes. One agency might model that a AAA CLO note can be supported by collateral with an average weighted rating of BB-, while another might require BB. This seemingly small difference in modeling can shift the entire capital structure of a transaction.

What I've learned is that working with rating agencies on CLO structure requires being very precise about the portfolio composition and rating profile. Arranger teams should explicitly model what happens if certain collateral gets downgraded, how that affects CLO note ratings, and what that implies for the CLO manager's ability to maintain its investment-grade notes. This is where being ahead of rating agency thinking matters most.

## Rating Shopping: The Dark Side of the Issuer-Pays Model

Let me be direct about this: rating shopping—the practice of arrangers and issuers shopping for favorable rating assumptions among the three agencies—is a feature of the leveraged finance market that I've participated in, observed, and ultimately come to view as a real if modest drag on market efficiency.

Rating shopping happens when an arranger believes that one or more of the three agencies will assign a more favorable rating than the others based on different methodology or different appetite for certain assumptions. The arranger then structures the deal to maximize the chance of obtaining the favorable rating. In extreme cases, the arranger might attempt to isolate the most favorable rater and present the package in a way that makes them the natural choice.

Here's how this worked in practice in the mid-2010s. Moody's was known for being relatively strict on working capital assumptions. S&P was in the middle. Fitch was known for being more aggressive on sponsor support and willing to model faster operational improvements. If I was arranging a sponsor-backed deal with aggressive working capital assumptions and operational improvement plans, I would naturally want Fitch's involvement. I might present to Fitch first with a detailed business plan document that really emphasized the sponsor's track record on operational improvements. When I went to Moody's, I might de-emphasize working capital improvements and focus on the quality of existing management systems.

This isn't quite lying, but it's certainly emphasizing different dimensions of the credit story to different audiences. And it absolutely happens in the leveraged finance market.

What I've learned is that rating agencies are aware this happens and have developed defenses against it. They share information internally and with each other about apparent inconsistencies in how transactions are being presented. If arranger team members are shopping different leverage assumptions to different agencies, and those agencies compare notes, the arranger loses credibility.

The rating shopping I've observed has generally had modest impact—you might get one notch of difference in outcome between the most aggressive and most conservative rater, but you rarely get two notches of real difference without something being genuinely misleading in the presentation. The agencies have converged sufficiently that shopping for truly dramatic differences is difficult.

But rating shopping has a real cost: it creates incentive for rating agencies to be less rigorous about assumptions. If Fitch knows that they're competing against Moody's for deal flow by being more accommodative on working capital assumptions, they have a subtle incentive to be less critical of those assumptions than they might otherwise be. This isn't the same as the pre-crisis dynamic when agencies competed on being aggressive on leveraged credit ratings. But it's a form of competitive pressure that doesn't serve the market optimally.

The issuer-pays model is fundamental to understanding why rating shopping exists and why it's hard to eliminate. When the issuer pays the rating agency for the rating, the issuer has an incentive to shop for the most favorable rating. There's no obvious alternative—if investors paid for ratings, the agencies might focus excessively on the preferences of buyers and neglect the views of sellers. But the current model creates moral hazard.

I want to note that in my observation, the big three agencies have genuinely tried to address this issue. They've put in place procedures to ensure that similar methodologies are applied across transactions and that competitive pressures don't corrupt analytical independence. But the structural tension remains.

One specific practice I've witnessed multiple times is what I call "package shopping." The arranger prepares different versions of the financial package for different agencies, each emphasizing different facets of the credit story. The most optimistic version goes to the most aggressive rater. The most conservative version goes to the most conservative rater. Neither is dishonest, but the variation is material. I've done this myself and have been on the receiving end of it. It's a rational game that people play within the current market structure.

## Split Ratings: What the Market is Really Saying

Split ratings—situations where the three agencies assign different ratings to the same credit—are one of my favorite windows into understanding how rating agency methodology actually works in practice and what the market genuinely believes about credit risk.

When you see a split rating, what you're observing is a real difference in how agencies weight different risk factors or different philosophies about how conservative to be. A common split pattern I've seen repeatedly is where one agency rates a credit BB and two rate it BB-, or where one rates BB+ and two rate BB. These single-notch splits are genuinely informative. They suggest that there's a legitimate range of reasonable opinion about the credit, and different analysts have landed in different places within that range.

The more interesting splits, to me, are the two-notch or wider splits, where one agency rates a credit B and another rates BB-. These splits are less common and typically signal a fundamental disagreement about risk or methodology. In 2013, I worked on a manufacturing acquisition where one agency was significantly more optimistic than the other two about management's ability to execute operational improvements. That agency rated the credit a notch higher. The optimal outcome for the market is that this difference surfaces—investors understand that there's a real difference of opinion about the credit, and they price accordingly.

Split ratings create some important practical challenges. If a CLO manager has a constraint to hold no more than X percent of below-investment-grade assets, how should they treat a split-rated security? If one agency rates it B and two rate it BB, is it below-investment-grade or not? Different CLO documents have different rules, but the typical approach is to take the most conservative rating. This means that split-rated securities where one agency is below-investment-grade face headwinds in CLO placement.

What I've observed is that arrangers genuinely prefer consensus ratings (all three agencies aligned) or at minimum have one "outlier" rater with the other two in agreement. A situation where ratings are genuinely split 1-1-1 creates distribution uncertainty and typically results in tighter pricing to compensate for the uncertainty.

The question I'm often asked is: what do split ratings say about the underlying credit? In my experience, split ratings are most common in the following situations: (1) credits with genuine operational turnaround stories that some agencies credit more fully than others; (2) credits in industries where different agencies have different views about secular trends; and (3) credits where working capital or cash conversion assumptions are contentious.

One excellent example was a European specialty chemicals company in 2014. The company had a real turnaround story after years of operational underperformance. One agency (Fitch, the most aggressive) believed the turnaround was credible based on management's track record in other settings. The other two agencies were more skeptical. The result: Fitch rated the credit BB-, while the other two rated it B+. This split persisted for two years, during which time the company demonstrated genuine operational improvement. Eventually all three converged to BB-, validating Fitch's original view.

But I've also seen situations where one agency was correct to be skeptical and the other two were too aggressive. In those cases, the skeptical agency's rating typically proves more durable, and the others have to downgrade later. This is where track record matters—certain agencies develop reputations as being early on downgrades in particular industries or particular economic states.

One nuance about splits that I think is worth understanding: sometimes a split rating reflects different notching decisions rather than different CFR assessments. Two agencies might agree on the corporate family rating (BB-) but differ on how many notches of recovery to give a senior secured term loan. One gives a three-notch uplift to achieve BB, the other gives four notches to achieve BB+. Same CFR, different instrument ratings. This signals a difference in how the agencies are modeling recovery, which is useful information for lenders thinking about their loss profile.

## Notching: The Technical Architecture of Rated Securities

If rating methodology were a building, notching would be the structural system. Understanding notching—how rating agencies adjust upward or downward from a corporate family rating based on the position of debt in the capital structure—is essential to sophisticated transaction structuring.

Notching starts from a simple principle: different debt instruments have different risk profiles based on their position in the capital stack. Senior secured debt is safer (because it has collateral) than unsecured debt. Unencumbered debt is safer than encumbered debt competing for collateral. Debt with structural subordination to other debt is riskier than debt at the same level. The notching framework quantifies these differences.

Typical notching frameworks work like this: Start with the CFR (corporate family rating). The most senior, most secured debt (think first lien term loan on unencumbered assets) gets rated upward from the CFR by typically 2-4 notches. Second lien debt might get upward notching of 1-2 notches. Senior unsecured debt typically gets downward notching of 1-3 notches. Subordinated debt typically gets downward notching of 3-6 notches.

The key word is "typically." The actual notching applied to a specific transaction depends on the rating agency's assessment of collateral quality, recovery prospects, and the specific capital structure. In 2016, I worked on a highly leveraged acquisition where the first lien term loan had notching of only +2 (versus a typical +3 or +4) because the rating agency assessed collateral coverage as relatively low due to the amount of second lien debt and senior unsecured debt competing for recovery. This meant the term loan couldn't achieve the rating the sponsor wanted without reducing leverage on the deal.

What's important to understand is that notching is driven by assumptions about loss-given-default and recovery. A senior secured facility gets more notching when the rating agency believes recovery is high. Recovery is typically assessed based on: (1) the value and quality of collateral; (2) the level of leverage in the capital structure; and (3) other senior claims on the collateral pool.

One practice I've engaged in repeatedly is challenging notching assumptions, particularly on recovery ratings. If a rating agency proposes three notches of uplift from the CFR to the first lien term loan, and you think that's conservative, you can argue for four notches based on: better collateral coverage than the agency modeled, higher probability of asset sales in distress being at acceptable valuations, or existing market precedent for wider notching in similar transactions.

Notably, this became a point of contention after the 2008-2009 crisis, when secured creditors experienced recoveries that were often lower than expected. Many secured lenders thought they had bullet-proof collateral positions and discovered in workouts that fire sales and economic deterioration meant recoveries of 60-70 cents on the dollar rather than 90 cents. Rating agencies became much more conservative about notching assumptions post-crisis.

I've noticed that notching is increasingly data-driven. The rating agencies have built databases of historical defaults and recoveries across different industries and different capital structures. When you sit down to discuss notching, they pull actual historical recovery data for similar companies in your industry. This is generally positive—it makes the conversation more objective—but it also creates challenges for companies in industries that had particularly bad recoveries historically.

One specific dimension of notching that I've grappled with repeatedly is how to treat structural subordination. If you have a holding company that borrows money, and that borrowing is structurally subordinated to the operating subsidiaries' debt (meaning subsidiary debt gets paid first), the holding company debt should be notched down relative to subsidiary debt. But how much? If a subsidiary is worth 70 percent of the consolidated enterprise, then substrate debt should be meaningfully subordinated. But rating agencies sometimes apply less notching for structural subordination than you might expect, relying on the assumption that the subsidiary will upstream cash to the holding company. This can create risk for creditors if the subsidiary deteriorates or if its cash generation disappoints.

I had one situation where a holding company took on debt that was supposed to be structurally senior to subsidiary debt, but the rating agency still applied significant downward notching because the subsidiary debt had covenant packages that protected its cash. In a distress scenario, the subsidiary couldn't upstream cash to pay the holding company debt without violating its own covenants. The notching reflected this structural reality, even though the holding company debt was technically senior.

Here's something I've learned through experience: notching that looks conservative in a going-concern scenario can look inadequate in a distress scenario. A first lien secured loan that looks like it should get +3 notching based on 90 percent collateral coverage might recover only 70 percent if the company goes into distress, needs quick asset sales, and faces a deteriorated market environment. The notching was based on orderly recovery; reality delivered fire-sale recoveries.

## Rating Outlooks and Credit Watches: The Signaling Game

Rating outlooks and credit watches are the rating agencies' mechanism for signaling that a credit's rating might change in the near term. Understanding how to interpret these signals and, from an arranger perspective, how to manage them, is essential to sophisticated market navigation.

A rating outlook is typically assigned annually and reflects the rating agency's assessment of the likelihood of a rating change over the next 12-24 months. Positive outlook means the agency sees upside risk (possibility of upgrade). Stable outlook is the baseline—no expected change. Negative outlook signals downside risk (possibility of downgrade). Rating watches are more tactical and are used when the agency wants to signal that a rating change is quite likely in the near term, typically within 90 days.

What I've learned over twenty years is that these signals have very real market consequences. A negative outlook on a leveraged credit often precedes a downgrade by several weeks. During that interim period, the credit trades wider as investors anticipate the downgrade. If you're an arranger thinking about refinancing a credit, a negative outlook makes it much harder to achieve tighter spreads, even if the credit hasn't been downgraded yet.

I've also learned that these signals are sometimes surprising to the issuer and arranger. A credit performing well operationally can still get put on negative watch if the rating agency sees deteriorating market conditions or changing industry dynamics. During the 2015-2016 energy downturn, many energy company credits that had performed relatively well operationally went on negative watch because agencies were concerned about sustained low commodity prices. The company's performance was fine; the external environment had deteriorated.

What happens in practice is that when a credit goes on negative watch, the issuer and arranger typically immediately escalate contact with the rating agency, trying to understand the concerns and provide information that might convince the agency to remove the watch rather than downgrade. In 2017, I worked on a credit where the rating agency put a company on negative watch based partly on concerns about customer concentration. We immediately engaged the company's sales team to provide evidence about customer stability and long-term contracts. The agency removed the watch after reviewing this new information, avoiding a downgrade.

The positive outlook is less discussed but equally important. A credit with a positive outlook might not get downgraded even if it hits financial targets that normally would trigger a downgrade, because the agency is explicitly signaling that it's considering an upgrade. Conversely, a credit on negative watch should absolutely not get downgraded further without removing the watch first, because multiple rating actions in close succession create market chaos.

One strategic practice I've employed is asking the rating agencies, when they place a credit on negative watch, what specific milestones would convince them to remove the watch versus downgrade. The answer typically involves financial performance targets, liquidity milestones, or strategic developments. If the issuer and arranger know exactly what the agency is looking for, they can focus efforts on achieving those milestones.

Rating watches and outlooks also interact with rating triggers in credit documents. Some credit documents distinguish between a rating action (upgrade or downgrade) and a watch or outlook change. Others treat watches as triggering certain covenant adjustments or pricing grids. The practical effect is that a negative watch can create financial consequences even before a downgrade occurs.

One situation that illustrates the practical importance: in 2019, a sponsor-backed industrial company received a negative outlook from Moody's but no downgrade. The negative outlook alone was enough to trigger a pricing grid adjustment in the loan document, increasing the cost of the credit by 25 basis points. The company was performing well, but the higher cost of debt made it harder to achieve target returns. Eventually the company proactively worked with the agency to address their concerns, and the outlook was revised to stable.

## Post-GFC Regulatory Changes: How the System Was Supposed to Improve

The 2008 financial crisis exposed catastrophic failures in the rating agency model and resulted in significant regulatory intervention. Understanding these regulatory changes is essential to understanding how rating agencies operate today and what incentives they face. Let me walk through the key regulatory developments and what they actually achieved.

The most significant regulatory change came through Dodd-Frank in the United States and equivalent regulations in Europe. These regulations made several key changes to how rating agencies operate: (1) rating agencies must register with the SEC and comply with extensive disclosure and transparency requirements; (2) rating agencies must implement disclosure and management of conflicts of interest; (3) rating agencies must stop issuing "unsolicited" ratings, which had created perverse incentive where they would issue aggressive ratings to attract issuers; and (4) the framework for rating agency determination and appeal was enhanced.

What I've observed is that these regulations have genuinely changed rating agency behavior. Disclosure and transparency requirements mean that rating agencies publish their rating methodologies in far more detail than they used to. This is good for transparency but also creates some challenges—when methodology is fully transparent and publicly available, it becomes easier for arrangers to game the system by structuring assets specifically to fit favorable methodology outcomes.

The prohibition on unsolicited ratings eliminated a particularly pernicious market dynamic. Before Dodd-Frank, rating agencies would sometimes issue unsolicited ratings on credits that hadn't hired them, and issue them aggressively, with the hope of being hired to provide official ratings. This created incentive for ratings inflation. Once unsolicited ratings were prohibited, this form of competition disappeared.

The disclosure requirements are genuinely useful. Investors can now see exactly how a rating agency calculated a rating and what assumptions went into it. But I've observed that the massive increase in disclosure sometimes obscures rather than clarifies—some rating documents are now so detailed that finding the actual analytical judgment is like looking for a needle in a haystack.

One regulatory change that I think had good intentions but mixed results is the requirement that credit rating agencies explicitly address the role of structured finance ratings in their business and governance. This was meant to address the obvious conflict of interest: rating agencies were getting a huge portion of revenue from structured finance ratings (including CLOs), and this created obvious incentive to rate aggressively. The regulations required separation of structured finance rating teams from other teams and explicit oversight.

What I've observed is that this regulation actually made rating agencies more conservative on certain structured finance products. They became concerned about reputational damage if they rated something aggressively and it deteriorated. This conservatism is probably appropriate in the abstract, but it also created challenges for CLO managers who wanted to invest in leveraged loans but found that rating constraints on CLO notes prevented them from holding as much below-investment-grade paper as they wanted.

The post-GFC regulatory changes also required rating agencies to document the historical accuracy of their ratings. This might sound neutral, but it had real effects. Agencies discovered that they had been systematically too aggressive on certain credit types and too conservative on others. This created pressure to adjust methodologies to improve historical accuracy. Moody's, for instance, conducted a comprehensive review of their leverage methodology for leveraged credits post-2008 and made changes to be more conservative on leverage multiples.

One specific regulatory change that matters operationally is the requirement that rating agencies have documented appeal processes for issuers who disagree with ratings. Before the regulations, there wasn't really a formal appeals process—if you disagreed with a rating, you basically had to leverage your relationships or complain to the agency's management. Now there are formal, documented procedures. I've been through these appeals processes a couple of times, and they have the characteristic of being thorough but slow. A rating appeal typically takes several weeks to several months.

What I want to emphasize is that post-GFC regulation has genuinely made the system better, but it hasn't solved the fundamental structural problems. The issuer-pays model still creates incentive conflicts. Rating agencies still have limited visibility into what happens after they rate a credit, so they still make systematic errors in evaluating certain risk factors. The structural subordination of equity to debt still creates agency costs. Regulations have addressed symptoms more than root causes.

## Sponsor-Backed Credits: Special Considerations and the Sponsor Support Question

Sponsor-backed transactions present unique rating agency challenges because they involve an explicit assumption that the financial sponsor will support the company and potentially inject additional capital if needed. How rating agencies think about sponsor support is crucial to understanding how sponsor-backed credits are rated.

Rating agencies have developed sophisticated methodologies for assessing sponsor support. The framework considers: (1) the sponsor's track record in supporting prior investments; (2) the sponsor's financial capacity to support the investment; (3) the sponsor's reputational incentive to support the investment; and (4) the nature of the operational expertise the sponsor brings.

Tier-1 sponsors with strong track records and demonstrated willingness to support investments in distress get material credit for sponsor support in ratings. Moody's and S&P have both published frameworks explicitly quantifying how much uplift a sponsor gets in the CFR. In 2015, when I worked on a secondary transaction for a large sponsor platform, the rating agency applied a 1.5x multiple on EBITDA as an assumption about incremental sponsor capital that would be available if needed. This uplift of sponsor support was worth roughly 0.5 notches in the rating.

What's important to recognize is that sponsor support is valuable precisely because it's discretionary. A sponsor isn't legally obligated to inject capital in most leveraged structures. The investment is "closed"—the sponsor owns equity and the creditors own debt, and equity gets paid last. But a rational sponsor will sometimes choose to inject capital if it's strategic to prevent a default that would destroy the investment.

The 2008-2012 period tested these assumptions empirically. Some sponsors did inject capital to save troubled investments—they believed the business had long-term value and capital injection was more valuable than allowing a default that would trigger a workout. Other sponsors walked away from troubled investments because they believed the capital injection wouldn't be justified. Rating agencies learned from this period that sponsor support is conditional, not unconditional, and adjusted their methodologies to be more conservative about sponsor support assumptions.

I've worked on situations where sponsor support was critical to achieving a particular rating. In 2017, a sponsor-backed healthcare platform wanted to access a particular leverage level that would have been constrained without sponsor support assumptions. We worked with the rating agencies to get them to model sponsor capital injection as a mitigation to the leverage risk. This was credible because the sponsor had supported prior investments and had the financial capacity to support this one.

One specific question that rating agencies grapple with is how much sponsor support is in the rating and how it affects covenant structure. If a rating assumes sponsor capital injection in a stress scenario, but the debt covenants are so tight that a stress scenario triggers an event of default before sponsor capital can be injected, the rating assumptions are inconsistent with the documentation. This is a form of rating arbitrage that we've tried to exploit occasionally, but rating agencies have become sophisticated about identifying it.

I've also observed that rating agencies are more skeptical of sponsor support in situations where the sponsor has a shorter holding period or where the investment is clearly a financial engineering transaction with no operational improvement story. A sponsor planning a 7-year hold with an operational improvement plan gets more credit for support than a sponsor doing a traditional LBO with expected exit in 5 years.

What I've learned through experience is that sponsor support is real but limited. A sponsor will inject capital to save a good business going through a temporary downturn. A sponsor won't inject capital into a business that's fundamentally broken or where capital injection would never be recovered. Rating agencies' frameworks for assessing sponsor support have become much more sophisticated post-GFC, and rightly so.

## Rating Agency Fees and the Issuer-Pays Model: The Fundamental Conflict

The issuer-pays model is perhaps the most debated aspect of rating agency economics. Let me be direct: this model creates real conflicts of interest that are difficult to fully resolve, but it's also the model we've been living with because the alternatives have seemed worse.

The way the model works: the issuer pays the rating agency a fee to rate the credit. The size of the fee typically depends on the size of the issuance (larger deals have higher fees), the complexity of the structure, and the number of agencies providing ratings. A typical fee for rating a $500 million leveraged loan might be $50,000 to $75,000 paid by the arranger/issuer to the rating agency. For a complex CLO with multiple agencies, fees might be higher.

These fees are material to rating agencies' economics but typically not material to issuers. A $500 million loan deal might cost $150,000-$200,000 in rating agency fees, which is less than 5 basis points of the deal size—modest in the context of overall transaction fees.

The conflict of interest is obvious: if the rating agency rates too aggressively (giving too high a rating), they remain an attractive rater for future deals. If they rate too conservatively, issuers will shop for a different rater. This creates subtle incentive for ratings inflation.

I've been on both sides of this dynamic. As a banker, I've benefited from the reality that rating agencies compete somewhat on being accommodative on assumptions. I've also recognized that this competition creates suboptimal outcomes for the market as a whole.

What's happened post-GFC is that rating agencies have implemented policies designed to mitigate this conflict. They've implemented regular methodological reviews to ensure they're not systematically biased toward aggressive ratings. They've published their rating action statistics to allow investors to compare rating agency behavior across transactions. They've created separation between the commercial teams (that deal with issuers) and the analytical teams to prevent commercial pressure from corrupting analysis.

But let's be honest: these mitigation measures address the most egregious conflicts but don't eliminate the structural tension. If one rating agency becomes known for being particularly accommodative on working capital assumptions, arrangers will gravitate toward that agency.

The alternative to the issuer-pays model would be an investor-pays model, where investors pay for ratings. But this creates a different set of conflicts: investors have incentive to want lower ratings on credits they're shorting, and higher ratings on credits they own. Rating agencies would face pressure from investors on both sides. It's not clear that this would be superior.

There's also been discussion about a subscription model where investors subscribe to rating agencies for access to all ratings, similar to market data subscriptions. This would eliminate per-issuance fees. The challenge is that this model requires a different business structure and may reduce incentive for rating agencies to conduct original analysis on non-consensus credits. Also, many investors have indicated they wouldn't subscribe at the required price point.

In my observation, the issuer-pays model is here to stay. The practical implications are that arrangers should be aware of potential conflicts, that rating agencies should be aware of their need to maintain credibility in the face of these conflicts, and that investors should maintain appropriate skepticism about ratings while recognizing that ratings are still useful signals of credit quality.

## The Relationship Game: Navigating Arrangers, Issuers, and Rating Agencies

One of the most underappreciated aspects of leveraged finance is the relationship dynamic between arrangers, issuers, and rating agencies. These relationships are real and consequential, and understanding them is essential to sophisticated transaction execution.

Rating agencies don't just evaluate credits—they manage relationships with the arranger and issuer communities. Analysts develop reputations for being reasonable or difficult, accommodating or obstinate, creative or rigid. An arranger learns which analyst at which agency is most likely to accept particular types of assumptions, and naturally gravitates toward that analyst.

But rating agencies also cultivate these relationships strategically. Moody's, S&P, and Fitch each develop relationships with major arranger teams and with repeat issuers. These relationships matter for deal flow—an arranger team that has a history of productive engagements with Moody's will preferentially engage Moody's on transactions.

I've benefited enormously from relationship capital. Over twenty years, I've built relationships with senior analysts and some management at all three rating agencies. These relationships don't mean I can get favorable ratings—the agencies are professional and rigorous—but they do mean I can have productive conversations about assumptions, get candid feedback early in the process, and avoid surprises.

One specific practice that's standard in the industry is what I call the "pre-rating call." Two weeks before formally submitting a rating package, the arranger calls the lead analyst informally to discuss the transaction, share preliminary financial packages, and get a sense of what the analyst is thinking. This call is invaluable because it surfaces preliminary concerns before they become formal objections. If the analyst signals that they're skeptical about management's operating margin assumptions, you can adjust your package before the formal submission.

I learned this practice early in my career and have used it on nearly every significant transaction since. It's not about deceiving the agencies—it's about having a productive dialogue. The analyst gets to understand the transaction better, and the arranger gets early warning about potential rating issues.

What I've also learned is that rating agencies appreciate when arrangers and issuers come to them with realistic rather than optimistic assumptions. A finance team that comes in with aggressive EBITDA projections and conservative margins gets questioned. A finance team that comes in with realistic projections that contemplate downside scenarios gets more credibility.

One thing that's important to recognize is that senior rating agency management are genuinely concerned about their agencies' reputations post-GFC. They don't want to be the agency that rated aggressively on credits that subsequently deteriorated. This creates conservatism bias in rating committees and sometimes creates frustration for arrangers who believe the agency is being unfairly conservative.

I've sat in calls where the analyst was willing to support a particular rating, but senior management overruled them and insisted on a more conservative rating specifically because they wanted to avoid the risk of an aggressive rating proving wrong. This is probably the right institutional bias post-GFC, but it does constrain credit availability and pricing efficiency.

## What Twenty Years Teaches: Reflections on Rating Agency Evolution

After twenty years in this business, I've had the opportunity to observe how rating agencies have evolved, what they've learned, what mistakes they've repeated, and where they're likely to go. Let me share some reflections that have shaped how I think about rating agencies and credit markets.

The first lesson is that rating agencies are, at heart, backward-looking institutions. They rate based on historical data, historical precedent, and historical recovery experience. This makes them conservative about forecasting new market structures or new types of credit risk. In the early days of sponsored leverage in Europe, the agencies were skeptical because sponsor-backed models had limited European precedent. They gradually became more comfortable as they accumulated experience. This backward-lookingness is both a strength (it prevents excessive optimism) and a weakness (it delays adjustment to new realities).

The second lesson is that rating agencies, despite their technical sophistication, make systematic errors in evaluating certain categories of risk. In particular, they struggle with: (1) tail risks that haven't historically materialized; (2) new forms of business model risk in industries undergoing transformation; and (3) multiple rounds of leverage and how portfolio-level risk compounds through a capital stack. These are hard problems, and rating agencies aren't uniquely bad at solving them. But recognizing that they'll get some things wrong is important for investors using ratings.

The third lesson is that rating methodology is driven not by pure analytical logic but by pragmatic considerations about what's implementable and what creates reasonable correlation to credit outcomes. When rating agencies adjust leverage thresholds or working capital assumptions, they're often not making pure analytical adjustments—they're adjusting to achieve better correlation between ratings and observed default rates. This is reasonable, but it means rating methodologies are partially backward-fitted to historical outcomes rather than being purely forward-looking frameworks.

The fourth lesson is that rating agency conservatism increases during periods of uncertainty and decreases during periods of market complacency. This creates a kind of counter-cyclicality where ratings are most aggressive during booms (when they should be most conservative) and most conservative during downturns (when they should be somewhat more confident). This is a feature of any human system that must make judgments under uncertainty, but it's worth recognizing.

The fifth lesson is that rating agencies are fundamentally constrained by the information available to them. They typically get one meeting with management pre-rating and occasional updates post-rating. They don't have visibility into day-to-day operational dynamics or real-time market signals. They rely heavily on what management tells them and on financial metrics. This information asymmetry means they systematically underweight certain types of risk and overweight metrics that show up in financial statements.

The sixth lesson is that the oligopolistic structure of the rating industry—three agencies controlling the vast majority of ratings—creates coordination challenges. The agencies would benefit from more competition, but more competition would likely make ratings less reliable because analysts would be working in smaller groups. The current oligopoly is bad for competition but might be acceptable in terms of information quality.

The seventh lesson is that rating agencies have become conscious of their role in the capital markets and try to take that responsibility seriously. They're not trying to inflate ratings for commercial advantage—they've implemented policies specifically designed to prevent this. But the structural incentives remain, and vigilance is required.

The eighth lesson is that the most valuable rating agency service is not the final letter rating itself—it's the framework for thinking about credit risk that the rating methodology provides. The disciplines that rating agencies impose (thinking about leverage multiples, coverage ratios, covenant packages, capital structure) are useful even if you ultimately disagree with the specific rating. Many sophisticated investors use rating methodologies as a starting point, then adjust them for their own views.

The ninth lesson is that the relationship between rating agencies and the leveraged finance market is fundamentally symbiotic. The agencies need the market to remain large and sophisticated to justify their cost structure. The market needs the agencies to maintain the infrastructure of ratings and methodologies that make structured finance possible. This interdependency means that both sides have incentive to make the relationship work, but it also creates the risk of moral hazard where the agencies become too accommodating and the market becomes over-leveraged.

The tenth lesson is that rating agencies have limited ability to prevent bad credits from being created. Their role is to evaluate and rate credits that others create. If arrangers and sponsors want to create highly leveraged credits with aggressive assumptions and tight covenants, rating agencies can rate them conservatively, but they can't prevent them from being created and sold. This is actually appropriate—the solution to bad credit creation isn't more conservative ratings but more rigorous credit selection by investors.

## Conclusion: Rating Agencies as Market Infrastructure

After twenty years, I've come to think of rating agencies not as arbiters of credit quality but as infrastructure—essential institutions that perform an important function despite their flaws. Like all infrastructure, they have limitations, they sometimes break down, and they require regulation to prevent abuse. But functioning markets require functioning infrastructure, and functioning leveraged finance requires functioning rating agencies.

The practical lessons I've drawn are these: First, understand the rating agency methodologies deeply—not to game them, but to have productive conversations about assumptions. Second, recognize that different rating agencies have different philosophies and different strengths—leverage this difference strategically. Third, maintain healthy skepticism about ratings while recognizing their utility. Fourth, build relationships with rating agencies based on trust and honesty rather than trying to extract every last favorable assumption. Fifth, recognize that rating methodology will continue to evolve in response to market experience, and stay abreast of these changes.

The leveraged finance market has survived and thrived despite genuine flaws in the rating agency system. This suggests that the market contains sufficient alternative information and sufficient skepticism to constrain rating agency errors. But complacency about ratings remains a risk—investors who blindly follow ratings are vulnerable to the same errors that drove the pre-crisis rating inflation.

As I look forward, I expect rating agencies to become somewhat more conservative (they've learned post-GFC that aggressive rating carry real reputational and economic costs) and somewhat more transparent (regulation and market pressure create incentive for greater disclosure). I also expect the issuer-pays model to remain, with periodic refinements. The fundamental structure of the rating market is unlikely to change dramatically because the alternatives are worse.

The rating agencies, like any large institution, are imperfect. But they're imperfect in known, measurable ways. And an imperfect infrastructure is better than no infrastructure at all.
