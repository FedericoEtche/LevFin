CHAPTER: ENVIRONMENTAL, SOCIAL AND GOVERNANCE (ESG) IN LEVERAGED FINANCE

When I first heard the term "ESG" seriously discussed at a leveraged finance conference in 2012, I remember thinking it was a passing fad — something the sustainability departments would get excited about for a quarter before returning to the real business of doing deals. I was wrong. More than a decade later, ESG has fundamentally reshaped how we structure transactions, how we evaluate credit quality, and how we think about the long-term viability of portfolio companies. This chapter reflects my journey from skeptic to someone who now sees ESG integration as essential to good credit analysis and value creation in leveraged finance.

The transformation has not been linear. It has been messy, contradictory, sometimes infuriating, and frequently marked by the tension between genuine sustainability ambitions and financial engineering disguised as environmental responsibility. I have watched sponsors game ESG metrics they publicly committed to, seen investors demand ESG credentials for deals they funded with no real conviction, and witnessed the fascinating evolution from blunt-force exclusion lists to sophisticated positive screening methodologies. This chapter captures what I have learned — the mechanics, the pitfalls, the genuine value creation opportunities, and the persistent fraudulence that still permeates the space.


SUSTAINABILITY-LINKED LOANS: THE MECHANICS AND THE MISTAKES

Let me start with what has become perhaps the most important structural innovation in leveraged finance over the past five years: the sustainability-linked loan. When I first encountered a true SLL structure in 2015, it was novel enough that we spent weeks debating with the arranging bank about what the documentation should actually say. Today, sustainability-linked loans have become market standard, and I have seen hundreds of variations, from genuinely thoughtful structures to transparently cynical exercises in financial theater.

A sustainability-linked loan, at its essence, is a credit facility where the cost of borrowing varies based on the achievement or non-achievement of predetermined Environmental, Social, or Governance performance targets. The most common structure ties the all-in spread to the achievement of specific KPIs — typically ESG metrics that the borrower commits to improve over a defined measurement period. If the company hits its sustainability targets, the spread tightens; if it misses them, the spread widens. This creates a theoretically elegant alignment of interests: the borrower benefits financially from improving its environmental footprint or social performance, while lenders get enhanced monitoring of management commitment to these metrics.

The reality is considerably more complex. I have built dozens of these facilities, and I can tell you with confidence that most SLLs are structured with such generous targets that they are virtually impossible to miss. I remember one transaction — a mid-market industrial services company with genuinely awful environmental practices — where the sustainability targets were so soft that the CFO openly joked to me that the company could meet them without changing a single operating procedure. The target was a 5% reduction in carbon intensity over three years, measured against a baseline that the company had already cherry-picked to be as high as possible. The "achievement" was essentially guaranteed.

This is the core tension in sustainability-linked lending: the structure only works if the targets are genuinely stretching, which makes them politically difficult to agree, while targets that are too soft become marketing exercises. I have increasingly taken the position that the quality of an SLL is directly inversely correlated with how easily the targets can be achieved. I have seen some excellent structures — particularly in the renewable energy and circular economy spaces — where the KPIs genuinely require fundamental operational changes, where missing the targets would materially damage the business case, and where lenders have real insight into whether the company is likely to succeed.

But I have also seen countless examples where the targets are so calibrated to the company's existing trajectory that they provide no meaningful incentive for change. One particularly egregious example involved a logistics company where the "carbon reduction target" was simply the anticipated reduction that would come from natural fleet turnover as older diesel vehicles were replaced with newer models — nothing the management team had to actively do to achieve it, just the passive benefit of technological improvement.

The structure of SLLs typically involves a "margin ratchet" — a grid showing how the all-in spread adjusts based on KPI achievement. A typical structure might look like this: if the company achieves 100% of its target by the measurement date, the spread drops by 5 basis points; if it achieves 75%-99%, there is no adjustment; if it achieves below 75%, the spread increases by 5 basis points. These grids can become byzantine — I have seen some that attempted to measure multiple KPIs simultaneously with different grids for each, creating a patchwork of adjustments that nobody fully understands.

The key issue with margin ratchets is that they generally do not provide sufficient economic incentive to move the needle on what really matters. For most borrowers, a 5-10 basis point adjustment in the all-in spread is essentially noise against the much larger financing costs and operational performance of the business. I have done the economics: for a typical €200 million leveraged buyout with a €150 million term loan, a 5 basis point spread adjustment on the term loan represents roughly €7,500 per year in interest savings. For a mid-market company with EBITDA of €30 million, that is 0.025% of cash flow. Meaningful? Not really.

Where SLLs can genuinely move behavior is where they include step-up provisions with larger spreads — typically 25-50 basis points or more — or where the covenant package is tied to ESG achievement such that missing targets creates technical defaults or materially tightens financial covenants. I have also seen structures where sustainability achievements unlock additional leverage or refinancing capacity, which does create real economic consequences. These tend to work better because the financial incentive is more substantial.

The documentation of SLLs matters enormously, and it is an area where many sponsors underinvest in technical precision. The definition of the KPI must be bulletproof: exactly how it will be measured, by whom, when, what baseline it will be measured against, and what constitutes reasonable verification. I have seen disputes arise — mostly resolved before they became serious problems — because the company and lenders had different understandings of whether a particular activity should be counted toward the metric. One case involved a renewable energy company where the sponsor and lenders disagreed about whether power generated by newly acquired assets should be included in the baseline calculation or treated as incremental. The contract was ambiguous, and the discussion got heated until a mediating senior lender's advisor (which happened to be me) worked out acceptable language.

The LMA and LSTA have published detailed principles for sustainability-linked loans, and I have found these helpful as a starting point, though I generally push back on some of the standardization because context matters. What makes sense as a KPI for a manufacturing company is completely different from what matters for a business services firm or a healthcare provider. The market tendency toward template language sometimes results in metrics that are either irrelevant or so generic that they fail to drive genuine change.

One of the most important lessons I have learned is that the most effective SLLs are those where the targets are aligned with the company's actual strategic objectives — where achieving the ESG target is also achieving something the company wants to achieve for business reasons. I did a healthcare transaction a few years ago where the sustainability-linked element tied the spread to improving patient safety outcomes and staff retention rates. These were genuinely important to the healthcare provider's long-term value creation, and the ESG structure simply aligned external financing incentives with internal strategic priorities. That worked remarkably well because there was no false tension between doing the right thing ESG-wise and doing the right thing financially.


GREEN BONDS AND SOCIAL BONDS IN LEVERAGED FINANCE

The green bond market has evolved dramatically since I first tracked it in the late 2000s, when green bonds were primarily the domain of supranational institutions and enlightened sovereign borrowers. By the early 2020s, green bond issuance by private companies had exploded, and leveraged finance sponsors and their portfolio companies have increasingly accessed this market. Social bonds — which I initially viewed skeptically as a variant of green bonds without the actual environmental benefit — have also emerged as a meaningful source of capital.

For leveraged finance professionals, understanding green bonds and social bonds is essential because they represent an alternative financing channel for portfolio companies, and their availability can meaningfully affect the overall capital structure and returns of a transaction. A portfolio company that can access the public green bond market can refinance portions of its bank debt at attractive rates, improving the equity returns for sponsors and reducing the leverage on the remaining bank facilities.

Green bonds are debt instruments where the proceeds are used exclusively for environmental projects. The typical definition includes renewable energy, pollution prevention, waste management, sustainable agriculture, and climate change adaptation. What qualifies as "green" is governed by taxonomies — most prominently the Climate Bonds Initiative taxonomy and, increasingly for European issuers, the EU Taxonomy, which I will discuss in detail later.

In the leveraged finance context, green bonds work best for companies with clearly identifiable environmental capex that can be financed separately. I have done numerous transactions in renewable energy where the underlying business would issue a green bond to finance specific wind or solar assets, with the proceeds clearly earmarked and verified. These transactions tend to be straightforward because the environmental benefits are tangible and easily demonstrated. A wind farm produces a certain amount of clean energy; that is measurable and verifiable.

The challenge comes when you try to apply green bond financing to more complex leveraged buyouts. I worked on a mid-market industrial conglomerate acquisition where the sponsor wanted to issue a green bond to finance portions of the acquisition, highlighting various environmental efficiency initiatives across the portfolio companies. The complexity was immense: demonstrating that the proceeds were truly being used for green projects, avoiding double-counting, ensuring that the projects actually had additionality (were not going to happen anyway without the green bond), and providing ongoing reporting that was credible to green bond investors.

In the end, we issued a social bond instead — defined as financing that addressed one of the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals. We tied the bond to the company's workforce development and local community engagement programs. This gave us more flexibility in how the proceeds were used while maintaining the sustainability story. Was it greenwashing? I have thought about this question many times. The company genuinely was investing in workforce development and community programs, and the financing enabled this. On the other hand, some of these initiatives were already in the company's capital plan, and the social bond label allowed the sponsor to finance them at slightly better rates than conventional debt. The financial benefit was real; the additional environmental or social benefit beyond what would have happened anyway was harder to argue.

This tension is at the heart of the green and social bond discussion in leveraged finance. These bonds have genuinely expanded the investor base for private company debt — there is real demand from dedicated ESG fund managers and investors with sustainability mandates who will finance companies that would be harder to finance through conventional bank debt. This has real value. But it has also created incentives for what I would call "sustainability-tinged marketing" — the relabeling of existing corporate activities as green or social to access this investor base.

The market standards for green bonds have generally tightened, and the green bond frameworks have become more sophisticated. A robust green bond framework includes detailed definitions of eligible green projects, clear use-of-proceeds language, independent verification (typically from an external auditor or specialized green bond reviewer), and ongoing reporting of environmental impact. The best frameworks I have seen include actual environmental metrics — megawatts of capacity added, tons of CO2 avoided, number of tons of waste diverted from landfills.

Social bonds are inherently messier because what counts as "social" is more subjective. I have seen social bonds issued to finance senior housing, workforce development programs, access to education, healthcare for underserved communities, and affordable housing. Some of these have genuine social benefit; others are more tenuous. One transaction involved a social bond issued by a business services company, where the "social" element was essentially the company's normal hiring of long-term unemployed workers and provision of training. This is great from a social perspective, but the company would have been hiring and training anyway; the social bond simply allowed them to finance these normal operating costs at better rates.

For portfolio companies in leveraged finance, accessing the green or social bond market can be genuinely valuable, particularly if the company has a strong ESG story and genuine environmental or social impact. But it requires discipline: only defining as green or social what actually meets those definitions, not stretching language to claim sustainability benefits that do not exist, and understanding that the reputational cost of being caught greenwashing is far higher than any marginal financing saving from a poorly justified green bond.


THE MECHANICS OF ESG MARGIN RATCHETS AND GAMING RISK

Beyond standalone sustainability-linked loans, ESG margin ratchets have become commonplace in standard leveraged finance facilities. These are mechanisms built into conventional credit agreements where the all-in spread adjusts based on ESG metrics or ratings. I have negotiated hundreds of these, and I can say with confidence that they are significantly more prone to abuse and gaming than many people realize.

The structure is straightforward in theory: the spread grid typically references an ESG rating from a recognized provider — Sustainalytics, MSCI, Bloomberg ESG, or similar — and the all-in spread adjusts based on whether the company's rating falls into a better or worse category. Alternatively, the margin ratchet might reference a specific ESG metric, like carbon intensity or diversity metrics, with the spread adjusting if the metric improves or deteriorates.

In practice, ESG margin ratchets create a set of bizarre incentives. Because the ratchet is based on ESG ratings or metrics, there is a strong incentive for the sponsor to game the rating or metric — to improve the appearance of environmental or social performance without necessarily improving the substance. I have seen sponsors hire consultant firms specifically to optimize their ESG rating (rather than to improve their actual ESG performance), and these consultant firms have become remarkably sophisticated at understanding what ESG rating methodologies reward.

One particularly illuminating example involved a manufacturing company where the sponsor wanted to improve its Sustainalytics rating to trigger a margin improvement. Sustainalytics has specific questions in its methodology about whether the company has published a detailed environmental policy and a climate strategy. The company did not have these formally published. So the sponsor worked with a consultant to draft a detailed climate strategy and environmental policy, had the board approve them, and published them prominently on the company's website. The Sustainalytics rating improved significantly, triggering a 10 basis point margin benefit.

Was this fraudulent? Technically no — the company genuinely did adopt a climate strategy. But was the climate strategy implemented? Did it change anything operationally? Not materially. The strategy included some efficiency targets, but these were soft and timelines were long. The sponsor got the margin benefit by creating the appearance of commitment without necessarily creating the substance of change. And this dynamic is replicated dozens of times in the market.

The core problem is that ESG ratings are based on disclosures and policies, not on actual environmental outcomes. A company that publishes a detailed climate strategy but does not actually achieve the targets will often get a better ESG rating than a company that quietly achieves environmental goals without the formal documentation. This misalignment between ratings and actual performance creates gaming incentives.

I have become increasingly skeptical of ESG ratings as triggers for financial provisions in credit agreements. I prefer to see SLLs where the margin ratchet is based on company-specific, measurable, verifiable metrics — carbon intensity, waste diverted from landfills, percentage of workforce in STEM careers, whatever is most relevant to that business. These require disclosure and third-party verification, but they are much harder to game because they are measuring actual outcomes rather than policy compliance.

That said, I recognize that most sponsors and lenders prefer the simplicity of rating-based ratchets. There is comfort in using an external provider's assessment, and it avoids the need for detailed ongoing monitoring of specific metrics. But this simplicity comes at the cost of real ESG ratchets becoming exercises in presentation rather than performance.

One of the more troubling trends I have noticed is sponsor hiring of specialists specifically to "optimize" their ESG rating. These are consultants who understand the specific methodologies of MSCI, Sustainalytics, and Bloomberg, and they work backward from the rating to identify which disclosures, policies, and positioning will move the rating up. This is not outright fraud — the companies do make the changes they recommend — but it is value-destructive from an ESG perspective because it optimizes for what improves the rating, not what actually improves environmental or social performance.

I was once in a meeting with a sponsor's ESG consultant who literally laid out a roadmap: "If you adopt this climate policy, Sustainalytics will improve your score by 8 points. If you add disclosure about your supply chain monitoring, that's another 5 points. If you improve your board disclosure on diversity, another 3 points. That gets you from the 35th percentile to the 52nd percentile, which moves you from a margin of +100 to +95 basis points relative to the ratchet baseline." This is not uncommon, and while it is technically not fraud, it represents a fundamental misalignment between what we are trying to measure (actual improvement in environmental or social performance) and what we are actually measuring (improvement in rating position based on disclosure and policy compliance).

The solution is not to eliminate margin ratchets — they do create some incentive for ESG improvement — but to design them more carefully. I have increasingly pushed for ratchets that are based on a combination of rating position and specific operational metrics. This is more complex to document and monitor, but it creates stronger alignment between the financial incentive and actual ESG improvement.


ESG KEY PERFORMANCE INDICATORS IN CREDIT AGREEMENTS

The design of ESG KPIs in credit agreements is deceptively difficult. Over the past decade, I have seen thousands of proposed KPIs, and I can tell you with confidence that most of them are poorly designed. The common failures tend to fall into a few categories: targets that are too easy to achieve, metrics that are not actually relevant to the company's ESG impact, KPIs that are difficult to measure consistently, and targets that are not aligned with the company's actual sustainability journey.

Let me work through some examples of good and bad KPI design. A good ESG KPI is specific, measurable, relevant to the business, reasonably challenging to achieve, and based on metrics that are not subject to excessive interpretation. An example of this would be: "Reduce carbon emissions per unit of production by 20% by December 31, 2028, measured on a Scope 1 and Scope 2 basis in accordance with the Greenhouse Gas Protocol, with measurement by the company's internal EHS team and verification by an independent third party." This is specific (20% reduction), measurable (carbon per unit), relevant (carbon is a material issue for most manufacturing companies), reasonably challenging (20% over a five-year period is ambitious but achievable), and based on clear methodology (Greenhouse Gas Protocol).

A bad ESG KPI would be something like: "Improve overall ESG performance." This is vague, unmeasurable, and provides no clear target. I have actually seen this in credit agreements, and it is essentially meaningless.

Another bad example I encountered was: "Reduce total carbon emissions by 15% by December 31, 2027, without any baseline adjustment for acquisitions or divestitures." This company was in an industry with significant M&A activity, and this KPI design meant that if the company acquired businesses with high carbon footprints, it would become mathematically impossible to achieve the target. The language was later amended to allow baseline adjustments for material acquisitions and divestitures, but this illustrates how important precision is.

I have learned that the most effective ESG KPIs are those that are integrated into the company's core business operations and strategy. If an ESG KPI feels like an add-on — something that the company is doing purely to satisfy lenders or investors — it tends to perform poorly. But if the KPI is aligned with strategic objectives, it performs much better. I did a case study years ago on a logistics company where the ESG KPI was to convert 30% of its vehicle fleet to electric or hydrogen fuel over five years. This mattered because fuel costs are a material portion of operating costs, and converting to zero-emission vehicles was both an ESG improvement and a potential cost improvement (depending on energy prices and technological development). The target was genuinely challenging, but the company was motivated to achieve it for business reasons.

In contrast, I have seen social KPIs around workforce diversity that the company had little economic incentive to achieve. If you are a manufacturing company and your ESG KPI is to increase female representation in engineering roles from 12% to 18%, that is a good target and a material objective, but the company will only achieve it if it is genuinely committed. If lenders are just checking a box and the company is not intrinsically motivated, the target will frequently be missed.

One important distinction in KPI design is whether the target should be absolute or intensity-based. Absolute targets (reduce total emissions by 20%) are simpler to understand but can create perverse incentives if the company is growing. If a company doubles its revenue while reducing emissions by 15%, it has actually improved its emissions intensity substantially, but it will miss an absolute emissions reduction target. Intensity-based targets (reduce emissions per dollar of revenue by 20%) better reflect actual efficiency improvements, but they are more complex to measure and document.

I generally prefer intensity-based targets for growing companies, but I have found that the market has standardized on intensity-based targets for carbon and other environmental metrics, and absolute or headcount-based targets for social metrics. This makes sense because social impacts (like workforce development) do not naturally scale with revenue the way environmental impacts do.

Another critical dimension is the distinction between targets and commitments. A true ESG KPI should be tied to specific, measurable targets with defined timelines and verification mechanisms. But I have seen many credit agreements that reference ESG "commitments" that are much softer — the company commits to "endeavor to" improve diversity, or to "work toward" carbon reductions. These commitments are essentially unenforceable and do not function as real KPIs.

The documentation of ESG KPIs should specify not just the target, but the methodology for measurement, the baseline, the measurement frequency, who is responsible for verification, what qualifies as independent verification, and the specific financial consequences (covenant tightening, spread adjustment, etc.) if the target is missed. I have seen many credit agreements where the KPI is defined but the verification methodology is vague — "measured in accordance with industry standards" or "as determined by the company." This creates disputes about whether targets have been achieved.

One of the most important lessons I have learned is that ESG KPIs are only as good as their verification. A KPI that relies on company self-certification is essentially unenforceable because the company has an incentive to report achievement whether or not it actually occurred. The best structures I have seen include independent third-party verification — either by external auditors or by specialized ESG verification firms.

I was involved in a transaction where we required third-party verification of ESG KPIs, and the company pushed back hard on cost (it added roughly €80,000 to annual reporting costs). But we held firm, and in the end the company accepted it. And interestingly, once the verification requirement was in place, the company's approach to measurement and data collection dramatically improved because they knew an external party would be reviewing the data for accuracy and methodology compliance.


GREENWASHING RISK AND THE CREDIBILITY CRISIS

If there is one thing I have become convinced of over my years in leveraged finance, it is this: greenwashing is endemic in the market, it is often intentional, and the reputational and financial consequences for actual greenwashing are still far too modest.

Greenwashing — the practice of making a company appear to have better environmental credentials than it actually does — has evolved from crude rhetoric about being "green" to sophisticated financial engineering around ESG ratchets, sustainability-linked loans, and green bonds. I have witnessed greenwashing at every level of the market, from large-cap sponsors to small mid-market players, and from supposedly sophisticated lenders to supposedly expert investors.

The greenwashing I have seen breaks down into a few categories. The first is deliberate misrepresentation — claiming environmental benefits that do not exist. I saw this in a case where a company claimed that operational efficiency improvements would reduce carbon emissions by 30%, but the claimed improvements had been consistently delayed for years and the probability of achievement was far lower than represented. When lenders dug deeper, it became clear that the projections were essentially fictional.

The second category is definitional gaming — redefining metrics in ways that make them more achievable without addressing the underlying issue. I mentioned earlier the case where a renewable energy company wanted to count new acquisitions in the carbon reduction baseline. That is a form of greenwashing because it makes the target easier to hit by changing the definition rather than by improving performance.

The third category is scope narrowing — improving performance on a narrow metric while ignoring broader impacts. A classic example is a manufacturing company that publicly committed to reducing its carbon footprint but only measured Scope 1 and 2 emissions (direct emissions and electricity), ignoring Scope 3 (supply chain, product use, end-of-life). When you looked at total lifecycle carbon, the company had not improved at all; it had just shifted the impact around. This is technically not false — the company did reduce Scope 1 and 2 — but it is misleading in its emphasis.

The fourth category is what I would call "aspirational greenwashing" — representing future commitments or targets as current achievements. I have seen numerous cases where a company makes a commitment to carbon neutrality by 2050, and this is then presented in ESG marketing materials as if the company is already on a path toward carbon neutrality, when in fact the company has made no material progress toward the goal.

The stakes around greenwashing have risen significantly as investors have become more sophisticated about ESG. I have seen several instances where investors have downgraded or divested from companies specifically because of greenwashing accusations. The reputational cost of being caught greenwashing is now material enough that I think many sponsors are becoming more conservative about their ESG claims.

That said, I think the market has not gone far enough in addressing greenwashing risk. Too many ESG claims are still essentially unverified. Too many margin ratchets reward disclosure over performance. Too many ESG KPIs are achievable without meaningful operational change. The result is that the ESG provisions in many leveraged finance transactions are more theatrical than substantive.

I have tried to push the market toward better practices. In recent deals, I have insisted on independent verification of ESG KPIs, clear methodology definitions, intensity-based rather than easily gamed absolute targets, and realistic timelines that reflect the difficulty of actually changing operations. But this adds cost and complexity, and many sponsors resist it. So the market standard remains somewhat permissive of greenwashing.

One interesting development has been the emergence of ESG audits of leveraged finance deals — where independent auditors review the ESG claims in transaction marketing materials and compare them to reality. These audits have uncovered systematic overstatement of ESG benefits in many transactions. I have found these audits valuable, even when they have been critical of deals I worked on, because they have improved my own approach to ESG structuring and made me more skeptical of optimistic projections.


THE EUROPEAN REGULATORY FRAMEWORK: SFDR, EU TAXONOMY, AND CSRD

No discussion of ESG in European leveraged finance can ignore the regulatory framework that has emerged over the past five years. The European Union has been the most aggressive regulator in imposing ESG requirements on financial institutions and large corporates, and this regulatory framework has materially shaped how we structure transactions.

The Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation (SFDR) was introduced in 2019 and came into full effect in 2021. SFDR requires financial institutions — including leveraged finance funds — to disclose how they consider sustainability risks and adverse sustainability impacts in their investment process. The implications of SFDR are profound: funds are now required to categorize their investments as either "Article 6" (minimal sustainability consideration), "Article 8" (promoting environmental and social characteristics), or "Article 9" (pursuing a sustainable investment objective).

In practice, SFDR has created a classification system for funds that maps onto their ESG orientation. A hardcore ESG fund might be Article 9; a mainstream buy-out fund promoting ESG characteristics might be Article 8; and a traditional leveraged finance fund that is not particularly focused on ESG would be Article 6. This classification materially affects fund raising: Article 8 and 9 funds have access to capital from investors with ESG mandates, while Article 6 funds are increasingly being screened out by institutional investors with ESG policies.

For leveraged finance sponsors, this has created pressure to ensure that their portfolio companies can be classified as sustainable investments under SFDR. This means implementing ESG policies, setting science-based targets, improving disclosure, and ensuring that the business does not conflict with clearly unsustainable activities (like fossil fuel extraction). The compliance burden is real but manageable for most sponsors.

The EU Taxonomy is another critical piece of regulation. The Taxonomy is a classification system that defines what qualifies as an "environmentally sustainable" economic activity. It covers climate mitigation, climate adaptation, water protection, circular economy, pollution prevention, and biodiversity protection. For each environmental objective, the Taxonomy specifies technical screening criteria that determine whether an activity qualifies as sustainable.

The practical relevance of the Taxonomy for leveraged finance is substantial. Under the Taxonomy Regulation, large companies are required to disclose what percentage of their turnover, capital expenditure, and operating expenditure qualifies as Taxonomy-aligned. This has become a key metric that lenders and investors look at when evaluating sustainability performance. I have increasingly found myself diving into Taxonomy compliance in due diligence to understand what percentage of a company's revenues come from genuinely sustainable activities versus activities that are not covered by the Taxonomy or do not meet the technical screening criteria.

Navigating the Taxonomy can be extraordinarily complex. The criteria are technical and sometimes opaque. I was working on a transaction in the waste management space where the question was whether waste recycling services qualified as Taxonomy-aligned circular economy activities. The regulation itself seemed to suggest they should, but the detailed technical screening criteria were written in a way that made it ambiguous. We ended up having to get external legal advice to determine Taxonomy alignment. And even after the advice, we were not entirely confident.

The practical implication of Taxonomy complexity is that it creates opportunities for interpretation and potentially for gaming. Companies can structure their disclosures to emphasize their Taxonomy-aligned revenues and downplay non-aligned revenues. This is not outright fraud — the companies are accurately reporting their activities — but the presentation and emphasis can be misleading.

The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), which came into effect in 2023 and will require public disclosure starting in 2025, represents another major regulatory step. CSRD requires large companies (initially those with more than 500 employees) to provide detailed ESG disclosure aligned with double materiality assessment — disclosing both the impact of the company on the environment and society, and the impact of environmental and social issues on the company's financial performance.

For leveraged finance, CSRD has significant implications. Most leveraged finance portfolio companies are not initially in scope of CSRD (because most are private and below the size thresholds), but as sponsors increasingly take companies public or as they grow beyond the size thresholds, CSRD requirements will apply. And many sponsors are getting ahead of CSRD requirements by implementing similar disclosure practices proactively.

CSRD compliance is demanding. Double materiality assessment requires companies to identify which ESG issues are material to their business (financial materiality) and which issues their business impacts (environmental and social materiality). This is more sophisticated than the simple ESG risk identification that many companies have done in the past. It requires engagement with stakeholders — employees, customers, suppliers, NGOs, regulators — to understand what ESG issues are genuinely important.

One thing I appreciate about CSRD is that it has pushed companies toward thinking about ESG issues more strategically rather than treating them as a compliance box to check. Good CSRD reporting requires understanding which ESG issues actually affect the company's long-term value, and this discipline of thinking about materiality has improved how we structure transactions.

The regulatory framework also includes the Taxonomy Regulation's alignment with the goals of the Paris Agreement and the EU's commitment to carbon neutrality by 2050. This has created a de facto regulatory expectation that large companies will align with climate transition pathways. For leveraged finance, this means that portfolio companies are increasingly expected to have science-based targets aligned with limiting global warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius.

Navigating this regulatory framework requires expertise, and I have become increasingly reliant on external counsel and ESG consultants to help interpret how regulations apply to specific transactions. The regulatory landscape continues to evolve, with new regulations and guidance emerging regularly. But the broad direction is clear: ESG disclosure and performance are becoming mandatory compliance requirements, not optional value-add features.


ESG DUE DILIGENCE IN SPONSOR-BACKED DEALS

When I was younger, ESG due diligence in leveraged finance deals was minimal. A sponsor would conduct a three-week commercial and financial due diligence process, and ESG might get half a day. The ESG checklist typically included basic questions: Does the company have environmental policies? Has it had any material environmental incidents? Is the company exposed to ESG risks?

The sophistication of ESG due diligence has evolved dramatically. In current transactions, ESG due diligence often comprises 10-15% of the total due diligence effort, and the best sponsors have integrated ESG into the overall investment theses rather than treating it as a separate workstream.

A comprehensive ESG due diligence process for a leveraged finance transaction typically covers several dimensions. First is the assessment of material ESG risks and opportunities. What are the environmental risks specific to the company's industry and operations? Are there regulatory risks around emissions, waste, or other environmental issues? Are there supply chain labor or human rights risks? What are the governance risks around board quality, executive compensation, related party transactions? This assessment should go beyond the company's own risk identification to include third-party perspective on what ESG issues are material to the industry.

Second is the assessment of the company's current ESG policies, practices, and performance. This includes reviewing environmental management systems, social policies, governance structures, disclosure practices, and ESG performance metrics. I typically request three years of historical ESG data to understand trends. Are carbon emissions decreasing or increasing? Has diversity improved? What is the company's track record on governance issues?

Third is the assessment of ESG risks in the capital structure and refinancing. How will the company's ESG profile affect its access to debt and equity capital? Are there ESG-linked covenants or margin ratchets in the existing capital structure that the sponsor will need to navigate? What is the sponsor's plan for improving ESG performance postacquisition?

Fourth is stakeholder engagement. I typically recommend that sponsors engage with key stakeholders — employees, customers, suppliers, regulators, NGOs — to understand their views on the company's ESG performance and opportunities for improvement. This sounds like a lot of work, but done efficiently, it can provide genuine insight into ESG risks and opportunities that the company itself has not fully identified.

Fifth is the assessment of greenwashing risk. This is where sponsor skepticism is essential. Just because a company claims an ESG commitment does not mean it is genuine. Has the company actually achieved stated targets in the past? Are the targets achievable? Are there any incentive misalignments? I have become increasingly ruthless about challenging sponsor and management representations about ESG on the grounds that "they said it themselves" without independent verification.

The integration of ESG due diligence into the overall investment thesis is critical. In the best deals I have seen, ESG considerations drive the overall investment rationale. The sponsor identifies that a company has material ESG risks that current management has not addressed, and the sponsor's thesis is that fixing the ESG issues will improve operational efficiency, reduce cost, and drive value creation. This is the opposite of the "ESG as constraint" thinking that views ESG as something you have to do to satisfy lenders and investors.

For example, I worked on a manufacturing business where the sponsor's investment thesis included a detailed plan to redesign the production process to reduce waste, both for environmental reasons and to reduce material costs. The ESG improvement (30% reduction in waste) and the financial improvement (5% reduction in input costs) were aligned. This kind of thematic integration of ESG and financial value creation is where ESG actually matters in leveraged finance.

In contrast, I have seen deals where ESG is treated as a separate workstream that is bolted onto the investment thesis ex post facto. The sponsor announces that the company will improve diversity or reduce carbon emissions because those are good ESG outcomes and will help the company access capital, but there is no clear operational plan for how these improvements will occur. These deals tend to underperform on ESG targets because the commitment is not genuine.


THE EVOLUTION FROM EXCLUSION LISTS TO POSITIVE SCREENING

One of the most interesting evolutions I have witnessed in leveraged finance over the past 15 years is the shift from negative screening (excluding "bad" companies) to positive screening (identifying and supporting "good" companies and transition stories).

In the early 2010s, the dominant approach to ESG in leveraged finance was a straightforward exclusion list. Certain industries — coal mining, nuclear power, weapons manufacturing — were off-limits. Certain practices — child labor, egregious corruption — would disqualify a company from financing. This was blunt-force exclusion based on industry and conduct.

The challenge with this approach is that it is relatively easy to comply with (just avoid the obviously bad stuff) but it does not create any incentive for improvement in companies that are not excluded. A manufacturing company that generates significant emissions but does not involve coal or weapons is not excluded, so there is no funding pressure to improve environmental performance.

Over time, the investor and lender community has moved toward more sophisticated positive screening. Rather than just asking "Is this company obviously bad?", sophisticated investors ask "Is this company better on ESG than its peers? Is management committed to improving ESG performance? Does the company have a credible transition plan?"

This shift has created more sophisticated ESG due diligence because you cannot just check boxes — you have to actually understand the company's ESG trajectory. I did a case study of this evolution with a mid-market industrial company where an older ESG investor would have focused on compliance with exclusion lists and high-level check-the-boxes ESG policies. But the newer approach we took was to assess whether the company was a "transition leader" in its peer group — was it improving faster than peers? Did it have a credible climate transition plan? Was management incentivized to deliver on ESG targets?

This shift has also created the "transition finance" concept — the idea that lenders should be willing to finance companies in their transition from "brown" (high environmental impact) to "green" (low environmental impact). I will discuss transition finance in more depth later, but the key insight is that if lenders only finance companies that are already green, they miss opportunities to finance meaningful environmental improvements in companies that are currently not green.

The move from exclusion to positive screening has also made ESG assessment much more nuanced. It is not just about whether a company meets a certain threshold of ESG performance; it is about the trajectory and the credibility of management's commitment. I have seen companies with relatively weak absolute ESG performance get favorable ESG treatment from lenders because management was visibly committed to improvement and had a credible plan. Conversely, I have seen companies with good absolute ESG metrics get challenged by lenders because management appeared not to be committed to further improvement or the company's ESG leadership was primarily attributable to industry characteristics rather than management action.


ESG RATINGS PROVIDERS AND THEIR RELEVANCE TO LEVERAGED FINANCE

The ESG ratings industry has exploded over the past decade, and now there are hundreds of ESG ratings providers ranging from mega-cap firms like MSCI and Sustainalytics to boutique specialists. For leveraged finance professionals, understanding ESG ratings is essential because these ratings increasingly affect the cost of capital and the availability of certain financing channels.

The major ESG ratings providers in the leveraged finance context are: Sustainalytics (owned by Morningstar), which focuses on ESG risk and materiality; MSCI ESG Ratings, which provides environmental, social, and governance ratings on a scale of 0-10; Bloomberg ESG Disclosure Score, which rates companies on the quality of their ESG disclosure; and RepRisk, which provides reputational risk assessment based on news monitoring.

Here is what I have learned about ESG ratings: they are important not because they are necessarily accurate or meaningful, but because they are increasingly used to make financial decisions. The fact that a company's Sustainalytics score improved matters not because the score is necessarily predictive of future environmental performance, but because it triggers SLL margin improvements and affects investor demand for bonds and debt.

This creates a perverse dynamic where the accuracy of the rating becomes less important than the financial consequences of the rating. This is particularly problematic because ESG ratings often have questionable methodological foundations and the methodologies are frequently opaque.

I have spent considerable time trying to understand the methodologies of major ESG ratings providers, and I have found them to be less rigorous than their marketing suggests. For example, MSCI's ESG ratings are based on a combination of disclosed data and proprietary analysis, but the exact weighting of different factors is not fully transparent. This means that companies can improve their ratings by gaming what they disclose, which is exactly what I described earlier.

Sustainalytics' ESG Risk Rating is particularly influential in leveraged finance because it is increasingly used as the basis for margin ratchets. The rating is based on company assessment of material ESG risks and the quality of management of these risks. In theory, this is a good approach because it focuses on what matters. In practice, the rating is heavily weighted toward disclosure and policy compliance, which creates the gaming incentives I described.

RepRisk is a different type of rating — it is based on monitoring news sources for ESG incidents and reputational risks. This makes it responsive to real-world events but also prone to hyperbole in media coverage. I have seen companies downgraded significantly because of isolated incidents that received media attention, even when the incidents were not particularly material to ESG risk.

For leveraged finance professionals, my recommendation is to use ESG ratings as an input into analysis but not as the sole basis for decision-making. Cross-reference multiple raters, understand the methodologies, and when ratings are used as triggers for financial provisions (like margin ratchets), build in some flexibility to account for rating changes that reflect methodology changes rather than real ESG changes.

One issue I have grappled with is whether to use ESG ratings at all in credit agreements. There is a strong argument that using ratings creates perverse incentives (the company optimizes for rating improvement rather than actual ESG improvement) and adds a layer of intermediation (relying on rating agencies' views rather than direct measurement). But in practice, most lenders find that using ratings is simpler than designing custom ESG metrics, and sponsors are often comfortable with rating-based triggers because they feel objective.

I have increasingly tried to use a hybrid approach: use ESG ratings as a general indicator of whether the company is on an improving or deteriorating trajectory, but tie material financial consequences (margin ratchets, covenant adjustments) to specific company-reported metrics that are independently verified. This gets us some of the simplicity of ratings without sacrificing as much precision.


THE FUNDAMENTAL TENSION BETWEEN LEVERAGE AND SUSTAINABILITY

This is perhaps the most uncomfortable topic in my field, and it is one that I think many leveraged finance professionals do not grapple with directly. Here it is: there is a fundamental tension between taking on substantial financial leverage and pursuing genuine ESG improvement. And in many deals, ESG commitments are made in a capital structure context where the financial constraints make those commitments extremely difficult or impossible to achieve.

Let me be concrete. A company that has been acquired in a leveraged buyout typically carries debt of 4-6x EBITDA. The cash flow servicing this debt leaves limited resources for capital investment in ESG improvements. An ESG transition — particularly an environmental transition — often requires significant upfront capital investment. Converting a manufacturing facility to reduce emissions might require €5 million in plant investment. Installing renewable energy capacity might require €3 million upfront. Upgrading supply chain monitoring for labor practices might require systems investment and audit costs.

In a highly levered context, these investments are difficult because cash flow is committed to debt service. The company might have only 10-15% of EBITDA available for discretionary capex and expansion, and this money is typically allocated to growth initiatives that drive EBITDA growth, not to ESG improvements. The mathematical reality is that highly levered companies struggle to finance ESG improvements from cash flow.

I have watched sponsors make ESG commitments in the context of highly levered deals and then struggle to achieve them because the leverage constrains investment capacity. I have seen SLLs fail their ESG targets not because management was not committed, but because the company lacked the capital to make the investments necessary to achieve the targets.

This tension has led me to increasingly ask: Is it possible to do authentic ESG improvement in a 5x leverage context? Or does authentic ESG improvement require a lower leverage multiple?

The answer is nuanced. Some ESG improvements can be achieved through operational changes that do not require major capital investment — changing procurement policies to source from suppliers with better labor practices, for example, or implementing more rigorous environmental management systems. These operational improvements can be largely free or nearly free and can still drive meaningful ESG impact.

But the harder environmental transitions — moving to renewable energy, redesigning production processes for circular economy principles, transitioning vehicle fleets to zero emissions — typically require significant capital that highly levered companies struggle to fund.

One approach to addressing this tension is to structure lower leverage multiples for companies with ESG-intensive transition plans. If a company is committing to a major environmental transition, it may make sense to target a 3.5-4x leverage multiple rather than 5-6x, leaving more cash flow available for ESG capex. But this is generally not what happens in practice because sponsors are incentivized to maximize leverage to maximize returns.

Another approach is to secure dedicated capital for ESG improvements. Some funds have created ESG-dedicated tranches of capital alongside traditional debt facilities — essentially using a blended capital structure where some portion is explicitly allocated to ESG investments. This works well when structured correctly but is still relatively uncommon in mid-market leveraged finance.

I have increasingly found that the companies that succeed most in ESG improvement post-acquisition are those where ESG investment has clear financial returns. If reducing energy consumption also reduces energy costs, if improving supply chain practices also reduces supply chain risk, if improving working conditions reduces turnover and hiring costs, then ESG investments can compete for capital allocation against growth capex. But if ESG investments are purely cost-adding from a financial perspective, they will struggle in a high-leverage context.

This has led me to focus more on identifying ESG improvements where the financial and environmental benefits are aligned. In a manufacturing business, I look for opportunities to reduce waste because waste reduction typically reduces material costs and improves margins. In a logistics business, I look for fleet optimization opportunities that improve both fuel efficiency and logistics performance. These kinds of "no regrets" ESG moves are where progress tends to happen in levered contexts.

But I will say plainly: there is a genuine constraint on authentic ESG improvement created by financial leverage. Companies at 5-6x leverage are not going to be able to fund major environmental transitions. And acknowledging this constraint is important to being honest about what is and is not achievable in leveraged finance.


TRANSITION FINANCING: BROWN-TO-GREEN STORIES

One of the more interesting evolutions in my field has been the development of "transition financing" — the idea that financial institutions should be willing to finance companies in their transition from high environmental impact ("brown") to lower environmental impact ("green").

Transition financing is the opposite of the exclusion approach I described earlier. Rather than saying "we will only finance already-green companies," the transition finance approach says "we will finance companies that are credibly committed to transitioning toward lower environmental impact, even if they are currently not green."

The theoretical case for transition financing is strong. If only lenders finance companies that are already green, they create a perverse incentive structure: it is easier to access capital if you are already green than if you are trying to become green. This creates a scenario where high-impact companies struggle to finance their transition, while low-impact companies access abundant capital. From a sustainability perspective, this is backwards — we want to incentivize the high-impact companies to transition.

In practice, transition financing is much harder than it sounds. The key question is: How do you differentiate between genuine transition stories and "greenwashing lite" — companies that claim to be transitioning but are not really committed?

I have worked on several transition finance cases, and the ones that have worked best share a few characteristics. First, they have a clear, quantified transition plan with specific milestones, timelines, and investment requirements. Second, management has personal skin in the game — the CEO and board are incentivized to achieve the transition targets. Third, there are technical experts on the board and management team who actually understand how to execute the transition. Fourth, the transition plan has been stress-tested against alternative scenarios and timelines.

In contrast, the transition finance deals that have struggled share these characteristics: a vague transition narrative without specific milestones, management that seems enthusiastic about the concept of transition but has not actually worked through the implementation details, and no clear plan for how the company will fund the transition.

I did a renewable energy transition case for a utility company that serves as a good example of the more successful approach. The company had committed to transitioning from 70% coal-fired generation to 50% renewable energy by 2030. The plan included specific investments in solar and wind capacity, timeline for decommissioning coal plants, and a detailed financing plan. The CFO could walk through the cash flow implications year by year. The board had appointed a Chief Sustainability Officer with deep renewable energy experience. The CEO's compensation was tied to the transition plan milestones.

This was a credible transition story, and lenders were willing to finance it at reasonable rates because the transition plan was real and achievable. The company was committed.

In contrast, I have seen energy companies make vague commitments to "reducing their carbon footprint" with no specific plan for how they would do it. These are not genuine transition stories; they are marketing. And I have learned to be skeptical of transition narratives that are not backed by specific capital plans and clear accountability.

One important development in transition financing has been the emergence of transition finance frameworks. The LMA and other standard-setters have published principles for transition-linked financing that provide guidance on what makes a credible transition plan. These frameworks require companies to set science-based targets, develop transition strategies that are credible relative to the company's starting point, and commit to transparent reporting on progress.

The challenge with transition financing in leveraged finance is that it conflicts with the high-leverage model. Transition requires sustained investment and patience. But LBO sponsors are typically on a 4-7 year exit timeline, and they are focused on returning cash to investors, not on long-term environmental transitions. This timing mismatch makes it difficult to do authentic transition financing in sponsor-backed deals.

I have found that transition financing works better in regulated utility contexts, where companies have longer planning horizons and greater stability of cash flows, than it does in industrial sponsor-backed contexts, where the exit timeline and cash return priorities create pressure that conflicts with long-term transition investment.

That said, I have seen some sponsors who are genuinely committed to transition as a value creation story, particularly sponsors who have explicitly positioned themselves as ESG-focused or impact-oriented. For these sponsors, transition financing can be a source of competitive advantage — they can finance companies that other sponsors cannot, at lower cost of capital, by credibly executing on transition plans.


ESG IN CLOs AND ITS IMPACT ON LOAN DEMAND

The integration of ESG considerations into collateralized loan obligations (CLOs) has been one of the most significant developments in leveraged finance credit markets over the past five years. CLO managers increasingly incorporate ESG criteria into loan selection, which has downstream effects on the types of companies that get financed and the pricing they can access.

A CLO is a securitized portfolio of leveraged loans. The CLO manager selects a portfolio of loans, securitizes them, and sells tranches to investors. The return to investors depends on the credit performance of the underlying loans. If loans default, investors in the lower-rated tranches of the CLO absorb losses.

Historically, CLO managers have been focused purely on credit metrics — leverage ratios, interest coverage, cash flow stability — in selecting loans. The assumption has been that if the credit metrics are strong, the loan will perform, regardless of the company's ESG profile.

But increasingly, CLO managers are incorporating ESG risk assessment into their loan selection, based on the view that ESG risks can materially affect credit performance. This is a more sophisticated view than the old binary "good credit" versus "bad credit" framing.

The typical ESG integration in CLOs involves screening for "ESG risks" that could affect credit performance. For example, a CLO manager might avoid companies with material labor risk (high labor turnover, labor disputes, poor working conditions) because these risks can affect operational performance and cash flow. Similarly, a CLO manager might avoid companies with unmanaged environmental risks because these risks could result in regulatory fines or remediation costs.

This ESG integration has created meaningful downstream effects on loan pricing and demand. Companies that have poor ESG profiles find that demand for their loans is lower, and they have to pay higher spreads to attract CLO investors. Conversely, companies with good ESG profiles or credible transition plans find that demand is stronger and spreads are lower.

I have worked on multiple financings where the ESG profile of the company materially affected loan demand and pricing. In one case, a chemical company with a history of environmental compliance issues found that large CLO managers would not include its loans in their portfolios, which reduced demand and increased spreads by 50-75 basis points relative to peers with cleaner environmental records. The company had to undertake a significant ESG remediation program (including environmental compliance audits, upgrading environmental management systems, and committing to emissions reductions) before large CLO managers would include it.

The impact of CLO ESG integration on the overall leveraged finance market has been substantial. Because CLOs purchase roughly 60-70% of the leveraged loans produced in the market, CLO managers' ESG preferences effectively shape which companies can be financed and at what cost. This creates a powerful incentive for sponsors and companies to improve their ESG profile — not because of intrinsic commitment to sustainability, but because better ESG profile means lower cost of capital.

This is not necessarily a bad thing. Even if the incentive is financial rather than moral, if it results in companies improving their ESG performance and reducing their environmental and social impact, the outcome is positive. But it does mean that ESG considerations in CLOs are primarily about ESG risk management (avoiding companies with material ESG risks) rather than about positive ESG impact creation.

One interesting development is that some CLO managers have begun to use positive ESG screens rather than just negative screens. Rather than just avoiding companies with bad ESG profiles, some managers are explicitly seeking out companies with strong ESG characteristics or credible transition plans, based on the view that these companies will have lower credit volatility and better long-term performance.

I have also seen the emergence of "ESG-focused CLOs" — CLOs that explicitly target loans with strong ESG characteristics or transition potential. These CLOs can market themselves to ESG-focused investors and potentially achieve better pricing. But I have noted that ESG-focused CLOs sometimes struggle with portfolio diversification because there are limited companies that meet both credit metrics and ESG criteria, reducing the available investment universe.

The integration of ESG into CLOs has also created some perverse incentives. CLO managers have an incentive to identify companies that can improve their ESG profile cheaply, because the ESG improvement can drive loan repricing upward, creating gains for the CLO investor. This has created a class of "ESG improvement arbitrage" opportunities where sponsors identify companies with fixable ESG issues, fix them, and capture the repricing benefit. This is legitimate value creation, but it is entirely driven by market pricing mechanics rather than by actual ESG impact.


SUSTAINABILITY-LINKED LOAN PRINCIPLES AND MARKET STANDARDS

The market has developed fairly detailed standards for sustainability-linked loans, primarily through the efforts of the Loan Market Association (LMA) and the Loan Syndications and Trading Association (LSTA), which together represent the bulk of the syndicated loan market. These standards have evolved over time, and understanding them is important for ensuring that SLLs are structured in a way that the market views as credible.

The LMA Sustainability-Linked Loan Principles (SLLP) were first published in 2018 and have been updated several times. The SLLP provide guidance on the core components of a credible SLL: selection of KPIs, measurement methodology, leverage of financial incentives, and verification and reporting.

The KPI selection guidance emphasizes that KPIs should be material to the company's business and ESG profile, relevant to the sustainability strategy, achievable with operational changes, and aligned with the company's trajectory. The guidance discourages KPIs that are too easy to achieve (which would lack credibility) and KPIs that are so difficult that they are unrealistic.

The measurement methodology guidance emphasizes that KPIs should have clear definitions, verifiable baselines, established measurement methodologies, and third-party verification. The guidance recommends that companies reference established frameworks (like the Greenhouse Gas Protocol for carbon) rather than developing proprietary methodologies.

The leverage of financial incentives guidance emphasizes that the financial incentives should be "material enough to incentivize achievement." The exact definition of "material enough" is subjective, but the market has generally settled on 5-50 basis points as a reasonable range for margin adjustments. The guidance discourages SLLs where the spread adjustment is trivial (like 1-2 basis points) because such adjustments are unlikely to meaningfully incentivize behavior change.

The verification and reporting guidance emphasizes that performance against KPIs should be reported in a transparent and verifiable way, typically through independent third-party assurance. The guidance recommends that companies disclose performance at least annually, with detailed methodological notes so that investors can assess the quality of the reporting.

One aspect of the SLLP that I have found important is the emphasis on "no artificial manipulation." The guidance explicitly cautions against companies changing how they measure KPIs or redefining KPIs to make them easier to achieve. This guidance has been useful when I have had to push back on sponsors who were attempting to redefine KPIs to make them easier to hit.

The market has also developed informal standards around what kinds of KPIs are credible. Carbon emissions (either absolute or intensity-based) are widely accepted. Diversity metrics (percentage of women in management, representation of minorities) are accepted but with some skepticism because these metrics are sometimes gamed through hiring practices rather than through real inclusion initiatives. Waste reduction metrics are accepted. Health and safety metrics are accepted. Supply chain labor practices metrics are more challenging because they are harder to measure and verify.

I have also noticed that the market has developed informal standards around what margin adjustments are appropriate. For carbon reduction targets, a 10 basis point adjustment for achieving an ambitious (e.g., 30% over five years) target is typical. For diversity targets, adjustments tend to be smaller (5-10 basis points) reflecting the smaller financial materiality to most businesses. For health and safety, adjustments vary widely depending on the industry.

The LMA principles have been helpful in standardizing market practice and in creating a common language for discussing SLLs. However, I have found that the principles still leave room for significant variation in how SLLs are structured, and some sponsors still attempt to stretch the principles to create SLLs that are less credible than the guidance would suggest.

One area where I think the market standards could be improved is around documentation of the rationale for KPI selection. Many SLLs include KPIs without a clear explanation of why these particular KPIs were selected and why the specific targets are appropriate for the company. Better documentation of KPI selection rationale would make it harder for sponsors to make arbitrary or gamed selections.

Another area for improvement is around independence of ESG assurance. Many SLLs are verified by the company's existing auditors, which is efficient but creates potential conflicts of interest. I have increasingly pushed for independent third-party ESG specialists to verify SLL performance, rather than relying on the existing audit firm.


CARBON MARKETS AND RELEVANCE TO LEVERAGED CREDITS

Carbon markets have become increasingly relevant to leveraged finance, both as a source of value from ESG improvement and as a financial risk for companies exposed to carbon pricing. There are two distinct carbon market regimes: mandatory carbon markets (like the EU Emissions Trading System) and voluntary carbon markets.

The EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) is the world's largest carbon market. The ETS requires companies in covered sectors (power generation, manufacturing, aviation) to hold allowances equal to their carbon emissions. Companies that exceed their allowances must purchase additional allowances from the market, creating a direct financial cost to exceeding their carbon budget. The ETS price has ranged from €5-€95 per ton of CO2 over its history, with current prices in the €75-€95 range.

From a leveraged finance perspective, the ETS creates two important dynamics. First, it creates a cost to carbon that companies must manage. For capital-intensive companies in covered sectors, the ETS can represent a material operating cost. I have worked on several deals where the ETS costs were projected to increase materially over the financing term (reflecting the EU's climate ambitions), and this had material effects on cash flow projections and debt capacity. A manufacturing company with 100,000 tons of annual emissions facing a €80 ETS price faces €8 million in annual ETS costs, and if ETS prices are projected to increase to €150 per ton by 2030, that represents nearly €15 million in future costs.

Second, the ETS creates incentives for companies to reduce emissions because emissions reductions directly reduce ETS costs. This means that environmental investments that reduce emissions have a financial return through reduced ETS costs, which makes the business case for environmental capex stronger.

From an SLL perspective, ETS exposure is relevant because companies can achieve KPI targets (particularly carbon reduction targets) by reducing emissions, which also reduces ETS costs. The financial incentive from reduced ETS costs is often much larger than the financial incentive from margin ratchets. I have seen companies achieve ambitious carbon targets not primarily because of the SLL margin incentive (which might be 10 basis points), but because reducing emissions reduced ETS costs by millions annually.

Voluntary carbon markets are less directly relevant to leveraged finance, but they can provide an additional financial incentive for emission reductions. Companies that reduce emissions below their baseline can sell carbon credits, creating a revenue stream. This is relatively early in development and not yet a major factor in leveraged finance, but as voluntary carbon prices increase and become more standardized, this could become more relevant.

One important consideration is the risk of carbon policy change. Many of my projections assume a certain carbon price or regulatory regime. But carbon policy is inherently political and subject to change. A company that has invested heavily in emissions reductions assuming continued ETS pricing could face stranded assets if carbon policy is weakened. Conversely, companies that have assumed baseline carbon policies could face much worse economics if carbon policy is strengthened.

I have become more sophisticated in my modeling of carbon policy risk, trying to build in scenario analysis that accounts for policy uncertainty. But the reality is that predicting carbon policy over a 10-year period is difficult, and this creates uncertainty in valuations and credit projections.

Carbon markets are also relevant to potential future ESG KPIs and SLLs. I have seen proposals for sustainability-linked loans that tie margin adjustments to the company's carbon price ("shadow carbon price") — a theoretical internal price that the company assigns to carbon emissions that exceeds the actual regulatory carbon price. This creates an incentive for companies to behave as though carbon is more expensive than it actually is, driving more aggressive emissions reductions. These shadow carbon pricing approaches are conceptually interesting but operationally complex.


THE INVESTOR PERSPECTIVE ON ESG IN LEVERAGED FINANCE

One of the most important developments in leveraged finance has been the shift in investor demand for ESG-integrated investments. The investor base for leveraged finance has evolved significantly, and understanding investor expectations for ESG is critical to securing financing.

Broadly speaking, the investor base for leveraged finance loans and CLO securities divides into several segments based on their ESG orientation. At one end are dedicated ESG investors — mostly institutional investors like pension funds and insurance companies with explicit ESG mandates. These investors are willing to accept lower returns in exchange for investments that meet their ESG criteria. At the other end are opportunistic investors who focus purely on financial returns and are largely indifferent to ESG considerations.

The rise of dedicated ESG investors has been one of the most important trends in capital markets over the past decade. Asset managers managing trillions of dollars of capital now have ESG mandates, and many of these managers have largely exited traditional leveraged finance or are only investing in deals that meet their ESG criteria. This has created real supply and demand dynamics: there is abundant capital for "ESG-friendly" deals and less capital for deals that do not meet ESG criteria.

From a sponsor perspective, this has created strong incentives to improve ESG profiles of portfolio companies, not because of altruism, but because better ESG profile means better access to capital at better pricing. A company that can be financed by dedicated ESG investors can often access capital 50-100 basis points cheaper than a company that can only be financed by opportunistic investors.

I have seen a few sponsors explicitly position themselves as ESG leaders in their market, using this positioning as a competitive advantage in fundraising and deal sourcing. Sponsors that have built credible ESG platforms have found that they can access capital more cheaply and attract better-quality assets because sellers perceive that the sponsor will improve ESG performance.

One interesting trend I have observed is that some investors are willing to accept lower financial returns in exchange for strong ESG characteristics. This is a material shift from the historical assumption that better ESG characteristics come at the cost of financial returns. Some dedicated ESG investors now assume that ESG improvements will drive operational improvements and value creation, and they are willing to pay for this optionality.

But there is also significant investor skepticism around ESG in leveraged finance. Some investors view ESG commitments in sponsor-backed deals as mere marketing, and they have found that many portfolio companies fail to achieve ESG targets. This skepticism has created higher verification standards — more investors now require independent verification of ESG claims and ESG performance tracking.

I have also observed that investor ESG expectations vary significantly by geography and investor type. European investors have much stronger ESG expectations than US investors, reflecting European regulatory requirements and cultural differences. Pension funds have stronger ESG expectations than hedge funds. This geographic and investor-type variation creates complexity in global transactions where you are trying to satisfy diverse investor bases with different ESG expectations.


ESG REPORTING REQUIREMENTS FOR PORTFOLIO COMPANIES

The regulatory environment around ESG reporting for portfolio companies has tightened substantially, and this creates both compliance requirements and opportunities for sponsors to differentiate through superior ESG reporting.

As I mentioned earlier, the main regulatory drivers are the Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation (SFDR), the EU Taxonomy, and the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD). These regulations create mandatory disclosure requirements for companies above certain size thresholds, and they are tightening over time as more companies come into scope.

For sponsors, these regulatory requirements create a compliance burden, but they also create an opportunity to differentiate. Sponsors that build robust ESG reporting and monitoring capabilities can offer portfolio companies superior ESG communications, which can benefit fundraising and brand reputation.

The quality of ESG reporting has improved substantially over the past five years. I have gone from seeing ESG reports that were essentially marketing materials with few hard facts to seeing reports with detailed performance metrics, third-party verification, and serious engagement with material issues. But the quality is highly variable, and some companies still produce ESG reports that are more theater than substance.

One important aspect of ESG reporting is the concept of "double materiality." CSRD requires companies to assess both the impact of the company on environmental and social issues (environmental materiality, sometimes called "outside-in") and the impact of environmental and social issues on the company's financial performance (financial materiality, sometimes called "inside-out"). This distinction is important because it focuses companies on the ESG issues that actually matter to their business and their impact.

I have found that the best ESG reporting comes from companies that have genuinely engaged with the double materiality concept and identified the ESG issues that are most material to their business. The worst reporting comes from companies that have checked the ESG reporting box by covering every conceivable ESG topic regardless of materiality.

The cost of compliant ESG reporting has also increased. Building the systems to track ESG metrics consistently, engaging third parties for verification, and producing compliant reports requires investment. For mid-market companies, I have seen ESG reporting costs of €50,000-€150,000 annually, depending on complexity. This is a real cost that sponsors need to budget for in their postclosing value creation plans.

One development I have found positive is the emergence of ESG data management platforms that help companies track and report ESG metrics. These platforms reduce the manual work required to gather ESG data and produce reports, and they improve data quality and consistency.


WHAT TWENTY YEARS TEACHES: REFLECTION AND PERSPECTIVE

As I reflect on twenty years in leveraged finance and my evolution in how I think about ESG, several deeper lessons stand out.

First, ESG is not going away. I was a skeptic in 2012, thinking ESG was a fad. I was wrong. ESG has become embedded in how capital markets work, how companies are valued, and how financial returns are generated. The integration of ESG into debt facilities, into CLO selection, into investor mandates is real and durable. Any leveraged finance professional who treats ESG as optional is not paying attention to market realities.

Second, ESG in leveraged finance is fundamentally about managing risk and creating value, not about being "green" or "virtuous." The best ESG integration I have seen is where ESG improvements drive operational improvements and financial value creation. The worst ESG integration is when ESG is treated as a separate box to check that has no connection to the core business. The sustainable leveraged finance deals I have done are the ones where improving ESG was aligned with improving the business financially.

Third, greenwashing is endemic and will likely persist. The financial incentives to present better ESG credentials are substantial, and many actors — sponsors, companies, even lenders — have incentives to stretch the truth about ESG performance. The solution is not to eliminate ESG from leveraged finance, but to build more rigorous verification and monitoring to catch the worst excesses and make greenwashing more costly.

Fourth, the constraint of financial leverage is real and should not be ignored. Companies at 5-6x leverage genuinely struggle to fund major ESG transitions. Acknowledging this constraint and being honest about what is achievable within a given capital structure is important to credibility.

Fifth, ESG is not a replacement for fundamental credit analysis. Good credit analysis requires understanding the company's competitive position, management quality, industry dynamics, and financial performance. ESG is an input into credit analysis, not a substitute for it. I have seen some lenders become so focused on ESG that they have underestimated credit risks in other dimensions. ESG matters, but it is part of a holistic credit assessment.

Sixth, investor expectations around ESG are material and evolving. The investor base for leveraged finance is increasingly divided between dedicated ESG investors and opportunistic investors, and the cost of capital differs significantly between these segments. Sponsors and companies that can credibly access ESG capital will have a competitive advantage.

Seventh, the regulatory environment will continue to tighten. The European regulatory framework is the most stringent in the world, but other geographies will likely follow. Sponsors and companies that get ahead of regulatory requirements will face lower compliance costs and better access to capital.

Eighth, the definition of what counts as "ESG" is contested and will continue to be contested. There is legitimate debate about which ESG issues are material, how to measure ESG performance, and what standards should apply. Rather than waiting for universal agreement, pragmatic actors focus on material ESG issues and rigorous measurement.

Finally, I have learned that the most interesting opportunities in leveraged finance increasingly involve ESG. The boring, high-leverage, commodity-like deals that defined much of the market in the 1990s and 2000s are being replaced by more complex, thematic deals where ESG improvement is a key part of the value creation thesis. The best sponsors I know are those who can identify companies with material ESG issues that can be fixed with disciplined management and operational improvement, and who can credibly execute on ESG improvement while maintaining financial discipline.

ESG in leveraged finance is neither a panacea nor a distraction. It is a core feature of modern capital markets that requires sophisticated understanding, rigorous application, and healthy skepticism about claims. My goal in this chapter has been to provide the practitioner's perspective on how ESG actually works, what pitfalls to avoid, and where genuine value can be created. The future of leveraged finance will be written by sponsors and companies that master this integration.
