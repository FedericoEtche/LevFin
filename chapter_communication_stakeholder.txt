COMMUNICATION AND STAKEHOLDER MANAGEMENT IN LEVERAGED FINANCE

INTRODUCTION: THE UNDERRATED SKILL THAT SEPARATES THE GREAT FROM THE MERELY COMPETENT

I've spent the last twenty-one years working in leveraged finance, and in all that time, I've noticed something that rarely appears in the technical textbooks that litter the desks of junior analysts: the vast majority of credit failures, deal breakdowns, and missed opportunities have almost nothing to do with the quality of the financial modeling. They stem from communication failures.

This might seem counterintuitive to those just entering the field. After all, leveraged finance is quantitative. It's about leverage multiples, debt-to-EBITDA ratios, interest coverage, cash flow waterfall analyses, and stress testing under various scenarios. The models are complex. The math is unforgiving. It's natural to assume that if you can build a pristine DCF model and stress it properly, you've done the hard part. You haven't. Not even close.

I learned this lesson the hard way, and it cost my firm millions of dollars.

It was 2009, in the depths of the financial crisis. We had committed to a large leveraged acquisition in the media space—call it Company X. The credit metrics looked reasonable on an entry basis, even accounting for the recession. The sponsor, a major private equity firm, had a solid track record. The business had some contractual revenue and limited working capital requirements. Our underwriting memo was thorough. The modeling was sound. Every analyst and associate who looked at the deal was confident in the credit.

But we made one critical error: we never clearly articulated to our credit committee why the recession wasn't going to destroy this company the way it was destroying nearly everything else. We presented the numbers. We showed the downside case. We didn't tell the story. We didn't explain, in language that a former manufacturing executive serving on our credit committee could understand, why this business model was actually resilient despite the apocalyptic economic environment. We assumed the numbers would speak for themselves. They didn't.

The credit committee approved the loan, but with a grudging tone and a lower allocation than we'd anticipated. Worse, the committee's skepticism infected the syndication process. When investors asked us tough questions about recession risk, we found ourselves relying on the numbers—and the numbers, presented in a vacuum, looked terrifying. We couldn't syndicate the deal. What we could have solved in five minutes of clear conversation—explaining the contractual revenue base, the strategic importance of the platform, the sponsor's commitment to maintain margins even at lower volumes—never happened because we hadn't had that conversation internally first.

We ended up holding a significant portion of the loan ourselves. Within eighteen months, the business hit trouble, and we had to take a meaningful haircut. The credit turned out to be as treacherous as our credit committee had secretly feared. But here's what matters: I believe that if we had communicated properly, if we'd made the case not just with numbers but with narrative, the credit committee would have been more aligned with our recommendation. The syndicate would have been more confident. The sponsor would have felt more supported. And most importantly, when the downturn came, there would have been more capital willing to work through the stress because the investors understood the story, not just the metrics.

That experience fundamentally shaped how I approach communication in leveraged finance. It taught me that the technician who can model anything but can't explain it is always going to be less effective than the technician who understands that communication is not supplementary to the analysis—it is the analysis. The way you present information, the story you tell, the questions you anticipate and address, the confidence you convey: these are not cosmetic elements. They are the substance.

This chapter is dedicated to that underappreciated truth. Most books on leveraged finance spend ninety percent of their pages on how to model a deal and ten percent on everything else. This is a mistake. I've seen dozens of partners and managing directors over the years, and the ones who've built the most durable careers and the most loyal client bases were rarely the smartest technicians. They were almost always the best communicators.

Why does communication matter so much in leveraged finance specifically? Because the business is built on persuasion, on relationship, on the ability to convince skeptical people that your analysis is sound. You're asking credit committees to allocate capital based on your recommendation. You're asking co-investors to commit billions of dollars to a thesis that could prove disastrously wrong. You're asking borrowers to trust you with their most sensitive information and their capital structure. You're asking regulators and compliance teams to trust that you've thought through the risks. None of these asks succeed without effective communication.

The best technical analysts I've ever met who didn't have strong communication skills topped out as senior analysts or managers. The mediocre analysts with exceptional communication skills became MDs. This isn't because the banking world is shallow. It's because in a business where nearly every transaction involves managing uncertainty and aligning stakeholder incentives, communication is the oxygen.

What I want to give you in this chapter is not a primer on grammar or public speaking. There are plenty of those. What I want to give you is a practitioner's guide to the specific communication challenges you'll face in leveraged finance, the frameworks that work, the mistakes I've made and watched others make, and the anecdotes and hard-won insights that might save you from learning these lessons the expensive way.

Let me start with the Credit Committee presentation, because that's where so many careers either gain momentum or stall.

THE CREDIT COMMITTEE PRESENTATION: WINNING SKEPTICS ONE SLIDE AT A TIME

Your credit committee is not your friend. I don't mean this unkindly. Many of them are colleagues you may have worked with for years. But when they're sitting in that conference room in their committee capacity, they are professionals whose job it is to say no. They're evaluators, not allies. They've seen thousands of deals over their careers. Most of them have taken losses on credits they approved. Many have survived multiple credit cycles and have the battle scars to prove it. They are naturally skeptical, they are hardened against persuasion, and they will ask you questions you haven't anticipated.

Your job in the credit committee presentation is to make saying yes to your deal easy and saying no difficult.

The first principle is what I call the pyramid principle, which Barbara Minto famously articulated in the context of business writing and speaking. The pyramid principle says this: lead with your conclusion, support it with three to five key supporting points, and then back those points up with data. It's the inverse of how most analysts naturally present deals.

Most analysts want to start with background on the industry, then discuss the borrower's competitive position, then walk through the financial analysis in excruciating detail, and finally, at the end, recommend approval. This is backward. Your committee doesn't want a journey. They want the destination first, and then they want to understand why it's the right destination.

Here's how I structure a credit committee presentation:

Slide one, your conclusion: "We recommend a commitment of $X at Y basis points to finance the acquisition of Acme Corp by SponsorCo Partners. The credit is investment grade equivalent with EBITDA leverage of 3.5x at entry, declining to 2.8x by year three. The business is counter-cyclical, has contractual cash flow, and SponsorCo has a proven track record with similar platforms."

That's it. Not flowery. Not hedged. A clean, simple statement of what you want your committee to do and why, in thirty seconds.

Then, the three to five supporting points. These are not random. They should directly address the main concerns your committee is likely to have. For a media business in 2009, those concerns would be: is the business defensible in a recession? Is there enough cash flow stability? Is the sponsor creditworthy? Is the leverage reasonable? Does the debt structure protect us?

Each supporting point should get three to four minutes of discussion. You show data. You walk through a scenario. You answer a question. Then you move to the next point. If you have a supporting point that takes longer than five minutes to explain, it means either you haven't been clear about what you're trying to say, or the point isn't actually as central as you thought.

What committee members actually want to hear is this: what is the one thing that could go wrong with this deal, and how are we protected if it does? They don't want you to pretend that nothing could go wrong. They want you to show that you've thought deeply about the risks and that you've either mitigated them or that you're compensated for them.

In 2015, I took a credit committee presentation to approve a covenant-lite term loan for a business services company. The business was growing, but it had meaningful customer concentration. Its largest customer represented about eighteen percent of revenue. The second-largest, about twelve percent. If either of them left, the business would be materially smaller.

Instead of hiding this or burying it in the risk section, I led with it. "The risk to this credit is customer concentration. Our largest customer is eighteen percent of revenue. If we lost that customer, this business would not meet our underwriting metrics. Here's how we're thinking about it." Then I walked through: the customer had been with the business for eight years, had a long-term contract, had been growing their spend, and had limited alternative providers. The concentration was a known risk, not a hidden one. And because I'd named it explicitly, the committee didn't spend twenty minutes trying to figure out if I was aware of the obvious problem. Instead, we could focus on whether the company's plans to diversify were credible.

We got approved. A year later, that customer did begin to reduce their spending, but the company had executed on its diversification plan, and the credit held up. Would we have gotten as strong approval if I'd buried the concentration risk? I doubt it. I also suspect that when the customer risk materialized, the relationship team would have felt blindsided, which would have complicated our management of the situation.

This speaks to a deeper principle about credit committee presentations: you have a responsibility to structure the discussion so that committee members, if they vote no, feel like they've made a deliberate, thoughtful choice to reject a deal, not like they've been tricked into approving something unsound. The best committees have high standards, and they respect presentations where the presenter clearly respects them enough to show them the hard realities of the deal.

How do you handle tough questions? The first rule is: don't be defensive. When a committee member challenges your recommendation, they're not attacking you. They're doing their job. If you get defensive, you'll lose credibility instantly. You'll also waste thirty seconds of committee time while you compose yourself. Instead, develop the habit of treating tough questions as gift-wrapping.

In 2012, I presented a credit on a regional healthcare company to a committee that included an MD who had spent fifteen years in healthcare lending before joining our bank. He was tough and skeptical, and he asked a question about patient collections that I hadn't fully thought through. Instead of saying, "Well, we stress-tested collections," I said, "That's a great question. Here's what we're assuming, and here's why it might be conservative in a stable environment. But you're right that if collections deteriorate, the leverage profile deteriorates quickly. Let me walk through what that looks like." I then showed the sensitivity, acknowledged that collections were a key metric to monitor, and proposed more frequent financial covenant testing and reporting on collections trends.

He didn't vote yes because he got a better answer. He voted yes because he felt heard, and because I'd shown that I'd thought about his concern seriously. I also gave the surveillance team something concrete to focus on, which meant the credit committee felt like we had a plan to catch trouble early.

The art of presenting risk without losing your audience is crucial. There's a temptation to soft-pedal risk because you're trying to get credit approval. This is a mistake. It's also unnecessary. Credit committees don't require deals to be risk-free. They require you to be honest about the risks and to show that the economics of the transaction compensate you for bearing those risks.

In a speech I gave to junior lenders about five years ago, I said something I've thought about ever since: "Your job is not to eliminate risk from a deal. Your job is to understand, quantify, and properly compensate yourself for the risks that remain." That's true. If you're presenting a credit to committee, you should show them that you've thought about every major risk category: sponsor risk, market risk, business risk, operational risk, financial risk. For each one, you should explain what you're relying on—whether it's covenants, historical performance, market structure, management strength—to protect yourself.

Body language and confidence matter more in person than I think most analysts appreciate. If you're presenting via video call, which is now common, you need to be especially deliberate about this. Sit up. Look at the camera. Speak clearly. Pause between points. Don't rush. When you're nervous, you rush. When you rush, you make mistakes, and the committee senses your nervousness.

I've sat in hundreds of credit committee meetings, and I can tell you that a presenter who is calm and unhurried will be perceived as more knowledgeable than a presenter who is frantically racing through slides. If you don't know the answer to a question, say so, and offer to follow up. Don't bullshit. Credit committees are full of people who've spent their entire careers developing instincts for bullshit.

Common mistakes that kill credibility: first, citing numbers you don't understand. If you're presenting EBITDA leverage or debt service coverage, make sure you can explain, from first principles, why those metrics matter and how they're calculated. If a committee member drills into your math and catches an error, it's over.

Second mistake: presenting a deal that you clearly don't believe in. I was in a credit committee meeting years ago where an associate presented a deal she'd been assigned to underwrite. You could tell thirty seconds in that she didn't think the credit was any good. The analysis was mechanical. The presentation had a going-through-the-motions quality. She recommended approval, but she didn't persuade anyone, because she hadn't persuaded herself. The deal was rejected, and to my knowledge, it turned out to be troubled, so the committee's instinct was right. But the associate had wasted everyone's time because she hadn't done the intellectual work of deciding what she actually thought.

This is important: before you present to committee, you need to have already decided whether you believe in the deal. If you don't, say so. If you're on the fence, say that too. But don't present something as investable if your gut tells you it isn't. Your gut is usually right.

Third mistake: being surprised by a fair question. If you've presented a deal with high leverage, and someone asks you about leverage, that's not a gotcha. That's what you should expect. Anticipate the hard questions. Model them into your presentation. Show that you've thought about the committee's likely concerns before the meeting even starts.

Now, let me give you templates and frameworks that actually work. Here's the structure I've used consistently:

Opening slide: your recommendation, in one clear sentence. Not a question. Not a hypothetical. A statement: "We recommend approval of X."

Next slides: the borrower and sponsor. Who are they? What have they done? What do we know about their management quality? Keep this brief, unless the sponsor's track record is a major positive or negative.

Then: the deal dynamics. What's being financed? What does the capital structure look like? At what entry is the leverage? What does it look like in year three? This is table-stakes material. You need it early.

Then: the business and market. What is this company? What does it do? Why does anyone care? What's the competitive position? What are the trends? This is where you tell the story. Don't bury this.

Then: the key value driver. What's the one thing that has to happen for this credit to perform? Is it volume? Is it EBITDA margin? Is it multiple contraction? Name it. Quantify it. Stress-test it.

Then: the downside case. What if that key value driver doesn't materialize? What does the leverage look like? Can the borrower service the debt? What's our recovery?

Then: risk factors, by category. Sponsor risk, market risk, business risk, operational risk, execution risk. Each one with a sentence or two about mitigation.

Then: the covenants and protections. What's our surveillance mechanism? How will we know if things are going wrong?

Then: conclusion. Reiterate your recommendation. Make it clear why you're comfortable with the risk and the return.

This structure is not fancy. It's not creative. It's battle-tested, which is better. It's also defensible. If a committee member questions you on any point, they're questioning you on something you explicitly presented, not something you buried or left out.

One more thing on credit presentations: know the room. Different credit committees have different personalities. Some are process-oriented; they want to make sure all the boxes are checked. Some are technical; they want to drill into the modeling. Some are risk-focused; they want to hear about what could go wrong. Some are relationship-focused; they want to know about the client and the sponsor.

Spend five minutes before your presentation understanding which kind of committee you're walking into. Adjust your emphasis accordingly. If the committee is process-oriented, make sure your covenants are airtight. If it's technical, make sure your model is bulletproof. If it's risk-focused, lead with risk mitigation. If it's relationship-focused, tell the sponsor's story well.

The best credit committee presentations I've ever sat through were delivered by people who understood this principle. They weren't generic. They were tailored to the people in the room. And they respected those people enough to give them what they actually cared about, not what the presenter thought they should care about.

THE CREDIT MEMO: WHERE YOUR ANALYSIS BECOMES REAL

A credit presentation lasts thirty minutes. A credit memo lasts forever. People will still be reading your memo about this deal five years from now, when the credit is performing well or badly, and they'll be trying to understand what you were thinking and whether you'd properly identified and quantified the risks.

This matters. It's why the credit memo is arguably the most important document you'll write in your career as a credit professional.

The structure of a credit memo is standard: executive summary, business overview, financial analysis, risk factors, and recommendation. But the way you use that structure, the information you choose to highlight, and the narrative you weave through the quantitative analysis—that's where you can write a memo that's not just competent but actually insightful.

Let me start with the executive summary. The executive summary is read by busy people who are scanning for the essential points. They're not reading your memo to understand the minutiae of the business model. They're reading it to know what you think and why. So the executive summary should be brief—I typically aim for two pages—and should contain:

One sentence: your recommendation.
One or two paragraphs: the business overview. What is this company? Why should anyone care?
Two or three bullet points: the key investment highlights. What's good about this credit?
Two or three bullet points: the key risks. What's the vulnerability?
Two or three paragraphs: your financial summary. What's the entry leverage? What's the trajectory? What's the sensitivity?
One paragraph: your conclusion. Why are the highlights enough to offset the risks?

That's it. If someone read only your executive summary, they should understand exactly why you're recommending approval or rejection.

Now, the business overview section. This is where I see a lot of memos go wrong. Analysts will write a detailed industry analysis. They'll discuss market trends, competitive dynamics, regulatory environment, all of which may be relevant context. But I've read hundreds of credit memos, and I've learned that most people skimming a memo don't care about the industry overview; they care about understanding the specific business in question and why it matters.

When I write a business overview, I start with a simple question: what does this company do, and why is that valuable? If I can't answer that question in a paragraph, I don't understand the business well enough to recommend approving a credit. Once I've answered that basic question, then I explain the competitive position, the market trends, the regulatory environment—but always in the context of how those factors affect this specific company.

Here's an example. In 2013, I worked on a credit for a specialty chemicals company. The traditional approach would be to write pages about the global chemicals industry, market sizes, growth trends, geographic dynamics, and then eventually drill down to this company. Instead, I wrote: "SpecialChem Inc. manufactures specialty adhesives and sealants used primarily in the automotive and aerospace industries. The company has a fifteen percent share of the addressable market and strong customer relationships with major OEMs. The business is counter-cyclical to automotive production cycles because customers tend to increase adhesive usage per vehicle as they reduce overall vehicle weight to improve fuel efficiency."

That one paragraph tells you what the company does, why it matters, and what trends are driving its business. The rest of the business overview can then discuss the company's competitive advantages, its customer base, its geographic footprint, and its growth strategy. But the core narrative is already established.

On quantitative precision versus narrative power: this is where credit writing is actually an art. You need both. The numbers tell you whether the credit pencils. The narrative tells you whether the credit makes sense. If you're analyzing a mature, stable business in a consolidating industry, the narrative might be simple. The company is losing market share. Margins are being compressed. The sponsor's thesis is that they'll stabilize margins and consolidate the space. That's the narrative. Then you show the numbers that would prove the narrative right or wrong.

If you're analyzing a growth business in a disrupting industry, the narrative is more complex. You need to explain how the disruption changes the economics of the business, why this company is positioned to win in the new environment, and what the timeline looks like. Then you stress-test whether they can finance that transition.

The best credit memos I've written and the best ones I've read blend these two elements seamlessly. You're not just showing numbers. You're telling a story with numbers, a story that a reasonable person reading the memo would find persuasive.

On presenting negative information: this is critical. If you're uncovering weaknesses in the credit, you have two choices. You can bury them in the risk section and hope no one notices them. Or you can acknowledge them directly, explain how you've thought about them, and show that you believe the credit is sound despite them.

The second approach is almost always better. If there's something obviously wrong with the credit, don't pretend it doesn't exist. Name it. Quantify it. Explain why you're comfortable with it anyway. If you can't get comfortable with it, then don't recommend approval.

I once read a memo that was drafted by a junior analyst and revised by a more senior person. The junior analyst had noted that the borrower's largest customer was somewhat sticky, but that they'd lost a significant customer three years prior. The senior person went through and removed mentions of customer concentration from the body of the memo, moved it to a footnote in the risk section, and reframed the historical loss as a non-recurring issue.

Two years later, the borrower lost their largest customer. The credit deteriorated materially. Everyone in the firm who'd approved that credit knew that customer concentration had been a known issue, but because it had been downplayed in the memo, no one had built portfolio oversight or covenant monitoring around protecting against concentration risk. The situation could have been better managed if the memo had been honest about the risk upfront.

The importance of intellectual honesty in credit writing cannot be overstated. Your memo is evidence of your thinking. If you recommended a credit that later goes bad, that memo will be reviewed, probably by someone who is looking to understand whether you missed something or just had bad luck. If the memo shows that you'd thought about the risks and decided they were acceptable, people will be more forgiving than if the memo shows that you'd missed something obvious. And if the memo shows that you knew about a risk and buried it, that's a career event.

How credit memos evolve through your career is interesting. When I was an analyst, my memos were methodical. Every section was there. Every calculation was shown. I was proving to my supervisor that I'd done thorough work. When I became a VP, my memos got tighter. I was more selective about what to include and what to emphasize. I was writing for a credit committee that already knew how to read a balance sheet; I didn't need to walk them through every calculation. When I became an MD, my memos became even more selective. I was writing them often for a credit committee that was going to spend maybe ten minutes on the document. I focused on judgment calls, on the decisions that were easy versus hard, on the analysis that had changed since the last time we looked at the borrower.

This doesn't mean the analysis gets worse as you get more senior. It means you become more efficient at communicating what matters.

Real examples of good versus bad credit writing: a good memo is one where someone who's not an expert in the business can read the recommendation section and feel like they understand why the deal is happening. A bad memo is one where you can read the first half and still be confused about what the company actually does or why the sponsor is interested.

A good memo is one where the risk section isn't a dumping ground for every conceivable risk; it's a focused articulation of the material risks and how they're being mitigated. A bad memo is one where the risk section is longer than the positive section, because it suggests you haven't organized your thinking around what really matters.

A good memo is one where you can tell the author believed in the recommendation. A bad memo sounds like someone going through the motions.

And here's something that matters more than people think: a good memo is well-written. Not flowery. Not creative. Clear. A sentence says one thing. A paragraph is about one idea. You use the same term to mean the same thing throughout. You avoid jargon unless it's necessary. You write to be understood, not to sound smart.

Some of the best credit memos I've read have been written by people who write clearly in their personal lives. They write emails clearly. They explain things clearly in meetings. That clarity transfers to the memo. Some of the worst memos have been written by people who seem to believe that credit writing requires a different, more complex register than normal communication. It doesn't.

MANAGING THE SYNDICATION PROCESS: SELLING YOUR CONVICTION

Once you've gotten your credit committee's approval, you've solved fifty percent of your problem. You now have to sell your deal to the market. And if you thought credit committee members were skeptical, wait until you meet a room full of professional investors, each of whom has seen thousands of deals and taken meaningful losses.

Syndication is where communication transforms from internal persuasion to external sales. You're no longer trying to convince colleagues. You're trying to convince people whose money and reputation are on the line.

Communication with the borrower during syndication starts before the bank meeting, before the presentation deck is even finalized. You need to set expectations with the sponsor and the borrower about what the syndication process is going to look like, what investors will care about, and what feedback you anticipate hearing.

I had a sponsor, a large PE firm, who had done a few deals with us but was new to working with my team. We structured a leverage ratio that was healthy by historical standards but a bit higher than what recent market conditions had tolerated. Before we went to market, I had a conversation with the sponsor's deal team. I said something like this:

"Investors are going to ask us why this leverage is appropriate. They're going to point out that comparable recent transactions came in at lower leverage. They're going to wonder if we're pushing the envelope because we want to capture the additional spread. I'm confident we can defend this leverage based on the business fundamentals and the downside case. But I want you prepared for the fact that this is going to be the lead question we get in the bank meeting. We need to be aligned on how we're answering it."

The sponsor appreciated the heads-up. When investors asked exactly that question, the sponsor's CFO was ready with a detailed explanation about why this business could support the leverage. It wasn't defensive. It was proactive. And it set the tone that we'd thought deeply about the concern, rather than hoping no one would notice.

The bank meeting—the initial presentation to a selected list of investors—is where the syndication process really begins. This is different from a credit committee presentation in several important ways. Your audience is larger and more diverse. Some people in the room are really sophisticated and have done deals like this before. Others might be new to the asset class or the sponsor. You have a set amount of time, usually around ninety minutes total, including Q&A. You're trying to generate interest and get investors to commit to participating in the deal.

Here's how I structure a bank meeting presentation:

Opening remarks: you, the arranger, briefly explain the deal. Why are we doing this? Why should investors care? What's the context? This should be three to five minutes.

Sponsor remarks: the sponsor talks. Why is this acquisition strategic? What's the plan? What value are they going to create? This should be ten to fifteen minutes. It's crucial that the sponsor is well-prepared and confident here. If the sponsor sounds uncertain, investors will be uncertain.

Business overview: often presented by the sponsor's operating team or management. What's this business? How does it work? What's the competitive position? This should be twenty to thirty minutes.

Financial overview: you or your co-arranger walks through the capital structure, the financial assumptions, the projected returns. This should be fifteen to twenty minutes.

Investment highlights: a brief recap of why this is a good deal. Five to ten minutes.

Q&A: investors ask questions. This can go long if investors are interested.

The thing about bank meetings is that most of the time, you don't actually win deals in the room. You win deals before the meeting, in the way you've prepared the sponsor, in the materials you've sent out, in the one-on-one conversations you've had with key investors. The bank meeting is where you confirm that the deal is as you've described it and that the sponsor is credible.

The lender presentations and information memoranda that you circulate are often where the actual persuasion happens. The IM is a detailed document—often fifty to a hundred pages—that walks through the business, the market, the financials, the risks. It's the document that investors will refer back to when they're doing their own analysis.

Here's my view on information memoranda: they should be comprehensive without being overwhelming. Investors need to be able to find the information they need, but they don't want to wade through five pages of boilerplate to find a critical data point. Use tables, use charts, use clear formatting. Make the document easy to navigate.

The risk section of the IM is particularly important. This is where you show that you've thought about what could go wrong. Be specific. Don't use vague language. Don't just say "competitive risk." Explain what the competitive risk is, what the barriers to entry look like, what would have to change for competitive intensity to increase.

Managing investor questions and concerns during syndication is where your communication skills really get tested. Investors will ask hard questions. Some of them will be skeptical. Some will have legitimate concerns. Your job is to address those concerns clearly and honestly.

I was in a bank meeting for a leveraged recapitalization of a business services company. The business had been performing well, but it was in a space where technology was starting to disrupt the traditional model. One investor asked a pointed question: "This is a really nice business, but in three years, if some tech startup figures out how to do this more efficiently, where does that leave us?"

It would have been easy to dismiss the concern or to suggest that disruption wasn't really a risk. Instead, the sponsor's CEO answered directly: "That's the risk we see too. Here's what we're doing about it: we've invested in our own technology platform, we're experimenting with new service delivery models, and we're watching the competitive landscape. If we're going to be disrupted, we want to catch it early and adapt." He then walked through their specific investments and timeline.

The investor didn't vote yes because the disruption risk disappeared. The investor voted yes because the management team had clearly thought about the risk and had a plan to address it. That's what investors want to hear.

The feedback loop: how to relay market feedback to the borrower. During syndication, you're hearing from dozens of investors, and some of them will express concerns or point out potential weaknesses in the deal thesis. You need a process for relaying this feedback to the borrower and the sponsor, because often, this feedback will inform how you manage the deal going forward.

I had a situation where we were syndicating a deal, and multiple investors asked about customer concentration in the borrower's business. It turned out that the sponsor hadn't fully appreciated that this was a potential concern. We brought that feedback back to the sponsor and suggested they build a more aggressive customer diversification strategy into the first hundred days. Having that framework in place made investors more comfortable, because they could see that the sponsor was taking the concentration risk seriously.

Handling negative feedback constructively means resisting the urge to be defensive. If investors have legitimate concerns, those concerns will matter. Better to address them head-on than to hope they'll be forgotten. If investors have concerns based on incomplete information, provide the information. If investors have concerns based on their own biases or experience in a different asset class, acknowledge the concern and explain why you think it's less relevant in this context.

The roadshow: in-person versus virtual dynamics. For larger deals, you might do a roadshow where you and the sponsor visit major investors' offices and present the deal. This is expensive and time-consuming, but it's often necessary for large syndications or for sponsors that don't have an established relationship with the investment community.

In-person roadshows are different from virtual ones. In person, you can read the room. You can sense when an investor is genuinely interested versus politely listening. You can have side conversations at lunch or coffee that give you deeper insight into what an investor cares about. You can build rapport in a way that's harder over video.

That said, virtual roadshows have become standard since the pandemic, and they work. They're actually more efficient in some ways. You can reach more investors without the travel. You can record the session, so investors can share it with their investment committee. The trade-off is that you lose some of the relationship-building that happens in person.

My advice: take the format that makes sense for the deal. For a small deal or a follow-on investment with an established investor base, virtual is fine. For a large deal where you need to do a lot of education about the sponsor or the business, in-person is worth the effort if you can manage it.

NEGOTIATING WITH COUNTERPARTIES: THE ART OF GETTING WHAT YOU NEED

Leveraged finance is full of negotiations. You're negotiating documentation with counsel. You're negotiating terms with the borrower. You're negotiating with other lender groups. You're negotiating with the sponsor about the capital structure. These negotiations can make or break a deal, and they require specific communication skills.

Understanding the other side's constraints and incentives is the first principle of effective negotiation in leveraged finance. Every party in a transaction has constraints: the borrower has a constraint on how much leverage it can take, driven by lender requirements; the sponsor has a constraint on how much equity they're willing to put in, driven by return hurdle rates; other lenders have constraints based on their credit policy; your own credit committee has constraints based on portfolio limits and risk appetite.

If you understand these constraints, you can negotiate more effectively because you're proposing solutions that work within those constraints rather than asking the other party to violate their stated policy.

I once negotiated credit agreement terms with a sponsor who was absolutely firm that they needed at least five years of covenant-lite status. This was non-negotiable from their perspective; they wanted operational flexibility. On our side, we were concerned about a five-year period without financial covenants. The traditional resolution would have been to fight about the number of years.

Instead, I asked: "What's driving your need for covenant-lite status? Is it operational flexibility, or is it about avoiding a default test?" The sponsor said it was both: they wanted room to manage the business, but also, if the business hit a soft patch, they didn't want the loan to default based on technical breaches.

So we negotiated a solution: three years of covenant-lite status, then two years of modified covenants with high thresholds that were unlikely to be breached unless the business was actually in material distress. This addressed both the sponsor's need for flexibility and our need for visibility into problems. The sponsor got most of what they wanted, and we got our eyes back on the business after the first three years.

The documentation negotiation is where a lot of people miss important communication opportunities. There's a markup process: one side's counsel provides a draft agreement, the other side provides comments, and then you go back and forth. This process can be adversarial, but it doesn't have to be.

Here's my approach: when I'm reading a counterparty's comments on our draft, I'm trying to understand not just what they're asking for, but why they're asking for it. Is it a matter of principle? Is it a belt-and-suspenders concern? Is it driven by their credit committee or their counsel's standard practice?

When I respond to comments, I try to address the underlying concern rather than just reject the proposed change. For example, if a lender's counsel marks up a borrower covenant to require monthly financial statements instead of quarterly ones, the concern might be that they want visibility into deterioration. Instead of fighting about the frequency, you could propose quarterly financials with a requirement to provide monthly unaudited results. That might address their concern and be acceptable to the borrower.

Email versus phone versus in-person: choosing the right medium is underrated. Email is wonderful for efficiency; you can send a message at midnight and your point is recorded. But email is also good for creating misunderstandings. Tone is hard to convey. A message that seems straightforward to the writer can seem aggressive or dismissive to the reader.

I have a rule: I use email for confirming decisions and documenting agreements, but if I'm trying to move someone from their position or understand why they're taking a particular stance, I pick up the phone or request a call. Phone calls are better than email for negotiation because you can hear tone, you can clarify misunderstandings in real-time, and you can often find creative solutions more quickly than you can via email.

In-person is best for initial negotiations on really substantive points. You can read body language. You can see whether someone is truly dug in or just defending a position for show. You can build rapport more easily. Video calls are a reasonable middle ground, though they're not quite as good as in person.

Managing multi-party negotiations is where communication becomes really complex. You might have a sponsor, a borrower, a lead arranger, co-arrangers, other lender groups, the borrower's counsel, the lender's counsel, the sponsor's counsel. Each party has slightly different interests. Some of those interests are aligned; some are not.

The key is to clearly understand where alignment and misalignment exist and to structure the negotiation accordingly. In some cases, you want to negotiate bilateral agreements first (arranger with borrower on the business terms, arranger with sponsor on the capital structure), and then bring those into the broader negotiation. In other cases, you want to get everyone aligned early because any bilateral agreement is going to affect the others.

I once worked on a complex restructuring where we had the borrower, three lender groups with different seniority, the sponsor, and multiple advisors. The temptation was to try to negotiate everything multilaterally. Instead, we structured the process as follows: first, the lead lenders aligned on what we needed from the borrower. Then, we negotiated bilateral agreements with the borrower. Then, we brought other lender groups in to confirm they were comfortable. Only then did we bring the sponsor into the discussion about whether the restructured capital structure was acceptable.

This sequential approach was more efficient than trying to negotiate all at once, because each bilateral agreement reduced the degrees of freedom for the next negotiation, and by the time we got to the sponsor, the main terms were already set.

When to push and when to concede: this is judgment. If something is truly important to the credit quality, you push. If something is important to the relationship but not critical to the credit, you concede. The mistake people make is treating every point as equally important.

I would characterize my negotiation style as "reasonable but not soft." I'm willing to move on points that don't matter materially to the credit, because I want the other party to feel like we're being fair. But when something matters—a covenant, a restriction, a payment priority—I won't move because I don't think it materially affects the dynamics. I have clear reasoning for each position, and I can explain why I'm pushing on some things and not others.

Cultural differences in negotiation: US negotiation tends to be direct. You state your position clearly and defend it. European negotiation tends to be more formal and hierarchical. You make your points through the process, and deviations from established protocols matter more. Asian negotiation often involves more relationship-building and less direct confrontation.

These generalizations break down at the individual level, but they're worth being aware of when you're negotiating across borders. I once negotiated on a deal that involved a US sponsor, a European borrower, and Asian lenders. The Europeans wanted more process and more formal documentation of agreements. The Asians wanted more relationship-building and personal connection with the deal team. The Americans wanted to move fast and make decisions.

Recognizing these different approaches made the negotiation more efficient. We spent time in the early meetings building relationships with the Asian lenders, we gave the European borrower more structured process and documentation, and we moved quickly on the specific points where the Americans needed speed. Everyone felt heard, and the negotiation closed faster than it would have if we'd pushed everyone to conform to one negotiation style.

STAKEHOLDER MANAGEMENT IN RESTRUCTURING: WHEN EVERYTHING GETS HARDER

Restructuring is where communication moves from the routine to the existential. If you're working through a restructuring—whether that's a covenant amendment, a refinancing, or a full debt exchange—you're managing parties with conflicting interests, you're dealing with asymmetric information, and you're often working under time pressure.

Communicating with a distressed borrower is completely different from communicating with a healthy borrower. The healthy borrower is trying to optimize. The distressed borrower is trying to survive. Their tone with you will be different. Sometimes desperate. Sometimes angry. Sometimes resigned. You need to be aware that you're not having a normal business conversation.

I had a call with a borrower's CFO in 2009, after their business had deteriorated faster than anyone had anticipated. The CFO was essentially shut down. They were going through the motions, answering our questions, but they were clearly overwhelmed. What they needed from us wasn't a detailed discussion of the financial forecast. What they needed was clarity and structure: what are the next steps? What do you need from us? What's the timeline? What happens if we hit this milestones?

Once I realized that, I changed how I communicated. Instead of a meandering discussion, I sent an email outlining what we needed, by when, and what the implications were if we hit or missed those milestones. The CFO later told me that email was helpful because it cut through the fog and told them what they needed to focus on. It didn't require a lot of back-and-forth or lengthy conversations.

Managing creditor groups with conflicting interests is the classic challenge of restructuring. A first-lien lender wants a rapid refinancing or restructuring that protects their principal. A second-lien lender might want to take some risk because they're hoping for equity upside. Equity is fighting for survival. Term loan holders might differ from notes holders because of different covenants or payment schedules.

The communication challenge is that you can't say the same thing to everyone, because everyone's interests are different. But you also can't be so different in your messaging to different groups that you get caught in a contradiction.

Here's how I approach it: I'm radically honest about the situation with each lender group. I might say to the first-lien group: "Here's my assessment of the business and where we stand. Here's what happens to your collateral if we take various actions." And I might say to the second-lien group something different, because their recovery depends on different variables. But I'm being honest about my assessment of the business. I'm not telling the first-lien lenders that the business is worse than I think it is, and I'm not telling the second-lien lenders that it's better than I think it is.

The role of advisors as communication intermediaries: in many restructurings, there will be financial advisors, legal advisors, maybe insurance advisors, all of whom are parties to the process. These advisors often become the communication intermediaries between groups with conflicting interests, particularly if the borrower needs to stay in the middle between creditor groups.

I worked with a restructuring advisor once who was incredibly skilled at this. She would have conversations with creditor groups, understand what each was willing to do, and then craft a transaction structure that could work for everyone. But she never tried to make someone give more than they were willing to. She understood that the transaction had to work for all the constituencies or it wasn't going to close. Her role was to find the solution set that worked for all parties, not to extract concessions.

Press and public communication in restructuring is its own challenge. If a company is restructuring, there's often media interest, particularly if it's a large or well-known business. The question is: what are you communicating to the public and who's doing the communicating?

Generally, the borrower should be doing the communicating, working with counsel and possibly advisors. The lenders are usually behind the scenes. But you need to coordinate the messaging. If the press is reporting that the restructuring involves a full debt exchange, and the borrower's statement suggests only a modest amendment, the lack of alignment will be noticed.

How information asymmetry affects negotiation power is crucial to understand. The party with more and better information has more power in a negotiation. In a restructuring, the borrower and the management team usually have more detailed information about the business than the lenders do. But the lenders have more detailed information about the financial situation than equity does.

This is why access to information—management calls, financial reporting, operational metrics—matters so much in distressed situations. The more information you have, the better your position in any negotiation.

The confidentiality problem with material non-public information and restricted lists is real. In a restructuring, you're often dealing with MNPI, which means you have legal restrictions on who you can talk to and what you can do with your capital. If you own shares of the company, you might not be allowed to sell them. If you're trying to form a creditor group to negotiate collectively, you need to be careful about who you can talk to and what you can communicate.

This can actually constrain your ability to communicate effectively, because you can't be as transparent as you'd like to be. The way to manage this is to be clear about the constraints upfront. I've had calls with borrowers where I've said: "There are some things I know that I can't share with you because of information barriers. But here's what I can tell you, and here's how I'm thinking about your situation."

Managing leaked information and rumor mills: in any restructuring, especially a large one, there will be rumors. Employees wonder if they're going to lose their jobs. Customers wonder if the company will survive. Suppliers wonder if they're going to get paid. In some cases, those rumors are based on accurate information. In some cases, they're speculation.

The leaks are usually not coming from the formal participants in the restructuring. They're coming from someone in the company or from someone who overheard something. Once information is in the rumor mill, you have a credibility problem. If you try to deny something that's partially true, people won't believe you. If you try to stay silent, the rumors will escalate.

I've generally found it's better to be straightforward about what you can and can't comment on. If you can say something that addresses the rumor without disclosing something you're legally restricted from disclosing, do that. If you can't comment, just say you can't comment. People generally understand MNPI restrictions, and they're more forgiving of "no comment" if they understand why you're not commenting.

INTERNAL COMMUNICATION: THE FOUNDATION OF EVERYTHING

Managing up—communicating with senior bankers and portfolio managers—is a skill that junior people often underestimate. Your senior people don't know everything that you know about a particular credit. They're managing multiple credits, multiple relationships, multiple responsibilities. If you want them to be in a position to advocate for your deal or to support your recommendation, you need to keep them informed.

I have a rule: I don't let a portfolio manager be surprised in front of the credit committee. If something has changed about a credit that might affect approval, the portfolio manager needs to know about it before the committee meeting. That means I'm in front of the PM ahead of time, walking through the issue, explaining what's changed and what I think it means.

This is not political. It's respect for the person who's ultimately responsible for the portfolio. They need to be informed in order to do their job. And it makes your life easier too, because if the PM is already comfortable with your analysis, they're not going to blindside you in the committee with a different point of view.

Managing across—coordinating with legal, compliance, operations—is where communication breaks down in a lot of organizations. Credit people, legal people, and compliance people often have different incentives and operate in different worlds. Credit people want to move fast and get deals done. Legal people want to get the documentation right. Compliance people want to make sure everything is properly reported and there are no regulatory issues.

These are not bad incentives. They're just different. The way to manage across these functions is to understand what each group cares about and to involve them at the right time in the process.

With legal, I want to involve them early enough that I'm not asking them to fix something at the last minute, but not so early that they're bogging down a preliminary discussion in documentation questions. Once I'm pretty confident the business terms are going to work, I brief legal on the structure and the key issues, and we start the documentation process in parallel with my syndication discussions.

With compliance, I want to make sure they understand the key risks and what we're monitoring. I might have a call early in the deal to walk through the sponsor, the borrower, the business, and the key risks from a compliance perspective—sanctions, FCPA, structuring concerns, anything else that's relevant. Then, once we've closed the deal, we have a surveillance protocol that makes sure we're tracking the things we said we'd track.

Managing down—mentoring junior team members—is where your communication style influences the next generation. If you communicate clearly, junior people learn to communicate clearly. If you communicate imprecisely, they will too.

I always try to explain the "why" behind communication choices when I'm mentoring junior people. Why are we leading with that point instead of this one? Why are we presenting the risks first instead of burying them? Why are we structured our email the way we did?

One junior analyst I mentored was writing credit memos that were technically sound but extremely long and somewhat disorganized. I sat down with him and we rewrote a section of his memo together, explaining as we went why we were making certain choices. I said something like: "This three-page explanation of the industry can become a paragraph because what we're really trying to say is that the industry is stable and growing, which is good for our credit. The reader doesn't need to understand the industry in detail; they need to understand how it affects our credit." That lesson transferred to how he wrote future memos.

The weekly update: keeping stakeholders informed. If you're managing a credit or a transaction, you're probably sending weekly updates to stakeholders. These are easy to do poorly. They become laundry lists of activities that happened over the past week, with no clear narrative about what matters.

A good weekly update has a clear structure: here's the status of the transaction or the credit situation. Here's what we're working on. Here's what we're waiting on. Here's what the next step is. If something has changed or if there's new information, you lead with that. If it's just routine updates, you can be more brief.

I've been on the receiving end of daily updates from my team members where the daily update is actually longer than my weekly update. That's a sign of disorganization. If you're sending daily updates, they should be genuinely material news. Otherwise, you're creating noise.

How to escalate problems effectively: when something goes wrong with a credit or a deal, your first instinct might be to try to solve it before anyone knows it's a problem. This is often a mistake. If there's a real problem, the longer you wait to escalate it, the worse it gets.

I once had a junior person on my team notice that a borrower's latest financial statement had revenue down significantly from the prior quarter. The analyst wanted to do more investigation before mentioning anything to me. But this was a key metric for the credit. I needed to know immediately so I could figure out what was going on and brief my portfolio manager. The analyst's instinct to investigate first was good. The instinct to delay telling me was bad.

Now, you shouldn't escalate every small issue. You should escalate issues that are material, issues that require senior-level decision-making, and issues where the longer you wait to disclose, the worse the problem becomes. The framework I use is: would my boss rather hear this problem from me in its early stages or from the credit committee in the middle of a default? If the answer is "from me in the early stages," then escalate early.

The art of the concise email: email is both a blessing and a curse. It lets you communicate asynchronously and create a record of your communication. But it also creates opportunity for people to be long-winded and for important information to get buried.

Here's my rule for email: one screen of information. If your email is longer than what fits on one screen, it's too long. That means you need to be really disciplined about what you include. You front-load the key point. You provide whatever context is necessary. You include a clear call to action or next step. That's it.

I once got an email from a senior colleague that was about a decision we needed to make on a restructuring. The email was three pages long. It covered the history of the situation, multiple different options, the pros and cons of each option, and eventually got around to asking for my input. By the time I finished reading the email, I'd forgotten what the actual question was.

I called the person and asked them to send me a one-screen email with their recommendation and the key facts supporting it. They resisted—they said they wanted to give me all the context. I said: "I have the context from our previous conversations and from the materials you've sent. What I need right now is your clear recommendation and the key facts that led you there." Once they sent that version, I could engage effectively.

BUILDING AND MAINTAINING CLIENT RELATIONSHIPS

Building long-term relationships with sponsors and borrowers is where the practitioner mindset really matters. Deals are transactional. Relationships are long-term. If you approach every interaction as transactional, you'll do okay on individual deals, but you'll miss the opportunity to build a durable relationship.

I've worked with certain sponsors dozens of times over the course of my career. We've done deals together that went beautifully and deals that were stressful. We've navigated changes in their portfolio companies, we've worked through restructurings, we've supported them when they were raising new funds. These relationships exist because I've treated them as long-term relationships, not as transaction-by-transaction interactions.

This means I've been patient with them when they've asked for terms I didn't want to give, because I understood that they had different incentives than I did. It means I've been honest with them when I thought they were making a mistake, rather than just going along with whatever they wanted. It means I've stayed engaged with them between transactions, rather than only calling when we have a deal to discuss.

The difference between transactional and advisory relationships: a transactional relationship is one where you're there to execute a specific transaction. An advisory relationship is one where you're a trusted advisor to the client. The transition from transactional to advisory happens slowly and is based on demonstrating that you understand their business, that you have their interests in mind, and that you can be trusted with sensitive information.

To move from transactional to advisory, you need to show up differently. You need to be willing to give perspective on their business and their strategy, not just on the specific deal you're executing. You need to understand their constraints and their objectives. You need to be able to say no to things that don't work for them, not just for you.

A PE sponsor I worked with was planning to do an acquisition that I thought was strategically questionable. They were going to finance it with us, and the credit looked okay from our perspective. But I didn't think the acquisition was good for the sponsor. I had a conversation with them. I said: "I think this acquisition is below your bar. Here's why. And I also think that if we finance it at terms that look okay to us, you're going to be overpaying and you'll regret it in a year."

The sponsor didn't do the acquisition. Later, they told me that conversation was important to them, because it was the first time a lender had given them honest perspective on their strategic decisions rather than just trying to be helpful by financing whatever they wanted to do. That conversation probably moved us from a transactional to an advisory relationship.

How to deliver bad news—repricing, downgrades, covenant concerns—is a critical skill. There are situations where you need to tell a client something they don't want to hear: the market has moved, and your pricing is now below market; we're downgrading your credit rating; we're concerned about covenant compliance.

The way to deliver bad news is to be clear, direct, and respectful. You don't sugarcoat it. You don't bury the lede. You say what's going on, you explain why it's happening, and you outline what you're going to do about it.

In 2013, I had to deliver a repricing to a borrower who had been a good client of ours. Their business was performing well, but the market had moved, and their pricing was now thirty basis points away from what new comparable issues were pricing at. We had two options: we could keep the pricing the same and underperform the market, or we could proactively offer to reprice them at a better level.

I called the CFO and said something like: "The market has moved. Your pricing is now uncompetitive. We want to offer you a repricing that brings you to better levels. Here's what we're proposing." The CFO was actually relieved. He'd been expecting us to ask them to accept the lower pricing or refinance elsewhere. Instead, we were offering a proactive solution.

That repricing didn't make us much money. But the signal it sent—that we cared about our clients and weren't going to take advantage of them—was worth a lot in terms of relationship equity.

Managing expectations throughout the lifecycle of a deal: this is where consistency in communication matters. What you promise during the sales process, you need to deliver during the transaction and afterward. If you've said that you're going to be a supportive lender and that you'll work with them if they have issues, then when they do have issues, you need to actually be supportive and willing to work with them.

I had a situation where we'd promised a sponsor that we'd be flexible on covenant testing if their business hit a soft patch. When they did hit a soft patch, we stuck to that promise. We didn't demand a waiver and repricing immediately. We worked with them to understand what was happening and to figure out a path forward. The covenant waiver we eventually gave them was part of a broader conversation about how to stabilize the business.

The sponsor later told me that this was the deciding factor in their decision to use us again for a future deal. It wasn't because we gave them something we wouldn't have given to someone else. It was because we kept our word.

The follow-up: staying engaged between transactions. I try to check in with important clients at least once a quarter, even if there's no active transaction. The check-in might be brief—a call to see how their portfolio companies are doing, to ask if there's anything we can help with, to share relevant market information. The point is to stay on their radar and to maintain the relationship.

I have a portfolio manager who takes the sponsor executives to a baseball game once a year. That one day of relationship-building is worth more than months of transactional emails. It keeps the relationship personal, not just professional.

How relationships drive repeat business: repeat business is the foundation of a sustainable credit business. If you do good work on one deal and maintain the relationship, you'll get the next deal. If you do good work on ten deals, you'll get a lot of next deals. If you have a bad experience with a client, they're going to take their next deal to someone else.

The sponsors and borrowers we work with repeatedly are the ones who've had good experiences with us and who trust us. The transactions we don't win are often to people who had a bad experience with us or who don't know us.

CROSS-CULTURAL COMMUNICATION IN GLOBAL FINANCE

Working across time zones and cultures in global leveraged finance has become standard over the last fifteen years. I've worked on deals with European sponsors, Asian borrowers, Middle Eastern lenders, and African operating companies. Each of these regions has different communication norms, different cultural expectations, and different business practices.

Working across time zones is a logistics problem that also has a communication component. If your sponsors are in Europe and your borrowers are in Asia, you're going to have a hard time getting everyone on the same call at a time that's reasonable for all parties. The way to manage this is to acknowledge the constraint and to have a clear protocol for asynchronous communication.

I worked on a deal where the sponsor was in London, the borrower was in Singapore, and the arranging team was in New York. Synchronous communication was basically impossible. So we structured the process as follows: the New York team would prepare a detailed document about a key issue, circulate it to both sides, give them 48 hours for written comments, and then on the call that worked for all time zones, we'd discuss and move forward.

This was slower than if everyone was in the same time zone, but it worked because the expectations were clear and the process was disciplined.

Communication style differences: US negotiators and communicators are typically direct. You state your position, and you defend it. There's a certain individualism to it—I have a point of view, and I'm going to argue for it.

European communicators are often more formal and more focused on process. There's a sense that how you arrive at a conclusion matters as much as the conclusion itself. There's also more hierarchy—the junior person is less likely to speak up or to directly challenge a senior person's point.

Asian communicators often prioritize harmony and relationship over directness. There's a tendency to avoid explicit disagreement or to express disagreement very indirectly. The senior person's opinion carries more weight. There's often more emphasis on personal relationships and trust as a foundation for business relationships.

These are generalizations, and they vary enormously by individual, by country, and by industry. But they're useful frameworks for understanding why communication that works perfectly in New York might feel aggressive in London or abrupt in Tokyo.

I was negotiating credit agreement terms with a large Asian lending group. In a meeting with the group's representatives, I was advocating for a particular covenant structure. The lead person from the Asian group listened politely, took notes, and didn't directly disagree with me. In an American context, I would have interpreted this as agreement or at least acceptance. But in this case, it meant they weren't comfortable, they just weren't going to tell me directly.

I followed up with a bilateral call with the lead person and explicitly asked: "Are you comfortable with the covenant structure we discussed?" He said: "Not completely. But in the group meeting, it wasn't appropriate for me to say that directly." We then had a separate discussion and found a modified structure that worked for him. If I'd assumed the group meeting agreement represented actual agreement, I would have been surprised when they came back with a different proposal.

Language considerations in cross-border deals: not everyone in a cross-border deal is a native English speaker. Even people who are very proficient in English may not be as precise in English as they are in their native language. This has implications for how you communicate.

First, you speak more slowly and more clearly. You use shorter sentences. You avoid idioms or slang. You check for understanding more frequently.

Second, you're careful about written communication. I've seen misunderstandings emerge from email exchanges where someone wrote something that was ambiguous in English, and people interpreted it differently.

Third, you're aware of the concept of legal English. The English used in credit agreements is specific and technical. If you're working with non-native speakers, you need to be extra careful that they understand the implications of specific language in the agreement.

I once had a discussion with a borrower whose CFO was a non-native English speaker. We were discussing a financial covenant, and there was a phrase in the covenant about how it would be calculated. The CFO asked me to explain it. When I explained it in simpler terms, the CFO said: "Oh, I didn't understand the agreement the way you're describing it." The language in the agreement was technically correct, but it wasn't as clear as it should have been. We updated the language to make it clearer.

How cultural misunderstandings can derail negotiations: I've seen transactions slow down or nearly break because of cultural misunderstandings. In one case, a sponsor and a lender group were negotiating leverage ratios, and the lender was being more aggressive than the sponsor expected. The sponsor interpreted this as the lender being greedy or not aligned with their interests. In reality, the lender's credit committee had simply set a more aggressive hurdle rate for this asset class, and the lender was trying to meet their credit committee's requirements.

Once we clarified that this was a portfolio-level constraint, not a negotiating position, the conversation shifted. The sponsor understood that the lender wasn't trying to gouge them; the lender was trying to meet internal requirements.

Building rapport across cultural boundaries: rapport is personal, but it's also cultural. What builds rapport in one culture might not work in another.

In a high-context culture (many Asian and European cultures), rapport is built through understanding, time, and relationship. You get to know someone, you spend time with them, you develop trust. In a low-context culture (much of the US), rapport is built through efficiency, directness, and demonstrated competence. You get things done, you're straightforward, you deliver on your promises.

When I'm working with people from a high-context culture, I invest in relationship-building. I take time to have conversations that aren't just about the transaction. I remember personal details about their lives. I follow up more frequently, even on small matters. When I'm working with people from a low-context culture, I invest in efficiency and execution. I say what I mean, I deliver what I promise, I focus on getting results.

The best cross-cultural communicators I've known are people who can read the room and adjust their approach accordingly. They can be efficient with people who value efficiency, and they can invest in relationship with people who value relationship.

COMMUNICATION IN CRISIS: WHEN THE STAKES ARE HIGHEST

When a portfolio company hits trouble—covenant breach, unexpected loss of a customer, departure of a key executive, competitive disruption—the communication protocols need to shift immediately. This is not the time for normal communication cadences.

Immediate communication protocols in a crisis: the first thing is to get the facts. You need to understand what happened and why. You need to talk to the borrower, understand their perspective, and figure out what information you need to make decisions.

Then, you need to inform the internal stakeholders. Your portfolio manager needs to know. Your credit committee might need to know. Your trading desk might need to know if you're managing a mark-to-market issue. Your compliance team might need to know if there are regulatory implications.

Then, you need to figure out the external communication strategy. Are you going to ask the borrower to notify other lenders? Are you going to meet with other lenders as a group? What's the timeline for that?

The board call—how to structure difficult conversations: in the midst of a crisis, you might be on calls with the borrower's board, other lenders, or your own credit committee. These are high-stress conversations where everyone is trying to understand what's happening and what's going to happen next.

The way to structure these calls is to have a clear agenda, to present facts cleanly, and to be explicit about what you're asking people to do or decide.

I was on a call with a borrower's board when we discovered that the borrower's largest customer was going to be lost. The board was panicked. People were asking different questions. The meeting was threatening to descend into chaos.

What helped was having one person run the meeting with a clear structure: here's what we know. Here's what we don't know yet. Here's what the customer loss means for the financials. Here's what we're going to do in the next 48 hours to understand the implications. Here's what we're asking the board to authorize us to do.

Just having that structure and sticking to it made the conversation more productive. People still had bad news, but they understood what was happening and what the plan was.

Managing information flow during a restructuring: in a restructuring, information is currency. The question is: what information is you sharing with whom and when?

Generally, you want to share more information rather than less, because information asymmetry creates distrust. If creditors feel like you're hiding information, they'll be more aggressive in their negotiating position.

That said, there are legitimate reasons not to share information: MNPI restrictions, confidentiality agreements with the borrower, strategic reasons to not reveal your hand before you're ready.

The way to manage this tension is to be transparent about what you can and can't share. I've said to lender groups: "There are some things I know that I can't share with you because of legal restrictions. But I can tell you X, Y, and Z." People are usually fine with that, because they understand the constraints.

How to communicate regulatory or compliance concerns: if you've identified a regulatory or compliance issue with a credit—something related to sanctions, FCPA, structuring, reporting—this needs to be communicated clearly and quickly to the right people.

The right people include your compliance function, your legal function, your credit team, and your senior management. The issue is that you can't afford to have ambiguity about what the concern is or how serious it is.

I had a situation where our compliance team flagged a potential sanctions issue with a borrower. The initial communication from compliance was somewhat unclear about how serious the issue was and what the implications were. This led to different people having different understandings of the severity of the problem.

What fixed this was having the compliance team write a clear memo that laid out: here's the issue, here's what we're concerned about, here's what we need the borrower to do, here's what happens if the issue isn't resolved. Once that memo was shared, everyone had the same understanding.

Press management and reputational considerations: if a credit is in default or restructuring, there's often media interest. The question is: how are you going to respond?

Generally, the borrower handles press communications. But as a lender, you might need to think about how this affects your reputation. If the press is reporting that you're being harsh with a struggling borrower, that might affect your relationships with other sponsors or borrowers.

I've been involved in situations where we've had to be very firm with a borrower on commercial terms, but we've also made sure the borrower understood our perspective and had the opportunity to share their perspective with the press. We haven't tried to control the narrative, but we've tried to make sure the narrative is fair and accurate.

The announcement—how to communicate a default or restructuring publicly: if a credit goes into default or if there's a material restructuring, this is usually announced through a press release or a regulatory filing (if the company is public).

The press release should be factual, should explain what happened in plain English, and should outline the path forward. It shouldn't be defensive, and it shouldn't make excuses. It should acknowledge the situation and outline the plan to address it.

WHAT TWENTY YEARS TEACHES ABOUT COMMUNICATION

I've watched hundreds of credit professionals over the last two decades. Some have built incredibly successful, durable careers. Others have topped out or struggled. The pattern is clear: the most successful people are not necessarily the smartest technicians. They're the people who communicate effectively.

The patterns of great communicators in finance share several characteristics. First, they're clear. They can explain complex things in simple terms. They can take a hundred pages of analysis and extract the five key points. They know what matters and what doesn't.

Second, they're honest. They don't sugarcoat. They don't hide. If something is wrong with a credit, they say so. If they don't know something, they say so. This builds trust over time, because people know that when this person says something is fine, it actually is fine.

Third, they're consistent. They treat people with respect in good times and bad times. They follow through on what they say. They maintain relationships over decades. This consistency creates loyalty that translates into repeat business.

Fourth, they listen. They understand the other person's perspective and constraints. They ask questions before they make assumptions. They're genuinely trying to understand, not just trying to persuade.

Fifth, they adapt. They understand that different people need different information presented in different ways. They can be data-focused when someone needs data, and narrative-focused when someone needs narrative. They can be efficient with efficient people and relationship-focused with relationship-focused people.

Why clarity beats complexity every time: I've seen people with incredibly sophisticated views of a deal bury their views in complexity. They want to sound smart, so they use jargon and complex constructions. The problem is that complexity makes your view harder to attack, but it also makes it harder to understand. And if people can't understand your view, they can't act on it.

The best communicators I've known have a discipline about clarity. They've thought deeply about their analysis, which means they can explain it simply. They don't need the complexity because they're not trying to hide uncertainty or weakness. The clarity is a sign of deep thinking, not shallow thinking.

How communication skills compound over a career: when you're young, communication skills matter less because you don't have much credibility anyway. Your senior people are going to check your work. But as you get more senior and more accomplished, communication skills start to matter more and more.

A senior banker who can communicate well and who has a strong network can move deals that a more junior person couldn't move, even if the junior person's analysis is better. A credit committee is more likely to trust a recommendation from someone they know is a strong communicator than from someone they're less familiar with.

The compounding happens because once you've built a reputation as someone who communicates clearly and honestly, that reputation follows you. New clients trust you faster. Credit committees approve your deals more readily. Sponsors want to work with you. The communication skills that let you build that reputation compound over time into a significant career advantage.

Building a personal communication toolkit: different people have different communication strengths. Some people are great written communicators but less comfortable in person. Some people are excellent presenters but struggle with one-on-one negotiations. Some people are great at managing up but less comfortable delivering bad news.

The successful people I've known have identified their strengths and their weaknesses, and they've built a toolkit that compensates for weaknesses while leveraging strengths.

If you're not a natural writer, you invest in becoming a better writer because written communication is so important in this business. If you're not a natural public speaker, you practice public speaking. If you struggle with difficult conversations, you have frameworks and scripts that help you navigate them.

I'm not naturally great at public speaking. I get nervous before presentations. What I've done is develop a routine that helps me get centered before big presentations. I also do a lot of practice, so when I'm presenting to a credit committee, I'm not figuring out what I'm going to say; I'm executing something I've already thought through. That reduces the anxiety and improves the execution.

Mentoring others in communication: this is important because communication is a skill that compounds over a career. If you can help junior people develop strong communication skills early, they're going to benefit for decades.

I try to invest time in coaching junior people on writing, presenting, negotiating, and managing relationships. I give them feedback on their memos. I ask them to present to me before they present to a committee. I do role-plays with them on difficult conversations. It takes time, but it's worth it because it accelerates their development.

One junior analyst I worked with was technically excellent but struggled with written communication. His memos were hard to follow, and he was burying key points. I had him rewrite a section of a memo with me and explained as we went why certain changes improved clarity. Then I asked him to apply those principles to another memo and I reviewed it.

After a few cycles of this, something clicked. His writing got dramatically better. Last I checked, he's now a managing director. I like to think the investment in communication coaching contributed to that trajectory.

CONCLUSION: WHY MASTERING COMMUNICATION CHANGES EVERYTHING

At the beginning of this chapter, I mentioned a deal that failed because of communication failures during the 2009 financial crisis. That deal was instructive, but it was not unique. Throughout my career, I've seen deals succeed or fail not because of the quality of the credit analysis but because of the quality of the communication.

The lesson I've learned over twenty-one years is that communication is not an ancillary skill in leveraged finance. It's a core skill, arguably the core skill. The technical ability to model a deal is necessary but not sufficient. You need to be able to communicate that model to skeptics, to manage stakeholders who have different interests, to deliver bad news without damaging relationships, to negotiate effectively across cultures and time zones.

The practitioners who've built the most successful careers are not the ones who've obsessed over getting the last basis point on their models. They're the people who've invested in becoming great communicators. They've understood that in a business built on relationships and trust, communication is the foundation.

If you're early in your career in leveraged finance, I'd encourage you to invest in communication skills now. Take the time to write clearly. Practice your presentations. Seek feedback from senior people. Think about how you're going to handle difficult conversations before you need to have them. Invest in understanding other people's perspectives and constraints. Build relationships before you need them.

The payoff is substantial. The most secure, most respected, most successful people I've known in this industry are not the ones who've built the most sophisticated models or who've mastered the most complex structures. They're the ones who've mastered the human elements of the business: communication, relationship, trust, and integrity.

Those are the elements that matter most, and they're the elements that you can influence directly, every day, through the choices you make about how you communicate.
