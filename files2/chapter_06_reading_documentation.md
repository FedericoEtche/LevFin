# Chapter 6: Reading a Credit Agreement

## Introduction

A European leveraged finance credit agreement can run to 300 pages. An indenture with its offering memorandum may exceed 400. With the intercreditor agreement, security documents, and ancillary papers added, the complete documentation package routinely exceeds the length of most novels.

Within those hundreds of pages, however, lie the answers to the questions that ultimately matter for any creditor: how much additional debt can be layered ahead of the existing position; how much value can leak out of the credit perimeter before creditors are paid; and what happens, contractually, when the borrower's performance deteriorates.

This chapter is a practical guide to navigating those documents efficiently. The first part addresses the architecture — what a credit agreement and offering memorandum actually contain, section by section. The second part examines the central stylistic distinction between "LMA-style" and "Bond-style" (or "High-Yield style") documentation, and explains how the Bond-style template has come to dominate the European market. The chapter concludes with a roadmap linking each section to the detailed analysis developed in later chapters.

The objective is not to make documentation enjoyable to read; the objective is to make the reader efficient at extracting what matters from documents that are deliberately structured to slow down rapid analysis.

## 6.1 The Architecture of a Credit Agreement

A European leveraged loan credit agreement follows a standard structure, though the specific organization varies between LMA-style and Bond-style documents. Understanding this architecture lets you navigate quickly to the provisions that matter for any particular analysis.

### The Typical Table of Contents

Below is a representative structure for a European leveraged loan credit agreement. The specific clause numbers and section names vary, but the logical flow is consistent:

---

**PART I: INTERPRETATION AND DEFINITIONS**

| Section | Content | Where to Focus |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| 1. Definitions and Interpretation | Hundreds of defined terms | **Critical.** Every substantive provision depends on these definitions. EBITDA, Consolidated Net Income, Permitted Investments—the battles are won and lost here. |
| 2. The Facilities | Description of RCF, Term Loans, their amounts, purposes | Starting point for understanding the capital structure |
| 3. Purpose | Permitted uses for loan proceeds | Usually boilerplate; confirms acquisition financing, refinancing, etc. |
| 4. Conditions Precedent | What must be satisfied before funding | Important for understanding "certain funds" protections |

---

**PART II: UTILISATION AND REPAYMENT**

| Section | Content | Where to Focus |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| 5. Utilisation | Mechanics for drawing loans | Operational; rarely analytically significant |
| 6. Repayment | Scheduled amortization, bullet maturities | Understand the repayment profile |
| 7. Prepayment and Cancellation | Voluntary prepayment rights, mandatory prepayments (asset sales, excess cash flow, change of control) | **Important.** Mandatory prepayment triggers affect cash retention |
| 8. Illegality, etc. | Standard provisions if lending becomes illegal | Boilerplate |

---

**PART III: INTEREST AND FEES**

| Section | Content | Where to Focus |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| 9. Interest | Reference rate (EURIBOR/SOFR), margin, payment dates | Economic terms; margin grid if applicable |
| 10. Fees | Commitment fees, arrangement fees, agency fees | Economics; usually in separate fee letters |
| 11. Tax Gross-Up | Withholding tax protections | Important for cross-border structures |

---

**PART IV: REPRESENTATIONS AND WARRANTIES**

| Section | Content | Where to Focus |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| 12. Representations and Warranties | Borrower's statements about itself—legal status, financial statements accuracy, no litigation, etc. | "Repeating representations" matter most; breach can trigger default |

---

**PART V: COVENANTS**

| Section | Content | Where to Focus |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| 13. Information Undertakings | Financial reporting obligations | Important for monitoring; defines what information lenders receive |
| 14. Financial Covenants | Leverage ratio, interest coverage (if any) | **Critical** if maintenance covenants exist; may be "springing" only |
| 15. General Undertakings | Affirmative covenants (maintain insurance, comply with laws, etc.) | Mostly operational |
| 16. Negative Covenants / Restrictive Covenants | Restrictions on debt, liens, restricted payments, investments, asset sales, etc. | **This is the heart of the document.** Chapters 8-11 analyze these in detail |

---

**PART VI: EVENTS OF DEFAULT**

| Section | Content | Where to Focus |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| 17. Events of Default | Payment default, covenant breach, cross-default, insolvency, etc. | **Critical.** What triggers lender remedies? |
| 18. Acceleration | Lenders' right to accelerate upon default | Thresholds, grace periods, cure rights |

---

**PART VII: CHANGES TO PARTIES**

| Section | Content | Where to Focus |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| 19. Changes to Lenders | Transfer and assignment provisions | Transferability restrictions, white lists, consent requirements |
| 20. Changes to Obligors | Conditions for adding/releasing guarantors | Guarantor coverage tests, release provisions |

---

**PART VIII: ADMINISTRATION**

| Section | Content | Where to Focus |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| 21. The Agent and Security Agent | Role, duties, limitations | Agent has no duty to monitor; important to understand limitations |
| 22. Conduct of Business | Day-to-day administration | Operational |
| 23. Payment Mechanics | How funds flow | Technical |
| 24. Set-off | Lender set-off rights | |
| 25. Notices | How communications work | |
| 26. Calculations and Certificates | How ratios are calculated, compliance certificates | Important for understanding testing mechanics |

---

**PART IX: GOVERNING LAW AND ENFORCEMENT**

| Section | Content | Where to Focus |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| 27. Governing Law | English law (typically) | Affects interpretation and remedies |
| 28. Jurisdiction | Courts with jurisdiction | Usually English courts |
| 29. Amendments and Waivers | Consent thresholds for changes | **Critical.** What requires majority vs. all-lender consent? |

---

**SCHEDULES**

| Schedule | Content | Where to Focus |
|----------|---------|----------------|
| Schedule 1: Original Parties | List of lenders and commitments | Starting point for understanding the syndicate |
| Schedule 2: Conditions Precedent | Documents required for closing | |
| Schedule 3: Mandatory Cost | Rate calculations | Technical |
| Schedule 4: Form of Compliance Certificate | Template for quarterly compliance | |
| Schedule 5: Agreed Security Principles | What security is required and what's excluded | **Critical.** Defines the actual security package |
| Schedule 6: Existing Investments / Debt / Liens | Grandfathered items | Important for understanding "day one" capacity |
| Schedule 7: Restrictive Covenants (Bond-style) | The negative covenant package | **If present, this is the core analytical content** |

---

### Where the Action Is

For most analytical purposes, you'll spend 80% of your time in three areas:

1. **Definitions (Section 1):** The definitions of Consolidated EBITDA, Consolidated Net Income, Permitted Investments, Unrestricted Subsidiary, and similar terms determine how every covenant operates. A loose definition of EBITDA renders even "tight" leverage covenants meaningless.

2. **Negative Covenants (Section 16 or Schedule 7):** These restrictions on borrower actions—limitations on debt, liens, restricted payments, investments, asset sales, mergers—are the substance of credit protection. Later chapters analyze each in detail.

3. **Events of Default and Amendments (Sections 17, 29):** Understanding what triggers lender rights and what thresholds govern changes tells you how protected you really are.

For analytical purposes, the Description of the Notes is where you'll spend most of your time. This section summarizes the indenture covenants in prose form. While the actual indenture governs, the Description of the Notes is far more readable and usually accurate for analytical purposes.

## 6.3 LMA-Style vs. Bond-Style Documentation

Understanding the distinction between LMA-style and Bond-style documentation is essential for navigating European leveraged finance. These are not just formatting differences—they represent fundamentally different approaches to structuring credit protection.

### The Historical Context

**LMA-Style Documentation** traces its origins to the Loan Market Association's recommended forms for leveraged facility agreements. These forms emerged from traditional bank lending, where relationship lenders expected:
- Tight, specific restrictions on borrower actions
- Maintenance-based financial covenants tested quarterly
- Separate, compartmentalized covenants for different types of activities
- Strong ongoing monitoring and consent rights

**Bond-Style Documentation** (also called "High-Yield Style" or "NY-Style") emerged from the U.S. high-yield bond market. This approach reflects the reality of dispersed public bondholders who:
- Cannot coordinate for ongoing monitoring
- Need standardized, self-executing provisions
- Accept incurrence-based tests (triggered only when actions are taken)
- Rely on unified covenant structures that are easier to analyze

### The Key Structural Differences

The table below summarizes the core differences:

| Feature | LMA-Style | Bond-Style (HY-Style) |
|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Governing Law Philosophy** | English law throughout | English law "chassis" with NY-style covenant schedules |
| **Financial Covenants** | Maintenance-based (tested quarterly regardless of actions) | Incurrence-based (tested only when restricted actions taken) |
| **Covenant Architecture** | Decoupled: 7+ separate covenants govern different activities | Unified: Single Restricted Payments covenant covers dividends, investments, junior debt |
| **Builder Basket** | "Acceptable Funding Sources" based on retained cash flow | 50% of Consolidated Net Income (accounting metric) |
| **Investment Restrictions** | Capped investments in non-guarantors | "Free flow of value" to Restricted Subsidiaries |
| **Ratio Debt** | Limited; debt capacity in specific baskets | Unlimited debt subject to coverage or leverage ratios |
| **Documentation Complexity** | Simpler structure but more granular restrictions | More complex definitions but unified architecture |

### The "One Covenant vs. Seven Covenants" Problem

Perhaps the most important structural difference is how restrictions on value leakage are organized.

**In Bond-Style Documentation:**

A single Restricted Payments covenant governs:
- Dividends and distributions to shareholders
- Stock repurchases
- Investments (including in Unrestricted Subsidiaries)
- Prepayments of junior debt

All of these activities draw from shared capacity—the builder basket and various carve-outs. The covenant functions as a unified "dam" holding back value from equity.

**In LMA-Style Documentation:**

These same activities are governed by *separate* covenants:
- Dividends, Distributions, and Shareholder Loan covenant
- Acquisitions covenant
- Joint Ventures covenant
- Loans covenant
- Guarantees covenant
- Unrestricted Subsidiary covenant (if present)
- Junior Debt Prepayments covenant

Each covenant has its own capacity pools. While the Acceptable Funding Sources basket is typically shared across these covenants, the separate capacity under each creates what practitioners call "vector risk"—the ability to extract value through one covenant when another is exhausted.

**Example of Vector Risk:**

Suppose a borrower wants to pay a €50 million dividend but has exhausted its capacity under the Dividends covenant.

Under Bond-Style documentation: The borrower cannot pay the dividend unless it has capacity under the unified Restricted Payments covenant.

Under LMA-Style documentation: The borrower might make a €50 million "Permitted Loan" to an affiliate (using capacity under the separate Loans covenant), which the affiliate then distributes. The economic effect is identical—€50 million leaves the credit group—but the transaction routes around the Dividends restriction.

This arbitrage opportunity is a design feature of decoupled covenants that doesn't exist in unified Bond-style structures.

### The Builder Basket Difference

**LMA-Style (Acceptable Funding Sources):**

Capacity typically builds from:
- Retained Excess Cash Flow (a cash metric: EBITDA minus interest, taxes, capex)
- Equity contributions
- Returns on investments
- Declined mandatory prepayments

The cash-based metric is more conservative—capacity grows only when the company generates actual cash.

**Bond-Style (50% CNI Basket):**

Capacity typically builds from:
- 50% of Consolidated Net Income (an accounting metric)
- Equity contributions
- Returns on investments
- A "starter" amount (increasingly common)
- Declined prepayments (in aggressive versions)

The accounting-based metric can create "phantom capacity." A company with high EBITDA but heavy capital expenditure requirements might show positive Consolidated Net Income while generating little or negative free cash flow. Under Bond-style, this company accumulates dividend capacity; under LMA-style, it might not.

**The Hybrid Approach:**

Modern Bond-style European loans often include *both* a 50% CNI basket *and* a separate "Available Amount" basket based on retained cash flow. This "double basket" approach gives borrowers the higher of two metrics at any given time—flexibility that pure LMA-style doesn't offer.

### How Bond-Style Has Taken Over

Over the past decade, Bond-style documentation has become dominant in the European institutional loan market. Several forces drove this convergence:

**1. Investor Base Evolution**

As CLOs and institutional funds displaced traditional bank lenders, the investor base began to resemble the high-yield bond market—dispersed holders who couldn't coordinate for ongoing monitoring. Documentation evolved to match.

**2. Sponsor Sophistication**

Private equity sponsors, advised by elite law firms, recognized that Bond-style documentation offered greater operational flexibility. Unified covenants, incurrence-based testing, and the 50% CNI builder basket all favor borrower flexibility. Sponsors demanded these terms, and competitive pressure among arranging banks meant they got them.

**3. The "Apples to Apples" Argument**

Many European capital structures include both loans and bonds. Bond-style loan documentation facilitates comparison—analysts can assess the full capital structure using consistent covenant concepts. This analytical convenience reinforced the trend toward Bond-style.

**4. The 2020-2021 Liquidity Surge**

The post-COVID liquidity environment created intense competition for assets. Sponsors extracted increasingly aggressive terms. Documentation that might have seemed aggressive in 2019 became "market" by 2021. The high-water mark of sponsor-friendly documentation was largely Bond-style.

**The Current State (2024-2025):**

Today, a "substantial majority" of European institutional term loans use Bond-style covenant packages. Pure LMA-style documentation persists primarily in:
- Traditional bank club deals
- Smaller transactions without institutional distribution
- Certain jurisdictions and sectors where relationship lending dominates
- Private credit (direct lending) where lenders retain tighter terms

Even deals marketed as "LMA-style" often incorporate Bond-style elements like incurrence covenants and ratio debt provisions, creating hybrid documents.

## 6.4 The Hybrid Governance Model

European Bond-style loans operate under what practitioners call a "hybrid" governance model that creates unique interpretive challenges.

### English Law Chassis, New York Law Engine

The typical structure:

**The Chassis (English Law):** The facilities agreement itself—mechanics of borrowing, interest, repayment, tax gross-ups, security agent role—is governed by English law. This provides:
- Certainty of contract interpretation
- Access to English restructuring tools (Schemes, Part 26A Plans)
- Established insolvency procedures

**The Engine (New York Law Concepts):** The restrictive covenants (negative covenants) are often contained in a separate Schedule that imports "New York style" drafting. Key definitions like EBITDA, Consolidated Net Income, and Restricted Payments follow U.S. high-yield precedent, interpreted through a commercial, borrower-friendly lens.

### The Practical Implication

This hybrid creates a "schizophrenic" document where:

- **Covenant definitions** are interpreted commercially (the "spirit of the deal"), giving borrowers benefit of the doubt on ambiguity
- **Enforcement mechanics** are interpreted literally under English law, providing precision when disputes escalate

**Example—Exit Consents:**

Under New York law (and U.S. indentures), "exit consents"—using majority consent to strip covenants from old bonds, forcing minorities to exchange—are standard practice.

Under English law, the *Assenagon* judgment creates a barrier, ruling that using majority power to destroy minority value is an "abuse of power."

A European loan with Bond-style covenants but English law governing sits at this intersection. Sponsors attempt to navigate it by drafting amendment provisions that explicitly permit covenant stripping—testing the limits of English law while operating within its framework.

## 6.6 A Practical Approach to Documentation Review

Given the volume of documentation, how do you efficiently extract what matters?

### The 30-Minute Triage

For initial assessment of a new credit, this triage process identifies the key issues:

**Step 1: Start with the Summary (5 minutes)**

Read the executive summary or term sheet. Understand:
- What are the facilities?
- What's the capital structure?
- What's the stated leverage?
- Are there financial maintenance covenants?

**Step 2: Check the Financial Covenants (5 minutes)**

If maintenance covenants exist:
- What ratios? What levels?
- What's the cushion to current performance?
- Is there an equity cure?

If "cov-lite" (springing only):
- What triggers the springing test?
- How much RCF can be drawn before testing?

**Step 3: Scan Key Definitions (10 minutes)**

Focus on:
- **Consolidated EBITDA:** What add-backs? Are synergies capped?
- **Consolidated Net Income:** Starting point for builder basket
- **Unrestricted Subsidiary:** How easy to designate?
- **Permitted Investments:** What's the "free flow of value" scope?

**Step 4: Identify the Headline Baskets (5 minutes)**

For each major covenant, note:
- What's the ratio-based capacity?
- What's the fixed basket capacity?
- What's the grower (percentage of EBITDA)?

**Step 5: Flag the Red Flags (5 minutes)**

Look for aggressive features:
- Unlimited EBITDA add-backs
- High grower baskets (>100% of EBITDA)
- Capacity reallocation ("pick your poison")
- Weak J.Crew/Serta protections
- Broad Unrestricted Subsidiary provisions

### The Deep Dive

For credits you'll hold or analyze in detail:

**1. Map the Capacity**

Build a spreadsheet showing:
- Current EBITDA and leverage
- All debt capacity (ratio + baskets)
- All RP capacity (builder + carve-outs)
- All investment capacity
- How these interact

**2. Run Scenarios**

Test the documentation against scenarios:
- What if the company wants to pay a large dividend?
- What if a competitor is available for acquisition?
- What if the sponsor wants to refinance junior debt?
- What if the company deteriorates to stress levels?

**3. Read the Intercreditor Agreement**

For secured credits, understand:
- Payment waterfall
- Enforcement rights
- Release provisions
- Standstill periods

**4. Check Amendment History**

If available, review any amendments since closing:
- What was changed?
- What consent was required?
- What does this signal about borrower behavior?

### What to Look For: The Red Flag Checklist

| Red Flag | What It Means | Where to Find It |
|----------|---------------|------------------|
| Uncapped synergy add-backs | EBITDA can be inflated significantly | EBITDA definition |
| "Grower" baskets >100% of EBITDA | Huge capacity that grows with (manipulated) EBITDA | Debt, RP, Investment provisions |
| "Pick Your Poison" / Capacity Reallocation | Unused capacity can be moved between covenants | RP covenant, Investment covenant |
| Weak Unrestricted Subsidiary definition | Easy to move assets outside the credit group | Unrestricted Subsidiary definition |
| No J.Crew Blocker | Material assets can be dropped to unrestricted subs | Investment covenant, RP covenant |
| No Serta Blocker | Majority lenders can subordinate minorities | Amendment provisions |
| Broad "Permitted Refinancing" | Enables debt layering without new consent | Debt covenant |
| Release of guarantees following loan release | Bondholders lose guarantees if loans are refinanced | Guarantee release provisions |
| Super senior capacity from day one | RCF ahead of term loans in waterfall | Credit Facilities definition, ICA |

## 6.7 The Documentation Evolution: Where We Are Now

The European leveraged finance market has experienced dramatic documentation evolution over the past decade:

### The Pre-2015 Era

Documentation was more conservative:
- Maintenance covenants were standard
- LMA-style structures predominated
- Fixed baskets were modest
- Grower concepts were limited

### The 2015-2019 Erosion

Progressive loosening accelerated:
- "Cov-lite" became the norm
- Bond-style documentation gained dominance
- Builder baskets expanded
- Add-back flexibility increased

### The 2020-2021 Apex

Post-COVID liquidity created peak sponsor leverage:
- Unprecedented flexibility in baskets
- Grower concepts reached new heights
- Portability became common
- Documentation protection reached its nadir

### The 2022-2024 Partial Retrenchment

Rising rates and market stress brought some discipline:
- Add-back caps returned (20-25% typical)
- LMT blockers gained traction
- Investors pushed back on the most aggressive terms
- But the baseline remained far looser than pre-2015

### The Current State (2025)

Documentation remains sponsor-friendly by historical standards, but investors have regained some leverage:

- **Standard features:** Cov-lite (springing only), Bond-style covenants, ratio debt, substantial growers
- **Negotiated features:** Add-back caps, J.Crew/Serta blockers, transferability protections
- **Investor focus areas:** LMT vulnerability, cooperation agreement rights, anti-co-op clause resistance

The documentation you encounter today reflects this evolution. Even "tight" current documentation would have seemed aggressive a decade ago. Context matters—always benchmark against the relevant vintage, not an abstract standard.

## Summary

Reading credit documentation efficiently requires understanding both structure and substance:

1. **Credit agreements follow a standard architecture:** Definitions, covenants, events of default, amendments. Know where to find what you need.

2. **Offering memoranda combine business disclosure with legal terms:** The Description of the Notes section contains the covenant analysis; Risk Factors often reveal real concerns.

3. **LMA-style vs. Bond-style is a fundamental divide:**
   - LMA-style: decoupled covenants, maintenance testing, cash-based builder basket
   - Bond-style: unified covenants, incurrence testing, CNI-based builder basket
   - Bond-style has become dominant in institutional European loans

4. **The hybrid governance model creates interpretive complexity:** English law chassis with NY-style covenant schedules means commercial interpretation of covenants but strict enforcement mechanics.

5. **Efficient review follows a triage process:** Start with summaries, check financial covenants, scan key definitions, identify headline baskets, flag red flags.

6. **Documentation has evolved dramatically:** Today's "tight" is yesterday's "loose." Benchmark against vintage and market context.

The chapters that follow dissect each covenant category in detail. But this architectural understanding is the foundation—knowing where provisions live, how they interact, and what documentation style governs makes the detailed analysis possible.
