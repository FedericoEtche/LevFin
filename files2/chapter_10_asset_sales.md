# Chapter 10: The Asset Sale Covenant

## Introduction

The Asset Sale covenant stands as a fundamental pillar of creditor protection within leveraged finance documentation. Its strategic importance lies not in a blanket prohibition of asset disposals, but in its function as a critical mechanism for controlling the flow of value. This covenant is the primary defense against the piecemeal dismantling of enterprise value that underpins the debt—preventing asset stripping that enriches equity holders at the direct expense of creditors.

At its core, the covenant manages the inherent conflict between a company's need for operational flexibility and a lender's need for security. A healthy business must be able to sell obsolete equipment or dispose of non-core divisions. However, creditors require assurance that proceeds from significant asset sales will be used productively—either to de-lever the company by repaying debt or to strengthen the business through reinvestment—rather than being siphoned off as dividends.

Yet the covenant's protections are not absolute. Its integrity is wholly dependent on the strength of the surrounding covenant ecosystem, particularly the Restricted Payments and Liens provisions, which can render its protections illusory if not carefully scrutinized.

---

## 10.1 The Gateway: What Constitutes an "Asset Sale"?

The covenant only bites if a transaction meets the definition of an "Asset Sale" or "Disposition." If a transaction is carved out of this definition, the borrower is generally free to sell the asset and retain the cash without restriction.

### The General Rule

An Asset Sale generally includes the sale, lease, transfer, or other disposition of any assets of the Restricted Group, including the sale of equity in a Restricted Subsidiary.

### The Critical Carve-Outs (The "Swiss Cheese" Effect)

The power of the "Asset Sale" definition lies not in what it includes, but in what it meticulously *excludes*. These carve-outs permit certain dispositions to occur without triggering the covenant's conditions or proceeds waterfall at all.

**Ordinary Course Dispositions:** Sales of obsolete or worn-out equipment, inventory sales, and other dispositions in the ordinary course of business.

**Intercompany Sales:** Dispositions of assets between the issuer and its Restricted Subsidiaries. While these keep assets within the credit group, watch for transfers to non-guarantor subsidiaries that create structural subordination.

**De Minimis Thresholds:** Sales below a specified fair market value (often structured as the greater of a fixed amount or percentage of EBITDA) are ignored entirely.

**Non-Core/Obsolete Assets:** Broad exclusions for assets "no longer used or useful" or "not core" allow issuers to dispose of potentially valuable assets without covenant oversight.

**The Most Consequential Carve-Out:** Dispositions structured as a Restricted Payment or Permitted Investment. This single exception acts as a gateway, allowing valuable assets to be moved outside the Restricted Group entirely. By classifying an asset transfer as an "investment," a company can exploit capacity under the Restricted Payments covenant to execute a "drop-down" transaction—effectively bypassing the entire proceeds waterfall.

**Unrestricted Subsidiary Sales:** The sale of equity in an Unrestricted Subsidiary is often excluded from the definition. This allows a sponsor to designate a subsidiary as Unrestricted, sell it to a third party, and keep the cash at the parent level without paying down debt.

## 10.2 The Quality Controls: Consideration Requirements

If a transaction *is* an Asset Sale, the issuer must typically satisfy two conditions:

### Fair Market Value

The company must receive consideration at least equal to fair market value for any asset sold. This foundational protection prevents transfers to affiliates or other parties at a discount.

However, FMV is usually determined in "good faith" by the Board or a responsible officer, leaving significant room for subjectivity. There is rarely an independent valuation requirement.

### The 75% Cash Rule

At least 75% of the consideration must be in the form of cash or cash equivalents. This ensures the company receives liquid proceeds that can be tracked and controlled by the subsequent proceeds waterfall, rather than illiquid securities or hard-to-value assets.

### The "Deemed Cash" Loophole

The 75% rule is heavily diluted by what the document "deems" to be cash. Aggressive definitions allow the following to count toward the 75% bucket:

**Assumed Liabilities:** If the buyer assumes debt of the seller, it counts as cash. This can include trade payables, pension obligations, and other liabilities—none of which represent actual liquidity.

**Designated Non-Cash Consideration:** A specific basket (often capped at a percentage of EBITDA) allows the borrower to accept "magic beans"—illiquid securities, promissory notes, or other non-cash assets—and treat them as cash for compliance purposes. This erodes the liquidity protection the covenant was designed to provide.

**Securities Converted to Cash:** Securities received that are converted into cash within a specified period (often 180 days) may be deemed cash from inception.

## 10.3 The Proceeds Waterfall: Where the Money Goes

The proceeds application provision is the covenant's ultimate enforcement mechanism, governing how "Net Cash Proceeds" from a permitted Asset Sale must be used within a specified timeframe (typically 365 or 455 days). Any proceeds not applied according to this waterfall become "Excess Proceeds" and trigger a mandatory offer to creditors.

### Stage 1: Repay Effectively Senior Debt

The issuer is first permitted to use proceeds to repay debt that is "effectively senior" to the notes. In a typical capital structure with high-yield bonds, this means the issuer will first comply with mandatory prepayment obligations of its senior secured credit facilities.

This provision acknowledges the payment priority of secured lenders—but also means bondholders watch asset value flow to bank lenders before receiving any benefit.

### Stage 2: Reinvest in the Business

The issuer has the option to reinvest proceeds back into the business through acquiring "Additional Assets," making capital expenditures, or investing in other long-term assets.

**The Asset Quality Risk:** Scrutinize whether proceeds from long-term assets (like a factory) can be reinvested into current assets (like inventory). If permitted, this converts "hard" collateral into "soft" working capital that can leak out of the credit box more easily.

**The Reinvestment Period:** Typically 365 days, often extendable to 455 days if a binding commitment exists. Some aggressive deals extend this to 545 days or longer.

### Stage 3: Make an Offer to Creditors

Any Net Cash Proceeds not used in Stages 1 or 2 within the specified period become "Excess Proceeds." The issuer must then make an "Asset Sale Offer" to purchase the notes (and often other pari passu debt) at a price of 101% of principal amount, plus accrued interest.

**The Accumulation Threshold:** The offer is typically only required once Excess Proceeds exceed a threshold (€25-50 million). Proceeds below this threshold may never trigger an offer.

**The Dutch Auction Dynamic:** In loans, borrowers may use proceeds to buy back debt at a discount via Dutch Auction. Bonds typically require a par (or 101%) offer—but watch for provisions allowing discounted buybacks.

## 10.4 Net Cash Proceeds: The Quantum Subject to the Waterfall

The "Net Cash Proceeds" definition determines the actual amount subject to the application waterfall. It starts with gross cash proceeds and allows a series of deductions:

- Reasonable transaction fees and expenses
- Taxes paid or payable as a result of the sale
- Amounts placed in escrow (pending release)
- Liabilities directly associated with the sold asset that are retained by the issuer

Each deduction reduces the quantum flowing through the waterfall. Aggressive definitions can materially reduce what creditors ultimately see.

## 10.5 The Primary Escape Hatch: Covenant Interaction

The Asset Sale covenant does not operate in isolation. Its effectiveness is wholly dependent on interconnected provisions, particularly the Restricted Payments and Liens covenants. Sophisticated issuers and sponsors lawfully circumvent the proceeds waterfall by exploiting this interplay.

### The J. Crew Trap Door

The most notorious method for bypassing the Asset Sale covenant hinges on the carve-out for dispositions that qualify as a Permitted Investment:

1. **Asset Transfer as Investment:** The company uses investment capacity under the Restricted Payments covenant to transfer valuable assets (IP, a business division) to a newly created or existing Unrestricted Subsidiary.

2. **Covenant Bypass:** Because the transfer is classified as a Permitted Investment, it is explicitly excluded from the "Asset Sale" definition. The transaction is not subject to fair market value requirements, 75% cash consideration, or the proceeds waterfall.

3. **Value Leakage:** Once transferred, assets are legally owned by the Unrestricted Subsidiary and removed from the Restricted Group. They are free from all covenants and removed from the creditors' collateral package. The assets can then be pledged to secure new, structurally senior debt—priming existing creditors.

### The "Solely for the Purpose" Loophole

Some covenants explicitly state that if proceeds are used to make a Restricted Payment (dividend) or Permitted Investment, the application of proceeds waterfall *does not apply*. This effectively short-circuits the covenant: the borrower sells an asset and immediately dividends the cash, bypassing any requirement to pay down debt—provided they have RP capacity.

## 10.6 Leverage-Based Step-Downs: The Value Leakage Mechanism

A significant modern innovation is the "step-down" provision, creating a major exception to the proceeds waterfall.

### The Mechanic

If the issuer's leverage ratio is below a specified threshold at the time of an asset sale, a certain portion of net proceeds is completely exempted from the application of proceeds rules.

For example:
- Above 4.0x leverage: 100% of proceeds subject to waterfall
- Below 4.0x leverage: 50% of proceeds subject to waterfall
- Below 3.0x leverage: 0% of proceeds subject to waterfall

### The Two Forms

**Form A (Highly Aggressive):** The percentage applies to *gross* proceeds. If 50% is exempted, half of all proceeds escape the waterfall immediately.

**Form B (Less Aggressive):** The percentage applies to *excess* proceeds after reinvestment. Only proceeds that would otherwise trigger an Asset Sale Offer are reduced.

### The Destination of Released Proceeds

Proceeds released by step-downs are often termed "Declined Proceeds" or "Retained Asset Sale Proceeds" and can be added to the Restricted Payments Builder Basket—allowing the cash to be dividended to sponsors.

This represents contingent subordination of creditor interests to equity interests, triggered by a leverage metric that can itself be manipulated through aggressive EBITDA adjustments. It is a backdoor dividend financed by asset disposals.

### The Net Leverage Gaming

Many step-downs are based on Net Leverage (Debt minus Cash). If a borrower sells an asset for cash, Net Leverage mathematically drops immediately because cash is netted against debt. This immediate drop can trigger the step-down (to 0% sweep), allowing the borrower to keep the cash—which they can then dividend out (raising leverage back up).

This circularity undermines the protection of the sweep entirely.

## 10.7 The Excess Proceeds Offer: Mechanics and Manipulation

### The Offer Process

Once Excess Proceeds exceed the accumulation threshold, the issuer must offer to repurchase notes at 101% of principal plus accrued interest. Bondholders may accept or reject the offer.

### Declined Proceeds

If bondholders reject the offer (perhaps because bonds are trading above 101%), those "Declined Proceeds" often increase the basket for dividends—permanently converting creditor collateral into equity value.

This creates a perverse dynamic: if the credit is performing well (bonds trading above par), bondholders rationally reject the offer, and proceeds become available for dividends. The covenant's protection is strongest precisely when it's least needed.

### Permissive Repayment Language

Watch for provisions allowing the issuer to use Asset Sale proceeds to repay other pari passu unsecured debt or even junior lien debt, rather than making a pro rata offer to bondholders. In an unsecured bond, this could result in proceeds being used to repay other unsecured creditors at the issuer's discretion.

## 10.8 Case Studies: The Covenant in Distress

### Adler Group: A Covenant Vacuum

Adler's proposed terms deliberately omitted any Asset Sale covenant. This was not an oversight—it signaled that while the company intended to liquidate assets, bondholders had no contractual claim on the proceeds.

The direct consequence was a complete lack of clear path to repayment. The company's stated intention to pursue asset sales was divorced from any obligation to use proceeds to repay bonds.

In early 2024, the UK Court of Appeal ruled against Adler's restructuring plan, finding that using asset sale proceeds to repay six series of pari passu notes sequentially by maturity date violated the fundamental principle of equal treatment.

### Tele Columbus: Tightening in Distress

As part of its 2023 amend-and-extend transaction, the Asset Sale covenant was substantially *tightened*—not weakened. In exchange for extending maturities, creditors successfully negotiated for removal of reinvestment rights. The amended covenant required that proceeds from all material asset dispositions be applied directly to pro rata repayment of debt.

This demonstrates that in distress, the covenant becomes a central negotiating point—and creditors with leverage can demand enhanced protections.

## Summary: The Practitioner's Diligence Checklist

**Definition Analysis:**
- Does "Asset Sale" contain a carve-out for dispositions structured as Restricted Payments or Permitted Investments? (The J. Crew gateway)
- Are Unrestricted Subsidiary sales excluded from the definition?
- How broad are the "ordinary course" and "non-core asset" exclusions?

**Consideration Requirements:**
- What can be "deemed" cash? Assumed liabilities? Designated non-cash consideration?
- Is there a cap on non-cash consideration?

**Proceeds Waterfall:**
- Is the issuer required to offer Excess Proceeds to notes at par, or can other pari passu debt be repaid first?
- How long is the reinvestment period?
- Are reinvestment criteria limited to core business assets?

**Step-Down Analysis:**
- Is there a leverage-based step-down?
- What triggers it? Net leverage or gross leverage?
- Does Form A (gross proceeds) or Form B (excess proceeds) apply?
- Where do released proceeds go? (Builder Basket? General corporate purposes?)

**Covenant Ecosystem:**
- How do weak Investment, Restricted Payment, and Unrestricted Subsidiary provisions interact with and undermine the Asset Sale covenant?

**Absence of Protection:**
- Is there a robust Asset Sale covenant at all? Complete absence is a major red flag.
