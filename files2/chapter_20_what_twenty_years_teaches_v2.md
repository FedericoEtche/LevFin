# Chapter 20: What Twenty Years Teaches

*This chapter is different from the others. The preceding pages gave you frameworks, mechanics, definitions. This one is personal. Take what resonates; leave the rest behind.*

---

## The Weight of It

I've been trying to figure out how to write this chapter for a while now, and I think the honest place to start is with a confession: most of this career has been hard. Not hard in the way that sounds impressive at dinner parties — "oh, the hours, the pressure, you know how it is" — but hard in the way that accumulates quietly over years until you look up and realize you're tired in a way that sleep doesn't fix.

If I had to put a number on it, I'd say the ratio is something like 90% frustration to 10% genuine satisfaction. That sounds bleak, and maybe it is, but I don't think my experience is unusual. The deals that go nowhere. The analysis that nobody reads. The politics that have nothing to do with the quality of your work. The constant sense that whatever you just accomplished has already been forgotten, and the next thing is already overdue.

And then, every once in a while, something clicks. A deal closes and you played a real part in it. A piece of analysis turns out to matter. Someone you respect notices what you did. For a few days, you feel like maybe this was all worth it.

Then the feeling fades, and you're back on the treadmill.

I don't say this to discourage anyone. I say it because I wish someone had told me earlier that this is normal — that the highlight reel you see from the outside has very little to do with the daily experience of doing this work. The people who last in this industry aren't the ones who feel successful all the time. They're the ones who've made peace with the fact that they won't.

## Where This Started

I should tell you where I come from, because it explains something about why I ended up here and why I stayed.

I grew up in a place where wearing a suit meant you'd made it to the other side. My father came home every evening dressed impeccably — the suit pressed, the tie straight, his hair combed back in that way that meant seriousness and success. I was young, and I didn't understand what he did exactly, but I understood what it represented. He wasn't struggling. He had crossed over to the place where life was more stable, more dignified, more safe.

That image stayed with me. The suit became a symbol of something I wanted, even before I knew what that something was.

And then, like half the people in finance, I watched Wall Street — the original one, from 1987 — and I wanted to be Gordon Gekko. I know how that sounds. It's a cliché, the kind of origin story that makes people roll their eyes. But clichés exist because they're true often enough, and this one was true for me. The skyline, the confidence, the sense of operating at the center of something important. I wanted that. I wanted it badly.

It took me a long time to understand that the image of success and the experience of success are very different things. The skyline is real, but so is the anxiety. The deals are real, but so is the emptiness that sometimes follows them. I got what I thought I wanted, more or less, and found that it felt different from the inside than it looked from the outside.

I don't regret the path. But I'm less naive about it now.

## No Map, No Shortcuts

The thing about my path is that there was no map. I had no connections in the industry, no family friends who could make introductions, no university network that opened doors automatically. I came from somewhere that didn't produce investment bankers, and nothing about my background suggested I belonged in this world.

I was also, and still am, not good at the social part. I don't mean I'm shy, exactly. I mean that I find the normal rhythms of professional socializing genuinely difficult. The small talk at conferences, the dinners where you're supposed to be "building relationships," the instinct for knowing what to say and when to say it — none of that comes naturally to me. I've learned to manage it over the years, but it still costs me energy that seems to come free to other people.

In an industry that runs on relationships, this is a significant handicap. I won't pretend otherwise.

What I had instead was a willingness to learn things the hard way. I didn't come up through a bulge bracket bank where someone would teach me the right way to do things. I figured it out by doing it wrong, and then doing it slightly less wrong, and then eventually — after more iterations than I'd like to admit — doing it more or less right. Trial and error, heavy on the error.

This path has one real advantage: when you learn something the hard way, you understand it deeply. You know not just the what but the why, because you've lived through the consequences of getting it wrong. The knowledge is yours in a way that borrowed knowledge never is.

But the costs are real too. The career progression is slower. The compensation lags. The credential gap never fully closes, no matter how much you accomplish. And there's a psychological weight to it — the constant low-level sense of having to prove yourself, of being one mistake away from being exposed as someone who doesn't quite belong. That feeling fades over time, but I'm not sure it ever disappears completely.

## The Territory Nobody Talks About

There's something I want to say about mental health, because it matters, and because almost nobody in finance books talks about it honestly.

There were periods in my career when I wasn't okay. Not in a dramatic, visible way — I kept working, kept functioning, kept showing up. But underneath that, something was wrong. The weight of the hours, the uncertainty about whether any of it meant anything, the way the pressure never seemed to let up no matter how much I accomplished. I pushed myself into territory that, looking back, I shouldn't have entered without support.

I told myself it was temporary. After this deal closes. After this quarter ends. After I get to the next level. But the pressure doesn't actually end; it just changes shape. And I kept pushing, because that's what you do, because admitting you're struggling feels like weakness in an industry that valorizes endurance.

I'm not going to tell you the details, because they're mine, but I want to say clearly: if you're reading this and you recognize something of yourself in what I'm describing — the exhaustion that doesn't lift, the anxiety that's become background noise, the sense that you're holding things together through willpower alone — please get help earlier than I did. Talk to someone. A professional, a friend, anyone. This industry will take everything you're willing to give it, and it has no internal mechanism to tell you when you've given too much. That limit has to come from you, and sometimes you need help to find it.

The people I admire most in this industry are not the ones who sacrificed everything. They're the ones who figured out how to be genuinely good at what they do without losing themselves in the process. I'm still learning how to do that.

## The Uncomfortable Truth About Technical Skills

I built my career on understanding things. Reading the documents that others skimmed. Building models that actually worked. Knowing the mechanics, the definitions, the why behind the how. This book is a product of that orientation — hundreds of pages on covenants and structures and the detailed machinery of leveraged finance.

And here's the uncomfortable part: I'm not sure that's what matters most anymore.

When I started, deep technical knowledge was genuinely rare. If you understood how covenants actually worked, if you could read documentation and see what others missed, that was a real advantage. You could build a career on being the person who actually knew things.

But the landscape has shifted. Technical knowledge is more accessible now. AI tools are making it even more so. The things that took me years to learn — a junior analyst can get most of the way there with the right resources and a few months of focused effort.

What remains scarce is something else: judgment, relationships, the ability to influence people, presence in a room. The soft skills, as they're called, though there's nothing soft about how hard they are to develop. These are the things that move careers, especially into senior positions. And they're the things I've never been naturally good at.

I don't regret my path. The understanding I've built is real, and it has value — this book is proof of that. But if I'm being honest about what the industry rewards, about what makes the difference between a solid career and an exceptional one, it's not the technical mastery. It's the other stuff.

## The Advice I'd Give (But Didn't Follow)

If I could talk to my younger self — the version who had some technical skills but hadn't yet figured out the larger game — there are things I'd want to say. They're things I learned late, and imperfectly, and I'm offering them knowing that advice is easier to give than to follow.

I'd tell him to stop eating lunch at his desk. Every meal alone is a missed opportunity to build a relationship, and relationships compound over time in ways that technical work doesn't. The document will still be there in an hour. The chance to connect with someone might not be.

I'd tell him to think about what he's known for. Not in a self-promotional way, but strategically. When people think of you, what comes to mind? What makes them seek you out rather than someone else? I spent too many years being the person who chased opportunities; the better position is being the person opportunities come looking for.

I'd tell him that technical skills, no matter how good, are ultimately replaceable. There's always someone who can do the analysis, run the model, read the document. What's harder to replace is the impact you have on people — the trust you build, the sense that working with you makes things better, the relationships that accumulate over a career.

I'm giving advice about things that don't come naturally to me. That's the point. I learned these lessons the hard way, and later than I should have, and I'm still not particularly good at applying them. But I've seen enough to know they're true.

## The Thing That Kept Me Going

So why did I spend twenty years building technical mastery in an industry that increasingly rewards other things? Why write a book about covenants and documentation when I could have been cultivating relationships instead?

The honest answer is that I couldn't help it.

Some people are motivated by the deals themselves, by the money, by the status and recognition. I'm not immune to those things — I'd be lying if I said I was. But they're not what actually gets me out of bed in the morning.

What drives me is understanding. The holy grail, for me, has always been the same thing: getting to the bottom of how something works. The fundamentals. The core logic. The why underneath the how.

I wanted to understand leveraged finance the way a watchmaker understands a watch — not just how to operate it, but how all the pieces fit together, why it works the way it does, what happens when you change one thing and not another. That pursuit has been the through-line of my career, even when it wasn't the most strategic use of my time.

This book is an attempt to write down what I've learned before it fades. Markets evolve, memory decays, and the understanding you've built over decades can slip away faster than you'd think if you don't capture it somehow. I'm not sure I've gotten all the way to the grail — you never do, really — but I think I've gotten close enough that it's worth sharing.

---

## The Treadmill and the View

One last thing, and then I'll let you go.

I've worked with successful people over the years. People whose names you might recognize, who've done deals that made the papers, who have the titles and the compensation to show for it. And almost none of them feel like they've arrived. Almost all of them are still chasing something — the next transaction, the next recognition, the next proof that they matter.

The treadmill doesn't stop. There's no level you reach where you finally feel satisfied and can relax into your success. If you're waiting for that moment, you'll wait forever.

What I've learned — slowly, imperfectly, and with plenty of backsliding — is that the only sustainable path is to find meaning in the work itself. Not in the destination, because there isn't one. In the process. In the craft. In the understanding you build along the way.

There will always be someone doing better than you. This is guaranteed. There will also be someone doing worse, probably much worse, than you can imagine from where you're standing. The comparison game has no winner, and playing it will make you miserable.

The only approach I've found that works is to define success on your own terms. Did I learn something today? Did I do work I can be proud of? Did I treat people decently? Some days the answer is yes. Many days it isn't. That's the job.

---

After twenty years, I know how covenants work. I know how deals get structured and why they sometimes fall apart. I know the games that get played and the moves available on both sides of the table. That's what the rest of this book is about.

But I also know some things that don't fit neatly into chapters: that the people who last aren't always the smartest, that relationships matter more than analysis in the long run, that the industry will take whatever you're willing to give and ask for more, that success looks different from the inside than from the outside.

And I know that you can get through the hard parts — the frustration, the doubt, the dark stretches — but you shouldn't try to do it alone.

I don't know if this chapter belongs in a book about leveraged finance. It's not like the others. But it's true, and after twenty years, that counts for something.

Good luck.
