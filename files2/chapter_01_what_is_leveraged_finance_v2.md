# Chapter 1: What Is Leveraged Finance?

## 1.1 The Milken Revolution

Leveraged finance is a broad and at times confusing subject. It encompasses a significant number of distinct topics — capital structures, credit agreements, indentures, covenants, intercreditor arrangements — each substantial enough to fill a textbook on its own.

Strip away the complexity, however, and at its core leveraged finance is simply debt. Corporate debt. More specifically, corporate debt provided to companies with a credit rating below investment grade.

That is the definition. Everything else is detail.

A brief history is useful, because the existence of this market is not self-evident. For most of the twentieth century, it was not obvious that below-investment-grade companies should be able to borrow in the capital markets at all.

Before the 1980s, the bond market was a restricted environment. Blue-chip corporations issued bonds. Governments issued bonds. A company with a credit rating below BBB- from Standard & Poor's or Moody's was effectively shut out of the capital markets. Such a company could negotiate with its bank for a loan, ask its shareholders for additional equity, or do without. Public debt was not an option.

The implicit assumption was that companies with lower credit ratings were not creditworthy. They were "junk." Lending to them was treated as imprudent on its face.

Michael Milken disagreed with that assumption.

Milken, a banker at Drexel Burnham Lambert, identified an analytical gap that the broader market had overlooked: below-investment-grade companies were not necessarily *bad* credits. They were *different* credits, requiring different analysis and different pricing. If the risks were properly understood and properly compensated, bonds issued by such companies could deliver attractive risk-adjusted returns. The higher yields paid to investors more than offset the higher default rates observed in the underlying portfolio. Empirically, the math worked.

Milken's biography — his innovation, his eventual conviction, his pardon — sits outside the scope of this book. What matters for present purposes is the structural change he produced. He created a market where none had previously existed. He demonstrated that capital could flow to companies that the prevailing orthodoxy had treated as uninvestable.

The instruments he pioneered were called "junk bonds" — bonds with the "junk" label sitting prominently in the name. The terminology was imprecise marketing, but the market did not care. Investors searching for yield bought the product. Companies previously locked out of the capital markets suddenly had an alternative funding channel. Drexel and Milken became, for a period, the most important financing platform in the United States.

Over time, the terminology evolved. "Junk bonds" became "high-yield bonds." Same product, less pejorative branding. As loans subsequently developed as an alternative to bonds for the same population of borrowers, the umbrella term "leveraged finance" emerged to describe the entire asset class.

That is where the terminology stands today.

## 1.2 A Working Definition

So what, precisely, qualifies as leveraged finance?

**Leveraged finance is corporate debt — whether bond or loan — provided to companies with credit ratings below investment grade.**

In practice, this means companies rated between BB+ and B- by Standard & Poor's or Moody's. Fitch is also active in the market, but for most purposes a transaction needs at least one rating from S&P or Moody's to be marketed efficiently to institutional investors.

The ratings hierarchy is set out below:

| S&P Rating | Moody's Rating | What It Means |
|------------|----------------|---------------|
| BBB- and above | Baa3 and above | Investment Grade |
| BB+ | Ba1 | Top of leveraged finance |
| BB | Ba2 | Leveraged finance |
| BB- | Ba3 | Leveraged finance |
| B+ | B1 | Leveraged finance |
| B | B2 | Leveraged finance |
| B- | B3 | Bottom of typical leveraged finance |
| CCC+ and below | Caa1 and below | Distressed territory |

The BB+ to B- range constitutes the heartland of the market. Below B-, transactions move into distressed territory, where the analytical question is no longer "will the borrower pay on time?" but "will the borrower pay at all?" Above BB+, transactions approach investment grade, where different conventions, different investors, and different pricing dynamics apply.

A company's rating is not permanent. Companies are upgraded. Companies are downgraded. A BB credit can migrate to investment grade if performance improves. An investment-grade credit can migrate into leveraged finance if performance deteriorates — these transitions are referred to as "fallen angels." The boundary between the two worlds is crossed regularly in both directions.

## 1.3 The Instruments

The leveraged finance market contains a diverse set of debt instruments that appear, in various combinations, in company capital structures. Each instrument is examined in detail in subsequent chapters; the orientation that follows is intended only as a reference map.

**Senior Secured Debt** sits at the top of the capital structure with first claim on the borrower's assets:

- **Leveraged loans** (most commonly issued as a "Term Loan B" or "TLB") — floating-rate debt, typically held by CLOs and credit funds.
- **Senior secured notes** — high-yield bonds backed by collateral.
- **Senior secured floating rate notes** — economically similar to senior secured notes but with a floating coupon rather than a fixed coupon.

**Subordinated Debt** ranks junior to senior secured debt and is compensated for the additional risk through higher yields:

- **Second lien loans and notes** — secured against the same collateral as the senior debt, but paid only after the first lien is satisfied in any waterfall.
- **Senior notes** — confusingly named, since they are typically unsecured and "senior" only relative to equity.
- **Senior subordinated notes** — explicitly junior to senior debt by contractual subordination.
- **Mezzanine** — hybrid instruments occupying the space between senior debt and equity, typically privately negotiated.
- **Holdco PIK notes** — debt issued at a holding company above the operating restricted group, often paying interest "in kind" (additional debt) rather than cash.

**Unitranche debt** is a more recent development: a single-tranche facility that blends senior and subordinated economic characteristics, typically provided by direct lenders rather than the syndicated market.

The distinctions between secured and unsecured debt, between first lien and second lien, and between contractual subordination and structural subordination are addressed in detail in later chapters. At this stage, the relevant point is that "leveraged finance" encompasses a spectrum of instruments, each with a distinct risk profile, pricing, and position in the capital structure.

## 1.4 A Note on Scope

A further distinction is necessary: the difference between the **mid-market** and **traditional leveraged finance**.

The mid-market involves smaller companies, smaller deal sizes, and frequently direct lending in place of syndicated transactions. Documentation is generally simpler. Investor bases are concentrated. Lending relationships are more bilateral. A representative mid-market transaction might involve a single direct lender providing a €100 million unitranche facility to a company with €20 million of EBITDA.

Traditional leveraged finance — the focus of this book — involves larger transactions: broadly syndicated loans and publicly traded high-yield bonds, distributed to institutional investor bases that include CLOs, credit funds, and asset managers. Deal sizes range from the low hundreds of millions into the billions of dollars or euros. Documentation is standardised enough to permit liquid secondary trading, yet bespoke enough to require careful analysis on each transaction.

The reason for focusing on traditional leveraged finance is that it is the first mover for innovation. The structures, the covenant technology, and the most consequential developments — both constructive and destructive — emerge first in the large-cap space and subsequently filter down to smaller deals. Understanding the trajectory of the broader market therefore requires close attention to what occurs in traditional leveraged finance.

The word "innovation" is used here with reservations. Not all developments in market practice serve all stakeholders equally, and several techniques discussed in later chapters were developed expressly to redistribute value from one constituency to another.

## 1.5 The Question of How Much Debt

A natural question is how market participants determine the appropriate amount and type of debt for any given company.

The answer, perhaps surprisingly, is that there is no formula.

**There are no absolute truths in debt structuring.**

What works for one company may not work for another. What worked for a particular company last year may not work for that same company today. The "optimal" capital structure that emerges from a financial model rarely coincides with what the company actually issues.

Debt structuring is a craft rather than a calculation. The financial model matters, certainly. But the model is only one input. Equally important are market conditions, prevailing investor sentiment, the identity and appetite of investors with capital available to deploy at a given moment, the instruments those investors are prepared to buy, and the terms they will accept.

The answer to "how much debt?" is therefore never purely analytical. It is the negotiated product of what a company needs and what the market will provide on the day the transaction is brought.

This dynamic — the interaction between issuer requirements and market capacity — is a recurring theme throughout the book. It explains why apparently similar companies finance themselves with materially different structures, why deal terms tighten and loosen across cycles, and why the same covenant package can be either lender-friendly or sponsor-friendly depending on when it was negotiated.

## 1.6 The Fruit Market Analogy

A simple analogy helps illustrate how the capital markets are organised.

Consider a fresh fruit market with many vendors. Some sell every type of fruit; others specialise in a single product. The more important distinction, however, is volume. Some vendors operate as corner shops, willing to sell a customer five bananas. Others operate as wholesalers — their business model requires bulk orders, and they will not transact in small quantities.

A family shopper looking for five bananas and five apples needs to find a vendor that (a) sells both fruits and (b) is willing to sell at retail volumes. Approaching a wholesaler with this request is futile. The wholesaler is not equipped, operationally or commercially, to fill a five-piece order.

A restaurant owner buying enough fruit to run a service for a day needs a different vendor. The corner shop does not stock the volumes required. The right counterparty is the vendor with delivery trucks and crates stacked in a warehouse.

The owner of a national supermarket chain operates differently again. That buyer walks past the fruit stands entirely, ending up at a desk where no fruit is even visible — only a buyer with a computer, taking orders for scheduled delivery to multiple regional distribution centres.

This stratification mirrors the capital markets exactly.

A consumer borrowing $500 to make a purchase swipes a credit card and obtains the funds instantly. A consumer borrowing $100,000 to purchase a house provides documentation to a bank and waits a number of weeks for the loan to be underwritten. A multinational corporation raising $10 billion to fund a data centre opens conversations with multiple investment banks, runs investor meetings, and assesses which pools of capital are accessible at the relevant moment, on what terms. The timeline extends over months rather than seconds.

Different sizes of capital requirement are served by different financing channels, with different counterparties, different processes, and different documentation. Leveraged finance is one such channel, dedicated specifically to companies with credit ratings in the BB+ to B- range. When such companies require capital, they approach a defined set of intermediaries that have the expertise and the investor relationships to deliver debt to that segment of the market on terms it will accept.

## 1.7 What Makes Leveraged Finance Different?

What distinguishes leveraged finance from, for example, investment-grade debt?

The answer is captured in a single word: **leverage**.

Consider an individual earning $100,000 per month with no existing debt who wishes to borrow $10,000. That request is trivial. A pre-approved facility likely already exists in the borrower's banking app, accessible with a few taps and no questions asked.

The same individual asking to borrow $2 million — almost twice annual gross income — provokes a different response. The bank will ask for documentation, conduct affordability analysis, and likely require security.

The same individual asking to borrow $10 million produces yet another response. The lender will conduct detailed underwriting. A spreadsheet will be built. Multiple scenarios will be modelled. Stress tests will be run against the borrower's income, asset base, and liquidity. The lender will examine what happens if income drops, if expenses rise, if asset values fall. Security will almost certainly be required, along with restrictive covenants — contractual prohibitions on what the borrower can do with the borrowed funds, the underlying assets, or other capital decisions.

The third scenario captures the essence of leveraged finance. The absolute size of the loan is not what matters. What matters is **the size of the loan relative to the borrower's earnings and asset base**.

A company raising €500 million can be conservatively financed or aggressively leveraged depending entirely on whether that company generates €50 million or €500 million of EBITDA. The ratio is the relevant measure.

Because the ratios are higher in leveraged finance — because lenders are extending credit at multiples that approach the borrower's capacity to service the debt — lenders demand more protection. More analysis. More covenants. More security. More ongoing control over capital decisions.

For institutions providing capital in this market, prudent underwriting requires reasonable confidence that the funds will be repaid, "one way or another." The set of mechanisms by which that confidence is maintained — covenants, security, intercreditor priority, formal restructuring tools — constitutes the bulk of the technical material in this book.

## 1.8 The Cast of Characters

Leveraged finance transactions involve a recurring set of participants. Each plays a distinct role and operates under distinct economic incentives.

**Private Equity Sponsors**

The most active users of leveraged finance are private equity firms — referred to throughout this book as "sponsors." Sponsors raise capital from institutional investors (pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, endowments, family offices, insurance companies) and deploy that capital to acquire companies. The purchase price for each acquisition is funded by a combination of equity (typically 30–50% of enterprise value) and debt (the balance). That debt is leveraged finance.

The largest sponsors are well known: Blackstone, KKR, Apollo, Carlyle, CVC, EQT, Permira. Several hundred additional firms operate in the mid-market and large-cap segments. The sponsor business model is structurally dependent on leverage: debt magnifies equity returns when investments perform, and magnifies losses when they do not.

**Corporate Issuers**

Not every leveraged finance transaction involves a private equity sponsor. Many companies access leveraged loans and high-yield bonds directly to fund acquisitions, refinance existing debt, or finance capital expenditure. Such issuers may be family-owned businesses, listed companies that happen to carry sub-investment-grade ratings, or former sponsor-owned companies that have subsequently been sold to strategic buyers or floated on a public exchange.

**Arranging Banks**

Investment banks occupy the centre of the market. They advise on capital structures, underwrite the debt (committing to take it onto their own balance sheets temporarily), and syndicate it to institutional investors. The principal European arrangers include JP Morgan, Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Bank of America, Deutsche Bank, Barclays, BNP Paribas, and a handful of others.

Arrangers are compensated through underwriting and syndication fees. Their willingness to underwrite — to commit capital before the ultimate buyers are identified — is what allows the primary market to function. When arrangers retreat from underwriting, as occurred in 2008 and again briefly in 2022, the primary market freezes.

**Institutional Investors**

The buyers of leveraged loans and high-yield bonds are predominantly institutional:

- **CLOs (Collateralised Loan Obligations)**: Structured vehicles that purchase leveraged loans, hold them in a portfolio, and finance the portfolio with tranches of debt securities and equity sold to outside investors. CLOs are by some margin the largest single buyer of leveraged loans, accounting for roughly 60–70% of demand. The structural constraints embedded in CLO documentation directly shape how loans are structured at issuance.
- **Credit Funds**: Dedicated investment funds focused on leveraged credit, typically managed by alternative asset managers.
- **Mutual Funds and ETFs**: Retail-accessible vehicles offering high-yield exposure.
- **Insurance Companies and Pension Funds**: Long-term investors seeking yield in excess of what investment-grade securities provide.
- **Hedge Funds**: Active investors pursuing relative-value, event-driven, or distressed strategies.

**The Supporting Cast**

A number of intermediaries provide the operational infrastructure that allows the market to function:

- **Administrative Agents**: Banks that process drawdowns, monitor compliance with covenants, and manage communications with the lender syndicate.
- **Trustees**: Independent fiduciaries acting on behalf of bondholders. Unlike administrative agents in the loan market, bond trustees do not negotiate on substantive matters; they act only at the direction of bondholders.
- **Legal Counsel**: Each party to a transaction retains its own counsel. In a syndicated transaction, multiple firms are typically involved on each side.
- **Rating Agencies**: S&P, Moody's, and Fitch assign the credit ratings that determine which investors can hold the debt and at what regulatory cost.

## 1.9 The Leveraged Buyout

The leveraged buyout — the LBO — is the canonical leveraged finance transaction, and an understanding of its mechanics provides essential context for the remainder of the book.

**Mechanics**

A private equity sponsor acquires a target company using a combination of equity (typically 30–50% of the purchase price) and debt (the remainder). The debt is raised at the level of the acquired company, secured against the assets and cash flows of that company, and serviced and repaid from those cash flows after the acquisition closes.

The economic logic is straightforward: debt reduces the equity cheque required to fund the acquisition, which amplifies equity returns relative to an all-equity purchase.

A worked example illustrates the point. Assume a sponsor acquires a company for €1 billion using €400 million of equity and €600 million of debt. Five years later, the sponsor sells the company for €1.5 billion, having repaid €200 million of debt during the holding period. The equity at exit is therefore €1.5 billion of sale proceeds less €400 million of remaining debt — €1.1 billion. Against the original €400 million of equity invested, that represents a 175% return on equity, or roughly 22% per annum on an unlevered basis.

Had the same acquisition been funded entirely with €1 billion of equity, the same exit at €1.5 billion would have produced a 50% return on equity, or roughly 8% per annum.

Leverage has therefore amplified equity returns by a factor of approximately three.

The amplification operates symmetrically on the downside. If the company's enterprise value falls to €800 million instead of rising to €1.5 billion, the equity at exit is €800 million less €400 million of remaining debt — €400 million, an outcome equal to the original investment with no return. With all-equity financing, the same value decline would have produced a 20% loss on €1 billion, or €800 million returned. Leverage has converted a moderate loss in one scenario into a complete loss of return in the other.

**Typical Capital Structure**

A representative LBO capital structure includes the following layers, in descending order of seniority:

- **Revolving Credit Facility (RCF)**: A committed but largely undrawn facility used for working capital and liquidity management. Senior secured. Provided primarily by relationship banks.
- **Term Loan B (TLB)**: The core senior secured term debt of the structure. Floating rate, typically priced over a benchmark (SOFR or EURIBOR), with a six- to seven-year maturity. Syndicated to institutional investors, principally CLOs and credit funds.
- **Senior Secured Notes**: High-yield bonds that may sit alongside or, in some structures, replace the TLB. Fixed-rate, typically with a longer tenor than the TLB (seven or eight years).
- **Subordinated Debt**: Second lien loans, mezzanine, or holdco PIK notes. Provides additional leverage at higher cost, structurally and contractually subordinated to the senior layer.
- **Equity**: The sponsor's invested capital, often supplemented by management rollover and, in some cases, co-investment from limited partners.

**Why the LBO Matters for the Rest of the Book**

The LBO structure shapes virtually every aspect of leveraged finance documentation. Covenants exist to protect lenders from sponsor behaviour that would benefit equity at the expense of creditors — excessive distributions, asset stripping, the layering on of additional priming debt, or the migration of value to subsidiaries that sit outside the credit perimeter.

The tension between sponsor flexibility and creditor protection is the central commercial and legal dynamic of every transaction. The covenant chapters that follow examine, in detail, the rules that have been developed to manage this tension and the techniques that sponsors and their counsel have developed to work within and around those rules.

Understanding the LBO is therefore essential context for understanding why leveraged finance covenants exist in the form they do, and why they are so heavily contested in negotiation.

## 1.10 What Lies Ahead

The remainder of the book moves from foundational concepts to detailed technical analysis, organised in five parts.

**Part I: Foundations** establishes the building blocks. Subsequent chapters in this part examine the leveraged finance ecosystem and the participants within it; the economics of leverage and how it amplifies equity returns; the anatomy of a transaction from origination through syndication; and the differences between loans and bonds as financing instruments. EBITDA — the deceptively simple metric that underpins virtually every calculation in the market — receives sustained attention.

**Part II: The Instruments** examines loans and bonds in detail. The two instruments serve overlapping economic purposes but differ materially in their structures, documentation, voting mechanics, and behaviour in distress. Each difference is examined and its commercial significance explained.

**Part III: Covenants** is the technical core of the book. Covenants are the contractual rules of engagement — the provisions that govern what a borrower can and cannot do while creditors' capital is at risk. Each major covenant category is addressed in turn: restricted payments, debt and liens, asset sales, change of control, guarantees and collateral. The interaction between covenants, the recurring carveouts, and the techniques developed to operate within their boundaries are all examined in detail.

**Part IV: Amendment Mechanics** addresses what happens when one or more parties wish to change the rules. Voting thresholds, sacred rights, consent solicitations, and the procedural differences between loan amendments and bond consent solicitations together determine who controls the documentation and under what circumstances. These mechanics are central to many of the most contested transactions in the modern market.

**Part V: When Things Go Wrong** covers restructuring. Amend-and-extend transactions, liability management exercises, distressed exchanges, and formal insolvency proceedings are all addressed. This is the part of the book in which documentation is tested against adversarial circumstances, where the protections that lenders believed they had either hold or fail, and where the practical meaning of "one way or another" is made concrete.

---

The objective throughout is to balance technical rigour with practical context. Legal primers on individual covenants and instruments are widely available. The contribution this book aims to make is different: a unified technical treatment that explains how the components of a leveraged finance transaction fit together, why they take the forms they do, and how they perform when stressed.

There are no absolute truths in leveraged finance. There are, however, recurring patterns, governing principles, and structural logic that, once understood, permit informed analysis of any new transaction. Articulating those patterns and that logic is the purpose of the chapters that follow.
